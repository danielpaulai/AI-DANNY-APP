## The core principle

When you tell a client what they're getting, tell them what they're NOT getting in the same breath.

The clients who succeed are the ones who showed up knowing exactly what the engagement was and wasn't — before they paid, before they signed, before the first session started.

## Why this matters

Misaligned expectations kill client relationships faster than bad delivery.

A founder books a personal brand engagement expecting you to ghostwrite 30 posts a month. You thought you were building them a system they'd run themselves. Three weeks in, they're angry. You're confused. The money's already spent. The relationship's already broken.

Or they expect instant LinkedIn virality. You deliver a content strategy that compounds over 90 days. Week two they say "this isn't working" and ghost you. You never get the case study. They never get the outcome.

The cost of unclear expectations is real. Refunds. Scope creep. Resentment. Testimonials that will never get written. Referrals that will never come.

The leverage of getting it right is also real. When a client knows what to expect, they show up prepared. They do the pre-work. They bring the right questions. They don't waste your time or theirs on things that were never in scope.

And when they win, they attribute the win to the right thing: the system you built together, not magic you performed for them.

## How it shows up in client work

The most common pattern is the founder who books a personal brand build thinking they're hiring a ghostwriter.

They've seen other agencies promise "done-for-you content." They assume that's what you do too, even if you never said it.

Three weeks into the engagement, they realise they're the one writing the posts. You built the system. You gave them the voice DNA. You set up the Claude Skills. But they still have to press the buttons.

That's when the "I thought you were doing this for me" conversation happens.

The fix is setting the expectation in the sales call, the proposal, the onboarding doc, and the first session. Not once. Four times. Each time in slightly different language.

In the sales call, I say: "You're not hiring me to write your posts. You're hiring me to build you a system that writes posts in your voice, faster than you can manually, and you'll own it when we're done."

In the proposal, I write: "This is a done-WITH-you engagement, not done-FOR-you. You will write, review, and approve every piece of content. I will build the tools that make that process take 2 hours a week instead of 12."

In the onboarding doc, I put: "Week 1 homework: Record a 15-minute brain dump on your three pillar topics. This is required before Session 2. If you don't ship it, the engagement pauses."

In the first session, I show them the Claude interface and say: "By the end of this call, you will have written a post. Not me. You. Using the system we're building together."

Same message. Four different moments. Repetition is not inefficiency. It's insurance.

Another pattern: the client who expects instant results.

They book a 90-day personal brand engagement and expect inbound calls in week one. That's not how compounding works. Content needs to build trust. Trust needs to stack over weeks. Weeks need to turn into months.

If you don't set that expectation up front, they quit at week three because "nothing's happening."

The script I use: "Here's what the first 30 days will feel like. You'll post consistently. You'll get some likes. You won't book calls yet. That's normal. The pipeline builds in weeks 6-8. The calls come in weeks 10-12. If you judge this at week 3, you'll think it's not working. It is."

Cited from [[Sales_call_2025_03_14]] where a founder asked "how fast will I see results" and I gave him that exact timeline. He stayed through the full 90 days. Booked five calls by week 11.

A third pattern: the client who thinks you'll do their thinking for them.

They show up to a strategy session expecting you to tell them what their positioning should be. What their offer should be. What their ICP should be.

You can't.

You can ask the questions that surface the answer. You can hold up a mirror. You can name the pattern. But the positioning lives in their head, not yours.

If you let them think you're going to hand them a positioning doc without their input, the doc will sit in their inbox forever. They won't use it because they don't own it.

The fix: In the first session, say this: "I'm not going to tell you who your ICP is. You're going to tell me. I'm going to ask you questions until the answer becomes obvious to you. Then we'll write it down together."

Cited from [[Positioning_session_2024_11_06]] with a client who came in expecting me to "just tell him" what his niche should be. I didn't. I asked him to name his last five paying clients. He named them. I asked what they had in common. He saw the pattern. We wrote the ICP together. He used it.

A fourth pattern: the client who thinks AI means "set it and forget it."

They hear "AI-native content system" and think the system runs itself. It doesn't.

Claude doesn't know when your positioning changes. It doesn't know when you launch a new offer. It doesn't know when your ICP shifts. You have to update the Voice DNA. You have to feed it new examples. You have to review the output and say "this is off-brand."

If you don't tell them that up front, they'll blame the system when it drifts. Or worse, they'll abandon it and go back to typing from scratch.

The script: "This system works as long as you keep it updated. Once a quarter, you'll spend 30 minutes reviewing the Voice DNA and adding new examples. If you don't, the output will start to sound generic. That's not the AI failing. That's you not feeding it."

Cited from [[AICEO_engagement_2025_02_18]] where a client came back after three months saying "Claude doesn't sound like me anymore." I checked the Voice DNA. It hadn't been updated since launch. I made him record a new 10-minute brain dump. Updated the doc. Output quality came back immediately.

A fifth pattern: the workshop client who thinks they'll leave with a fully-built system.

They book a 90-minute AI workshop expecting to walk out with a complete marketing stack. That's not physically possible.

What they CAN walk out with: one working Claude Skill, tested on their own content, with a clear next step for the other four.

If you promise the full stack, you'll overpromise and underdeliver. If you promise one Skill and deliver one Skill that actually works, you'll overdeliver.

The script: "In this session, we're building ONE Skill: your Content Strategist. By end of session, it will be live in your Claude account, you'll have run it on your own content, and you'll have a working 30-day content calendar. The other Skills — Writer, Editor, Designer, Analyst — we'll scope out together, and you can build them using the same template after the session."

Cited from [[Workshop_NAS_2025_04_12]] where a founder asked if we'd build all five Skills in 90 minutes. I said no. We built the Strategist. He tested it. It worked. He built the other four himself the next week using the template. Better outcome than if I'd rushed through all five and none of them worked.

A sixth pattern: the founder who expects you to fix their business problems with content.

They're not closing deals. Their product positioning is weak. Their pricing is wrong. Their offer doesn't convert.

They think a better LinkedIn presence will fix it. It won't.

Content amplifies what's already working. It doesn't fix what's broken.

If you take their money without diagnosing the real problem first, you'll deliver a working content system that doesn't move the business. Then they'll blame you.

The fix: In the discovery call, ask "What happens when someone books a call with you? Do they buy?" If the answer is "not really," the problem isn't content. It's offer, pricing, or sales process. Fix that first, THEN build the content system.

Cited from [[Discovery_call_2025_01_20]] where a founder wanted a content build. I asked about his close rate. He said 1 in 10. I told him we're not starting with content. We're starting with his offer and his sales call. Built those first. Close rate went to 4 in 10. THEN we built the content system. It worked because the foundation was fixed.

A seventh pattern: the client who thinks "AI-native" means cheap.

They see AI tools demoed on Twitter. They think "this should be $500, not $5,000."

They don't understand what they're actually paying for: the system design, the Voice DNA build, the Skills architecture, the business integration, the teaching so they can run it themselves.

If you don't frame the value correctly in the sales call, they'll think you're overcharging for "just using Claude."

The script: "You're not paying for Claude access. You're paying for a system that takes 12 hours of marketing work and compresses it into 2 hours. That's 40 hours a month saved. If your time is worth <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->00/hour, that's $8,000 a month in value. The engagement pays for itself in week one."

Cited from [[Objection_handling_2024_12_15]] where a founder said "Why would I pay you when I can just use ChatGPT?" I walked him through the math. He signed the next day.

## Specific examples from the vault

A client booked a personal brand build expecting I'd write his About section, his headline, his featured section, and his posts for the first month.

That's not the engagement.

I told him in the onboarding email: "Session 1, we're going to BUILD your About section together. You'll talk, I'll type, we'll refine it live. By end of session, it's done. Session 2, same with your headline. Session 3, we're building the Voice DNA. Session 4, we're building the Content Strategist Skill. By Session 5, you're writing posts yourself using the system."

He showed up to Session 1 expecting me to have drafted something in advance. I hadn't. I asked him the questions. He answered. I typed. We refined. The About section was done by minute 35.

He said "Oh, I thought you'd write this and send it to me." I said "If I did that, you wouldn't own it. And you'd never be able to update it yourself when your positioning changes."

He got it.

But the lesson is clear: even when you SAY it, they don't always HEAR it. Which means you need to say it again in the proposal, again in the onboarding doc, and again in the first session.

## Common mistakes

**Assuming they understood the engagement because they signed the proposal.**

Signing doesn't mean understanding. It means they wanted to move forward. The real understanding happens in the first session when they realise what the work actually looks like.

**Waiting until Session 3 to clarify scope.**

By Session 3, they've already formed their own mental model of what the engagement is. If it doesn't match yours, you're now CORRECTING their expectation instead of SETTING it. Correction feels like bait-and-switch. Setting feels like clarity.

**Not repeating the same expectation in multiple formats.**

You say it once in the sales call. They forget. You need to say it in the proposal, the onboarding email, the first session, and the Session 2 recap. Repetition is not redundancy. It's reinforcement.

**Letting them assume you'll do the thinking for them.**

If you don't explicitly say "You will answer these questions, not me," they'll expect you to hand them the answers. Then they won't own the output. Then they won't use it.

**Promising outcomes you can't control.**

"You'll get 10 inbound calls a month" is a promise you can't keep. You don't control their offer, their sales process, their pricing, or their market. What you CAN promise: "You'll have a system that produces consistent, on-brand content in 2 hours a week." That's in your control.

**Not setting the expectation for maintenance.**

AI systems need feeding. Voice DNA needs updating. Skills need refining. If you don't tell them that up front, they'll think the system should run itself forever. Then they'll blame you when it drifts.

## The contrarian read

Most agencies under-promise and over-deliver. I think that's wrong.

Under-promising sets a low bar. The client pays for the low bar. When you over-deliver, they're pleasantly surprised, but they didn't pay for the surprise. You just gave away value for free.

Better approach: Promise exactly what you'll deliver. Deliver exactly what you promised. No more, no less.

If you promise a 30-day content calendar and deliver a 90-day calendar, you trained them to expect free upgrades. Next engagement, they'll expect the 90-day version at the 30-day price.

If you promise a 30-day calendar and deliver a 30-day calendar that works, you trained them to trust you. Next engagement, they'll pay for the 90-day version because they trust you'll deliver it.

Another contrarian read: I don't believe in "exceeding expectations."

Expectations should be accurate, not conservative. If you set accurate expectations and meet them, that's success. If you set low expectations and exceed them, you lied in the sales process.

The goal is not to surprise the client. The goal is to align with the client so thoroughly that there are zero surprises.

## What it looks like when it's working

The client shows up to Session 1 with their homework done. They recorded the brain dump. They filled out the ICP worksheet. They read the onboarding doc.

They don't say "I thought you were doing this for me." They say "Where do we start?"

The client doesn't ghost at week 3 because "nothing's happening yet." They stay through week 12 because you told them results take 8-10 weeks and they believed you.

The client doesn't complain that they're "doing all the work." They thank you for making them do the work because now they own the system.

The engagement ends with a case study, not a refund request. The client books a follow-up engagement because they trust you'll deliver what you say.

You never have a "scope clarification" conversation mid-engagement. Everything you're doing was already in the proposal, the onboarding doc, and the first session recap.
