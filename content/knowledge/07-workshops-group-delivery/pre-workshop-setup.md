## The core principle

Pre-workshop setup is not logistics. It's the first 30% of the result.

Most founders treat setup as admin work—upload the slides, send the Zoom link, hope people show up. Then they wonder why half the room drops off by Day 2, why nobody buys, why the energy feels flat from minute one.

The setup IS the workshop. It's where you lock in the show-up rate, pre-frame the sale, seed the next step, and turn strangers into a community before anyone clicks Join.

## Why this matters

The difference between a 52% show-up rate and a 70% show-up rate is <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->0K in a $49 workshop with 50 registrations.

The difference between attendees who arrive cold and attendees who arrive primed is whether they buy on Day 2 or ghost you after.

Setup done right means people show up expecting to buy, not expecting free content. It means they've already done the pre-work, already introduced themselves, already committed publicly to shipping the outcome. By the time you hit record on Day 1, the room is warm, the sale is seeded, and half the objections are handled.

Setup done wrong means you spend the first 45 minutes of a two-hour workshop troubleshooting Claude installation errors while paying attendees sit in silence wondering if this was worth $49.

The cost shows up three places. First, show-up rate. Every percentage point below 60% is pipeline you paid to acquire and then burned. Second, energy. A cold room that hasn't bonded stays cold. Third, close rate. If the first time they hear the upsell is on Day 2, it's too late. They needed to hear it in Email 3.

## How it shows up in client work

The pattern I see most often is founders who think setup is one Zoom link email sent 10 minutes before the event.

Then they're shocked when 28 of 51 registered attendees show up, and of those 28, half are still installing Claude 20 minutes into Day 1 [[Workshop campaign analysis and results - 8e33619f]].

The 2 hours you lose to installation troubleshooting on Day 1 is a $5K mistake if it kills the momentum going into the close on Day 2.

That's why the workshop setup I built for the June 2026 "Run Your Marketing and Sales on Autopilot With Claude" event includes a Pre-Call on T-1 [[Workshop setup - 50e1cd6b]]. Not optional. Mandatory.

The Pre-Call handles three jobs. First, it walks every attendee through Claude setup in real time so Day 1 starts with working tools, not tech support. Second, it collects one public commitment from each person: what they'll ship by end of Day 2. Third, it introduces them to each other in breakout rooms so they arrive on Day 1 already knowing three names.

The result: Day 1 starts on time, with energy, with tools working, with a room that feels like a team instead of a Zoom grid of strangers.

Another pattern: founders who send one reminder email the morning of Day 1 and call it setup.

The system I run is six show-up emails from T-6 through T-0 [[Workshop setup - 50e1cd6b]]. Not reminders. Show-up emails.

Email 1 at T-6: "You're in. Here's what to expect."
Email 2 at T-5: "The one thing you need to do before Day 1."
Email 3 at T-3: "Meet your breakout group."
Email 4 at T-2: "Tomorrow at 12:00. Here's the agenda."
Email 5 at T-1: "Pre-Call tonight at 20:00. Install your tools."
Email 6 at T-0: "Starting in 30 minutes. Click here."

Each email does one job. Each email assumes the previous one wasn't read. Each email earns its open by delivering one piece of value they can't get anywhere else.

The Pre-Call email doesn't say "join if you want." It says "we're doing Claude setup together at 20:00. If you miss this, you'll be behind on Day 1."

The real move in the setup phase is seeding the upsell before Day 1 starts.

In the June 2026 workshop, the Standard ticket was <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->,225 and the Premium was $3,997 [[Workshop setup - 50e1cd6b]]. The Premium included post-workshop implementation support, a private Slack, and three 1:1 calls.

The upsell wasn't mentioned in public marketing. It was seeded in Email 3 of the show-up sequence: "A few people asked about deeper support after the workshop. If that's you, reply to this email and I'll send details."

By the time we hit the close on Day 2, 12 people had already raised their hand. The close rate on hot leads was 41.7% because they self-selected before the pitch [[Workshop campaign analysis and results - 8e33619f]].

The other thing that shows up in client work: founders who don't collect attendee data before Day 1.

I run a Pre-Call signup form that asks three questions: What's your business? What's the one thing you want to walk away with? What's blocking you right now?

Those three answers become the opening of Day 1. "I read all your Pre-Call answers. Here's what I saw. Half of you said X. A third said Y. Two people said Z. We're solving all three today."

The room feels seen before you've said anything else.

The last pattern I see is founders who treat the attendee workbook as a PDF they email 10 minutes before the session.

The workbook is a pre-framing document. It should arrive at T-3 with instructions: "Print this. Bring it to Day 1. We're filling it out together."

The workbook I built for the Autopilot workshop has six sections: Current State Assessment, The Autopilot Blueprint, Skills You'll Build, Weekly Workflow Template, 90-Day Roadmap, and Next Steps [[Workshop setup - 50e1cd6b]].

By the time they hit Page 6, they've already written down what they'll buy on Day 2. They just don't know it yet.

## Specific examples from the vault

The May 2026 "Build a Business That Runs By Itself using Claude" workshop had 51 registrations against a target of 40 [[Workshop campaign analysis and results - 8e33619f]].

Show-up rate was 52%. 31 peak attendees on Day 1. That's 20 people who paid and didn't show.

Why? Because the Pre-Call wasn't mandatory. It was offered as optional on T-1. Only 14 people joined. The other 37 showed up cold on Day 1, half of them without Claude installed, half of them not clear on what the workshop was even about.

The first 45 minutes of Day 1 was spent troubleshooting API keys and walking people through Desktop app setup. By the time we got to content, energy was flat.

Day 1 to Day 2 retention was 93%, which is strong. But we lost 20 people before Day 1 even started because setup wasn't treated as non-negotiable.

The fix for June 2026: Pre-Call moved to mandatory. Show-up sequence rebuilt to frame the Pre-Call as "this is where you get your tools working so Day 1 starts with strategy, not setup" [[Workshop setup - 50e1cd6b]].

Target show-up rate: 60%. That's 6 more paying attendees in the room, which at <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->,225 Standard pricing is $7,350 in additional revenue from setup alone.

Another example: the email campaign for the May workshop sent 22,383 emails at 19.27% open rate [[Workshop campaign analysis and results - 8e33619f]].

But the show-up sequence wasn't tracked separately from the promotional sequence. We don't know which of the six show-up emails drove Day 1 attendance and which were ignored.

The June system fixes this. Every show-up email gets its own open/click tracking. The goal is 40%+ open rate on the T-0 email (the one sent 30 minutes before start time). If that email opens below 30%, the room will be half-empty.

The DM outreach for May sent 1,000 DMs at 0.5% close rate [[Workshop campaign analysis and results - 8e33619f]]. That's 5 registrations from 1,000 DMs.

The lesson: DMs are not a registration channel. They're an awareness channel. The DM job is to get the lead onto the email list so the show-up sequence can do the real work.

The testimonial gap in May was critical. The Campaign Metrics tab showed 0 testimonials while the Testimonials tab had 5 filled [[Workshop campaign analysis and results - 8e33619f]]. That's an audit trail issue, but it's also a setup issue.

If you don't collect testimonials immediately after the workshop ends, you lose them. The June system includes a T+0 email sent 2 hours after Day 2 ends: "You just shipped X. Reply with one line about what changed."

Those replies become the testimonials for the next workshop. Setup for the next event starts the moment the current event ends.

## Common mistakes

Mistake 1: Treating the Pre-Call as optional.

If it's optional, half the room skips it. Then you lose the first hour of Day 1 to tech support. The Pre-Call must be framed as mandatory: "If you miss this, you'll be behind."

Mistake 2: Sending one reminder email the morning of Day 1.

One email is not enough. The show-up sequence needs six emails from T-6 through T-0. Each one assumes the previous one wasn't read. Each one delivers one new piece of value.

Mistake 3: Not collecting attendee data before Day 1.

If you don't know what they want to walk away with, you can't open Day 1 by naming it. The Pre-Call form should ask: What's your business? What do you want to ship? What's blocking you?

Mistake 4: Waiting until Day 2 to mention the upsell.

If they don't know the upsell exists until the pitch, it feels like a bait-and-switch. Seed it in Email 3: "A few people asked about deeper support. If that's you, reply." By Day 2, the hand-raisers are already qualified.

Mistake 5: Emailing the workbook 10 minutes before the session.

The workbook is a pre-framing tool. It should arrive at T-3 with clear instructions: print it, bring it, fill it out during the workshop. If it arrives 10 minutes before start time, nobody prints it.

Mistake 6: Not tracking show-up email performance separately.

If you don't know which of the six show-up emails drove attendance, you can't optimize the sequence. Track open rate and click rate on every email. The T-0 email should open above 40%.

## The contrarian read

Most workshop operators think setup is about reducing friction. Send the link. Make it easy to join. Remove every barrier.

I think setup is about adding friction in the right places.

The Pre-Call is friction. Asking people to print a 12-page workbook is friction. Requiring a public commitment on the Pre-Call is friction.

But that friction filters for commitment. The people who skip the Pre-Call are the same people who won't do the work on Day 1. The people who print the workbook are the same people who buy on Day 2.

You don't want a room full of people who showed up because clicking a Zoom link was easy. You want a room full of people who did the pre-work because they're serious about the outcome.

The other contrarian take: most people think setup ends when the workshop starts.

Setup ends when the testimonial is collected. The T+0 post-workshop email is setup for the next event. The immediate follow-up with hand-raisers is setup for the close. The Day 3 LinkedIn post with client results is setup for the next registration campaign.

Setup is not a phase. It's a system that loops.

## What it looks like when it's working

Show-up rate above 60%. In a 50-person workshop, that's 30+ people in the room on Day 1 instead of 26.

Day 1 starts on time with tools working. No installation troubleshooting. No "can everyone hear me?" for 10 minutes. You hit record and dive straight into content.

The room feels warm before you've introduced yourself. People know three names from the Pre-Call breakout rooms. They've already committed publicly to shipping an outcome. The energy is live.

By the time you hit the upsell on Day 2, 10-15 people have already raised their hand in Email 3. The close rate on hot leads is 40%+.

Testimonials arrive within 24 hours of the workshop ending because you asked in the T+0 email while the result is still fresh.

The next workshop fills faster because the setup system from the previous event fed the pipeline.

Observable numbers: 60%+ show-up rate, 40%+ open rate on T-0 email, 90%+ Day 1 to Day 2 retention, 10+ hand-raisers before the pitch, 5+ testimonials collected within 48 hours.
