## The core principle

Price is a positioning choice, not a math problem. Every cohort you run teaches you what your market will actually pay, not what you think they should pay.

## Why this matters

Most founders underprice their first offer, then lock themselves into a volume trap they can't escape. They promise "accessible" pricing, book 30 people at $99, deliver 40 hours of work, and burn out before cohort two ships.

The cost of getting pricing wrong compounds across cohorts. Cohort one at $99 trains your market to expect $99. Cohort two at <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->99 looks like a bait-and-switch. Cohort three never launches because you're still delivering cohort one's lifetime support.

The leverage of treating each cohort as a pricing experiment is that you learn what value the market assigns to your outcome, not your effort. A $5K cohort that sells three seats teaches you more than a $99 cohort that sells 50. The $5K buyers tell you what they're actually paying for. The $99 buyers tell you nothing except that people like cheap things.

When you experiment deliberately across cohorts, you compress years of market education into months. You find the price ceiling, the willingness threshold, the discount anchor that doesn't cannibalize future sales. You learn which bonuses move buyers and which bonuses add cost without adding conversions.

The founders who nail pricing early run fewer cohorts, serve fewer people, and bank more revenue. The ones who guess wrong run themselves into the ground serving an audience that can't afford what the offer actually costs to deliver.

## How it shows up in client work

The pattern I see most often: founder launches cohort one at "accessible" pricing, hits capacity, delivers great results, then tries to raise the price for cohort two and conversion falls off a cliff. They assume the market rejected the new price. Actually, the market rejected the positioning mismatch. Cohort one was positioned as "affordable access." Cohort two was positioned as "premium transformation" but still marketed to the same $99 audience.

One client ran a design academy concept. First instinct was to target college students and first-job seekers at low ticket. The thinking: "I want to help people who can't afford expensive courses." Noble intent, wrong business model. We mapped the delivery cost. Fifteen hours of live sessions, portfolio reviews, one-on-one feedback. At $99 per seat, that's $6.60 per hour of instructor time before platform costs, before marketing, before support. The math doesn't work unless you're running a 500-person cohort, which kills the intimacy that makes the offer valuable in the first place.

The reframe: charge <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->K, target mid-career designers who already have jobs and want to specialize or transition into UX leadership. Smaller cohort, higher engagement, same instructor hours, 20x revenue per cohort. The offer didn't change. The price and the ICP did. Another client ran workshops at $69 early-bird, $99 regular. Sold 150 tickets across three workshops in two months. Delivered great sessions, got testimonials, made <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->2K total revenue. Then looked at the actual cost: 60 hours of prep, delivery, and follow-up per workshop. That's $66 per hour before taxes and platform fees. They were running a high-effort, low-margin content treadmill disguised as a workshop business.

The experiment: cohort four at $499, cap at 30 seats, add a 90-day implementation track with three group calls post-workshop. Sold 22 seats, <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->0,978 revenue, same prep time, triple the engagement because buyers at $499 show up and do the work. Buyers at $69 treat it like a Netflix subscription. The pricing experiment that broke through for most clients: stop anchoring to competitor pricing, start anchoring to client outcome value. If your workshop helps a founder compress 12 hours of content work into 2 hours per week, that's 10 hours saved. At a <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->00/hour founder rate, that's <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->K/week, $8K/month in reclaimed time value. A <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->K workshop that delivers that outcome is cheap. A $99 workshop that delivers the same outcome trains the market to undervalue the result.

I've also seen the reverse mistake: pricing too high too early without proof of outcome. One client launched a $5K cohort with zero testimonials, no case studies, no social proof. The offer was solid, the positioning was sharp, but the market didn't know them yet. Zero sales in 30 days. We didn't drop the price. We ran a $999 pilot cohort, capped at 10 people, explicitly marketed as "founding member pricing, testimonials required." Sold out in 10 days. Delivered, captured results, raised to <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->,999 for cohort two with proof. Sold 12 seats. Cohort three at $5K with case studies. Sold 8. Same founder, same offer, different proof level at each price point.

## Common mistakes

**Anchoring to competitor pricing instead of outcome value.** You see someone running workshops at $99, so you price yours at <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->29 to "position slightly higher." You're still in the same pricing band, which means you're competing on cost, not transformation. Your buyers will comparison-shop across every $99–<!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->99 workshop. They'll pick based on the instructor's follower count or the bonus workbook design, not the outcome you promise.

**Discounting to drive urgency instead of building proof to justify price.** Early-bird $99, regular <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->49, last-chance <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->99. You're training your market to wait for the discount. The buyers who pay <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->99 will see the next cohort's $99 early-bird and feel ripped off. You're eroding trust every time you drop the price. Build the proof, raise the price, keep it there.

**Selling to an audience that can't afford the offer you're actually delivering.** Targeting college students for a <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->K design cohort. Targeting broke founders for a <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->0K implementation program. The market doesn't owe you a sale. If your ICP is broke, your offer is mispriced or mis-targeted.

**Locking in lifetime pricing for early buyers, then regretting it.** "Get in now at founding member pricing, locked in forever." Great for the founding members. Terrible for you when cohort five is $5K and the founding members are still paying $999. Either time-limit the lock-in or don't offer it at all.

**Running a profit-share model when you don't control the client's execution.** "I'll take 10% of your revenue increase." You deliver the strategy, the client doesn't implement it, you get nothing, and you spent 40 hours on the engagement. Charge for the value you deliver, not the value they might capture if they do the work.

**Raising price without raising the offer.** Cohort one at $999, cohort two at <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->,999, same offer, no new bonuses, no new guarantees, no new proof. The market sees a price increase with no value increase. Conversion dies. If you raise the price, add proof, add a guarantee, add a deliverable, or narrow the ICP to buyers who value it more.

## The contrarian read

Most pricing advice says "test low, learn, then raise the price." I'd flip it: test high, learn who says yes, then decide if you need to expand down-market.

When you start low, you attract price-sensitive buyers who will never pay high ticket. You learn how to sell to broke people. When you start high, you attract outcome-focused buyers who will tell you exactly what's missing from your offer to justify the price. You learn how to sell to people who have money and problems.

The other contrarian take: profit-share models almost never work for service businesses. They sound founder-friendly. "I only make money if you make money." In practice, they misalign incentives. You do all the work upfront for a fraction of the fee, then chase payments from clients who ghost, quit, or blame you when results don't land. The client has no skin in the game. You have all of it.

The cleanest model: charge what the outcome is worth, guarantee it, keep working until it ships. The client pays in full, you deliver in full, no one chases anyone.

## What it looks like when it's working

You raise your price and conversions hold steady or improve. The buyers at the higher price point show up, do the work, and get results. The ones who ghost or complain disappear, because they self-selected out when they saw the price.

Your cohorts get smaller but more profitable. Cohort one at $99 × 50 people = $4,950. Cohort four at <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->,999 × 8 people = <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->3,992. Same instructor hours, 5x revenue, better engagement, better testimonials.

You stop apologizing for the price. You don't offer discounts, you don't run "last chance" sales, you don't panic when someone says "too expensive." You say "Here's the outcome, here's the guarantee, here's the price. Yes or no?"

You book discovery calls with qualified buyers. The $99 audience asks "What's included?" The $5K audience asks "What's the result and how fast can I get it?" The questions tell you whether your pricing filtered correctly.

Your testimonials include revenue numbers, time saved, and outcomes shipped. The $99 cohort says "Great course, loved the content." The $5K cohort says "Booked six clients in 90 days, $48K closed, system is still running."
