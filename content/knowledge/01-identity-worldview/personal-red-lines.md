## The core principle

I will not build for someone I wouldn't work for as a person.

This line decides everything downstream: who I pitch, who I onboard, what I say yes to, what I walk away from. The work and the relationship cannot be separated. If I can't respect the person across the table, the offer doesn't matter.

## Why this matters

Personal red lines are the operating system underneath the business.

Without them, you optimize for revenue and end up trapped in work that drains you. You take clients who argue over invoices, question every decision, demand rewrites on finished work, or want you to sound like someone you're not.

The cost shows up slowly. You start editing yourself. You write in a voice that isn't yours. You pitch ideas you don't believe in. You justify it by saying "it's just business" but the compromise leaks into everything else. The work gets worse. The brand gets muddy. The clients you actually want start sensing something is off.

The leverage of getting this right is existential. When you know what you will not do, the entire sales process changes. You stop chasing. You disqualify fast. You say "this isn't a fit" without flinching. The clients who stay are the ones who respect the line. Those are the only relationships that scale.

Red lines create clarity. Clarity attracts the right people. The right people become the case studies that attract more right people. That's the flywheel.

## How it shows up in client work

The most common red line violation I see is the client who wants to sound smarter than they are.

They send me a draft written in corporate jargon, packed with "synergy" and "leverage" and "robust solutions," and when I rewrite it in plain English they push back. They say "this sounds too simple." What they mean is: this doesn't make me look impressive.

That's a dealbreaker. I work with founders who want to be understood, not founders who want to sound clever. The moment someone prioritizes how they're perceived over whether they're clear, the engagement is over. [[Personal_Branding_-_consultation_2023_11_27]] captured this exact pattern with a client who kept adding layers of complexity to every post until nothing landed. The breakthrough came when I said: "You're not writing for your audience. You're writing for your ego." She either rewrites in her actual voice or we stop.

The second pattern: clients who refuse to be specific.

They want authority but won't name a client. They want credibility but won't cite a number. They want engagement but won't tell a real story. Every post becomes a vague generality about "the importance of mindset" or "the power of consistency." That's content theatre. It looks like work but moves nothing. [[Three-Day_Virtual_Bootcamp_(5_hours_each)_2025_12_20]] had three participants who fell into this trap during the personal story exercise. I gave them 20 self-reflection questions designed to surface real moments. They answered in abstractions. "I learned resilience." "I overcame challenges." When I asked them to name the actual challenge, the actual moment, the actual outcome, two of them shut down. One said "I'm not comfortable sharing that publicly." Fine. Then we're not building a personal brand. A personal brand without specifics is a LinkedIn profile with better lighting.

The third red line: clients who want me to write what they're afraid to say themselves.

This shows up in two ways. The first is outsourcing courage. They want a post that challenges their industry but they won't attach their name to it without three rounds of edits to soften the language. The second is identity laundering. They want to be seen as the founder who "tells it like it is" but they edit out every sharp edge before posting. [[Kruti]] hit this in week two of her LinkedIn build. She wanted to break into home furnishings leadership but every draft positioned her as a safe, agreeable candidate. I wrote a hook that said: "Most retail execs talk about customer experience. Almost none of them have worked a register." She asked me to change it to "Many retail execs have diverse experience." That's the moment I know the relationship won't scale. Either you're willing to say the thing that costs you something to say, or you're writing résumé bullets on LinkedIn.

The fourth pattern: clients who treat personal branding as a side project.

They book a consultation, nod at everything I say, agree to the exercises, then disappear for two weeks. When I follow up they say "I've been busy." That's fine. Everyone's busy. But personal branding isn't a thing you do when you have time. It's the thing that creates time. If the founder isn't prioritizing their own visibility, I'm not going to do it for them. [[Noman]] is the counterexample. He's a cybersecurity executive building a consulting practice. We mapped out a LinkedIn growth plan, content strategy, and outreach system. He executed every piece within 48 hours. That's the engagement model that works. The client who shows up gets the result. The client who "plans to show up later" never does.

The fifth red line, and the one I enforce most strictly: I will not build a brand for someone who lies.

This sounds obvious but it's shockingly common. A client will claim they "scaled a business to seven figures" and when I ask for the specifics, the revenue evaporates. Or they say they "helped hundreds of clients" and the number turns out to be six. Or they position themselves as an expert in a field they worked in for nine months. The lie doesn't have to be malicious. Most of the time it's just exaggeration creep. They rounded up. They included aspirational numbers. They confused revenue with profit. But once I catch it, the trust is gone. I will not write content that makes someone sound more credible than they are. [[Creating compelling offers with strong promises and guarantees - 1b2a681a]] ran through this exact scenario when we built the AICEO offer. I asked Danny to name the specific outcome, the specific timeframe, the specific proof. Every number had to be real. Every claim had to be defensible. The offer landed at "<!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->,999 standard, <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->,999 VIP plus $97/month ongoing" because those were the actual prices and the actual results we could prove. If he'd inflated the promise, the guarantee would have been a liability. Honest offers scale. Exaggerated offers collapse.

The final pattern: clients who want AI to replace their voice instead of amplifying it.

They come in asking for a system that "writes my posts for me so I don't have to." That's not what I build. I build systems that capture your voice so Claude can write in your tone, but the thinking still comes from you. The moment someone says "I just want to press a button and have it done," they're describing a ghostwriter relationship, not a personal brand build. [[Building nasops transcript-based documentation and email generation app - 78e52862]] is the clearest example of what the system actually does. It takes transcripts, session notes, and context, then generates documentation, emails, and summaries in Danny's voice. But the input is still Danny's work. The AI compresses the execution. It doesn't replace the strategy. Clients who want full automation don't want a personal brand. They want content theatre.

## Specific examples from the vault

The clearest red line I've drawn in the past year came during a consultation with a founder who wanted to position himself as a "thought leader in AI."

He had no clients. No case studies. No proof. When I asked what he'd actually built, he said "I'm working on a course." That's not a thought leader. That's someone with an idea. I told him: "Come back when you've helped ten people solve a real problem. Until then, you don't have a brand to build." He pushed back. He said he'd seen other founders "fake it till they make it" and it worked. I said: "Then hire one of them." [[Personal_Branding_-_consultation_2023_11_27]] ended the same way. The client wanted authority without evidence. I don't build that.

The second example: a three-day bootcamp where one participant refused to complete the 20-question personal story exercise.

She said it was "too personal" and asked if she could skip to the LinkedIn profile work. I said no. The profile work is cosmetic. The story work is foundational. If you can't articulate why you do what you do, the banner and the headline won't save you. She dropped out on day two. [[Three-Day_Virtual_Bootcamp_(5_hours_each)_2025_12_20]] documented the full sequence. The participants who finished the exercise wrote content that booked calls. The ones who skipped it posted generic updates that got likes and no business.

The third example: a client who wanted me to rewrite a carousel to make it "more viral."

The original draft was clear, specific, and true. It named a real problem, cited a real number, and offered a real solution. She said it was "too plain" and sent me examples of carousels with bold claims, dramatic language, and no proof. I rewrote one slide to match her examples. It said: "Most founders are invisible. Here's how to 10x your reach in 30 days." She loved it. I told her I wouldn't post it. The promise was fake. The timeframe was fake. The outcome was fake. She said "but it would perform better." I said: "Then post it yourself." She didn't. [[Nas.io document redesign with branding - 6b8ef772]] showed the same tension when we rebuilt session titles. Nuseir Yassin kept rejecting anything that sounded like hype. He wanted plain English. He wanted truth. The titles that passed his filter were the ones that could be defended in a conversation. That's the standard.

## Common mistakes

The first mistake: treating red lines as preferences instead of constraints.

A preference is "I'd rather not work with clients in this industry." A red line is "I will not work with clients in this industry." The difference is enforcement. Preferences get negotiated. Red lines don't. Most founders blur the distinction. They say no, then talk themselves into yes when the offer is big enough. That's how you end up in a six-month engagement with someone who makes you miserable.

The second mistake: setting red lines based on what sounds good instead of what's true.

A founder will say "I only work with mission-driven companies" but they've never turned down a client for lacking a mission. Or they say "I don't work with anyone who doesn't value personal branding" but they've taken money from three clients who posted once a month. Red lines have to cost you something or they're not real. If you've never lost a deal because of a red line, you don't have red lines. You have aspirations.

The third mistake: confusing red lines with positioning.

Positioning is who you serve. Red lines are who you won't serve. They're related but not the same. You can serve B2B SaaS founders and still refuse to work with B2B SaaS founders who lie about their revenue. The positioning attracts. The red lines filter. Both are necessary.

The fourth mistake: not naming the red line until you're already in the engagement.

You take the client. You start the work. Two weeks in, they do the thing you said you'd never tolerate. Now you're stuck. You either fire them and refund the money, or you compromise and finish the work while resenting every call. The red line should be named in the sales conversation. I ask: "Have you ever exaggerated a number on your profile?" If they hesitate, I know. If they say "just a little," I say: "This won't work." Disqualify early or suffer later.

The fifth mistake: letting someone else define your red lines.

A mentor, a coach, a peer tells you what they won't tolerate and you adopt it without testing whether it's true for you. The result is a set of borrowed principles that don't hold under pressure. Your red lines have to come from your experience. They have to be the thing you learned the hard way. If you've never been burned by a bad client, you don't know what your red lines are yet. That's fine. You'll learn.

## The contrarian read

The conventional advice says: "Take every client when you're starting out. You need the revenue. You need the testimonials. You need the experience."

That's backwards.

Taking bad clients teaches you how to serve bad clients. It trains you to tolerate behavior you should fire. It builds a portfolio of work you're not proud of. And it signals to the market that you'll work with anyone who pays.

The contrarian position: fire fast, even when you can't afford to.

I've walked away from three clients in the past 18 months. Two of them were in the first two weeks of the engagement. One of them was a <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->5K contract. Every time, someone told me I was making a mistake. Every time, the next client who signed was better. The revenue gap closed faster than I expected because I wasn't spending energy managing someone I didn't respect.

The other contrarian take: red lines make you more expensive, not less.

Most founders think boundaries shrink the market. They don't. They clarify it. When you say "I don't work with clients who won't name a specific outcome," the clients who do name outcomes trust you immediately. They know you won't waste their time. They know you've thought about this. The refusal becomes the credential.

## What it looks like when it's working

You say no without guilt.

A client asks you to soften a post and you say: "This is the version I'm comfortable with. If it's too direct for your brand, we're not a fit." They either respect the boundary or they leave. Either outcome is fine.

You stop justifying your prices.

Someone says "this is expensive" and you say: "Yes." No defence. No discount. No explanation beyond "this is what it costs." The clients who stay are the ones who see the value. The ones who leave were never going to become advocates anyway.

You attract people who respect the line.

The founder who reads your content sees how you talk about clients, how you enforce standards, how you say no. They reach out because they want someone who will hold them to the same standard. The red line becomes the reason they hire you, not the reason they don't.

Your case studies all sound like you.

Every client you've worked with speaks the same way about the engagement. They say: "Danny told me the truth when I didn't want to hear it." They say: "He made me rewrite the offer three times because the promise was vague." They say: "He wouldn't let me post anything that sounded like AI." That consistency is the brand.

You work fewer hours and close better deals.

You're not managing problem clients. You're not chasing invoices. You're not rewriting work because someone changed their mind. The clients who cross the line are gone before they cost you time. The ones who stay fund the business.
