## The core principle

The first 60 seconds of a sales call determine whether you run the conversation or the prospect runs you.

Frame-setting isn't rapport-building. It's control transfer. You're taking a prospect who came in expecting to interview you and flipping the dynamic so they're now pitching themselves as qualified to work with you.

## Why this matters

Most founders open calls the way they'd open a coffee chat: "So, tell me about your business." That's not a sales call. That's a therapy session where you're the therapist and you're working for free.

The cost of a weak open is the next 45 minutes. You spend the call answering questions about pricing, timeline, scope, deliverables. The prospect stays in buying mode. You stay in selling mode. By the end, they say "I need to think about it" because you never established that this was a mutual evaluation.

When you set the frame in 60 seconds, the prospect relaxes. They stop testing you. They start trying to qualify themselves. The entire conversation shifts from "convince me" to "here's why I'm a fit."

The leverage is in the first minute. Get that right and the close happens 40 minutes early. Get it wrong and you're in a 12-email follow-up loop where nobody wins.

## How it shows up in client work

The pattern I see most often: founder books the call, shows up on time, says "Hey, great to meet you, I saw your form—tell me what you're working on," and then wonders why the prospect asks about pricing before they've even explained the offer.

That opening handed control to the prospect. No frame was set. The founder is now a vendor being interviewed, not a specialist evaluating fit.

Here's what changes when you open with frame-setting instead.

**Pattern 1: The founder who kept getting "I need to think about it"**

Client ran a personal branding consultancy. Booked 6-8 calls a week. Closed maybe one. Every call ended the same way: prospect asks for pricing, founder sends a proposal, prospect goes quiet.

I listened to three of his recorded calls. Every single one opened with: "So, walk me through what you're looking for."

No wonder he was losing them. He was asking the prospect to set the agenda.

We rebuilt the open to this:

"Before we dive in, let me set expectations for the call. I'm going to ask you a bunch of specific questions about your business, your offer, and where you're stuck. By the end, I'll know if I can help. If I can, I'll tell you exactly what that looks like. If I can't, I'll tell you that too and point you somewhere better. Sound good?"

First call with the new open: prospect said yes, answered every question without pushback, and asked to start immediately at the end. No "I need to think about it." No proposal follow-up.

The frame did the work. [[Personal_branding_client_2024_11_08]]

**Pattern 2: The consultant who kept getting price-shopped**

Client sold cybersecurity advisory. Every prospect wanted to "compare a few options" before deciding. He kept losing to cheaper competitors even though his service was better.

The problem wasn't his pricing. It was his positioning on the call. He opened every conversation by explaining what he did. That positioned him as one option among many.

We changed the open to flip the script:

"Quick heads-up before we start: this isn't a pitch call. I don't know if we're a fit yet. What I'm going to do is ask about your current security posture, where the gaps are, and what a breach would cost you. By the end, I'll know if I can fix it. If I can, I'll tell you how. If someone else is better suited, I'll tell you that too. Work for you?"

The shift was immediate. Prospects stopped asking "how much do you charge" in the first 10 minutes. They started volunteering information. They stopped treating him like a vendor and started treating him like the specialist.

Three calls later, he closed a <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->8K engagement that he would have lost to a <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->2K competitor under the old model. [[Sales_call_2025_03_14]]

**Pattern 3: The founder who couldn't get past discovery**

Client ran AI implementation workshops for founders. Great offer, strong positioning, couldn't close. Every call felt productive but ended with "let me discuss with my co-founder."

I watched him run a live call. He spent 15 minutes explaining how the workshop worked before asking a single question about the prospect's business. By the time he got to discovery, the prospect had already decided this was a standard vendor pitch.

We stripped the intro down to 45 seconds:

"Here's how this call works. I'm going to ask about your business, what you're trying to build, and where AI fits. By the end, if it's clear a workshop would move the needle, I'll walk you through exactly what that looks like. If it's not the right fit, I'll tell you what would be. That work?"

Next call: closed in 32 minutes. No co-founder consultation. No "send me a proposal." The prospect asked to book the workshop date before the call ended.

The difference was control. The old open positioned the workshop as something to evaluate. The new open positioned the call as a mutual evaluation where the founder gets to decide if the prospect qualifies. [[Workshop_sales_2024_12_02]]

**Pattern 4: The coach who kept getting ghosted after discovery**

Client sold high-ticket business coaching. Booked plenty of calls. Great at asking questions. Terrible at closing. Every prospect said "this has been really helpful, let me think about it" and then disappeared.

The issue wasn't the questions or the offer. It was the frame. She opened with "I'd love to learn more about your business," which positioned the call as an information-gathering session, not a decision-making session.

We rebuilt the open to set a decision frame:

"Before we start, let me tell you how I run these calls. I'm going to ask specific questions about your revenue, your offer, and where you're stuck. By the end, I'll know if I can help you get unstuck. If I can, I'll tell you what working together looks like and we'll decide if it makes sense to move forward. If it doesn't, I'll point you to someone who can. Make sense?"

That one change cut her ghost rate in half. Prospects stopped treating the call as a free coaching session and started treating it as a buying decision. [[Coaching_sales_2025_01_09]]

**Pattern 5: The agency founder who kept explaining services**

Client ran a content agency for SaaS founders. Every call started with a 10-minute walkthrough of their service packages. By the time they got to discovery, the prospect had already tuned out or started price-comparing.

I told him to delete the service walkthrough entirely and open with this:

"Quick note before we dive in: I'm not here to pitch you services. I'm here to figure out if your content problem is something we can solve. I'm going to ask about your current content, what's working, what's not, and where you want to be in 90 days. If we can get you there, I'll show you how. If we can't, I'll tell you why. That cool?"

First call with the new open: prospect stopped asking "what's included in the package" and started answering questions. Call closed at 41 minutes with a signed contract. No proposal sent. No follow-up needed.

The frame positioned him as the expert evaluating fit, not the vendor explaining options. [[Agency_sales_2025_02_11]]

## Specific examples from the vault

**Example 1: The "I'll know by the end" line**

I was on a discovery call with a prospect who kept asking questions about how the engagement would work before I'd even understood their business. Classic buying-mode behaviour.

I stopped them mid-question: "Hold on. I don't know if I can help you yet. Let me ask a few things first, and I'll know by the end if this makes sense. Cool?"

They laughed, said yes, and didn't interrupt again. The rest of the call was clean. We closed the same day.

That line—"I'll know by the end"—resets the frame. It reminds the prospect that you're evaluating them, not just pitching them. [[Sales_call_2024_09_15]]

**Example 2: The "point you somewhere better" commitment**

One of my clients was losing prospects because they thought every call was a hard sell. They'd show up defensive, expecting pressure.

I added one line to his open: "If this isn't a fit, I'll tell you who would be a better fit and why."

That single line dropped the defence. Prospects relaxed. They started being honest about where they were stuck instead of performing competence. Close rate went from 20% to 60% in three weeks.

Why it works: it signals you're not desperate. You're willing to walk away if it's not right. That's expert positioning. [[Sales_coaching_2024_10_22]]

**Example 3: The "mutual evaluation" frame**

I had a prospect on a call who asked "what's your pricing structure" in the first five minutes. I hadn't even asked about their business yet.

Instead of answering, I reset: "I don't know what pricing looks like yet because I don't know if I can solve your problem. Let's figure that out first. If it's solvable, I'll tell you exactly what it costs. If it's not, I'll tell you that too. Work?"

They said yes. I got 30 uninterrupted minutes of clean discovery. By the time I quoted pricing at the end, they said yes without hesitation.

The mutual evaluation frame kills price resistance before it starts. [[Sales_call_2025_01_18]]

**Example 4: The silence after the frame**

I was training a founder on frame-setting. He delivered the frame perfectly, then immediately started talking again. He didn't let it land.

I told him: "Say the frame. Then shut up. Let them say yes."

Next call, he set the frame, went silent, and waited. The prospect said "Yeah, that makes sense," and the call shifted instantly. No pushback. No questions. Just compliance.

The silence is part of the frame. It's a micro-commitment test. If they say yes, they've agreed to your rules. If they hesitate, you know you're dealing with a control fight and you adjust. [[Sales_training_2024_11_30]]

**Example 5: The "I don't pitch" line**

One of my clients kept getting prospects who wanted him to "walk them through the service" before they'd answer any questions. He was spending 20 minutes presenting before discovery even started.

I gave him this line: "I don't do pitch calls. I do fit calls. I need to understand your business before I know if I can help. Once I do, I'll tell you exactly what working together looks like. That work for you?"

First call with that frame: prospect said "Oh, okay, yeah," and started answering questions immediately. No resistance. No presentation requested. Call closed in 35 minutes.

That line—"I don't do pitch calls"—repositions the entire interaction. You're not a vendor. You're a specialist evaluating a case. [[Sales_call_2025_02_20]]

## Scripts and exact phrases

**The full 60-second frame:**

"Before we dive in, let me set expectations for this call. I'm going to ask you specific questions about your business, your offer, and where you're stuck. By the end, I'll know if I can help. If I can, I'll tell you exactly what that looks like. If I can't, I'll tell you that too and point you somewhere better. Sound good?"

**The short version:**

"Quick heads-up: this isn't a pitch call. I'm going to ask questions to figure out if I can solve your problem. If I can, I'll show you how. If I can't, I'll tell you who can. Work for you?"

**The reframe for early price questions:**

"I don't know what pricing looks like yet because I don't know if I can solve your problem. Let's figure that out first."

**The line that kills "I need to think about it":**

"By the end of this call, I'll know if I can help. If I can, I'll tell you what working together looks like and we'll decide if it makes sense to move forward. If it doesn't, I'll point you somewhere better."

**The line that stops interruptions:**

"Hold on—I don't know if I can help you yet. Let me ask a few things first, and I'll know by the end."

**The specialist positioning line:**

"I don't do pitch calls. I do fit calls. I need to understand your business before I know if I can help."

**The mutual evaluation commitment:**

"This is a two-way evaluation. I'm figuring out if I can solve your problem. You're figuring out if you want to work with me."

**The line that signals you're not desperate:**

"If this isn't a fit, I'll tell you who would be and why."

## Common mistakes

**Mistake 1: Asking permission instead of setting the frame**

Weak: "Would it be okay if I asked you a few questions?"

Strong: "I'm going to ask you specific questions about your business. By the end, I'll know if I can help."

The first one is asking for permission. The second one is setting the agenda. Prospects follow strength, not politeness.

**Mistake 2: Explaining before framing**

Most founders open with "Here's what we do..." and then try to ask questions 10 minutes later. By then, the prospect has already positioned you as a vendor explaining services.

Frame first. Discovery second. Explanation last.

**Mistake 3: Framing without the pause**

You say the frame, then immediately start talking again. You don't let them agree to it.

Say the frame. Stop. Wait for them to say yes. That micro-commitment is part of the frame.

**Mistake 4: Hedging the frame with softeners**

"If it's okay with you, I'd like to maybe ask a few questions if that's cool?"

Cut every softener. You're not asking permission. You're setting the structure for the call.

**Mistake 5: Not enforcing the frame when they break it**

Prospect interrupts with "How much does this cost?" before you've finished discovery. Instead of redirecting, you answer.

Redirect: "I'll get to pricing once I know if I can solve your problem. Let me ask two more things first."

**Mistake 6: Opening with rapport-building instead of frame-setting**

"Hey, how's your day going? Excited to chat!"

That's fine for friends. It's weak for sales calls. Skip the small talk. Set the frame. Get to work.

## The contrarian read

Most sales training tells you to "build rapport first" and "make them feel comfortable." That's correct for transactional sales. It's wrong for expert positioning.

If you're selling expertise, rapport happens after the frame, not before. The prospect respects you because you took control, not because you made small talk about the weather.

The other contrarian piece: frame-setting is not manipulation. It's clarity. You're telling the prospect exactly how the call will run so they don't have to guess. Unclear calls feel slimy. Structured calls feel professional.

The biggest pushback I get: "Won't this feel too aggressive?"

No. It feels confident. Prospects buy confidence. They don't buy uncertainty.

If you're worried about sounding aggressive, test it. Record your next five calls. On three of them, set the frame. On two, don't. Watch the difference in how the prospect behaves. The framed calls will close faster and cleaner every time.

## What it looks like when it's working

You set the frame in the first 60 seconds. The prospect says "yeah, makes sense" and stops interrupting.

You ask discovery questions. They answer without resistance. No "why do you need to know that?" No "can you just tell me how much it costs?"

You get to the end of discovery. You say "Here's what I can do for you." They lean in instead of leaning back.

You quote pricing. They don't flinch. They either say yes or they ask a clarifying question about scope, not about cost.

You end the call with a decision, not a follow-up. Either they're in, they're out, or there's one specific blocker that gets resolved before you hang up.

The observable signal: your close rate goes from 30% to 60%+ without changing anything else. The only variable was the open.

The time signal: your average sales cycle compresses from 3 weeks to 3 days because prospects aren't "thinking about it." They're deciding on the call.

The quality signal: the clients you close are better fits. They don't ghost during onboarding. They don't renegotiate scope. They bought because the frame made it clear whether they qualified, not because you convinced them.
