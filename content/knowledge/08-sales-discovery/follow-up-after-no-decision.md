## The core principle

Most follow-up sequences die because they chase a decision instead of moving a conversation.

The prospect who says "I need to think about it" or goes quiet after a proposal isn't waiting for you to check in. They're waiting for new information that changes the math in their head.

## Why this matters

The gap between "maybe" and "yes" is where most revenue gets lost.

Not because the offer was wrong. Not because the prospect wasn't qualified. Because the founder sends three "just checking in" emails, gets no reply, and moves on.

Here's what's actually happening in that gap: the prospect forgets the specific pain you named on the call. They get distracted by three other priorities. They convince themselves the timing isn't right. They talk to a cheaper option. They decide to try it themselves first.

Every day you're not in their inbox with something useful, someone else is.

The cost of bad follow-up isn't just one lost deal. It's the compounding effect of being known as "another person who chases but doesn't add value." That reputation spreads. Future prospects see your name and think "oh, that's the guy who kept emailing me after I said no."

The leverage of getting this right: a systematic follow-up cadence can convert 20-30% of maybes into yeses without a single additional sales call. That's pure margin. No extra ad spend. No new content. Just staying present with value until the decision window opens.

## How it shows up in client work

The pattern I see most often: founder closes a great discovery call, sends a proposal, gets "I need to think about it," waits three days, sends "just wanted to follow up," gets nothing, waits a week, sends "wanted to see if you had any questions," gets nothing, gives up.

Total follow-up: two emails. Zero value delivered. Zero reason for the prospect to re-engage.

When I walk clients through their CRM, the "no-decision" column is always the biggest. Deals that went quiet. Proposals that got ghosted. Calls that felt like wins but led nowhere.

The actual issue isn't the prospect. It's that the founder has no system for what happens after maybe.

One client, a leadership coach running <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->00K ARR, had 14 "thinking about it" prospects in his pipeline. Average deal size $8K. That's <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->12K sitting in limbo. I asked him: "What's your follow-up sequence?" He said: "I send a couple emails and if they don't reply I assume they're not interested."

We built a six-touch sequence. First email: case study of a client with the exact pain the prospect named on the call. Second email: a one-page diagnostic they could fill out to self-assess readiness. Third email: a contrarian take on why most people fail at the outcome he promises. Fourth email: a specific stat or story from his vault that proved the method works. Fifth email: "I'm closing this thread — here's the last thing I'd tell you if we never speak again." Sixth email: "I lied, one more thing" with a final specific proof point.

Four of the 14 booked follow-up calls within three weeks. Two closed. <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->6K in revenue from deals that were dead. The follow-up cost him two hours to write the sequence and zero hours to send (all automated via his CRM).

Another pattern: the founder who follows up too fast with nothing new to say.

Prospect says "I need to run this by my co-founder." Founder emails the next morning: "Hey, did you get a chance to chat with your co-founder?" Prospect thinks: "It's been 12 hours, calm down."

The correct move: "Totally fair. I'll send you a one-pager tomorrow that makes it easy to share with them. In the meantime, here's the exact ROI calculation I walked through on our call in case they ask."

Now the follow-up adds value. The prospect has something to forward. The co-founder sees professionalism, not desperation.

Third pattern: the founder who doesn't define what "no-decision" actually means.

"I need to think about it" could mean: I don't have budget. I don't trust you yet. I don't believe the outcome is possible. I need to talk to my spouse. I want to try the cheap version first. I'm comparing you to two other options.

Those are six different objections. They need six different follow-up sequences.

When I run discovery call audits with clients, I make them listen to the recording and name the REAL objection. Not what the prospect said. What they meant.

If the prospect said "I need to think about it" but the real objection was "I don't think this will work for my industry," the follow-up sequence should deliver case studies from their industry. Not generic check-ins.

One client ran a personal branding agency for executives. Prospect: VP of Sales at a SaaS company. Said yes to everything on the call, then went quiet after the proposal. Client sent two "checking in" emails. Nothing.

I asked: "What was the actual blocker?" Client: "He said he needed to check budget but I think he was scared his company would see his LinkedIn presence as a distraction."

Correct follow-up: case study of another VP who built a personal brand AND got promoted because the CEO saw him as a thought leader.

Client sent that email. Prospect replied in 20 minutes. Booked the follow-up. Closed three days later.

The objection was never budget. It was fear. The follow-up had to speak to the fear directly.

## Specific examples from the vault

Real sequence from a workshop client who sold $3K strategy sessions to solo founders.

After a "maybe" from a prospect, the sequence ran like this over 10 days:

Day 1: "Here's the recording of our call + the three frameworks I mentioned. No action needed, just wanted you to have it."

Day 3: Case study. Subject line: "This founder had the exact same hesitation you did." Body: 300 words on a client who said "I need to think about it," signed two weeks later, and hit their revenue goal in 90 days. Ended with: "Not saying this is you, just wanted to share."

Day 5: A one-page diagnostic. Subject line: "Fill this out or don't — your call." Body: "I built a 10-question self-assessment that tells you if you're ready for this kind of engagement. Takes 3 minutes. If you score below 7/10, we probably shouldn't work together yet. Here's the link."

Day 7: Contrarian take. Subject line: "Why most strategy sessions are a waste of money." Body: Four paragraphs on why founders pay for strategy and never implement it. Ended with: "The only reason my sessions work is [specific structural thing]. If you're going to hire anyone for strategy, make sure they do this."

Day 10: Final email. Subject line: "I'm closing this thread." Body: "No hard feelings if this isn't the right fit. One last thing I'd tell you: [specific tactical advice from the call]. Use it whether we work together or not. If you change your mind in the next 6 months, my calendar link is below."

Result: prospect replied on Day 11. "I've been thinking about this every day. Let's do it."

## Common mistakes

Checking in without adding value. "Just wanted to see if you had any questions." The prospect thinks: "If I had questions I would have asked."

Following up too fast. Prospect says "I need to think about it." Founder emails 24 hours later. Prospect thinks: "I haven't even had time to think yet."

Following up with the same message twice. First email: "Do you have any questions?" No reply. Second email, three days later: "Just wanted to follow up — do you have any questions?" Prospect thinks: "You already asked me that."

Not segmenting by objection. Sending the same follow-up sequence to everyone who says "maybe" regardless of why they said maybe. Budget objection needs a different sequence than timing objection.

Giving up after two emails. Most founders send two follow-ups, get no reply, assume the deal is dead. Real conversion happens between touch 3 and touch 6.

Apologizing for following up. "Sorry to bother you again…" or "I know you're busy but…" Apologizing trains the prospect to see your emails as an interruption. Don't apologize. Add value and they'll want to hear from you.

## The contrarian read

Most sales advice says "follow up 5-7 times." I say: follow up as many times as you can add value. If you can add value 12 times, send 12 emails. If you can only add value twice, send two and stop.

The number of touches doesn't matter. The value per touch does.

I've had clients close deals on the 9th follow-up email because every single email delivered something useful. I've also had clients burn bridges by sending 4 worthless "checking in" emails in two weeks.

The other contrarian take: don't automate the entire sequence.

Most founders build a 5-email drip in their CRM, set it, forget it. The emails go out on schedule whether or not the prospect opens them, clicks them, or replies.

Better approach: automate emails 1-3. Personalize emails 4-6 based on what the prospect actually engaged with.

If they opened the case study email but not the diagnostic email, that tells you something. Send email 4 as another case study, not another diagnostic.

If they clicked through to your pricing page after email 2 but didn't book a call, that tells you price is the sticking point. Email 4 should address pricing directly (payment plans, ROI calculator, risk reversal).

Automation saves time. Personalization wins deals. Use both.

## What it looks like when it's working

The prospect replies to email 3 or 4 instead of ghosting.

The prospect forwards your email to a decision-maker with "thoughts?" in the subject line.

The prospect books a follow-up call without you asking.

The prospect says "I've been thinking about this every day" when they re-engage.

The prospect closes faster on the second call because the follow-up sequence already handled objections.

You close 20-30% of "maybe" deals instead of 5%.

Your CRM "no-decision" column shrinks because deals either close or you consciously decide to stop following up (instead of letting them rot in limbo).

Prospects who don't close still say "that was the most helpful follow-up I've ever received" and refer you to others.
