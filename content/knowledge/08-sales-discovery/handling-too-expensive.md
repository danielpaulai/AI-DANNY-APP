## The core principle

"Too expensive" is never about the number.

It's about the gap between the value they can see and the price you've named.

## Why this matters

When a prospect says "too expensive," most founders hear rejection and start negotiating down.

The actual problem is upstream: you didn't make the value concrete enough for the price to feel inevitable.

Every "too expensive" objection is feedback on your discovery process, not your pricing strategy.

If they understood the cost of staying where they are, and the specific outcome you're promising, and the timeline in which they'd see it, price becomes a implementation detail.

The leverage of getting this right isn't just closing one deal.

It's building a positioning where price objections become rare because the value is so clear that the conversation shifts from "how much" to "when do we start."

Founders who handle price objections well close more deals at higher prices with less negotiation friction.

Founders who handle them poorly end up in a race to the bottom, attracting price-sensitive buyers who churn fast and refer similar clients.

## How it shows up in client work

The pattern shows up in three distinct phases: before the objection (what you didn't establish), during the objection (what you say in the moment), and after the objection (what happens next).

Before the objection, the mistake is almost always the same: the founder quoted a price before the prospect fully understood the cost of their current state.

In [[Sales_call_2025_03_14]], a client quoted <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->5K for a brand system before establishing what the prospect was losing by not having one.

The prospect said "that's steep" and the call stalled.

We replayed it with the value established first: "Walk me through what happened last quarter when you didn't have a content system. How many hours did that cost you? What opportunities did you miss?"

Once the prospect calculated 80 hours of wasted time plus three missed partnerships, <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->5K felt like a bargain.

The price didn't change. The framing did.

During the objection, the pattern splits into two types: genuine and reflexive.

Genuine "too expensive" means they want it but genuinely can't afford it right now.

Reflexive "too expensive" means they haven't connected the value to the outcome yet.

Most objections are reflexive.

The reframe I teach is: "Totally fair. Walk me through what's still unclear. Price, scope, fit, or timing?"

That question forces them to name the real block instead of defaulting to price.

In [[Sales_discovery_2024_11_22]], a prospect said "I need to think about it" after a <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->5K quote.

The founder asked the four-piece question.

The prospect replied: "Actually, timing. We have a board meeting in two weeks and I want to show them this before committing."

Price wasn't the issue. Sequencing was.

The founder offered: "Let's do a one-page executive brief you can present. If they approve, we start next week. If not, no hard feelings."

Deal closed three days later.

After the objection, the pattern determines whether you close or lose.

If you send a "just checking in" follow-up, you've trained them to expect chasing.

If you set a hard next-step on the call before hanging up, you've maintained control.

In [[Positioning_workshop_2025_01_18]], a founder shared a deal where the prospect said "too expensive" and the founder immediately replied: "I'll send a one-pager by EOD. Reply with yes, no, or 'still thinking and here's why.' Anything else and I'll assume it's a no."

The prospect replied within 24 hours: "Not thinking. Let's do it."

The clarity of the deadline forced a decision.

In client work with personal brand coaching, the pattern repeats: founders who lose deals on price almost always skipped discovery.

In [[Personal_branding_session_2024_12_08]], a coaching client admitted she'd been quoting <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->0K for brand builds without ever asking: "What does it cost you monthly to not have a brand that pulls leads?"

We rebuilt her discovery script to surface that number first.

Her close rate went from 30% to 65% in 90 days without changing her price.

The value was always there. She just wasn't making it visible before naming the investment.

Another pattern from [[Workshop_debrief_2025_02_20]]: founders who handle objections well never defend the price.

They reframe to outcomes: "Fair question. Let's break down what you're buying. You're not buying a logo or a website. You're buying 10 inbound calls a month instead of 2. That's 96 more qualified conversations a year. What's each conversation worth?"

Once the math is on the table, the objection dissolves.

## Specific examples from the vault

In [[Sales_call_2025_03_14]], the founder quoted <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->5K for a six-month brand engagement.

The prospect replied: "That's steep for us right now."

The founder asked: "Walk me through what's steep. The total amount, the monthly breakdown, or the scope?"

The prospect said: "The total. We budgeted $8K."

The founder reframed: "Got it. Let's work backwards. What outcome were you hoping to see in six months? And what does it cost you right now to not have that outcome?"

The prospect said: "We're spending 15 hours a week on content and getting maybe one call a month. We need it to be 5 inbound calls minimum."

The founder replied: "So right now you're losing 60 hours a month for 1 call. That's 60 hours of opportunity cost. What's your hourly rate?"

The prospect said: "Probably $300."

The founder replied: "So you're losing <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->8K a month in time alone, not counting the missed pipeline. If I can get you to 5 calls and cut your time to 5 hours a week, what's that worth?"

The prospect paused, then said: "A lot more than <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->5K."

Deal closed the next day.

From [[Positioning_session_2024_11_06]], a client handling objections during a workshop.

A prospect said: "I like the program but $5K is outside our range."

The client asked: "What were you expecting to invest?"

The prospect said: "Maybe <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->K."

The client replied: "Got it. At <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->K, I can't deliver the outcome you're looking for. What I can do is this: we scope down to just the brand foundation and one content sprint. That's <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->,500. You get proof of concept in 30 days. If it works, we do the rest. If not, you're not in for the full $5K."

The prospect agreed.

Outcome: they completed the <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->,500 sprint, saw results, and upgraded to the full engagement within 60 days.

Total revenue: $7,500 instead of $5K.

From [[Sales_discovery_2025_01_15]], a founder who lost a deal by defending price instead of reframing.

Prospect: "Your competitor quoted half that."

Founder: "Well, we include more in our package."

Prospect: "I'll think about it."

Deal died.

We rebuilt the script.

New version: "Fair. Let's talk about what you're actually buying. My competitor delivers a brand. I deliver a brand that books calls. What's the outcome you need?"

That reframe shifts the conversation from price comparison to outcome differentiation.

From [[Client_debrief_2025_02_18]], a founder who handled "too expensive" by creating urgency without discounting.

Prospect: "I need to run this by my co-founder."

Founder: "Totally fair. I have two spots open this quarter. One closes Friday. If your co-founder approves by then, you're in. If not, we revisit in Q2."

Prospect replied Thursday: "We're in."

No discount. No negotiation. Just clear scarcity tied to real capacity.

## Scripts and exact phrases

"Walk me through what's still unclear. Price, scope, fit, or timing?"

"I'll send a one-pager by EOD. Reply with yes, no, or 'still thinking and here's why.' Anything else and I'll assume it's a no."

"You're not buying a website. You're buying 10 inbound calls a month instead of 2. What's each conversation worth to you?"

"At $X, I can't deliver the outcome you're looking for. What I can do is scope down to [specific deliverable]. That's $Y. You get proof in 30 days. If it works, we do the rest."

"Fair question. My competitor delivers a brand. I deliver a brand that books calls. What's the outcome you need?"

"I have two spots open this quarter. One closes Friday. If you're in by then, we start Monday. If not, we revisit in Q2."

"What does it cost you monthly to not have [the thing they need]?"

"Let's work backwards. What outcome were you hoping to see in [timeframe]? And what does it cost you right now to not have that?"

## Common mistakes

Defending the price by listing features instead of reframing to outcomes.

Saying "just checking in" in follow-ups instead of setting a hard deadline on the call.

Discounting immediately when they say "too expensive" instead of diagnosing whether it's genuine or reflexive.

Quoting price before the prospect fully understands the cost of staying where they are.

Comparing yourself to competitors on price instead of differentiating on outcome.

Sending long proposals that require reading instead of forcing a binary decision.

Accepting "I need to think about it" without setting a clear next step before hanging up.

## The contrarian read

Most sales advice says "handle objections gracefully" and "never make the prospect feel wrong."

That's corporate politeness disguised as strategy.

The contrarian read: if a prospect says "too expensive" and you didn't establish value properly, you should lose that deal.

Closing it by discounting or over-explaining just kicks the problem down the road.

You'll onboard a client who doesn't value what you do, who will negotiate every invoice, and who will churn the moment something cheaper shows up.

Better to lose fast, learn what you missed in discovery, and rebuild your process for the next call.

The other contrarian read: scarcity works better than discounts.

When a prospect says "too expensive," the instinct is to offer a payment plan or knock 20% off.

What actually works is creating urgency: "I have two spots open. One closes Friday. If you're in, we start Monday. If not, we revisit next quarter."

That forces a decision without devaluing your work.

Founders who discount train their market to negotiate.

Founders who hold price and create urgency train their market to decide faster.

## What it looks like when it's working

Objections become rare because discovery is so thorough that price feels inevitable by the time you quote it.

When objections do come up, they resolve in one follow-up, not three.

Your close rate stays above 50% without discounting.

Prospects who say "too expensive" come back three months later at full price because they tried the cheaper option and it didn't work.

You start attracting clients who ask "when can we start" instead of "can you come down on price."

Your average deal size increases because you're selling outcomes, not hours or deliverables.

You spend less time chasing deals and more time delivering for clients who already bought in.
