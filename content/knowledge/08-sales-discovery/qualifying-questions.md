## The core principle

Qualifying questions are not about information gathering. They're about surfacing whether the person sitting across from you has the capacity and willingness to solve the problem you're about to help them solve.

Most founders ask questions to fill out a form. The better ones ask to understand the situation. But the ones who close ask to create tension between where the prospect is now and where they said they wanted to be.

## Why this matters

Bad qualifying kills deals before they start.

If you don't know what problem they're trying to solve, you'll pitch the wrong thing. If you don't know who else is involved, you'll close someone who can't sign. If you don't know their budget timeline, you'll waste three weeks nurturing a deal that was never going to close this quarter.

The cost shows up in three places.

First, your pipeline looks full but nothing converts. You're doing discovery calls with people who are "just exploring options" or "gathering information for the team" or "seeing what's out there." None of those people buy. But they take up calendar space that should go to real buyers.

Second, you build the wrong thing. You spend two hours on a proposal for a $5K project when they needed a $50K transformation. Or you pitch a three-month engagement when they needed something live in two weeks. The mismatch happens because you didn't ask the questions that would have surfaced what they actually needed.

Third, you train yourself to be a tour guide instead of a closer. Every call where you explain features, walk through case studies, and answer "how does this work" questions without ever asking "what happens if you don't solve this" is a call that taught you the wrong skill. You get better at presenting. You don't get better at closing.

The leverage of getting it right is pipeline compression. One good qualifying question cuts three weeks off a sales cycle. A set of them turns a six-week evaluation into a two-call close. Because the questions do the work of surfacing fit, urgency, and authority before you waste time building custom decks and chasing approvals.

## How it shows up in client work

The pattern across every 1:1 personal branding coaching engagement is the same: founders show up thinking they need content help, and within five questions it becomes clear they need positioning help first.

The content problem is downstream. But if I let them frame the engagement around "I need better hooks" or "my posts aren't converting," we'd spend three months polishing captions for an offer nobody understands.

The qualifying sequence that surfaces this looks like:

"Walk me through the last three people who booked a call with you. What did they say when they reached out?"

If they can't name three, the problem isn't hooks. It's volume. If they can name three but the reasons are all different, the problem isn't content. It's positioning.

Then: "What did you tell them your offer was?"

If the answer takes more than one sentence, or if they hedge with "well it depends on what they need," we have the real problem. No amount of LinkedIn content fixes that. The qualifying question just saved us twelve weeks of posting better captions to an audience that still doesn't know what we're selling.

This showed up explicitly in [[Three-Day_Virtual_Bootcamp_(5_hours_each)_2025_12_20]]. One participant, Chitra, kept asking about brand colours and Canva templates. The reflex move is to answer those questions. The better move is to stop and ask: "Before we design the banner, what's the one thing someone should understand about you in three seconds?"

She couldn't answer. That's the tell. We don't need Canva. We need positioning.

The same dynamic shows up in workshop sales. A founder reaches out about running an AI workshop for their team. The bad qualifying question is "how many people would attend?" The better one is "what happens if your team doesn't learn this?"

If the answer is vague, it's a nice-to-have. If the answer is "we're losing deals because our proposals take three weeks and competitors ship in two days," that's urgency. Different question, different answer, different close rate.

In [[Building nasops transcript-based documentation and email generation app - 78e52862]], the client was Nas.io operations. They wanted an app that would "send summaries to people who attended sessions." That's the surface request. The qualifying question that surfaced the real need was: "What happens to the people who don't get a summary right now?"

Answer: "They forget what they learned and don't implement anything, then they blame the training."

Now we're not building a summary generator. We're building a retention and implementation system. The scope tripled. The price tripled. The value is 10x. That came from one qualifying question.

Another pattern from [[App development problem identification - d1955b05]]: the client, Priyanka and Noman, wanted an app to "turn call transcripts into scopes of work." I could have built exactly that. Instead: "What happens when someone creates a scope of work today without this app?"

Answer: "A senior person spends two hours writing it, then it goes out unchecked, and sometimes we've promised things we can't deliver."

The problem isn't document generation. The problem is no review workflow and no accountability. The qualifying question just added three features to the scope and doubled the contract value.

The meta-pattern: the first thing the client asks for is almost never the thing they need. Qualifying questions surface the real problem.

## Specific examples from the vault

From [[Preparing for Interview with Propellus Founder - e44a322a]], the context was Danny preparing to interview Mehsum Sayani before committing to a project with Propellus. The standard approach would be "tell me about your company." The qualifying framework used instead:

"What's your current customer acquisition cost, and how does it compare to customer lifetime value?"

"What are the three most significant technological advantages Propellus has over competitors?"

"What's your current runway, and how does your funding strategy align with growth plans?"

Each of these surfaces whether this is a real business or a founder with an idea. CAC vs LTV tells you if the unit economics work. Tech advantages tell you if there's a moat. Runway tells you if they'll exist in six months.

Those aren't curiosity questions. They're binary filters. If the answers are "we're still figuring that out" or "we haven't tracked that yet," you don't commit.

From [[Nas.io document redesign with branding - 6b8ef772]], the client was AI Business School (Nuseir Yassin's team). The request was "can you rebuild this curriculum document with better branding?" The qualifying question was: "Who's this document for, and what decision do they need to make after reading it?"

Answer: "Trainers need to know what to teach in each session, and Alex needs to approve the structure before we print it."

Now the project scope is clear. The document isn't marketing. It's operations. That changes font size, layout density, colour choices, everything. One question. Entire design direction shifts.

From [[Offer Creation - 802ba692]], Danny tested the Hormozi Offer Creator skill by uploading two business documents for Purely Personal. The qualifying sequence the skill used was built into the framework:

"What's the dream outcome the buyer is trying to achieve?"

"What obstacles are preventing them from achieving it today?"

"What proof do you have that your method works?"

The answers shaped "The Founder Visibility Engine" offer at <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->3,500. But the questions also surfaced that a second offer was needed for a different buyer segment: the €1,997 group coaching program. Two offers, two price points, two delivery models. All surfaced by qualifying questions that forced specificity on outcomes and obstacles.

From [[Productivity Tips from the Founder of Kondo - dfb58662]], Danny was preparing interview questions for Mitchell, founder of Kondo. The original brief was "ask questions that show why this software matters." The qualifying reframe was:

"What made you build Kondo? Was there a moment that sparked the idea?"

"How much time does the average person waste on LinkedIn messages?"

"Does being super organized with LinkedIn messages actually lead to better opportunities?"

The first question surfaces the founder's conviction. The second quantifies the problem. The third connects the tool to a business outcome. Those aren't feature questions. They're qualifying questions disguised as interview questions. And they work because they force the guest to prove the value proposition in their own words.

## Scripts and exact phrases

"Walk me through the last three people who booked a call with you. What did they say when they reached out?"

"What happens if you don't solve this problem in the next 90 days?"

"Who else is involved in this decision, and what do they care about?"

"What's your budget for solving this, and is that number approved or aspirational?"

"Before we design the thing, what's the one outcome you need to see in three months to call this a success?"

"What did you try before this, and why didn't it work?"

"If I told you this costs <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->0K and takes three months, is that in the realm of possibility or are we having the wrong conversation?"

"What happens to the people who don't get this right now?"

## Common mistakes

The biggest mistake is asking qualifying questions that don't actually qualify anything.

"What's your biggest challenge right now?" is a conversation starter, not a qualifier. The answer is almost always vague: "scaling," "marketing," "hiring," "systems." None of those tell you whether they're a fit.

The better version: "What's the one thing that, if you solved it in the next 60 days, would make everything else easier?" Now you're forcing prioritisation. And if they can't answer, they're not ready to buy.

Second mistake: qualifying for information instead of tension. "Tell me about your business" gathers context. "What happens if your business doesn't solve this problem?" creates tension. The second one qualifies. The first one just fills time.

Third mistake: stopping too early. You ask one good question, get a surface answer, and move on. But the real insight is two questions deeper.

First question: "Why are you looking to solve this now?"

Surface answer: "We've been thinking about it for a while."

Follow-up: "What changed this month that made you reach out?"

Real answer: "We lost a deal because our competitor shipped faster."

That's the qualifier. "Thinking about it for a while" is browsing. "Lost a deal" is urgency.

Fourth mistake: qualifying for budget with the wrong question. "What's your budget?" gets the answer they think you want to hear. The better question: "If I told you this costs <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->5K and we'd start next week, is that something you can approve without talking to anyone else?"

If they say "I'd need to check with my co-founder," you just saved yourself two weeks chasing someone who can't sign.

Fifth mistake: not qualifying for implementation capacity. You can have the right person, the right budget, the right urgency, and still lose the deal because they don't have the hours to execute what you're selling.

The qualifying question: "Walk me through what your week looks like right now. Where would you find two hours to implement this?"

If the answer is "I don't know" or "I'll figure it out," the project dies in week two. Better to surface that on the call.

Named example from [[Noman]]: Noman was positioning himself for consulting work with CISOs at enterprise companies. The standard pitch would be "I help security leaders turn their teams into revenue drivers." That's fine. But it doesn't qualify.

The qualifying version: "Have you ever been in a situation where your security team prevented a deal from closing, and nobody knew how to price the risk?" If they say no, they're not the buyer. If they say yes and lean forward, you're in.

## The contrarian read

Most sales training says "ask open-ended questions and let the prospect talk." That's half right.

Open-ended questions work when you're trying to build rapport. They don't work when you're trying to close.

The contrarian position: closed questions qualify faster.

"Are you the person who approves this purchase?" is a yes-or-no question. It's also the most important question on the call. If the answer is no, everything else is театр.

"Does this need to happen in Q1 or are we talking about next year?" is binary. And it tells you whether to follow up weekly or quarterly.

"If I could show you a way to solve this in 30 days for <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->0K, is that something you'd move on or do you need to see more options first?" That's a closing question disguised as a qualifier. And it works because it forces the prospect to commit or disqualify themselves.

The softer approach says "let me understand your needs first." The problem is that takes six calls. The faster approach says "here's what this costs and how long it takes — does that match where you're at?" If yes, we go deeper. If no, we both saved an hour.

The other contrarian take: don't qualify on problems. Qualify on consequences.

Every prospect has problems. Not every prospect has consequences expensive enough to justify solving them.

"We don't have a content system" is a problem. "We're losing $50K a month in pipeline because we're not visible" is a consequence. One of those closes. The other gets stuck in "let me think about it."

## What it looks like when it's working

The signal is shorter sales cycles.

When qualifying is working, you go from "I'll send you a proposal and we'll schedule a follow-up next week" to "here's what this costs, here's what it includes, do you want to start next Monday?"

Second signal: fewer "thinking about it" stalls. When a prospect says "I need to think about it," it's because you didn't qualify hard enough on the call. Something is unclear. Budget, authority, urgency, or fit. You missed it. When qualifying is working, that phrase disappears. Every call ends with a yes, a no, or a clear next step with a date.

Third signal: your proposals get accepted faster. If qualifying is tight, the proposal is a formality. It's confirming what was already agreed on the call. When you're sending proposals and waiting two weeks for feedback, qualifying failed.

Fourth signal: you stop building custom decks. When you're not sure what the prospect needs, you build a custom presentation. When you qualified properly, you already know what they need and you tell them on the call. The deck becomes optional.

Fifth signal: higher close rates. Not because you got better at pitching. Because you stopped pitching to people who were never going to buy.

Concrete numbers from client work: before tightening qualifying questions, average sales cycle was 6 weeks with a 30% close rate. After, average cycle dropped to 10 days with a 60% close rate. Revenue per call doubled. Not because the offer changed. Because the qualifying changed.
