## The core principle

Every discovery call exists to answer one question: is there a mutual fit worth spending money on?

Not to educate. Not to build rapport. Not to "explore possibilities." To decide yes or no, fast, with both parties walking away clear on what happens next.

## Why this matters

Most founders treat discovery calls like networking coffee chats. They ask open-ended questions, let the prospect ramble, and walk away with a vague "let's stay in touch."

That burns time. Theirs and yours.

The cost of a bad discovery process compounds fast. You spend 45 minutes on a call that should have been disqualified in 8. You book follow-ups with prospects who aren't buyers. Your pipeline fills with maybes that never close.

The leverage of getting this right is mechanical. A tight discovery call structure does three things:

1. Qualifies the prospect in under 10 minutes (saves you 35 minutes per bad fit)
2. Surfaces the real objection early (so you either handle it now or walk)
3. Sets a hard next step before you hang up (so nothing dies in "I'll get back to you" limbo)

When a founder can't close deals, the problem is almost never the close. It's that they're closing the wrong people. Discovery is where you filter.

## How it shows up in client work

The pattern I see most: founders who book 10+ discovery calls a month but close 1 or fewer.

When I ask to listen to a recording, the structure is always the same. They open with "tell me about your business," let the prospect talk for 20 minutes, then pitch their offer for 15, then ask "does this sound like a fit?"

By that point, the prospect has already decided. You just don't know it yet.

The fix is always the same. Flip the order. You talk first.

In [[Sales_meeting_2024_04_11]], James spent the first 12 minutes of the call letting me (playing prospect) explain my process. By minute 15 I was describing my tech stack in detail. He still hadn't told me what he was selling.

When we debriefed after, I told him: "I would have hung up by now if this were a real call. You gave me no reason to stay."

The reframe: your first job is to earn the right to ask questions. You do that by saying in 90 seconds what you do, who it's for, and what result they can expect.

Then you ask: "Does that sound like something you'd want?"

If yes, you've earned 30 more minutes. If no, you've saved both of you 40.

I ran this exact structure with a prospect in [[Discovery_call_2024_01_25]] with Arber. 6 minutes in, he said "I don't think we're the right fit right now." I thanked him, asked him to refer anyone who was, and ended the call at 8 minutes.

That's a win. Not every call should close. Every call should decide.

Another pattern: founders who can't handle "I need to think about it."

In [[Sales_meeting_2024_04_09]], I walked James through the "think about it" reframe I use: "Totally fair. Walk me through what's still unclear. Price, scope, fit, or timing?"

80% of the time, they name one. Then you work it right there on the call.

If they genuinely need to leave the call to decide, you set a hard next step: "I'll send a one-pager by EOD. Reply with yes, no, or 'still thinking and here's why.' Anything else and I'll assume it's a no."

The thing I never do: send "just checking in" follow-ups. They train the prospect to expect chasing. I close on the call or I don't close.

The third pattern: discovery calls that book a second meeting instead of closing.

This happens when the founder doesn't ask for the sale.

In [[Sales_meeting_2024_04_22]], James and I role-played this exact scenario. He presented the offer, I said "this sounds good," and he said "great, let's book another call to go over details."

I stopped him. "Why another call? If I just said yes, close me now."

The script I gave him: "Based on what you've told me, this is the right fit. The next step is I send you a proposal by EOD, you review it, we jump on a 15-minute call Friday to answer any questions, and if it's a yes, we kick off Monday. Does that work?"

If the prospect says yes, you've closed. If they say "I need more time," you've surfaced the real objection (which was hiding behind "let's book another call").

A fourth pattern: founders who don't know when to walk.

In [[James_Sales_meeting_2024_04_03]], James asked me "how do you know when to disqualify someone?"

My answer: three disqualifiers, always.

1. They can't afford it (and won't be able to in the next 60 days)
2. They're not the decision-maker (and won't introduce you to the person who is)
3. They're shopping, not buying (they're comparing 5+ vendors with no urgency)

If any of those three are true, end the call politely and move on.

The cost of ignoring this: you spend 3 weeks nurturing a prospect who was never going to buy. That's 3 weeks you could have spent closing someone who would.

## Specific examples from the vault

In [[Sales_Navigator_2024_10_29]] with Nea, I opened the call by saying: "I help solo founders compress 12 hours a week of LinkedIn content work into 2 hours using AI. The outcome is you post 4x a week, it sounds like you, and you book 3-5 inbound calls a month. Does that sound like something you'd want?"

She said yes. That earned me the right to ask discovery questions.

8 minutes in, I asked: "What's your content workflow right now, start to finish?"

She described a 14-hour-a-week process involving Canva, ChatGPT, and manual editing. Perfect fit.

Then I asked the budget question: "If I could cut that to 2 hours a week and prove it in the first session, what's that worth to you?"

She said "easily €2,000."

I closed at €3,500. She said yes on the call.

Total call time: 34 minutes. No follow-up needed.

In [[Sales_Navigator_2024_10_07]] with Sam, I opened the same way. He said "not right now, maybe in 6 months."

I asked: "Is the timing bad, or is the offer not a fit?"

He said: "Timing. I'm mid-rebrand and don't want to start LinkedIn until that's done."

I thanked him, asked him to reach out when the rebrand shipped, and ended the call at 11 minutes.

That's also a win. I didn't chase. I didn't book a follow-up. I moved on.

In [[Giosg_Sales_meeting_2024_10_15]] with Ari, the call went 52 minutes. Why? Because I let him talk for 30 minutes about their sales process before I explained what I was selling.

By the time I pitched, he'd already mentally checked out. He said "let me think about it" and never replied.

That was a loss. Not because he didn't buy. Because I wasted 52 minutes on a call that should have been 12.

In [[cap_sales_2023_11_29]] with Shabistan, I asked the decision-maker question 18 minutes in: "Are you the person who signs off on this, or is there someone else I need to talk to?"

She said: "I need to run it by my co-founder."

I asked: "Can we get them on a call this week?"

She said: "They're traveling for two weeks."

I thanked her and ended the call. No point building a proposal for someone who can't say yes.

## Scripts and exact phrases

**Opening (first 90 seconds):**
"I help [ICP] achieve [outcome with number] in [timeframe]. The result is [specific benefit]. Does that sound like something you'd want?"

**Earning the discovery:**
"Great. Let me ask you a few questions to make sure this is the right fit. First: what's your [workflow/process/current state] right now, start to finish?"

**Budget qualifier:**
"If I could [compress the pain], what's that worth to you?"

**Decision-maker qualifier:**
"Are you the person who signs off on this, or is there someone else I need to talk to?"

**Urgency qualifier:**
"If this is the right fit, what's stopping you from starting next week?"

**Handling 'I need to think about it':**
"Totally fair. Walk me through what's still unclear. Price, scope, fit, or timing?"

**Setting the hard next step:**
"Based on what you've told me, this is the right fit. The next step is I send you a proposal by EOD, you review it, we jump on a 15-minute call Friday to answer questions, and if it's a yes, we kick off Monday. Does that work?"

**Closing on the call:**
"Great. I'll send the agreement over today. Reply with any questions. If it's a yes, sign it and we'll kick off [date]."

## Common mistakes

**Opening with "tell me about your business."** This hands control to the prospect. They ramble. You lose 15 minutes learning things you don't need to know. Open with what YOU do instead.

**Not asking the budget question.** Founders are terrified of this one. They think it's rude. It's not. The rude thing is wasting someone's time on an offer they can't afford. Ask it at minute 10.

**Booking a follow-up instead of closing.** If the prospect says "this sounds good," close them. Don't say "let's book another call." That's just fear disguised as process.

**Chasing after "I'll get back to you."** Set a hard deadline. "I'll send the proposal by EOD. Reply by Friday with yes, no, or 'still thinking and here's why.' Anything else and I'll assume it's a no." Then stick to it.

**Letting the call run long.** If it's not a fit, end the call. 12 minutes is better than 52. Your time is the asset. Protect it.

**Pitching before qualifying.** If you pitch before you know they can afford it, have authority to buy, and have urgency, you're just doing free consulting. Qualify first.

## The contrarian read

Most sales advice says "build rapport first." I say the opposite.

Rapport is a byproduct of clarity. When someone knows exactly what you're offering, who it's for, and what they'll get, they relax. That's rapport.

When you open with small talk and "tell me about yourself," you create anxiety. The prospect is thinking: "What do you want from me? What's the catch?"

Lead with the offer. Rapport follows.

Another contrarian take: not every discovery call should convert.

If your close rate is 80%+, you're not qualifying hard enough. You're saying yes to people who should be maybes.

A 40-50% discovery-to-close rate means you're filtering properly. Half the people who book a call shouldn't buy from you. That's the point of discovery.

## What it looks like when it's working

You book 8 discovery calls in a month. 3 close on the call. 2 say "not right now" and you end the call in under 15 minutes. 3 are genuine maybes — you send a proposal, set a hard deadline, and 2 of those close by Friday.

Your average discovery call is 25 minutes. Not 45. Not 60.

Your pipeline has 5 active prospects, not 25. Each one has a hard next step with a date attached. None of them are "waiting to hear back."

You spend 3 hours a week on sales calls, not 12. The rest of the time you're delivering, not chasing.

When a prospect says "I need to think about it," you know exactly what they're thinking about. Because you asked.
