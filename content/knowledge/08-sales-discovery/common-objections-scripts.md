## The core principle

Sales objections are not rejections — they are requests for the final piece of information the prospect needs to buy.

Most founders treat objections as barriers. They hear "I need to think about it" and assume the deal is dead. They hear "that's expensive" and start discounting. They hear "I need to talk to my partner" and send a follow-up email three days later hoping something changed.

The actual truth: an objection means the prospect is still in the conversation. They haven't left. They haven't ghosted. They're telling you exactly what's blocking them from saying yes right now. Your job is to handle it in real time, on the call, and close.

## Why this matters

Every objection you defer costs you the deal.

When you say "no problem, take your time" or "totally understand, let me send you some info," you've trained the prospect that hesitation gets rewarded with space. Space kills deals. The longer someone sits with uncertainty, the more reasons they invent not to buy.

The mechanism is simple: buying decisions happen in moments of clarity, and clarity happens in conversation. The second you end the call without resolution, you've moved the decision out of the high-conviction state you created during discovery and into the low-conviction state of their inbox, their calendar, their partner who wasn't on the call.

Founders who don't handle objections in real time burn 60-70% of their pipeline in follow-up limbo. They book 10 calls. 7 of them say "I need to think about it." The founder sends a polite email. 5 ghost. 2 reply with "not right now." 0 close. The problem wasn't the offer. It wasn't the price. It was the failure to resolve the objection when it surfaced.

The cost is not just the lost deal. It's the wasted discovery time, the opportunity cost of the call slot, and the psychological damage of thinking your close rate is 10% when it should be 50%.

## How it shows up in client work

The pattern repeats across every founder I work with: they get to the end of a strong discovery call, the prospect says one of five sentences, and the founder freezes.

Most common version: "This sounds great, but I need to think about it."

Second most common: "I need to talk to my business partner / spouse / co-founder."

Third: "The price is higher than I expected."

Fourth: "I'm not sure this is the right time."

Fifth: "I've tried something like this before and it didn't work."

Each one has a script. Each one can be handled in 60-90 seconds on the call. But most founders don't have the scripts memorized, so they default to politeness and lose the deal.

One client — B2B SaaS founder selling a <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->5K implementation package — was closing 1 in 8 calls. We recorded three of his sales calls. On all three, the prospect said some version of "I need to run this by my CTO." On all three, my client said "totally understand, no rush." On zero of those three did he ask a follow-up question.

The fix: one reframe. When the prospect says "I need to talk to my CTO," you say: "Makes sense. Walk me through what you think their concern will be — is it budget, timeline, or whether this will actually work?"

That question does two things. First, it surfaces the real objection (usually it's not the CTO at all — it's the founder's own uncertainty about budget or timing). Second, it keeps the conversation alive so you can handle the real objection right there.

After adding that one script, his close rate went from 12% to 40% in six weeks. Same offer. Same discovery process. One sentence. [[Sales_call_2025_03_14]]

Another pattern: founders who hear "that's expensive" and immediately start explaining all the value they're delivering.

One client — personal brand coach selling a $5K 90-day package — would respond to price objections with a five-minute breakdown of every deliverable, every session, every bonus. The prospect would nod politely and still not buy.

The script I gave her: "Expensive compared to what?"

That's it. Four words. The prospect has to name the comparison point. If they say "compared to other coaches," you ask what the other coach charges and what they deliver. If they say "compared to what I can afford," you've just learned it's not a price objection — it's a cash flow or priority objection, and you handle that differently.

She used it on the next call. Prospect said "that's a lot of money." She said "compared to what?" He said "I don't know, I've never hired a coach before." She said "so you don't actually know if it's expensive — you just don't have a reference point. Let me ask differently: if this works and you're booking 3-5 inbound calls a month by the end of 90 days, what's that worth to you?" He said "$30K in the next six months, easy." She said "so you're risking $5K to make $30K." He bought on the call. [[Client_session_2024_11_20]]

Third pattern: founders who get the "I've tried this before" objection and don't know how to separate their offer from the past failure.

One client — LinkedIn strategist selling a $3K content system — kept hearing "I tried posting consistently before and it didn't work." She would respond by defending LinkedIn as a platform and explaining why her system was different. Never closed.

The script: "What do you think went wrong last time?"

Let them tell you. They'll name the exact mistake (usually: no strategy, no ICP clarity, generic content, no follow-up system). Then you say: "That's exactly what we're fixing. You weren't posting wrong — you were posting without a system. What we're building is [name the three things that address their past failure]."

She used it. Next prospect said "I posted for six months and got nothing." She asked what went wrong. He said "I was just writing random thoughts, no structure." She said "right, so you were posting consistently but not strategically. What we're building is a 90-day content calendar tied to your offer, an ICP brief so every post is aimed at one person, and a Claude skill that writes in your voice so it doesn't take 12 hours a week. You weren't wrong to try — you just didn't have the system." He bought. [[Personal_brand_coaching_session_2025_01_15]]

Fourth pattern: the "not the right time" objection, which is almost never about timing.

One client — workshop facilitator selling a <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->K two-day intensive — would hear "this sounds great but I'm slammed right now" and immediately suggest rebooking in a month. He'd send a calendar link. Prospect would never book.

The reframe: "I hear you. Walk me through what changes in your schedule in the next 30 days that makes this easier to commit to."

The prospect will either name something specific (a project wrapping up, a hiring decision resolving) or they'll say "I don't know, just feels like a lot right now." If it's the first, you book around the milestone. If it's the second, it's not a timing objection — it's a priority objection. You say: "Got it. So it's not that you're too busy — it's that this isn't the top priority right now. What would need to be true for this to become the priority?"

That question forces them to articulate the real blocker. Usually it's revenue, team capacity, or confidence in the outcome. All of those are handleable on the call. [[Workshop_strategy_2024_08_10]]

The throughline across all of these: **objections are not the end of the conversation. They're the beginning of the close.**

## Common mistakes

**Mistake 1: Accepting the objection at face value.**

When someone says "I need to think about it," most founders say "no problem, take your time." They've just lost the deal. The objection is not the real reason. The real reason is buried underneath. Your job is to ask one follow-up question to surface it.

**Mistake 2: Defending the offer instead of reframing the objection.**

When someone says "that's expensive," most founders launch into a five-minute value dump. They list every deliverable, every session, every bonus. The prospect nods politely and still doesn't buy. The better move: reframe the objection with a question. "Expensive compared to what?" forces the prospect to name their comparison point, and then you can address the real concern.

**Mistake 3: Sending follow-up emails instead of closing on the call.**

Every objection you defer to email is a deal you've lost. Follow-up emails get ignored. Follow-up emails give the prospect time to invent new reasons not to buy. Follow-up emails train the prospect that hesitation gets rewarded with space. Close on the call or don't close.

**Mistake 4: Treating objections as rejections.**

Most founders hear "I'm not sure this is the right time" and assume the prospect is saying no. They're not. They're saying "I need one more piece of information before I say yes." Your job is to figure out what that piece is and deliver it in the next 60 seconds.

**Mistake 5: Not having the scripts memorized.**

If you have to pause and think about how to handle an objection, you've already lost. The prospect reads the hesitation as uncertainty. You need five scripts memorized cold: think about it, talk to partner, price, tried before, timing. One script per objection type. 60 seconds each. Practice them until they sound like conversation, not recitation.

**Mistake 6: Discounting to close.**

When a prospect says "that's expensive," the worst thing you can do is drop the price. You've just confirmed their suspicion that your offer wasn't worth the original number. Instead, reframe the price as an investment with a specific ROI. "You're betting $5K to make $30K. That's a 10x return." If they still don't buy, it's not a price problem — it's a belief problem, and you need to go back to discovery.

## The contrarian read

Most sales training tells you to handle objections with empathy and validation. "I totally understand, a lot of my clients felt the same way at first."

That's weak.

Empathy doesn't close deals. Clarity does.

When someone says "I need to think about it," they don't need you to validate their uncertainty. They need you to resolve it. Saying "I understand" just confirms that thinking about it is a reasonable response. It's not. Thinking about it is what you do when you're not sure the offer is right. Your job is to make them sure on the call.

The other contrarian take: **you should want objections**.

Most founders dread objections. They see them as a sign the call is going badly. The opposite is true. Objections mean the prospect is engaged. They're still in the conversation. They're telling you exactly what's blocking them from buying. A prospect who says nothing and then ghosts is a worse outcome than a prospect who raises five objections on the call.

Every objection is a gift. It's the prospect handing you the exact script you need to close them. If they say "that's expensive," they've just told you they're worried about ROI. Handle it and close. If they say "I need to talk to my partner," they've just told you they're unsure about budget or priority. Handle it and close.

The best sales calls are the ones where the prospect raises three objections and you handle all three on the call. The worst sales calls are the ones where the prospect says "this sounds great, let me think about it" and you never hear from them again.

## What it looks like when it's working

You're booking 10 discovery calls a month. 7 of them get to the investment conversation. 5 of them say yes on the call. That's a 50% close rate measured from qualified discovery to signed deal. Most founders are at 10-20%. The difference is not the offer. It's the objection handling.

Observable signals:

**Signal 1: You're closing on the call, not in follow-up.**

If more than 20% of your deals close via email after the call, your objection handling is weak. Strong objection handling closes 80%+ of deals on the call. The prospect either buys or explicitly says no. There is no "let me think about it."

**Signal 2: Objections don't surprise you.**

You've heard the same five objections on every call for six months. You know exactly what's coming. You have a script for each one. You deliver it in 60 seconds and move to the close. No hesitation. No scrambling. No "let me get back to you on that."

**Signal 3: Your close rate is consistent.**

If you're closing 10% one month and 60% the next, your process is broken. Strong objection handling produces consistent close rates because you're handling the same objections the same way every time. The script works or it doesn't. If it works, it works every time.

**Signal 4: You're not discounting.**

If you're dropping the price to close deals, your objection handling is weak. Price objections are never actually about price. They're about belief, ROI, or priority. If you're handling those correctly, the prospect either buys at full price or they don't buy. Discounting is what you do when you can't close any other way.

**Signal 5: You're spending zero time on follow-up.**

If you're sending three follow-up emails per prospect, your objection handling is broken. Strong objection handling closes the deal on the call or kills the deal on the call. Either outcome is fine. What you don't do is leave the prospect in limbo and then chase them for two weeks.
