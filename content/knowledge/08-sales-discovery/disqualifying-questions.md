## The core principle

You close deals by saying no faster than the buyer does.

## Why this matters

Most founders treat discovery like a sales pitch disguised as a conversation. They ask surface questions, nod along, then write the proposal hoping the price works. By the time they hear "too expensive" or "we need to think about it," they've already wasted 6 hours on a call that was never going to close.

Wrong-fit deals don't just waste time. They poison your pipeline. You spend emotional capital chasing people who were never buying. Your confidence drops because you're getting rejected more. Your positioning weakens because you're saying yes to everyone. Your referrals suffer because the few clients you do close aren't the right ones.

The expensive part isn't the lost deal. It's the three real deals you didn't work on because you were chasing the wrong one.

Disqualifying questions exist to catch the wrong-fit early. Before the proposal. Before the price conversation. Before you've invested any real work. You're not trying to convince them. You're trying to discover whether this is a deal you actually want.

The leverage is brutal. One disqualifying question in the first 5 minutes can save you 20 hours of proposal work, three follow-up calls, and two weeks of "just checking in" purgatory. It also signals something the buyer respects: you have a standard. You don't take everyone. That alone makes them want to qualify FOR you.

## How it shows up in client work

I see the pattern every time I review a founder's sales pipeline. Twenty-five "opportunities" in the CRM. Only three have a real shot. The rest are polite conversations with people who were never going to buy, couldn't afford it, or don't have authority to sign.

The symptom: "I'm doing so many sales calls but nothing's closing."

The diagnosis: They never asked a single disqualifying question.

One client I worked with in early 2024 was running a LinkedIn personal branding offer for consultants. Great positioning. Strong case studies. Pipeline full. Zero closed deals in six weeks. I listened to three of his recorded sales calls. Every single one followed the same script: he'd ask about their business, pitch the offer, show the pricing, then ask if they had questions. Never once did he ask what they'd already tried, what their budget was, or who else needed to approve the deal. He was pitching to people who had no money, no urgency, and no authority.

I gave him three questions to ask in the first 10 minutes of every call: "What have you already tried to fix this?", "What budget have you set aside for solving this problem?", and "Who else is involved in this decision besides you?" Within two calls, he disqualified someone who'd been "very interested" for three weeks but had zero budget and needed board approval. The next week he closed two deals with people who passed all three filters.

The pattern repeats in every 1:1 coaching engagement. Founders don't want to disqualify. It feels rude. It feels like closing a door. So they keep the conversation alive hoping momentum will turn into a deal. It never does. What actually happens is the prospect ghosts after three weeks because they were never serious, and the founder blames their pitch instead of their intake process.

Another pattern: the founder asks softballs. "Are you serious about growing your business?" Yes. "Is this a priority for you?" Yes. "Do you see the value in what we do?" Yes. Every answer sounds positive but commits to nothing. These questions don't disqualify because they don't cost the buyer anything to say yes. Real disqualifying questions make the buyer uncomfortable. That's the test.

I've seen this exact mistake kill deals for a founder running AI implementation workshops for SMBs. His pitch was strong. His case studies were real. But he never asked "Have you actually used AI internally yet?" or "Do you have someone on your team who'll own this after the workshop?" He spent hours prepping custom proposals for people who hadn't touched ChatGPT once and had no one to execute after the session. The deals died in "we'll get back to you" limbo every time. When I walked him through adding just two disqualifiers — "Show me one AI tool you've already tested" and "Who on your team will be responsible for rolling this out?" — his close rate doubled in 30 days. Not because his pitch got better. Because he stopped pitching to people who were never going to implement.

The biggest leverage moment comes when you disqualify someone live on the call and they fight to stay in. That's when you know they're real. If you say "It sounds like this isn't the right time for you" and they immediately agree, you just saved yourself two weeks. If they say "No wait, let me explain" and start selling YOU, you know they're serious. The disqualifying question doesn't kill the deal. It reveals whether the deal was ever real.

His pipeline looked healthy: eight active conversations. But nothing was closing. I asked him to walk me through the last three calls. Every single one ended with "this looks great, let me discuss with my team." No one ever came back.

I asked him one question: "Did you ask who else needs to approve this decision?" He paused. No, he hadn't. He'd assumed the person who booked the call was the decision-maker. In all three cases, they weren't. They were ICs gathering options for their CMO. The CMO never heard the pitch. The deal died in internal Slack channels he never saw.

I gave him a script for the first 5 minutes of every discovery call: "Before we go deep, help me understand — who else besides you is involved in making this decision? And what's the process once you've decided this is the right move?" Within two calls, he caught someone who needed sign-off from a CEO who was traveling for the next month. He disqualified on the spot, told them to reconnect when the CEO was back. The prospect respected it. A month later, they booked the call with the CEO present and closed same-day. The disqualifying question didn't kill the deal — it revealed the real timeline and saved him four weeks of chasing someone with no authority.

## Scripts and exact phrases

"What have you already tried to solve this, and why didn't it work?" — disqualifies people who haven't tried anything (no urgency) or who tried but didn't do the work (won't succeed with you either).

"What budget have you set aside for this?" — disqualifies people with no allocated budget. If they haven't put money on the table yet, they're browsing.

"Who else is involved in this decision besides you?" — catches ICs gathering information for someone with actual authority. If the decision-maker isn't on the call, you're pitching to the wrong person.

"Walk me through what happens after you say yes. What's the internal process?" — reveals bureaucracy, procurement workflows, board approvals. If the process takes 90 days, you either wait or walk.

"What's your timeline for making this decision, and what's the cost if you don't solve this by then?" — separates urgency from interest. No cost to delay = no urgency = no deal.

"Help me understand — is this a priority you're actively solving, or something you're exploring for later?" — gives them permission to admit it's not urgent. If they say "exploring," you disqualify and tell them to reconnect when it's urgent.

"After we deliver this, what's your plan for implementation?" — for productized services or workshops. Catches people who think the deliverable IS the outcome.

"On a scale of 1 to 10, how committed are you to solving this in the next 30 days?" — anything below an 8 is a polite no. If they say 6 or 7, ask what would make it a 10. If the answer is vague, disqualify.

## Common mistakes

**Mistake 1: Asking disqualifying questions at the wrong time.** If you open the call with "What's your budget?" before establishing value, you sound like a vendor. Disqualifiers work after you've confirmed the problem is real and they care about solving it. Earn the right to disqualify by proving you understand their situation first.

**Mistake 2: Softening the question so much it stops working.** "I'm just curious, have you thought about budget at all?" is not a disqualifier. It's a softball. The buyer will say "yeah we're flexible" and you've learned nothing. The question has to cost them something to answer honestly. "What budget have you set aside?" costs more than "have you thought about budget?"

**Mistake 3: Accepting the first answer without digging.** Prospect says "I'm the decision-maker." You move on. Two weeks later you find out they need CFO approval. The mistake wasn't asking the question — it was not confirming the answer. Follow-up: "So once you decide this is the right move, you can sign today? Or is there a process after that?"

**Mistake 4: Disqualifying in your head but not out loud.** You ask about timeline. They say "we're just exploring options right now." You know it's not a real deal, but you keep the conversation going because you don't want to be rude. This is where deals go to die. If you've disqualified them, say it. "It sounds like this isn't urgent for you yet. Let's reconnect when it is."

**Mistake 5: Treating disqualification as rejection.** The founder hears "disqualify" and thinks "be mean to prospects." That's not it. Disqualifying is respect. You're respecting their time by not pitching something they're not ready for. You're respecting your time by not chasing deals that won't close. The script is kind but direct: "Based on what you've shared, I don't think we're the right fit right now. Here's why."

**Mistake 6: Asking the question but ignoring the answer.** Prospect says they have no budget allocated. You pitch anyway because "maybe they'll find budget." They won't. You've just spent 40 minutes pitching to someone who told you in minute 5 that they can't buy. The disqualifier only works if you act on the answer.

## The contrarian read

Most sales advice says "always be closing." I say always be disqualifying.

The standard playbook is: get them on the call, build rapport, pitch the value, handle objections, close. That works if your goal is activity. It doesn't work if your goal is revenue. You end up with a full calendar and an empty bank account.

The better strategy: get them on the call, confirm the problem, disqualify fast, pitch only to the ones who pass. You do fewer calls. You close more deals. Your pipeline has 8 real opportunities instead of 25 maybes.

Another contrarian take: disqualifying makes you MORE attractive, not less. When you tell a prospect "I don't think we're the right fit," two things happen. If they were never serious, they agree and you both move on. If they were serious, they fight to stay in. That fight is the signal. The prospect who says "wait, why not?" and starts explaining why they ARE the right fit is the one who closes. You've flipped the dynamic. Now they're selling you.

The trap most founders fall into is thinking interest equals intent. Someone books a call. They ask good questions. They nod along. The founder thinks "this is closing." But interest is cheap. Intent costs something. Disqualifying questions separate the two. If the prospect can't answer the budget question, the timeline question, or the authority question, they were never intending to buy. They were interested in learning.

One more: most founders think a "no" is a failed call. I think a fast "no" is a successful call. You just saved 20 hours. The goal isn't to close every prospect. The goal is to close every RIGHT prospect and disqualify every wrong one before you waste time on them.

## What it looks like when it's working

You book 12 sales calls in a month. You disqualify 7 of them in the first 10 minutes. You do full pitches for 5. You close 4. Your close rate is 80% on the deals you actually pitch. Your calendar has space. Your pipeline is clean. Your confidence is high because you're no longer getting rejected by people who were never going to buy.

Observable signal: the deals you DO pitch close faster. Because everyone involved has authority, budget, urgency, and a plan for after delivery. There's no "I need to think about it" or "let me run this by my team." Those filters already ran.

Another signal: prospects start respecting your process. When you disqualify someone and explain why, they don't get defensive. They say "that makes sense, I'll reach out when we're ready." Six months later, they come back with budget and close in one call. Because you didn't waste their time pretending it was a fit when it wasn't.

Numbers from real clients: one founder went from 30% close rate (3 closed out of 10 full pitches) to 70% close rate (7 closed out of 10) by adding three disqualifiers to his discovery process. He didn't change his pitch. He didn't lower his price. He just stopped pitching to people who were never going to buy.

The before state: "I'm doing so many calls but nothing's closing. I think my offer is wrong." The after state: "I'm doing fewer calls and closing most of them. I think my offer is fine, I was just pitching to the wrong people."

One founder I worked with tracked it precisely. Before disqualifiers: 18 discovery calls, 12 proposals sent, 2 closed. After disqualifiers: 18 discovery calls, 6 proposals sent, 5 closed. Same top-of-funnel volume. Half the proposals. More than double the closed deals. The disqualifiers didn't reduce his pipeline — they concentrated it.
