## The core principle

A warm prospect is not someone who replied "sounds interesting."

A warm prospect is someone who has consumed at least three pieces of your content over at least two weeks, engaged with at least one of them publicly, and can name the problem you solve without you saying it first.

The arc from engaged-with-content to closed is not a sales process. It's a recognition sequence.

## Why this matters

Most founders think the close happens on the call.

It doesn't.

The close happens in the 47 micro-decisions a prospect makes before they ever book the call. Did they save that post? Did they click your profile after reading it? Did they scroll your last five posts? Did they read your About section? Did they visit your website? Did they check your case studies? Did they watch that video you linked?

By the time they book, they've already decided. The call is confirmation.

The cost of getting this wrong is that you treat every inbound call like a cold call. You re-explain yourself. You re-sell your credibility. You ask discovery questions they already answered by booking. You waste the first 15 minutes on setup that should have happened before they ever hit your calendar.

The founder who understands the warm prospect arc shows up to every call with a prospect who already knows what they do, believes they can do it, and is choosing between "yes now" and "yes later."

That's leverage.

## How it shows up in client work

The pattern I see most often: founders get a comment on a LinkedIn post and immediately try to close in the DMs.

One founder sent me a screenshot of a DM thread where someone had commented "love this perspective" on a post about positioning. The founder replied in DMs with "glad it resonated — I help founders with positioning if you ever need support."

The prospect ghosted.

The founder thought the problem was their pitch. The actual problem was they skipped the recognition sequence. One comment is not warm. One comment is cold-with-a-pulse.

I walked this founder through the real warm prospect definition: three touches, two weeks, named the problem. We rebuilt their response to engagement. New rule: if someone comments once, reply publicly and keep posting. If they comment twice, send a DM that references both comments and asks one question about their situation. If they answer that question with specifics, then offer a call.

Three months later, this founder closed two <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->5K contracts from LinkedIn. Both prospects had engaged with at least five posts before the DM. Both named the problem in the DM thread before the call was booked. Both calls closed in under 30 minutes.

Another pattern: founders track engagement as vanity metrics instead of buying signals.

One client showed me their LinkedIn analytics. 47 post reactions last month. 12 comments. "Why isn't this converting?" they asked.

I asked: "How many of those 47 people have engaged more than once?"

Seven.

"How many have engaged three times or more?"

One.

That's the number that matters. Not 47. One.

We rebuilt their content system to optimize for repeat engagement instead of reach. Stopped posting viral-bait hooks. Started posting specific problem-statements that only their ICP would recognize. Three posts a week, each one naming one micro-problem their offer solved.

Within six weeks, the repeat-engager count went from 1 to 14. Four of those 14 booked calls. Three closed. Total revenue: $67K.

The shift wasn't content quality. It was content targeting. [[LinkedIn_content_strategy_2024_08_15]]

A third pattern: founders don't track who's watching.

One founder had 2,400 LinkedIn connections. Posted consistently for four months. Booked zero calls.

I asked: "Who's viewing your profile after they see your posts?"

They didn't know.

I had them check LinkedIn's "Who Viewed Your Profile" data for the last 30 days. 73 profile views. I had them cross-check those 73 names against their connection list and their post engagement.

12 people had both viewed the profile AND engaged with a post in the last month.

Those 12 names went into a spreadsheet. Every week, we tracked: did they engage again? Did they view the profile again? Did they visit the website?

Within two weeks, three of the 12 had engaged twice more. The founder sent a DM to all three. Two replied. Both booked calls. One closed at <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->2K.

The lesson: warm prospects self-identify through behavior patterns. Your job is to notice the pattern and act on it before they move on. [[Profile_view_tracking_2024_09_03]]

A fourth pattern: founders confuse "inbound interest" with "ready to buy."

One client got a DM from someone who said "I've been following your content for a while — would love to chat about working together."

The founder booked the call immediately. Showed up prepared to close.

The prospect spent the first 20 minutes explaining their business. Spent the next 20 minutes asking questions about the process. Spent the last 10 minutes saying "this is really helpful, let me think about it."

No close.

The founder was confused. "They reached out to me. They said they wanted to work together."

I asked: "Did you ask them what changed between following your content and deciding to reach out now?"

No.

That's the question that separates warm-and-ready from warm-and-curious.

The prospect who's ready to buy has a trigger. Something shifted. A deadline hit. A problem escalated. A competitor launched. A result came in below target. A founder can't just be "ready." They need a reason they're ready now instead of next month.

We rebuilt the founder's DM response framework. New rule: when someone reaches out, reply with: "Love to chat. Quick question first — what's changed recently that made this a priority now?"

Three outcomes:
1. They name a specific trigger → book the call, they're ready
2. They give a vague answer → ask one more clarifying question, then decide
3. They don't answer → they weren't actually warm, they were browsing

This founder closed their next four inbound DMs at a 75% rate. [[Sales_qualification_framework_2024_10_22]]

## Specific examples from the vault

One call I ran with a founder who was stuck at 3 clients. Posted every day. Got decent engagement. Nobody converting.

I asked: "Walk me through the last three people who engaged with your content but didn't book a call."

First person: commented on one post, never engaged again.

Second person: liked four posts, never commented.

Third person: liked six posts, commented twice, visited the profile three times, clicked the website link.

I said: "Why didn't you DM the third person?"

"I didn't want to seem pushy."

I explained the recognition sequence. The third person had already decided they liked the work. The DM wasn't pushy. It was overdue.

The founder sent the DM that day. The prospect replied within two hours. "I've been hoping you'd reach out." Booked the call. Closed at <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->8K.

The lesson: the warm prospect is waiting for permission to move forward. Your job is to give it to them. [[Client_call_2024_07_18]]

Another example: a founder who was stuck in "coffee chat" mode.

Every inbound call turned into a 60-minute discovery session where the founder explained their entire methodology, answered every question, and then ended the call with "let me send you a proposal."

Conversion rate: 0%.

I asked: "What does the prospect know before they book the call?"

"They've seen my content."

"What content specifically?"

"Uh… whatever posts they saw?"

The problem was clear. The founder had no content-to-call bridge. The prospect showed up to the call not knowing what to expect. The founder spent the whole call educating instead of closing.

We built a pre-call content sequence. Three specific posts the founder would link in the calendar confirmation email:
1. A post explaining the core problem
2. A post showing a before/after result
3. A post outlining the process

New rule: if they book the call, they get those three links. If they read them, the call is 30 minutes and outcome-focused. If they don't read them, they're not actually warm yet.

First call after implementing this: the prospect opened with "I read all three posts you sent. I'm sold on the approach. My question is just timing."

Call length: 22 minutes. Close: <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->5K. [[Pre-call_content_sequence_2024_11_09]]

Third example: a founder who thought "engagement" meant "interest."

They had one prospect who had liked 14 posts over two months. Never commented. Never visited the profile. Never clicked a link.

I asked: "What do you think this person wants?"

"They must be interested, right? They keep liking my posts."

Wrong. Liking is not interest. Liking is acknowledgment. Interest is when they take an action that costs them something. Commenting costs reputation. Profile visits cost time. Clicking a link costs attention.

Likes cost nothing.

I told the founder: ignore the likers. Track the clickers.

They rebuilt their content to include one link per post. Either a case study, a resource, a landing page, or a Calendly link. Then they tracked who clicked.

Over the next month: 47 likes, 3 clicks.

All three clickers got a DM. Two replied. One booked. One closed. $31K.

The likers? Still liking. Still not buying. [[Engagement_quality_framework_2024_12_02]]

## Scripts and exact phrases

When someone engages twice: "I noticed you commented on both the [topic A] post and the [topic B] post — are you working through [specific problem] right now?"

When someone reaches out to you: "Love to chat. Quick question first — what's changed recently that made this a priority now?"

When booking a call: "Before we jump on, I'm going to send you three posts that outline the problem, the process, and a result. If those resonate, we'll spend the call on fit and timing. Sound good?"

When someone says "let me think about it" on a call: "Totally fair. Walk me through what's still unclear. Price, scope, fit, or timing?"

When someone ghosts after a call: "Circling back — I'm assuming this isn't a priority right now, which is fine. If that changes, you know where to find me."

When a warm prospect comments but doesn't DM: "You've asked great questions on the last few posts — happy to jump on a 20-minute call if you want to dig into [specific topic] for your situation specifically. Here's my Calendly: [link]."

When someone visits your profile three times in a week: "Noticed you've been checking out the profile — if you're evaluating whether this is a fit, happy to answer any questions over DM or a quick call."

When someone engages with a post about a specific problem they have: "This clearly resonates — are you dealing with [exact problem from post] right now, or just interested in the approach?"

## Common mistakes

**Mistake 1: Treating one engagement as permission to pitch.**

One comment is not warm. One like is not interest. The warm prospect has engaged at least three times over at least two weeks. If you pitch on engagement one, you're skipping the recognition sequence. You'll sound desperate. The prospect will ghost.

**Mistake 2: Not tracking who's engaging repeatedly.**

If you're counting total engagements instead of repeat engagers, you're measuring the wrong thing. The vanity metric is 47 likes. The buying signal is one person who liked three posts, commented once, and visited your profile.

**Mistake 3: Asking for a call before giving value.**

If the prospect doesn't know what they're getting on the call, they won't book. The DM that converts doesn't say "let's chat." It says "I saw you engaged with the [topic] post — I have a [specific resource/framework/answer] that might help with [their specific situation]. Want me to send it, or would a 20-minute call make more sense?"

**Mistake 4: Not pre-qualifying in the DM.**

If you book a call with someone who hasn't named the problem, you'll spend the whole call doing discovery instead of closing. The warm prospect should name the problem in the DM thread before the call is booked. If they can't, they're not ready yet.

**Mistake 5: Letting warm prospects go cold.**

If someone engaged with three posts two months ago and you never reached out, they've moved on. The window for a warm prospect is two weeks. After that, they either booked with someone else or decided it wasn't a priority. Don't let recognition momentum die.

**Mistake 6: Confusing "following" with "buying."**

Having 2,400 connections doesn't mean you have 2,400 warm prospects. It means you have 2,400 people who might see your content. The warm prospect is the person who saw your content, engaged with it, came back for more, and took an action that cost them something.

## The contrarian read

Most marketing advice tells you to "nurture" prospects with email sequences and retargeting ads.

That's the slow way.

The warm prospect arc isn't about nurture. It's about recognition and action.

The conventional wisdom says: post content, build a list, send emails, wait for them to raise their hand.

The contrarian truth: post content, track who engages, reach out directly, close the deal.

Email sequences work when you have 10,000 people on a list and you're optimizing for 2% conversion. DMs work when you have 14 people who engaged three times and you're optimizing for 50% conversion.

The other contrarian truth: you don't need more traffic. You need better tracking.

Most founders think their problem is reach. "If I could just get in front of more people…"

Wrong.

The problem is you're not noticing the people who are already in front of you. The warm prospect is already on your profile. They're already engaging. They're waiting for you to notice and reach out.

Stop chasing new eyeballs. Start closing the ones already watching.

The founder posts three times a week. Not viral hooks. Not generic advice. Specific problem-statements that only their ICP would recognize.

Each post gets 20-40 reactions. 3-5 comments. 5-10 profile views.

The founder tracks the names. Who engaged this week? Who engaged last week? Who engaged both weeks?

By the end of the month, there are 8 names on the "engaged three times" list.

The founder sends DMs to all 8. Six reply. Four book calls. Three close.

Revenue from one month of content: $85K.

The founder doesn't run ads. Doesn't send cold emails. Doesn't do outbound. Just posts consistently, tracks engagement, and reaches out when the pattern says "ready."

Conversion rate on warm inbound: 75%.

Average close time: 3 weeks from first engagement to signed contract.

Average deal size: <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->8K.

The system runs itself. The founder spends two hours a week on content and one hour a week on DMs. The rest of the time is spent on delivery.
