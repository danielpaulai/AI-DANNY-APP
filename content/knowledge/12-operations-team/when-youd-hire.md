## The core principle

You hire when the cost of not hiring is greater than the cost of hiring badly.

Most founders delay hiring until they're drowning. They measure the cost in hours lost, deals missed, or burnout brewing. But the real cost is invisible: the strategic work that never happens because you're trapped in execution. The threshold isn't "I'm too busy." It's "this role would unlock the next stage of revenue, and I can measure that unlock in dollars."

## Why this matters

Hiring too early burns cash on coordination overhead before the business can support it. You add a person, split focus, create management debt, and slow down the very thing you thought you were accelerating.

Hiring too late traps the founder in a loop where they're simultaneously the rainmaker, the operator, and the quality control. The business flatlines because growth requires the founder's attention, but operations consume it. Pipeline goes quiet. Strategy gets deferred. The founder becomes the bottleneck.

The cost of getting this wrong shows up in three places. First, revenue stalls because the founder can't do both sales and delivery at the scale the business needs. Second, client experience degrades because the founder is stretched too thin to maintain quality. Third, the founder burns out and the business loses its only irreplaceable asset: the person who holds the vision.

The leverage of getting it right is that one person in the right role can unlock the founder's highest-value hours. A VA handling admin means the founder can take three more sales calls a week. A junior ops person running fulfillment means the founder can focus on product strategy. The business doesn't just run smoother—it grows faster because the founder is back in their zone of genius.

The decision rule: if adding this person would free up 10+ hours a week of founder time, and those 10 hours would generate more revenue than the hire costs, you're already late.

## How it shows up in client work

The pattern I see most often is founders who know they need help but can't articulate what help means. They say "I need someone to handle marketing" when what they really need is "I need someone to execute the content calendar so I can stop writing posts at 11pm." The role isn't clear, so they hire for vibes instead of outcomes.

I had a client running a $300K/year consulting practice who was spending 15 hours a week on admin: invoicing, scheduling, CRM updates, proposal formatting. We calculated that those 15 hours, redirected to sales calls and client strategy, would generate an additional $5K/month in revenue within 90 days. A VA at <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->,200/month was a 4x ROI in the first quarter. But she delayed six months because "I don't know if I can afford it." The real question was: can you afford not to? [[Session_1_2025_03_28]]

Another client, a founder running a $500K/year service business, was doing everything: sales, delivery, ops, content. Pipeline was strong but she couldn't scale delivery. Every new client meant working weekends. She hired a junior ops person to handle onboarding and client check-ins. Within 60 days her delivery capacity doubled, client satisfaction scores went up, and she closed two deals she wouldn't have had time to service before. The hire didn't just free up hours—it removed the cap on revenue. [[Onboarding_2024_11_13]]

The opposite mistake: hiring before the role is proven. I worked with a founder who hired a marketing manager at $60K/year when the business was doing <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->00K annually. The founder hadn't done the marketing work themselves yet, so they couldn't define success. Six months in, the marketing manager was producing average content that didn't move the needle, and the founder was frustrated but couldn't articulate why. The role failed because it was hired speculatively, not strategically. You can't delegate what you haven't defined.

The clearest signal you're ready to hire: you're turning down revenue because you don't have delivery capacity, or you're spending more than 10 hours a week on work that a <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->5-25/hour person could do better than you.

The second signal: you've mapped the role's outcomes and you can measure success in 90 days. "Handle social media" is not a role. "Post 5x/week on LinkedIn, manage DM replies, and book 3 discovery calls per month from inbound" is a role. If you can't write that sentence, you're not ready to hire.

Third signal: the cost of the hire is less than 20% of monthly revenue, or the hire unlocks revenue growth that makes the ratio irrelevant. A <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->K/month VA is risky at $5K/month revenue. It's a no-brainer at <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->0K/month revenue. It's strategic if that VA frees you to close <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->0K/month in new deals.

I've also seen founders hire out of ego or loneliness, not need. They want to feel like a "real company" with a team. They bring someone on before the role is clear, before the business can afford it, and before they've done the work to define what success looks like. Six months later they're paying someone to sit in Slack and "help out" with no measurable outcomes. That's not a hire—it's overhead disguised as progress.

The diagnostic I use with clients: make a list of every recurring task you do weekly. Categorize each as Rainmaker (only you can do this), Strategic (high-value but could be trained), or Execution (anyone competent could do this). If more than 30% of your hours are in Execution, you're leaving money on the table by not hiring. If you're in Strategic but not Rainmaker, you're avoiding the work that actually grows the business.

One client did this exercise and realized she was spending 8 hours a week formatting proposals and updating spreadsheets. We hired a VA for 10 hours/week at <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->0/hour. Those 8 hours went to sales calls. She closed $40K in new contracts in the next 60 days. The VA paid for herself in week three. [[LSCS_2024_10_04]]

The anti-pattern: hiring to solve a workflow problem when the real issue is positioning or offer clarity. If you're not closing deals, hiring a salesperson won't fix it. If your content isn't landing, hiring a social media manager won't fix it. Hire to scale what's already working, not to fix what's broken.

## Common mistakes

**Hiring before the role is proven.** You can't delegate work you haven't done yourself. If you've never run ads, don't hire an ads manager. If you've never built a content system, don't hire a content person. Do it badly first, document what works, then hire someone to do it better.

**Hiring for vibes instead of outcomes.** "I need help" is not a job description. "I need someone to handle client onboarding: send welcome emails, schedule kickoff calls, upload assets to the portal, and respond to questions within 2 hours" is a job description. If you can't define success in one sentence, the hire will fail.

**Hiring out of loneliness or ego.** Building a team feels like progress. But if the business can't afford it, or the role isn't clear, you're just burning cash to feel less alone. Hire when the business needs it, not when your ego does.

**Hiring too junior for a strategic role.** If the role requires judgment, systems thinking, or the ability to operate without supervision, a <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->5/hour VA won't cut it. You'll spend more time managing them than doing the work yourself. Hire at the level the role demands.

**Hiring too senior too early.** A <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->00K marketing director at a <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->00K/year business is overhead, not leverage. You need execution, not strategy. Hire for the stage you're at, not the stage you want to be at.

**Not firing fast enough.** If someone isn't hitting the outcomes you defined in 90 days, and you've given them clear feedback and tools, let them go. Keeping a bad hire out of guilt or hope costs more than the salary—it costs momentum.

## The contrarian read

Most hiring advice says "hire slow, fire fast." I say hire fast if the cost of delay is measurable in lost revenue.

If you're turning down <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->0K/month in contracts because you don't have delivery capacity, and a <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->K/month hire solves that, waiting another quarter to "be sure" just cost you $30K. Hire fast, define outcomes clearly, and fire fast if they don't hit them.

The other contrarian take: you don't need full-time employees at the early stage. You need outcomes. A VA at 10 hours/week can do more for a <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->00K/year business than a full-time employee, because they're focused on execution, not entitled to benefits, and you're not paying for idle time.

The line where full-time makes sense: when the role requires deep context, ongoing relationships, or strategic thinking. Admin, data entry, and content formatting? Contractors. Client success, product strategy, and team leadership? Full-time.

## What it looks like when it's working

The founder's calendar shifts from 70% execution to 70% rainmaker work. Sales calls increase. Strategic thinking sessions happen weekly instead of never. Client satisfaction scores go up because delivery is consistent.

The business scales without the founder working more hours. Revenue grows 20-30% within 90 days of the hire. The founder stops saying "I don't have time for that."

The hired person hits their outcomes within 90 days without constant supervision. They know what success looks like, they report against it, and they improve the process over time.

The founder can take a week off and the business doesn't collapse. That's the real signal the hire worked.
