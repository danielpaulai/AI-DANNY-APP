## The core principle

Templates are leverage. Either you own them or they own you.

Every hour spent building a contract, writing a scope, or defining a workflow is an hour you'll spend again next month unless you capture it once and re-use it forever. The principle isn't about paperwork. It's about capturing your best decision once, making it repeatable, and never starting from zero again.

## Why this matters

Most founders spend 6–12 hours a month recreating the same documents from scratch. Sales proposals written fresh for every prospect. Onboarding emails typed out manually for every new client. Contract terms negotiated from memory instead of from a tested template. That's 72–144 hours a year doing work they've already done before.

The cost isn't just time. It's inconsistency. When you write from scratch every time, quality varies. Client A gets your best thinking because you were sharp that day. Client B gets a rushed version because you were overloaded. Client C gets different payment terms because you forgot what you agreed to with Client A.

Worse: when the work lives in your head instead of in a file, it can't be delegated. You become the bottleneck. Every proposal waits for you. Every contract needs your review. Every onboarding sequence needs your approval. The business scales but you don't.

The leverage is brutal once you capture it. A contract template you build once gets used 20 times. A proposal structure you define once closes 15 deals. An onboarding SOP you write once handles 30 clients. That's 20x, 15x, 30x return on the same hour.

The founders who scale without burning out are the ones who stop treating every client like a one-off. They productise the work. They turn their best thinking into reusable assets. They build systems that run without them.

## How it shows up in client work

Most clients who come to Purely Personal don't have a contracts folder. They have versions scattered across email threads, Google Docs they can't find, and memory of "what I did last time." When I ask "can I see your standard proposal," they send three different files and say "I use whichever one feels right." That's not a system. That's chaos.

The first thing we do is audit what already exists. Pull every proposal they've sent in the last six months. Every contract. Every onboarding email. Every scope document. Line them up side by side and find the pattern. What shows up every time? What changes? What's optional?

Then we build the base template. Not a Word doc with blanks to fill in. A living document in Claude that knows their positioning, their pricing tiers, their standard terms, their risk reversals, and their process. When they need a proposal, they don't start typing. They tell Claude the client name, the offer, and the outcome. Claude writes the full proposal in their voice using the approved structure.

One client (B2B SaaS consultant, <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->50K ARR) was spending 4 hours per proposal. Writing, formatting, checking terms, remembering what worked last time. We built her proposal template in Claude with 5 pricing tiers, 3 guarantee options, and her entire SOW structure. Now it's 20 minutes. She tells Claude the client and the tier. Claude outputs the full proposal. She reviews, tweaks one or two lines, and sends. That's 3.5 hours back per deal. She closes 12 deals a year. That's 42 hours she's not spending on proposals anymore.

Another client (personal brand coach, solo operator) didn't have an onboarding sequence. Every new client got a different version of "here's how we work together." Some clients got Loom videos. Some got email threads. Some got nothing and figured it out as they went. Three months in, half of them didn't know what deliverables they were getting. We built a 7-email onboarding sequence that fires automatically when the contract is signed. Email 1: welcome, what to expect this week. Email 2: how to book your first call. Email 3: how to prep for the call. Email 4: tools you'll use. Email 5: how to give feedback. Email 6: week 1 deliverables. Email 7: what success looks like by week 4. Now every client gets the same high-quality onboarding whether she's available or not. She onboards 8 clients a quarter. That's 32 onboarding conversations she's not having manually anymore.

The pattern across every engagement: the more you productise, the more you scale. The more you scale, the more you need templates. Templates don't limit you. They free you. You still customise when it matters. But you start from 80% done instead of 0%.

## Scripts and exact phrases

"Can I see the last three versions of this document you sent to clients?" (The audit question that starts every template build.)

"What stays the same every time, and what changes based on the client?" (The structuring question that defines which parts are locked and which parts are variables.)

"If you couldn't touch this for the next five clients, what would need to be true for it to still work?" (The stress test that forces them to think about what's actually required versus what's just habit.)

"We're not building a blank template. We're building a working document with your voice, your structure, and your terms already in it. You just change the client name and the specifics." (How I position the template so they don't think it's generic.)

"This template should make you faster, not more careful. If you're spending 30 minutes reviewing it before you send it, the template isn't good enough yet." (The speed test. If the template doesn't make them confident, it's not done.)

"Every hour you spend building this template buys you back 20 hours over the next year." (The ROI frame that makes them prioritise template work even when it feels like busywork.)

"The goal isn't to never customise. The goal is to start from 80% done instead of 0%." (How I explain why templates don't kill creativity or personalisation.)

"If this lived in Claude and it knew your voice, your pricing, and your process, what would you tell it to write a proposal for you?" (The framing question that shifts them from "filling in a Word doc" to "briefing an AI assistant who already knows the structure.")

## Common mistakes

**Mistake 1: Building templates as blank Word docs with brackets to fill in.**

This is what everyone does first. They copy-paste an old proposal, delete the client-specific parts, and save it as "Proposal_Template_v1.docx." Then they open it next month, realise they forgot half the structure, and spend 45 minutes rebuilding it anyway. A template isn't a blank page. It's a working document pre-filled with your voice, your structure, and your terms. The only thing that changes is the client name and the offer details.

**Mistake 2: Storing templates in random places.**

Google Drive folder called "Templates (old)." Desktop folder called "Client stuff." Email drafts saved as templates but buried under 200 other emails. If you can't find the template in 10 seconds, it doesn't exist. Templates live in one place. One folder. One Claude Project. One Notion database. One location you can access from anywhere without searching.

**Mistake 3: Not versioning the template.**

You tweak the proposal template after a client asked for net-30 terms instead of net-15. You update the onboarding email because a client said the original was confusing. You adjust the contract because legal flagged a clause. But you don't save the old version. Now you don't know what changed or why. Version your templates. Name them "Proposal_v3_2024_11_15.docx" or tag them in Claude as "Version 3 (net-30 added)." You need to know what you're working from.

**Mistake 4: Building templates for one-off situations.**

Client asks for a custom deliverable you've never done before. You spend 3 hours writing it. It goes great. You don't save it as a template because "I'll probably never do that again." Six months later, another client asks for the same thing. You start from scratch again. If you spent more than 1 hour creating it, template it. Even if you only use it twice, that's 2x return.

**Mistake 5: Not teaching the team how to use the template.**

You build the perfect proposal template. Your team doesn't use it. They still write from scratch because they don't trust it or they don't know where it lives or they don't understand what to change and what to leave alone. Templates without training are files that sit in a folder. Train the team. Walk them through it once. Record a Loom. Write a 2-minute guide. Make it easy to use or it won't get used.

**Mistake 6: Treating templates as final.**

You build the template. You use it 10 times. You never update it. A year later it's outdated. Your pricing changed. Your process changed. Your positioning changed. But the template still says the old thing. Templates are living documents. Review them quarterly. Update them when something changes. A stale template is worse than no template because it trains clients to expect something you no longer deliver.

## The contrarian read

Most people think templates make you sound robotic. Like you're sending the same canned message to everyone. That's only true if you're using bad templates.

A good template sounds more like you than writing from scratch does. Because when you write from scratch, you're distracted. You're thinking about what to say next. You're second-guessing your tone. You're adjusting on the fly. The result is inconsistent. Sometimes sharp, sometimes generic, sometimes confusing.

A template captures you at your best. It's the version of the document you wrote when you had time to think, when you weren't rushing, when you structured it properly. Every client gets that version. Not the tired version. Not the rushed version. The best version.

The other contrarian take: templates are client-facing positioning tools, not just internal efficiency tools. When a prospect receives a proposal that's clearly structured, professionally branded, and detailed in the right places, they assume you run a tight operation. When they receive a proposal that's formatted inconsistently, has typos, or feels like it was rushed, they assume your work will be the same. The template is the first impression of how you operate. Make it good.

Third take: contracts and SOPs aren't just for scaling. They're for clarity. Even if you're solo and you only have 5 clients a year, having a standard contract eliminates 90% of scope creep conversations. Having a standard onboarding SOP eliminates 90% of "wait, how does this work?" emails. Templates don't just save time. They set expectations. And clear expectations are what prevent refund requests, bad reviews, and clients who ghost halfway through.

## What it looks like when it's working

You close a deal on a Tuesday. The proposal was sent 20 minutes after the call. The client signed the contract by Thursday. The onboarding email sequence started Friday. By Monday, the client knows exactly what to expect, when to expect it, and how to give feedback. You didn't write a single email manually. The system ran.

Your team closes deals without you. They use the same proposal template. Same structure. Same terms. Same pricing tiers. You review the client name and the offer tier, approve it in 5 minutes, and move on. The proposal sounds like you even though you didn't write it.

You onboard 10 clients in a quarter and every single one says "this is the most organised onboarding I've ever experienced." Because it is. It's the same high-quality sequence every time. No one gets forgotten. No one gets a half-done version. Everyone gets the full experience.

You haven't rewritten a contract in 8 months. Same terms. Same structure. Same language. Clients sign it without negotiating because it's clear, fair, and professional. No legal back-and-forth. No "can we change this clause?" emails. Just sign and start.

You can take a week off and the business still runs. Proposals still go out. Contracts still get signed. Clients still get onboarded. Because the system doesn't need you. It's running on templates.

Before: 12 hours a week on proposals, contracts, onboarding, client setup. After: 2 hours a week reviewing what the system already did. That's 10 hours back. That's 520 hours a year. That's 3 months of full-time work you're not doing anymore because you templatised it once.
