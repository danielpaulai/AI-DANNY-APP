## The core principle

Plain English isn't optional. It's the filter every piece of work passes through before it ships.

## Why this matters

Most founders write like they're trying to impress their college professor. They use words like "leverage" and "synergy" and "robust" because those words sound smart. They bury the point three paragraphs deep because that's how business writing is supposed to work.

Nuseir Yassin taught me the opposite. If a 14-year-old in Malaysia or the Philippines can't understand what you wrote, you failed. Not because those audiences are less intelligent. Because plain English is the only language that crosses every border, every industry, every education level.

The cost of ignoring this shows up everywhere. A training document that nobody reads. A session title that gets zero clicks. An email campaign that tanks because people didn't understand the value proposition in the first two lines.

The leverage of getting it right compounds daily. When a founder can explain their offer in one sentence a stranger understands, they close deals faster. When a team can write internal docs everyone follows, execution speeds up. When a landing page uses plain words, conversion rates double.

This isn't about dumbing things down. It's about respecting the reader's time. Every word that doesn't carry weight is a word that slows comprehension. Every sentence that requires a re-read is a sentence that loses attention.

## How it shows up in client work

I ran the Nas Academy AI School program from late 2023 through early 2026. My role was Programming and Retention Lead, which meant I built curriculum documents, wrote session titles, designed challenge flows, and trained the operations team on how to communicate with 50,000+ students across three user buckets: paid Nas.io members, paid AI School students, and free users.

Every single document I produced went through what I call "the Alex test." Alex was the head of operations, based in Malaysia. If Alex couldn't immediately understand what I wrote without clarification, I rewrote it. Not because Alex wasn't technical. Because Alex represented the actual audience: operators in different time zones, working in their second or third language, who needed to execute immediately without back-and-forth.

The most common failure pattern I saw across trainer briefs and session materials was **assuming shared context**. Trainers would write things like "leverage the platform's native integrations" or "facilitate a deep dive into prompt optimization." Those phrases mean nothing to someone who just needs to know what to do in the next 30 minutes.

I rebuilt every trainer brief to follow a strict structure: what you're building, why it matters in one outcome-based sentence, the exact steps to execute, and what success looks like in observable terms. No jargon. No assumed knowledge. If a step required using a specific tool, I named the tool and linked the exact page they needed to open. [[Nas.io document redesign with branding - 6b8ef772]]

Session title optimization was where plain English proved its value most directly. We had a session originally titled "Content Transformation Strategies." Zero engagement. I rewrote it using Nuseir's framework: specific action word, specific deliverable, specific timeframe. The new title: "Create 5 Lead-Generating Posts From Any Content in 30 Minutes." That session booked 3x the registrations. [[March Madness Marathon problems and solutions - 508640a8]]

The March Madness Marathon debrief with Alex exposed the pattern at scale. We had built a Sunday event expecting 250+ attendees. We got 112. Alex asked the hard question: "Why weren't we able to achieve that?" The answer wasn't just wrong day choice or late marketing. The core problem was nobody could explain in plain terms what the event actually delivered. The promotional copy said things like "springboard to trying to boost engagement" and "comprehensive learning experience." Those phrases sound like every other online event. They don't answer the only question that matters: what do I walk out with? [[March Madness Marathon problems and solutions - 508640a8]]

When I rebuilt the promotional strategy for the April 1 summit, I applied the plain English filter at every layer. The event title: "Walk Out With Your First AI Employee." Not "Transform Your Business With AI" or "Unlock AI-Powered Productivity." The 14-day challenge leading up to it: "Your First AI Employee in 14 Days." Every email, every landing page, every session description had to pass the same test: can someone in Syria, working in software for $50 a month, immediately understand what this will do for them? ## Scripts and exact phrases

**The 14-year-old test:** "If a 14-year-old in Malaysia can't understand this, rewrite it."

**The Alex test:** "Think about what Alex would say. If Alex needs clarification, the brief failed."

**Session title formula:** "[Specific action word] + [Specific deliverable] + [Specific timeframe]." Example: "Create 5 Lead-Generating Posts From Any Content in 30 Minutes."

**The scholarship question:** "Why you? That's the answer."

**Plain outcome language:** "You'll walk out with [concrete deliverable]." Not "You'll gain insights into" or "You'll explore methodologies for."

**Email hook structure:** Start with the outcome. "Here's how to turn one piece of content into 5 posts in 30 minutes." Not "Imagine if you could unlock your creative potential."

**The reframe from jargon:** Replace "leverage native integrations" with "use the tool." Replace "facilitate a deep dive" with "build." Replace "comprehensive learning experience" with "what you'll walk out with."

**Document structure rule:** What you're building. Why it matters in one sentence. The exact steps. What success looks like.

## Common mistakes

**Corporate jargon creep.** The default mode for most business writing is to reach for words like "synergy" and "robust" and "seamless" because those words feel professional. They're not. They're filler. Nuseir would reject a session title instantly if it contained any of those words.

**Assuming shared context.** The most common failure in trainer briefs and internal docs: writing as if the reader already knows what you know. "Leverage the platform's native integrations" means nothing unless you name the platform, name the integration, and link to the page where they click the button.

**Burying the outcome.** Most session descriptions, landing pages, and email campaigns save the value proposition for paragraph three. By then, the reader is gone. Lead with what they'll walk out with. Everything else is context.

**Using "imagine if..." hooks.** This was a pattern I saw constantly in draft content for AI School promotional materials. "Imagine if you could 10x your productivity with AI." That's not a hook. That's a question that makes the reader do work. Start with the outcome: "Here's how to cut your content workflow from 12 hours to 2."

**Hedging with academic language.** "Participants will have the opportunity to explore..." is hedging. "You'll build..." is committing. Nuseir's style never hedges. You either promise the thing or you don't run the session.

**Overloading with features instead of outcomes.** A session description that lists "We'll cover prompt engineering, Claude Projects, custom instructions, and knowledge base setup" tells the attendee nothing. The reframe: "You'll walk out with a Content Writer skill that writes 10 posts a week in your voice."

## The contrarian read

Most branding and positioning advice says "sound premium" or "establish thought leadership" or "demonstrate expertise." Nuseir's positioning does the opposite. Nas Daily and Nas Academy succeeded by sounding like a friend explaining something over coffee, not a consultant delivering a keynote.

The conventional wisdom in B2B says "speak to the pain, speak to the sophistication level of your buyer." That's correct. But most people interpret "sophistication" as "use industry jargon to signal you're one of them." Nuseir proved the opposite. The more sophisticated your buyer, the more they value clarity. CEOs don't have time to decode "synergistic go-to-market alignment." They want: "This will cut your sales cycle by 30 days."

The content marketing playbook says "build authority through depth and nuance." Nuseir built authority through clarity and repetition. His one-minute videos on complex geopolitical topics got 50M views not because they were nuanced. Because they were clear. Plain English is the moat. Everyone else is trying to sound smart. You're trying to be understood.

The contrarian read on Nas Academy itself: most online education platforms optimize for course catalog breadth and certification prestige. Nas Academy optimized for outcome clarity and instructor credibility. Every course had a "Walk Out With" section. Not "Upon completion you will have gained..." but "You will walk out with a portfolio site, 3 paying clients, and a $500/month retainer system." That's the promise. That's the standard. If the course can't deliver that in plain terms, don't run the course.

## What it looks like when it's working

Engagement metrics shift immediately. The session title rewrite from "Content Transformation Strategies" to "Create 5 Lead-Generating Posts From Any Content in 30 Minutes" tripled registrations in one week. Same session. Same content. Different language.

Internal execution speeds up. When I rewrote the trainer briefs to remove all jargon and assumed context, Alexa (ops lead) stopped sending clarification questions. The brief itself answered every question she would have asked.

Sales calls close faster. When a founder can explain their offer in one sentence a stranger understands, objections disappear. Not because the objection was invalid. Because the buyer didn't have to work to understand what they were buying.

Email open rates climb. The "Takemore style" emails Nuseir's team used followed one rule: lead with the outcome in the subject line, then prove it in three sentences. Open rates on those campaigns consistently hit 40%+ because the value was clear before the click.

Content gets shared. Plain English content travels faster than expert content. A post that says "Here's how to write 10 posts in 2 hours" gets saved and sent to colleagues. A post that says "Optimizing your content production workflows through AI-native methodologies" gets scrolled past.

The observable signal at scale: when the March Madness Marathon failed to hit attendance targets, the debrief with Alex identified plain English as the missing filter. When we rebuilt the April summit strategy with that filter applied at every layer, registrations jumped 2.5x in the first week.
