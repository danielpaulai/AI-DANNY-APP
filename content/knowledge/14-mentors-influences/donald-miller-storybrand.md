## The core principle

StoryBrand taught me that confusion kills conversion, and that most founders fail because they're talking about themselves instead of solving the customer's problem.

The framework is simple: the customer is the hero, you're the guide, and your job is to give them a plan to win.

## Why this matters

Every founder I work with shows up with the same invisible tax: they're explaining their offer like they're writing an instruction manual, not like they're starting a conversation with someone who has a problem.

The result is LinkedIn profiles that read like résumés, landing pages that open with "We are a leading provider of innovative solutions," and DMs that say "I'd love to connect and explore synergies."

All of it sounds professional. None of it books calls.

StoryBrand gives you the architecture to flip that. Instead of "Here's what we do," you lead with "Here's what you're facing, here's what it costs you, here's how we help you win."

The leverage is immediate. A founder can rewrite their LinkedIn headline, their About section, and their first cold DM using the framework in under two hours, and the response rate changes that week.

The cost of ignoring it is quiet failure. You post consistently, people like your content, nobody books. You send 50 outreach messages, 48 ghost you. You build a great product and nobody understands why they need it.

## How it shows up in client work

I use StoryBrand architecture in three places: positioning statements, LinkedIn profiles, and outreach scripts.

The first place it shows up is when a client can't explain what they do in one sentence. They'll say something like "We help companies leverage AI to optimize their workflows and unlock new efficiencies."

I'll stop them and ask: "Who is the hero? What problem are they facing? What does winning look like for them?"

Then we rebuild it using the StoryBrand structure: "[ICP] wants [specific outcome]. [Obstacle] is in the way. We [specific action that removes the obstacle]."

Example from [[Todd_1_1_2024_10_30]]: Todd is an entertainer and storyteller who was struggling to articulate his offer. His original pitch was "I do performances and workshops and I've written four books."

I walked him through the StoryBrand reframe: "Elementary schools want kids engaged in learning. Boring lectures are in the way. I do live storytelling performances that make lessons stick."

That's the difference. The first version is about Todd. The second version is about the school.

The second place it shows up is LinkedIn profiles. Most founders open with "Founder | CEO | Thought Leader" or some variation of job-title stacking.

I rewrite every headline using the StoryBrand "who you help + what they want + what you do" structure.

From [[Simplify Your LinkedIn Banner with StoryBrand - a2347066]], I rewrote a banner for a client in the game-based training space. The original banner said something about "leveraging gamification for measurable impact."

The rewrite options I gave them all followed the same pattern:
- "Transform Your Team's Potential Into Measurable Results — Through Engaging Game-Based Solutions"
- "Help Your Team Win — Expert Game-Based Training For Measurable Business Growth"
- "Build Stronger Teams — Game-Based Learning That Creates Real Business Impact"

Every version makes the customer the hero, names their desire, and positions the founder as the guide.

The third place is outreach. Cold DMs fail because founders open with "I'd love to connect" or "I saw your profile and wanted to reach out."

Both of those are about the sender, not the recipient.

StoryBrand fixes it by forcing you to open with the problem: "Most [ICP] are dealing with [specific pain]. I help [specific people] solve that by [specific method]. Would you be open to a 15-minute call?"

From [[Personal_branding_-_Session_5_2023_11_10]], I was coaching Ricardo on his studio business positioning. His original outreach was "We're a premium photography studio in Singapore."

I rewrote it as: "E-commerce brands need product photos that convert. Most studios deliver generic stock-style images. We shoot lifestyle product photography that looks like editorial content, and our clients see 20-30% higher CTR on their ads."

That's a StoryBrand script. The hero is the e-commerce brand. The problem is generic photos. The guide is Ricardo. The plan is lifestyle photography. The result is higher CTR.

## Specific examples from the vault

The clearest example of StoryBrand in action is from [[Nas.io document redesign with branding - 6b8ef772]], where I was helping the NAS.IO operations team structure session summaries and email campaigns.

The original format was "Here's what we covered in the session" followed by a list of topics.

Nobody clicked. Nobody engaged.

I rebuilt the structure using StoryBrand: "You joined this session because [specific problem you were facing]. We walked through [specific solution]. Here's what to do next: [specific action with a deadline]."

Engagement doubled within two weeks.

Another example is from [[Guide_Cohort_Meeting_2023_12_21]], where I was running a cohort call for StoryBrand guides. One of the participants, Jen, was struggling to position her funnel and website work.

Her original pitch was "I do funnels and websites for online education companies."

I pushed her to name the hero's problem: "Online education founders are losing leads because their funnel is confusing. I rebuild the funnel so people know exactly what to do next, and conversion rates go up 20-40%."

That's the shift. From "I do funnels" to "You're losing leads because your funnel is confusing, and I fix that."

A third example is from [[March Madness Marathon problems and solutions - 508640a8]], where we were diagnosing why a major event underperformed. The marketing copy opened with "Join our March Madness Marathon" — which told people what the event was called, but not what they'd walk away with.

I rewrote it using the StoryBrand promise structure: "Walk away with your first AI employee in 14 days. We'll build it together, step by step, in daily 30-minute sessions."

The rewrite named the outcome (first AI employee), the timeframe (14 days), and the method (daily sessions). That's a complete StoryBrand script in two sentences.

## Scripts and exact phrases

Here are the specific lines I use or teach clients to use when applying StoryBrand:

**For LinkedIn headlines:**
"I help [specific ICP] [achieve specific outcome] without [common obstacle or pain]."

**For About sections:**
"[ICP] wants [specific result]. [Specific problem] gets in the way. I [specific action] so they [specific win]."

**For cold outreach:**
"Most [ICP] are dealing with [specific pain]. I help [specific people] solve that by [specific method]. Open to a quick call?"

**For landing pages:**
"You want [specific outcome]. Here's the problem: [specific obstacle]. Here's the plan: [3 steps]. Here's what you'll walk away with: [specific deliverable with number and timeframe]."

**For closing objections:**
"Let me make sure I understand. You want [restate their goal]. The thing stopping you is [restate their obstacle]. If we can solve that in [timeframe], would that be worth [price]?"

**For session descriptions:**
"You'll walk away with [specific deliverable] in [specific time]. We'll build it together step by step."

**For email subject lines:**
"[Specific result] in [specific time] — here's how"

## Common mistakes

The biggest mistake is treating StoryBrand like a fill-in-the-blank template instead of a thinking tool.

Founders will write "I help businesses succeed" or "I help people achieve their goals" — which technically follows the format but says nothing.

The fix: name the specific person, the specific outcome, and the specific method. If you can't, you don't know your ICP well enough yet.

Second mistake: making the plan too complicated. StoryBrand says "give them a plan," and founders will write a 7-step process with sub-bullets.

The rule I use: three steps maximum. Each step is one short sentence. If you need more than three steps to explain how someone works with you, the offer is too complex.

Third mistake: skipping the stakes. StoryBrand has two failure scenarios — what you avoid (the negative) and what you gain (the positive). Most founders only talk about the positive.

But nobody buys transformation. They buy escape.

The script that works: "Without this, you'll keep [specific painful thing]. With this, you'll finally [specific desired thing]."

Fourth mistake: using StoryBrand to write content instead of using it to structure positioning first. Content is downstream of positioning. If your positioning is vague, your content will be vague even if you're following the framework.

Fix the one-line positioning statement first. Then write content.

Fifth mistake: assuming StoryBrand is only for landing pages. I use it for LinkedIn headlines, cold DMs, sales calls, session descriptions, and offer documents. The framework works anywhere you're trying to get someone to take action.

## The contrarian read

StoryBrand is right about the structure, but wrong about one thing: the idea that you're always the guide and the customer is always the hero.

That works for mass-market products where the buyer is genuinely confused and needs a guide.

But when you're selling to high-level operators — founders running <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->M+ businesses, executives making six-figure decisions — they don't want a guide. They want a peer.

The shift I make: I position as the operator who's already done the thing they're trying to do, and I'm sharing the playbook.

Instead of "I'll guide you," it's "Here's what I did, here's what worked, here's what didn't. Take it and run."

That positioning change books calls with founders who would ghost a "guide."

Second contrarian take: StoryBrand says "make it about them, not about you." True for landing pages. Not true for personal branding.

On LinkedIn, people follow founders because of the founder's voice, POV, and story. If you only talk about the customer, you sound like a generic service provider.

The balance: your content is about you. Your offer is about them.

Third contrarian take: StoryBrand focuses on clarity, which is correct. But clarity without personality is boring.

Most StoryBrand-trained copywriters write like robots. Clean, clear, and completely forgettable.

The fix: apply StoryBrand for structure, then rewrite every line in your actual voice. If it sounds like someone else could have written it, rewrite it again.

## What it looks like when it's working

When StoryBrand is working, the founder can explain what they do in one sentence, and the person they're talking to immediately says "Oh, I know someone who needs that."

That's the signal. If people nod politely and change the subject, the positioning is still vague.

On LinkedIn, the founder's headline and About section get profile views from the right ICP. Not just any views — views from people who then DM or book a call.

In cold outreach, response rates go from 2-5% to 15-25%. Not because the founder is suddenly a better writer, but because the message is finally about the recipient instead of the sender.

On landing pages, the founder sees people reading all the way to the CTA instead of bouncing after the first paragraph.

In sales calls, the prospect says "Yes, that's exactly the problem I'm dealing with" within the first two minutes. That's the StoryBrand script working.

The number I track: how many seconds it takes for someone to understand what you do. If it takes more than 10 seconds, the positioning needs work.

When StoryBrand is fully applied, the founder's positioning becomes a self-qualifying filter. The right people lean in. The wrong people disqualify themselves. Both outcomes save time.
