## The core principle

Coaching is the art of holding space for the client to find their own answer. Consulting is the art of delivering the answer they couldn't have found alone.

The skill is knowing which one the moment demands.

## Why this matters

Most people who sell "coaching" are actually doing consulting disguised as questions. They know the answer. They're just Socratic-method-ing their way to it because someone told them "real coaches never tell."

That's theater.

The cost of getting this wrong runs in both directions.

Tell too much and the client never builds their own conviction. They execute your plan, hit the first obstacle, and freeze. They didn't arrive at the insight themselves so they don't own it when it gets hard.

Ask too much when they genuinely don't know the answer and you waste their time. They're paying you because you've seen the pattern before. If the answer is "charge more" and you spend 20 minutes asking them questions to arrive at "charge more," you just stole 19 minutes they'll never get back.

The mechanism that matters: **conviction transfer only happens when the client discovers the answer themselves, but pattern recognition only happens when you've seen the same mistake 50 times and they've seen it once.**

Know which problem you're solving in the room.

## How it shows up in client work

The clearest pattern: when a client is stuck on positioning, they almost never lack data. They lack permission.

In [[Victoria_Feldman_and_Daniel_Paul_2024_06_17]], Victoria came in saying "I don't know if my ICP makes sense." She's working at Amazon. She's already getting clients through word of mouth. She's charging <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->00 per session and getting $500 speaking gigs.

The actual problem wasn't "does the ICP make sense." The actual problem was "I'm afraid to pick one thing and exclude everything else."

That's a consulting moment, not a coaching moment.

I didn't ask her ten discovery questions about her ideal client. I told her: "You're in a very good position in Amazon. You could use that as leverage to get clients to make them pay more. Right. So without knowing anything, if I jump on a call with you, like, okay, you're working on Amazon and thinking, okay, this person has a lot of connection."

Direct. Consulting. She needed to hear from someone who's closed 100+ calls that her Amazon positioning is worth 10x what she's charging.

Then after that, the coaching started: "What is your sales process? I'm assuming you don't have one."

She said no. That's when I asked, not told.

But the permission to charge more? That had to be delivered.

In [[Personal_branding_2025_01_15]], Uma came in with "I don't have connections. I need visibility. I'm technically good but I'm not branded."

He's an executive director at IBM Cloud looking for a VP role. He knows what the problem is. He just doesn't know how to solve it.

That's pure consulting.

I didn't ask him "what do you think you should do about visibility?" I told him exactly what I did with Chandu (Intel VP), showed him the posts that worked, explained the pattern, and walked him through the next 90 days.

He didn't need space to think. He needed a map.

The opposite pattern shows up in [[Daniel_and_Ignacio_Ferreira_2024_05_16]]. Ignacio was asking about LinkedIn process and lead generation. He's building a tool. He's in research mode. He's asking discovery questions himself.

In that call, I coached. I asked what his current process was. I asked what part was hardest. I asked about his timeline.

Why? Because he was already oriented toward action. He didn't need me to tell him what to build. He needed me to help him clarify what mattered most so he could build it faster.

The decision rule I use: **If the client is stuck because they lack conviction, coach. If they're stuck because they lack the pattern, consult.**

Victoria lacked conviction. Uma lacked the pattern. Ignacio already had both — he just needed a sounding board.

## Scripts and exact phrases

**When you need to consult (tell):**

"Here's what I'd do if I were you: [specific action with number and timeline]."

"I've seen this pattern 50+ times. The answer is [X]. Here's why."

"You're in a very good position with [credential]. You could use that as leverage to charge [10x current rate]."

"Take a PDF, put an image of five clients. Here's the results. Here's what I'll do with you week by week."

"The price isn't the question. The promise is. Once that's clear, the price negotiates itself."

"I'd actually push back on the premise. Here's why: [specific counter-pattern]."

**When you need to coach (ask):**

"Walk me through what's still unclear. Price, scope, fit, or timing?"

"What is your sales process? I'm assuming you don't have one."

"What's stopping you from charging that today?"

"If you had to pick one thing to ship this week, what would it be?"

"What would have to be true for you to say yes to this right now?"

"You said X, but then you did Y. What's the gap?"

**The transition line between consulting and coaching:**

"Now that we've locked that in, here's what I'd ask you..."

Said after delivering the answer they couldn't have found alone, before opening space for them to figure out the execution.

## Common mistakes

**Mistake 1: Asking when you should tell because you think "real coaches only ask questions."**

Pattern: Client is stuck on pricing. You know the answer is "charge 5x more." You spend 20 minutes asking them Socratic questions to get them to say it themselves.

Cost: You just wasted 19 minutes they paid for. They hired you for pattern recognition, not self-discovery theater.

Cited in [[Victoria_Feldman_and_Daniel_Paul_2024_06_17]] — I didn't ask her what she thought she should charge. I told her $5K minimum. That's the value.

**Mistake 2: Telling when you should ask because you want to look like the expert.**

Pattern: Client says "I'm stuck on my offer." You immediately deliver a 10-step framework without asking what they've already tried.

Cost: You just positioned yourself as a consultant when they hired you to coach. They execute your plan, hit an obstacle, and come back saying "your plan didn't work." Because they never owned it.

**Mistake 3: Asking open-ended questions when the client is venting.**

Pattern: Client starts the call with "I'm overwhelmed. I have no time. Everything is broken."

You respond with: "Tell me more about that. What does overwhelm mean to you?"

Cost: You just added 15 minutes of emotional spiraling to a call that should have been: "Sounds like you're doing 12 hours a week on content. Let's compress that to 2. Here's how."

**Mistake 4: Telling generic advice instead of specific consulting.**

Pattern: Client asks "how do I get visible on LinkedIn?"

You respond with: "Post consistently. Engage with others. Build relationships."

That's not consulting. That's regurgitating what they already know. Consulting would be: "Here's the exact hook structure that worked for my last 3 clients. Here's the post. Here's the engagement it got. Here's why it worked. Now write one."

**Mistake 5: Switching modes mid-answer without flagging it.**

Pattern: You deliver a specific action (consulting), then immediately ask a discovery question (coaching), then deliver another answer (consulting again), all in the same breath.

Cost: The client leaves confused about whether they're supposed to execute what you said or keep thinking about it.

Fix: Flag the switch. "Now that we've locked that in, here's what I'd ask you..." or "Before I tell you what to do, tell me what you've already tried."

## The contrarian read

Most coaching certification programs teach the opposite of what works in high-ticket 1:1 personal branding coaching.

They teach: "Never give advice. Always ask questions. The client has the answer within them."

That's true when you're coaching someone on their feelings. It's false when you're coaching someone on their business.

If I'm charging a founder $5K for 90 days and they ask "what should I charge for my workshop?" and I respond with "what do you feel is the right price?" I just stole their money.

They didn't hire me to facilitate their feelings. They hired me because I've priced 100 workshops and they've priced zero.

The real skill isn't "always coach" or "always consult." The real skill is **reading the room fast enough to know which one the moment demands.**

The second contrarian position: most consultants over-tell because they're insecure.

They think if they don't deliver 47 frameworks in the first call, the client won't see the value. So they dump. And the client leaves overwhelmed and never executes.

The higher-leverage move: tell them the one thing, then coach them through the execution.

Consulting gets them to the starting line. Coaching gets them across the finish.

## What it looks like when it's working

The client says: "I wouldn't have figured that out on my own, but now that you've said it, it's obvious."

That's consulting landing.

The client says: "I knew the answer the whole time. I just needed someone to ask me the right question."

That's coaching landing.

When both are working in the same engagement:

The client executes fast because you told them the specific next move (consulting), and they don't come back asking for permission because they arrived at the "why" themselves (coaching).

In [[Victoria_Feldman_and_Daniel_Paul_2024_06_17]], the session started with consulting (charge $5K minimum, here's the sales process you need) and ended with coaching (what's stopping you from doing this today?).

She left with a number, a process, and conviction. That's the signal.

In [[Personal_branding_2025_01_15]], Uma got the pattern (here's how Chandu's posts work, here's the CEO engagement, here's the repost from inside the company) and then got the next step (now write your story).

Observable outcome: he knows what good looks like and he has a concrete action to execute by Friday.

When it's not working:

The client leaves the call saying "that was really helpful" but doesn't execute anything.

The client comes back next week with the same question you already answered.

The client says "I'm still thinking about it."

All three signals mean you coached when you should have consulted, or consulted when you should have coached.
