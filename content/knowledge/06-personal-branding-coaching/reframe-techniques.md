## The core principle

Every client paralysis is a positioning problem disguised as a confidence problem.

The founder believes they are stuck because they do not know what to say, do not have the right offer, or need more time to think. The real issue is they are standing in the wrong frame. Reframe the positioning — who they serve, what they promise, how they describe the transformation — and the paralysis breaks. Not slowly. Instantly.

## Why this matters

Founders lose months circling the same decisions because they are asking the wrong question.

"Should I niche down or stay broad?" is the wrong question. The right question: "Which niche can I serve so specifically that my offer sounds custom-built when I describe it?" That reframe ends the debate.

"How do I get confident enough to post?" is the wrong question. The right question: "Who is the one person I would send this post to privately, and would they find it useful?" That reframe ships the post.

The cost of staying in the wrong frame is not just time lost. It is pipeline lost. Positioning momentum lost. The founder spends 12 hours a week producing content that does not book calls because the frame they are operating inside — "I need to build my personal brand" — is too vague to guide execution. Reframe to "I need five posts this month that make my ICP realize they have a problem I can solve" and suddenly the work has a target.

The leverage of reframing is that it does not require the founder to learn a new skill, hire help, or wait for market conditions to shift. It requires 90 seconds of strategic discomfort where they accept a narrower, clearer, more uncomfortable truth about who they are and what they sell. Then everything moves.

## How it shows up in client work

The most common pattern: the founder shows up to the call listing everything they could do, waiting for me to pick one.

"I could target coaches, consultants, or agency owners. I could do done-for-you, done-with-you, or group coaching. I could charge <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->K, $5K, or <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->0K. What do you think?"

The reframe: "Who have you already helped get a result you are proud of, and what did they pay you?"

That question cuts through six months of positioning paralysis. The founder names a real client, a real result, a real price. Now we have a frame to build inside. The work stops being hypothetical. [[Ilvy_Westhovens_and_Daniel_Paul_2025_09_09]] shows this exactly. Ilvy listed five ventures. None profitable. I asked: "If you had to sell yourself with one niche, what would it be?" She said mindset coach. I said: "Everything else has to go." The paralysis broke. Within 24 hours she delivered a new one-liner, ICP, and 90-day content challenge commitment.

Another pattern: the founder believes they need a better offer when the real issue is they are describing the wrong transformation.

"I help founders scale their business with AI" is not a transformation. It is a category. The client does not know what they are buying. The reframe: "What does the founder's business look like the day before they hire you, and what does it look like 90 days after?" That forces specificity. "They are spending 12 hours a week on content and getting average engagement. 90 days later they have a system that runs content in 2 hours a week and books 5+ inbound calls a month." Now the offer is real.

[[Business_call_2024_07_29]] with Holly Wiggins is this pattern. She said she wanted to do group coaching on resiliency but had not defined the offer. I pushed: "What does the offer look like? How does it work?" She hesitated. The reframe: "What is the one outcome you can promise in six weeks that someone would pay <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->,500 for?" That forced her to stop theorizing and start packaging. By end of call she had clarity: a six-week resiliency sprint with a specific framework, a pre-sell plan tied to a Virginia Tech speaking gig, and a public commitment to ship.

Third pattern: the founder is posting consistently but no one is booking calls. The diagnosis is always the same: their content is not asking for anything.

The reframe: "What is the one action you want the reader to take after reading this post? Book a call, reply with a question, save it for later, share it with someone, or do nothing?" Most founders have never asked themselves that question. They write the post, hit publish, hope for engagement. The reframe forces a CTA decision before the post is written. "If you want them to book a call, the post needs to make them realize they have a problem. If you want them to reply, the post needs to ask a question that costs them something to answer. Pick one." That reframe turns content theatre into pipeline.

Fourth pattern: the founder is stuck in execution because the plan is too big.

"I need to rebuild my LinkedIn profile, write 30 posts, launch a lead magnet, set up a CRM, and start cold outreach." The reframe: "Cut the plan in half. What is the one thing that, if you shipped it by Friday, would prove this entire strategy works?" Usually it is one post. Or one outbound sequence. Or one offer page. The rest is decoration. Ship the one thing. Test the hypothesis. Build from there.

Fifth pattern: the founder does not know who they are as a brand yet and they are trying to force positioning before identity has emerged.

The reframe: "Stop trying to position. Spend the next seven days writing only about clients you have helped. What they said, what changed, what the result was. Identity emerges from the work, not the planning." [[Ilvy_Westhovens_and_Daniel_Paul_2025_09_09]] again: I told Ilvy to stop listing options and focus on the clients who already found her valuable. High-performing corporate executives. That is her identity. The positioning followed.

Sixth pattern: the founder is afraid to niche because they believe it will exclude potential clients.

The reframe: "You are already excluding them by being vague. A founder scrolling LinkedIn who does not immediately recognize themselves in your content will scroll past. Niche does not shrink your market. It makes your market visible." This reframe lands every time because it is true. Vague positioning is invisible positioning.

## Specific examples from the vault

Holly Wiggins was running multiple ventures and none were profitable. The reframe: "What is the one thing that brings you the most income?" She said: "None of them." I said: "That is the problem. You are doing ten things and no one can remember what you do." The shift: pick one. Mindset coaching. Everything else parks. By end of call she had a niche (corporate executives), a six-week offer, and a pre-sell plan. [[Business_call_2024_07_29]]

Ilvy Westhovens listed five ventures on the intake form: coaching, supplements, smart coffee, AI technology, a solopreneur network. I asked: "What is the one thing that brings you the most income?" She said: "None of them." The reframe: "If you had to sell yourself with one niche, what would it be?" She said mindset coach. I said: "That is all you need to focus on. Everything else has to go." Then: "Who is your ideal customer? You said health-conscious professionals or small business owners. There should never be 'or' in your ICP." She picked high-performing corporate executives. The paralysis broke. Within 24 hours she delivered a new one-liner, mission, vision, and service transformation. [[Ilvy_Westhovens_and_Daniel_Paul_2025_09_09]]

A founder asked: "Should I run another workshop or focus on 1:1s?" The reframe: "Workshops, easily. Three reasons. Leverage: a workshop teaches 20 people what a 1:1 teaches one. Lead source: workshops feed the pipeline, 1:1s drain it. Positioning: workshops position you as the operator, 1:1s position you as the consultant." The decision made itself once the frame shifted from "which do I like more" to "which has more leverage."

Noman Khan was posting leadership content on LinkedIn and getting low engagement. The reframe: "You are teaching niche concepts to a broad audience. Flip it. Talk about trending topics — Agentic AI, identity management — and apply your cybersecurity lens to them. The engagement will follow." The shift: stop trying to educate. Start engaging with what people are already talking about. [[Noman]] captures this pattern across multiple sessions.

A founder said: "My content gets likes but nobody books a call." The reframe: "Likes are not the goal. Booked calls are. Every post needs to make the reader realize they have a problem you can solve, or it is decoration." That single reframe changed the founder's entire content strategy. They stopped optimizing for engagement and started optimizing for pipeline.

Another founder: "I need to think about the offer before I commit." The reframe: "Walk me through what is still unclear. Price, scope, fit, or timing?" They named one. We worked it on the call. The thinking-about-it frame is almost always a missing piece, not actual thinking. Name the piece. Fill it. Close.

## Common mistakes

**Mistake one: treating the symptom instead of the frame.**

The founder says they need more confidence. You give them affirmations or a pep talk. The real issue: they do not know who they are talking to. Reframe the ICP and the confidence returns.

**Mistake two: reframing too gently.**

"Have you considered narrowing your niche?" is not a reframe. It is a suggestion the founder will ignore. "Everything else has to go" is a reframe. It forces a decision.

**Mistake three: accepting the founder's framing as the starting point.**

If the founder says "I need a better LinkedIn profile," do not start there. Ask: "What do you want the profile to do? Book calls? Attract speaking gigs? Position you for a specific role?" The reframe shifts the conversation from tactics to strategy.

**Mistake four: reframing without proof.**

"You should niche down" does not land. "Look at your last five clients. Four of them were corporate executives. That is your niche" lands. Use their own data to reframe.

**Mistake five: offering multiple reframes at once.**

The founder is already overwhelmed. One reframe. One decision. One next step. Anything more is noise.

**Mistake six: reframing the offer before reframing the ICP.**

If the ICP is vague, the offer will be vague. Always reframe who they serve before what they sell.

## The contrarian read

Most coaches ask: "What do you want to achieve?" and build the plan from there.

I ask: "What have you already achieved that you are undervaluing?" and build from there. The founder usually has proof they ignore. A client who got a result. A post that booked a call. A session that landed perfectly. They dismiss it as luck. The reframe: "That was not luck. That is your niche. Do more of that."

The other contrarian move: I do not let founders "explore" positioning.

Exploration is procrastination. Pick one niche, one ICP, one offer. Test it for 90 days. If it does not work, pivot. But most founders never test anything because they are stuck exploring. The reframe: "Commit to one thing for 90 days. If it is wrong, we will know by day 30 and we can adjust." That removes the fear of choosing wrong.

Third contrarian position: I believe most founders do not need more content. They need one piece of content that actually sells.

The founder posts five times a week. None of the posts book calls. The industry advice: post more, be more consistent, optimize your hooks. My reframe: "Stop posting for a week. Write one post that makes your ICP realize they have a problem. Send it privately to three people in your ICP and ask: does this land? If yes, post it. If no, rewrite it." One post that sells is worth 50 posts that get likes.

## What it looks like when it is working

Observable signals:

The founder stops listing options and starts making decisions. "I am focusing on corporate executives who need mindset coaching" instead of "I could do coaching, supplements, or AI consulting."

The founder ships within 24 hours instead of circling for weeks. One-liner delivered. Offer clarified. First post written.

The content starts booking calls. Not because the hooks got better. Because the positioning got clearer.

The founder stops saying "I need to think about it" and starts saying "Here is what I am doing next."

Numbers:

Before reframe: 12 hours a week on content, zero inbound calls, three months circling the same positioning debate.

After reframe: first post ships within 48 hours, first inbound call within two weeks, 90-day content calendar locked, ICP and offer documented.

Before reframe: vague one-liner ("I help founders scale with AI"), no one books a call.

After reframe: specific one-liner ("I help corporate executives turn burnout into clarity in six weeks"), three discovery calls booked in the first month.
