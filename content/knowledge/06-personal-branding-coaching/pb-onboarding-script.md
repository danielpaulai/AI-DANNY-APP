## The core principle

The onboarding call is not a discovery call. The contract is signed. The money is paid. The client already said yes to working with you.

The onboarding call's only job is to collect the raw material you need to build their system and set the expectation that this is a working engagement, not a consulting theatre engagement.

## Why this matters

Most agencies and coaches treat onboarding as a relationship-building exercise. They spend 60 minutes asking the client how they're feeling, walking through the timeline again, and restating what's in the contract.

That's a waste of everyone's time.

The client didn't pay you to be their friend. They paid you to build them a system that books calls and moves their business forward. The onboarding call is the moment you demonstrate that you understand the difference between talking about work and doing work.

If you don't collect the right information in the first session, you spend the next three weeks chasing the client for answers. The build stalls. The client starts to wonder if they made a mistake. You lose momentum before you've shipped anything.

The cost of a bad onboarding call is a 30-day engagement that drags to 60 days because you're waiting on the client to "get back to you" with their positioning, their offer structure, their voice, their ICP. You can't build a content system without that material. You can't write in their voice if you don't know what their voice sounds like.

The leverage of getting this right is that you walk out of the first call with everything you need to build the first deliverable. The client sees progress in the first week. They get excited. They stay engaged. The engagement closes on time or early.

## How it shows up in client work

The pattern across every successful onboarding is the same: I open with a one-line reframe, then I collect structured information in a specific order, then I set the deadline for the first deliverable before we hang up.

The reframe sounds like this: "This call is not about me explaining the process again. It's about me collecting everything I need to build your system. By the end of this call, I'll have enough to ship your first deliverable by Friday. Sound good?"

That one line changes the tone. The client stops expecting a relationship call and starts expecting a working call. They lean forward. They take notes. They answer faster.

The information I collect falls into six categories, in this exact order:

1. **Voice DNA** — I ask them to pull up three pieces of content they've written that sound like them. Could be LinkedIn posts, emails to clients, blog posts, Slack messages, anything. I read them out loud on the call. I ask: "Does this sound like you, or does it sound like you trying to sound professional?" Most of the time they say "trying to sound professional." Then I ask: "When you're talking to a client on a call and you're fired up about something, how do you actually say it?" They answer. I write it down word for word. That's their voice.

2. **ICP and offer** — I ask them to name one person they've worked with in the last six months who was a dream client. Not their best client ever. Their most recent dream client. Then I ask: "What did they pay you? What was the outcome? Why did they choose you over someone cheaper?" Those three answers give me their ICP, their pricing anchor, and their positioning in one shot.

3. **Current content workflow** — I ask them to walk me through what they did the last time they posted on LinkedIn. How long did it take? Where did they get stuck? What part felt like busywork? This tells me what to automate first.

4. **Business goals for the next 90 days** — I ask them what has to happen in the next 90 days for this engagement to be worth the money. Not vague goals like "build my brand." Specific outcomes like "book five discovery calls" or "close two clients at <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->5K each" or "launch a <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->K offer and sell ten spots."

5. **Access and logins** — I collect LinkedIn login, Claude.ai login, Notion access, Google Drive access, whatever tools we're using. I don't ask for permission. I just say: "I'll need your LinkedIn login and your Claude login to set this up. Can you drop those in the Slack channel after this call?" Ninety percent of clients just say yes.

6. **First deliverable and deadline** — I tell them what I'm shipping and when. "By Friday, you'll have your Voice DNA document, your ICP brief, and your first three Claude Skills built and tested. I'll record a Loom walking you through how to use them. If anything's off, we'll fix it on the next call." Then I ask: "Does Friday work, or do you need it sooner?"

The entire call takes 45 to 60 minutes. I'm not trying to be their therapist. I'm not trying to make them feel seen. I'm collecting structured information so I can start building immediately.

The second pattern is that the best onboarding calls reveal what the client doesn't know yet. Most founders think they have clear positioning. They don't. They think they know their ICP. They don't. They think their voice is "professional but approachable." It's not.

The onboarding call is where you surface those gaps without making the client feel stupid. I do this by asking them to be specific. "What does 'build my brand' actually mean in numbers?" They pause. They say something like "I don't know, maybe more visibility?" Then I reframe: "Let's say in 90 days, what's the one outcome that would make you feel like this was worth the money?" They name a real thing. "Five inbound calls a month." Now we have a target.

[[Onboarding_Bastian_&_Kasia_2025_08_18]] with Bastian and Kasia demonstrates this. The onboarding call started with broad goals and ended with a specific 12-week content plan. The clarity came from forcing specificity early.

The third pattern is that onboarding is where you set the pace of the engagement. If you treat the onboarding call like a relationship call, the client will treat every call like a relationship call. If you treat it like a working call, they show up ready to make decisions.

I set the pace by moving fast and making small decisions on the call. "Should your hook style be contrarian or question-based?" I don't leave that for the next call. I ask them to pick one right now. "Should your LinkedIn banner focus on your offer or your outcome?" Pick one. "Do you want your Voice DNA to sound like this, or like this?" Pick one.

Most clients have never been asked to make that many small decisions in 60 minutes. It's uncomfortable at first. But by the end of the call, they realize that forward momentum feels better than endless planning.

## Common mistakes

**Mistake 1: Treating onboarding like discovery.** The contract is signed. You already know why they hired you. The onboarding call is not the place to re-ask "What are your goals?" You're collecting structured information to start building. If you treat it like discovery, the client will think you don't know what you're doing.

**Mistake 2: Not collecting voice on the call.** Most people say "send me some examples of your writing and I'll review them later." That's a mistake. The client forgets. You chase them for a week. The build stalls. Collect voice on the call by having them pull up examples live and reading them out loud together.

**Mistake 3: Leaving decisions for later.** "Let's think about your hook style and we'll decide next week." No. Decide on the call. Small decisions made fast create momentum. Small decisions deferred create drag.

**Mistake 4: Not setting a deadline for the first deliverable.** If you don't commit to a specific deadline on the onboarding call, the client assumes you're still planning. Commit to a date. "By Friday, you'll have X, Y, and Z." Then ship on time.

**Mistake 5: Asking for permission to collect logins.** "Would it be okay if I got access to your LinkedIn?" That's weak. Just say: "I'll need your LinkedIn login to set this up. Can you drop it in Slack after this call?" Ninety percent of clients just say yes. The ones who say no will tell you why, and you can handle it then.

**Mistake 6: Recording the call but not using it.** Most people record the onboarding call and never listen to it again. The recording is the raw material for the Voice DNA document. Listen to the recording. Pull out the exact phrases the client uses when they're fired up about something. That's their voice.

## The contrarian read

Most coaches and agencies think onboarding is about making the client feel comfortable. It's not. It's about making the client feel like progress is happening.

Comfort is what the client had before they hired you. They were comfortable posting once a week and getting no results. They were comfortable spending 12 hours a month on content and booking zero calls. Comfort is the problem, not the solution.

The onboarding call should feel slightly uncomfortable. The pace should be faster than the client expects. The number of decisions they have to make should feel like a lot. That discomfort is a signal that something is changing.

The second contrarian take is that you don't need the client's permission to do the work. Most people treat the engagement like a collaboration where the client has veto power over every decision. That's a mistake. The client hired you because they don't know how to do this. If you ask them to approve every small decision, you're asking them to do the job they hired you to do.

The onboarding call is where you set the expectation that you will make most of the decisions, and the client's job is to review the output and say yes, no, or "change this one thing." That's the engagement. You build. They approve. You don't build together.

## What it looks like when it's working

You walk out of the onboarding call with everything you need to build the first deliverable. You don't need to chase the client for voice examples. You don't need to send follow-up emails asking for their ICP. You don't need to schedule another call to "clarify the goals." You have the material. You start building immediately.

The client receives their first deliverable within seven days. It could be their Voice DNA document, their ICP brief, their first three Claude Skills, or their first content calendar. The deliverable is complete. It's usable. It's exactly what they need to start posting or prospecting or booking calls.

The client's first reaction is: "This is exactly what I needed." Not "this is interesting" or "let me think about it." They use it immediately. They post the first piece of content. They send the first DM. They see a result within two weeks.

The engagement stays on schedule. You don't push deadlines because you're waiting on the client. You don't add extra calls because something wasn't clear. The timeline you committed to on the sales call is the timeline you deliver on.

The client refers someone within 60 days. Not because you asked for a referral. Because the engagement worked. They saw progress. They got results. They told a founder friend. The referral shows up in your inbox without you doing anything.
