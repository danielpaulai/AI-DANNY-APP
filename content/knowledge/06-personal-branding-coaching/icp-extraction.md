## The core principle

The ICP brief is not a marketing exercise. It's the operating system that every other decision loads from.

If the ICP is vague, every piece of content will be vague. Every sales call will drift. Every offer will hedge. The founder will spend six months posting to nobody and wondering why LinkedIn isn't working.

The ICP extraction process is how you force clarity before a single word gets written.

## Why this matters

Most founders think they know their ICP. They'll say "I work with founders" or "I help consultants grow" or "anyone who needs marketing."

That's not an ICP. That's a guess with no edges.

Without a real ICP, the founder writes content that could apply to anyone, which means it applies to no one. They price based on what feels safe instead of what the market will pay. They talk to every lead that shows up instead of the three that actually convert. They burn months chasing logos that were never going to close.

The cost of skipping this step is invisible until you look back at a year of posting and realise you booked two calls, both from people you already knew.

The ICP extraction process fixes this by making the founder face three questions they've been avoiding: who actually pays you, what do they fear more than your price, and what language do they use when they're in pain.

When those three questions have real answers with names and numbers, the rest of the work gets 10x easier.

## How it shows up in client work

I run this process in the first 90 minutes of every personal brand build. No exceptions.

The pattern is always the same: the founder shows up thinking we're going to write LinkedIn posts. I tell them we're not writing anything until we know who we're writing for. They push back. "I already know my ICP." I ask them to name the last three people who paid them, what those people were afraid of, and what exact phrase each one used when they decided to buy.

Silence.

That's the gap. Knowing your ICP in theory versus knowing them in practice.

The extraction happens in three passes. First pass: who actually paid you in the last 12 months. Not who you want to work with. Not who you think you should target. Who actually handed over money. We list them by name. If the list is short, we go back 24 months. If it's still short, we list every inbound call they took. We're looking for the pattern in the people who already said yes.

Second pass: what were they afraid of. Not the generic fear ("I need more clients"). The specific fear that made them act now instead of six months from now. I cite [[Purely_Personal_Pre_work_2024_11_20]] where Nathalya said "I'm a mom of four kids, three of them are out of the home" in the intro but the real fear surfaced 20 minutes later: she's scaling a biotech without a marketing team and losing visibility in a space where credibility is survival. That's the ICP insight. The stated problem was "I need LinkedIn help." The real problem was "I'm the founder and face of the company, and if I go quiet, we lose deals."

Third pass: what exact language did they use. Not the words the founder wishes they'd use. The actual phrases. When a client books a call and says "my content gets likes but nobody books a call," that's a verbatim ICP line. When they say "I know I need to post consistently but I don't have the hours," that's another one. These lines go straight into the ICP brief under "Their pains (in their words)." This is the language every post and every hook will echo back.

The breakthrough happens when the founder sees the pattern and stops trying to be for everyone.

I worked with a founder in [[Adisseo_cohort_2026_01_07]] where Ricardo was building country-level ICPs for an APAC rollout. The mistake he was about to make: treating APAC as one audience. The fix: build separate ICP snapshots by country, species, operation size, and regional pain point. One grid for Thailand broiler farms, one grid for Vietnam shrimp operations, one grid for Indonesia layer farms. Same product, different fears, different CTAs, different proof points. The work tripled, but the conversion rate went from 2% to 11% because the messaging stopped hedging.

The ICP brief I build has 10 sections, and every section answers one question:

1. **Who you serve** — Title, company size, revenue range, industry. One sentence per bullet. No hedging. If the ICP is "<!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->00K-$5M ARR founders," it's not $50K and it's not <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->0M. Draw the line.

2. **Where they are today** — What their week looks like right now. How many hours they're spending on the thing you solve. What's breaking. Cite real clients. "Closing deals, building the product, and managing the team at the same time" came from a verbatim client transcript.

3. **Their pains (in their words)** — Six to eight direct quotes. No paraphrasing. When a founder says "I'm paying for AI tools that aren't making me any money," that's the line. Put it in quotes. Use it in a hook next week.

4. **What they want** — The outcome they'd pay for today. Not the outcome you think they need. The outcome they already tried to buy and failed to get. "A system that runs content marketing for them" is a want. "To sound like themselves at scale without writing every post from scratch" is a want. "Proof that AI can do more than save them 10 minutes on an email" is a want.

5. **What they pay for** — The offer they actually close on. Not the offer you wish they'd buy. If they keep asking for done-with-you instead of done-for-you, that's the signal. Build the offer they're already trying to buy.

6. **Disqualifiers** — Who doesn't fit. This is as important as who does. "Enterprise buyers with procurement cycles" is a disqualifier. "Non-founder employees" is a disqualifier. "Anyone looking for the cheapest option" is a disqualifier. This section saves you 20 hours a month on calls that were never going to close.

7. **Top 3 KPIs** — What they measure success by. For a founder, it's usually: inbound leads per month, close rate, revenue per client. For a CISO, it's compliance audit pass rate, incident response time, board confidence in security posture. Know what they're measured on and you know what messaging lands.

8. **Top 3 problems hitting those KPIs** — The gap between where they are and where they need to be. If the KPI is 10 inbound calls a month and they're getting 2, the problem is "LinkedIn presence isn't pulling its weight." If the KPI is 50% close rate and they're at 30%, the problem is "sales process has no follow-through." Name the gap.

9. **Top 3 fears** — What they're afraid will happen if they don't fix this. "Pipeline goes quiet every time they go quiet" is a fear. "Looking inconsistent or amateur on LinkedIn" is a fear. "Falling behind other founders who are posting daily" is a fear. Fears drive action. Features don't.

10. **Buying signals and interest signals** — When do they raise their hand. If they comment on a post about AI workflows, that's an interest signal. If they DM you asking "how does this work for service businesses," that's a buying signal. Track both. The interest signal tells you what content to make more of. The buying signal tells you when to move to DMs.

The whole brief is two pages. No more. If it's longer, you're hedging.

## Specific examples from the vault

I ran this process with a founder building an LPG distribution system in [[LPG distribution inventory and billing management system - 3a9ec055]]. The stated problem was "we need better inventory tracking." The real problem surfaced when they said "many of our customers are still very paper-based and prefer physical invoices delivered to their office." The ICP insight: this isn't a tech problem, it's a trust and process problem. The system we built had to match how Indonesian distributors actually operate, not how a SaaS company in San Francisco thinks they should operate. The ICP brief included "customers who require exchange TT process" as a segment, and "debt collection runs manually with physical slips" as a pain point. That one insight changed the entire build.

I ran it with a NAS.IO ops lead in [[Building nasops transcript-based documentation and email generation app - 78e52862]]. The stated need was "we want to send session summaries faster." The real need came out in the transcript: "any time they teach a session, they need to send a summary of what happened, what the main points were, and how to step-by-step implement this." The ICP wasn't "operations teams." It was "school companies teaching live sessions who need to convert transcripts into worksheets and follow-up emails within 24 hours." That specificity built the entire feature set.

I ran it with an Adisseo marketing lead in [[Adisseo_cohort_2026_01_07]] where Ricardo said "we need better LinkedIn content." The real need was "we have 11 APAC marketers who don't know what messaging is safe to localise and what has to stay consistent." The ICP brief split into two layers: the end customer (farm operators by country and species) and the internal user (regional marketers who need guardrails, not just templates). That layered ICP led to a cultural intelligence framework and a messaging hierarchy system, not a content calendar.

In [[Purely_Personal_Pre_work_2024_11_20]], I had four founders introduce themselves in a pre-work call. Nathalya: biotech founder, four kids, building home diagnostics, needs to establish credibility without a marketing team. Francesca: tour guide pivoting to remote work, loves Ireland and Italy, wants to scale beyond physical tours. Karen: real estate investor in D.C., wants to train and share what she knows. Each one opened with a title. The ICP insights came in the gaps. Nathalya's real problem: "I'm the face of the company and if I go quiet, we lose deals." Francesca's real problem: "I love what I do but I'm geographically trapped." Karen's real problem: "I know real estate but I don't know how to package what I know." Those are the ICP briefs. Not "biotech founders" or "tour guides" or "real estate investors." The brief is the gap between the stated work and the real fear.

## Scripts and exact phrases

"Who paid you in the last 12 months? Name them."

"What were they afraid of the week before they hired you?"

"What exact words did they use when they said yes?"

"If I interview that client today and ask why they bought, what would they say? Not what you hope they'd say. What they'd actually say."

"Who do you never want to work with again? Why?"

"When you get on a sales call and you know in the first five minutes it's not a fit, what did they say that told you?"

"If you could only work with 10 more clients in your life, who would they look like?"

"What problem do they think they have versus what problem they actually have?"

"What language do they use when they're in pain? Not marketing language. Real language."

"What's the thing they're afraid to say out loud on a call?"

"When was the last time someone told you your price was too high? What were they actually saying?"

## Common mistakes

**Mistake one: defining ICP by demographics instead of psychographics.**

"I work with 30-50 year old founders" tells me nothing. "I work with founders who are closing deals manually and losing pipeline every time they take a week off" tells me everything. The ICP is not age and location. It's fear and context.

**Mistake two: writing the ICP you wish you had instead of the ICP you have.**

A founder tells me "I want to work with 7-figure CEOs." I ask how many 7-figure CEOs paid them last year. The answer is zero. The paying clients were all 6-figure founders. That's the ICP. Build for the people who already buy, then move upmarket later. Don't start by writing content for an audience that doesn't know you exist.

**Mistake three: listing too many personas.**

"I work with founders, consultants, coaches, and agency owners" is four ICPs, which means zero ICP. Pick one. The tightest ICP I ever wrote was 11 words: "Solo founders running $500K-<!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->M service businesses who are the brand." That's it. Every post, every hook, every offer ran through that filter. If it didn't land for that person, it didn't ship.

**Mistake four: skipping the "in their words" section.**

The founder writes "My ICP struggles with time management and wants to scale efficiently." That's not how a human talks. A human says "I'm doing five jobs and I don't have the hours." Use their language. If you don't have their language, you don't have the ICP yet.

**Mistake five: building the ICP alone.**

The founder sits in a room and guesses what the ICP wants. That's not extraction. That's projection. The ICP comes from transcripts, sales calls, client interviews, and post-mortems on deals that closed. If you're guessing, stop. Go interview three clients and record it.

**Mistake six: treating ICP as a one-time exercise.**

The ICP shifts as the business shifts. The ICP I had in 2024 is not the ICP I have in 2026. Every six months, re-run the extraction. List the last 10 clients who paid. What changed. What stayed the same. Update the brief. The ICP is a living document, not a PDF you write once and forget.

## The contrarian read

Most people say "broaden your ICP to reach more people."

I say the opposite. Narrow it until it makes you uncomfortable.

The tighter the ICP, the easier everything gets. The looser the ICP, the more you sound like everyone else.

I worked with a founder who said "I can help any business with marketing." I told them to pick one type of business, one revenue range, one specific problem. They pushed back. "But I don't want to exclude people." I said "You're not excluding people. You're making a choice about who you're trying to reach first. You can expand later. Right now, you need one person to say 'this is exactly for me.'"

They picked: "I help service-based businesses doing <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->00K-$500K who are spending 12+ hours a week on content and getting no leads from it."

That ICP took their close rate from 15% to 40% in 90 days. Not because the offer changed. Because the messaging stopped hedging.

The other contrarian take: you don't need a huge TAM to build a 7-figure business.

If your ICP is 5,000 people and your close rate is 2%, you need 100 clients to close. If your average deal is <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->0K, that's <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->M. You don't need a TAM of 100,000. You need a TAM of 5,000 where you can name 500 of them by company.

Niche is not a limitation. Niche is leverage.

## What it looks like when it's working

You know the ICP is right when three things happen.

One: a prospect reads your content and says "how did you know that about me?"

Two: you can write five hooks in 10 minutes because you know exactly what language lands.

Three: you can disqualify a lead in the first two minutes of a call because you know what fit looks like.

When the ICP is right, sales calls stop feeling like pitches and start feeling like triage. You're not convincing. You're diagnosing. And if the diagnosis matches the ICP, the close is automatic.

The observable signal: your close rate goes up and your sales cycle goes down. If you're closing 40%+ of qualified leads in under two weeks, the ICP is tight. If you're closing 10% after six follow-ups, the ICP is still vague.

The second signal: people start referring others who look exactly like them. When a client sends you someone and says "they have the same problem I had," that's proof the ICP is real. When they send you someone random, the ICP is still blurry.

The third signal: you stop second-guessing your content. You write a post, you know exactly who it's for, you ship it, and it books a call within 48 hours. That's a working ICP. When you're posting and hoping, the ICP isn't done yet.
