## The core principle

Positioning isn't what you say about yourself. It's the one line your client would use to introduce you to a friend who needs what you do.

Most founders think positioning is the tagline on their website or their LinkedIn headline. It's not. Positioning is the specific problem you solve, for a specific person, in a way no one else does. If you can't fit it in one sentence that a 14-year-old would understand, you don't have positioning yet. You have word soup.

## Why this matters

Without clear positioning, every marketing decision becomes a guess.

You don't know which content to post. You don't know which prospects to message. You don't know what to charge. You spend 12 hours a week creating content that gets likes but books zero calls.

The founder who can't position themselves ends up everywhere and nowhere. They post on every platform. They talk to every audience. They offer three different services. And at the end of 90 days, the pipeline is still empty because nobody knows what they actually do.

The cost isn't just lost time. It's lost confidence. Every week that goes by without clarity, the founder starts questioning if the business is even viable. They watch competitors with worse offers win clients. They wonder if they should pivot. They burn through savings while waiting for the "right positioning" to magically appear.

The founder with clear positioning makes one-tenth the content and books five times the calls. They know exactly who they're talking to. They know exactly what promise to make. They know exactly what to charge. Positioning is the difference between posting for therapy and posting for pipeline.

## How it shows up in client work

The positioning extraction process starts the same way every time: I ask the founder to explain what they do.

What comes back is almost always one of three patterns. Pattern one: the service description. "I help businesses with their marketing strategy." Pattern two: the credential dump. "I'm a certified consultant with 15 years of experience in B2B SaaS go-to-market." Pattern three: the vision statement. "I empower leaders to transform their organizations through purpose-driven change."

None of these are positioning. They're job descriptions.

The first move is always the same. I ask: "If you were at a wedding and someone asked what you do, what would you say in ten seconds before they walk away?"

That question forces them out of corporate mode and into human mode. The answer that comes back is usually closer to real positioning than anything on their website. [[Mohab_Shalash_One_on_One_Personal_Branding_2024_11_08]] shows this pattern clearly. Mo opened the call confused about whether to talk about supply chain solutions or his resignation story. The positioning question revealed the real tension: his audience cared about his story (quitting, starting fresh), not his offer (supply chain management). The breakthrough happened when we named what was actually landing: "I help supply chain founders quit corporate and build their own thing." One line. Specific audience. Specific outcome. That's positioning.

The second pattern shows up with founders who have done the work but still can't land on one line. They know their ICP. They know their offer. But they keep hedging. "I help founders with content and sales and operations." "I work with B2B and B2C." "I serve early-stage and scaling companies."

The problem isn't knowledge. It's fear. They're afraid that picking one thing means saying no to money. So they stay broad. And staying broad makes them invisible.

[[Building a market positioning app - dcce301a]] documents this exact problem. The positioning app concept emerged from watching founders struggle with the same stuck point: they've done the ICP work, they've niched down, but they still can't name what makes them different. The app's core job was to force one specific claim: "You are the only ___ who helps ___ do ___ in ___ way." Filling in those blanks revealed whether the positioning was real or still generic.

The third pattern is the founder who keeps repositioning every 90 days. They launch with one message, get no traction, panic, and pivot to something new. Three months later, same cycle. The problem isn't the positioning. It's the commitment. They never stay in one lane long enough to test if it works.

The extraction process for these founders isn't about finding new positioning. It's about locking them into one position and refusing to let them change it for six months. I make them write the one-line positioning on a Post-it note and stick it on their monitor. Every piece of content gets checked against that line. If it doesn't support the positioning, it doesn't ship.

The most common breakthrough happens when I force the founder to pick one specific client they've already helped and write positioning for that person only. Not "businesses." Not "founders." One human. [[Building a strong personal brand to scale your business - 24f0d737]] shows this pattern. The ICP work started with generic answers ("small business owners struggling to scale"). The positioning didn't land until we named a real person: Marcus, 38, boutique consultant, three years in, referrals dried up, pipeline unpredictable, freezes when showing up online. Positioning for Marcus: "I help solo consultants turn their expertise into consistent inbound leads without hiring a marketing team." That's specific. That's one line. That converts.

The positioning extraction process also surfaces where the founder is stuck on identity. They don't know who they are as a brand yet. They're still trying to position before they've done the work. [[Ernest Call Transcript Search - 9f74bec6]] shows this clearly. Ernest's positioning kept shifting because the business was in crisis mode. The real positioning wasn't "orthodontist who does great work." It was "orthodontist who's rebuilding after his partner's suicide and needs referrals now." The positioning had to match the reality, not the aspiration. We landed on: "I help referring dentists send their complex cases to someone they trust who'll take care of their patients." That's positioning that serves the actual need, not the brand fantasy.

Another pattern: founders who confuse positioning with differentiation. They think positioning is about listing what makes them unique. "I use AI." "I have 15 years of experience." "I'm the only one who does X and Y together." None of that is positioning. Positioning is the outcome the client cares about, delivered in a way they can't get anywhere else.

The extraction question that reveals this: "If your client had to explain to their boss why they hired you instead of someone cheaper, what would they say?" The answer to that question is the positioning. It's never about your process. It's always about their result.

## Specific examples from the vault

The Mo Shalash call [[Mohab_Shalash_One_on_One_Personal_Branding_2024_11_08]] is the clearest example of positioning extraction in real time. Mo had quit his corporate job, started his own supply chain consulting practice, and was confused about what to post. His impressions dropped. He thought it was because he removed the company name from his profile. It wasn't.

The positioning question I asked: "Should you talk about supply chain management solutions or your journey of quitting?"

The answer was in the data. His resignation post got massive engagement. His supply chain thought leadership posts got "so what" reactions. His audience wanted the story, not the service. The positioning that landed: "I help supply chain founders leave corporate and build their own consulting practice." That's the line. That's what people care about. That's what books calls.

The Ernest example [[Ernest Call Transcript Search - 9f74bec6]] shows positioning extraction under crisis. Ernest's business was in critical state. He was working Saturdays at other offices to pay bills. His positioning kept shifting because the business situation kept shifting. The breakthrough came when I asked: "Who actually sends you clients right now?" The answer: referring dentists. The positioning locked: "I help referring dentists trust me with their complex cases." That's not brand positioning. That's business positioning. It matched the reality.

The Noman project [[Noman]] shows positioning extraction for someone transitioning from corporate executive to consultant. Noman had extensive cybersecurity experience at Imperva, Emerson, SDG. Worked with GE, Target, T-Mobile. Private equity experience through Thoma Bravo. But positioning as "cybersecurity executive" wouldn't convert consulting clients. The extraction process forced the question: "What outcome do your clients actually pay for?" The answer wasn't cybersecurity. It was revenue growth. The positioning that emerged: "I turn security organizations into revenue drivers." That's the line. That's what differentiates him from every other cybersecurity consultant.

The positioning app conversation [[Building a market positioning app - dcce301a]] documents the frameworks I pulled from multiple books to build a systematic positioning extraction tool. The core frameworks: Al Ries and Jack Trout's "own a word in the prospect's mind" concept. April Dunford's "Obviously Awesome" framework on context setting and differentiation. Blue Ocean Strategy's "create new category" approach. The app's job was to force founders through a structured process: define your ICP, identify your unique capability, name the outcome, claim the category. The app never shipped, but the process became the manual extraction method I use in every positioning call.

The March Madness Marathon debrief [[March Madness Marathon problems and solutions - 508640a8]] shows what happens when positioning is unclear at the event level. The event was called "March Madness Marathon" but nobody could explain in one line what attendees would walk away with. Was it training? Was it implementation? Was it networking? The positioning confusion killed attendance. Alex's question during the debrief: "What were the learnings and why weren't we able to achieve the goal?" The answer: no clear positioning. The fix: reposition future events with one specific outcome and one specific deliverable. "Walk out with your first AI employee in 90 minutes." That's positioning. That converts.

## Common mistakes

The biggest mistake is thinking positioning is the tagline on your website. It's not. Positioning lives in how clients describe you when you're not in the room. If you've never asked a client "How would you introduce me to a friend who needs what I do?" you don't know your positioning yet. You have a guess.

Second mistake: staying broad to avoid saying no to money. "I help founders with content, sales, and operations." That's not positioning. That's three different businesses. Broad positioning attracts nobody because it's too vague to be valuable. The founder who positions as "I help B2B SaaS founders book 10+ sales calls a month from LinkedIn content" is 10x more likely to book a call than the founder who says "I help businesses with marketing."

Third mistake: confusing positioning with differentiation. Listing your unique features is not positioning. "I use AI" is not positioning. "I have 15 years of experience" is not positioning. "I'm the only one who does X and Y together" is not positioning. Positioning is the outcome the client cares about, delivered in a way they can't get anywhere else. The features only matter if they serve the outcome.

Fourth mistake: repositioning every 90 days. The founder who keeps changing positioning is the founder who never commits long enough to test if it works. Positioning takes six months to know if it's working. If you're repositioning every quarter, you're running from the work, not toward clarity.

Fifth mistake: positioning for who you want to serve instead of who you've already served. The founder who's helped five clients all in the same industry but positions as "I work with anyone who needs marketing" is lying to themselves. The positioning should match the actual work, not the aspirational client list.

Sixth mistake: writing positioning in corporate language. "I empower leaders to transform their organizations through purpose-driven change" is not positioning. It's LinkedIn mad libs. If you wouldn't say it at a wedding, don't say it in your positioning. Use plain English. One sentence. A 14-year-old should understand it.

## The contrarian read

Most positioning advice says "find your niche first, then position within it." I do it backward.

I extract positioning from the work the founder has already done. The clients they've already helped. The problems they've already solved. The positioning reveals the niche. Not the other way around.

Why? Because most founders lie to themselves about who they want to serve. They say "I want to work with enterprise clients" but every paying client they've closed is a solo founder. They say "I serve B2B SaaS" but their case studies are all e-commerce. The stated ICP and the actual client base don't match.

Positioning from real work forces honesty. If five of your last six clients were solo consultants who needed LinkedIn content systems, that's your positioning. "I help solo consultants turn their expertise into consistent inbound leads without hiring a marketing team." You don't get to reposition as "I help SaaS founders scale to Series A" just because that sounds better.

The other contrarian take: positioning isn't permanent. It evolves as your client base evolves. But evolution is different from panic repositioning. Evolution happens after six months of consistent work in one lane. Panic repositioning happens after two weeks of no traction.

The test: if you've worked with fewer than five clients in your current positioning, you haven't earned the right to reposition yet. Stay in the lane. Do the work. The positioning either proves itself or it doesn't. But you have to commit first.

## What it looks like when it's working

Clear positioning shows up in three observable ways.

First: strangers can repeat your positioning back to you after one conversation. If someone meets you at an event, talks to you for five minutes, and can explain to their colleague the next day what you do, your positioning works. If they say "He does something with marketing, I think?" your positioning is broken.

Second: your content attracts the right people and repels the wrong people. You start getting DMs from exactly the ICP you named in your positioning. You also start getting "this isn't for me" responses from people outside that ICP. Both are good signals. Repulsion is proof of specificity.

Third: sales conversations get shorter. When positioning is clear, the prospect already understands what you do before the call. They're not asking "What do you do?" They're asking "Can you do this for me?" The call moves from explanation to qualification. You close faster because positioning did half the work before you opened your mouth.

The before/after on positioning shows up in time-to-close. Founders with unclear positioning spend 3-5 calls explaining what they do before the prospect decides. Founders with clear positioning close in 1-2 calls. The positioning is doing the heavy lifting. The call is just confirming fit.
