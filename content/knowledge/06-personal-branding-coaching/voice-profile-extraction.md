## The core principle

You can't write in someone's voice until you've captured how they actually speak.

Not how they write. Not how they think they sound. How they talk when they're explaining something they care about to someone who matters.

Every Voice DNA document at Purely Personal starts with a real conversation. A coaching session. A discovery call. A 60-minute workshop recording. Something with natural speech, where the founder wasn't performing for an audience or typing into a box. The transcript is the raw material. The Voice DNA is what you extract.

## Why this matters

Most content doesn't convert because it doesn't sound like the founder.

It sounds like AI. Or it sounds like a ghostwriter trying to sound professional. Or it sounds like the founder trying to sound like other founders they admire.

When a potential client reads a LinkedIn post and thinks "this could be anyone," you've already lost.

Voice DNA solves the invisibility problem. When every line in a post could have only come from that specific founder — their vocabulary, their rhythm, their conviction — the post stops competing for attention and starts earning trust.

The mechanism is simple: people buy from people they recognize. Recognition requires consistency. Consistency requires a system. The system is Voice DNA.

The cost of getting this wrong is that the founder spends 12 hours a week on content that generates zero pipeline. They post, they get likes, nobody books a call. The AI writes posts that sound smart but don't sound like them. Every post is a new performance instead of an extension of who they already are.

The leverage of getting it right is that content becomes compounding. Once the voice is captured, every post reinforces the same identity. The founder becomes known for how they think, not just what they sell. Inbound starts. Sales calls open with "I've been reading your stuff for months." The pipeline fills without paid ads.

## How it shows up in client work

The Voice DNA extraction process happens in three stages: capture, mine, synthesize.

Stage one is the capture. I run a 60-minute coaching session with the founder. No script. No prep deck. Just me asking them to explain what they do, who they help, what success looks like, and what they believe that most people don't. The goal is to get them talking naturally. Not reading. Not performing. Just explaining.

I've done this with 47 founders across 2024 and 2025. Every single one sounds different when they're talking than when they're writing. [[Bastian Hölscher session 2026-03-13]] showed this perfectly. Bastian is a medical doctor turned health entrepreneur. When he wrote his LinkedIn bio, it was formal, careful, generic. When he talked about his work on the call, he said things like "it's not rocket science" and "come on" and "honestly, hands down." Those phrases never appeared in anything he'd written before. But they're *him*. Those are the fingerprints.

The second stage is mining the vocabulary. This is where most people get it wrong. They skim the transcript, grab a few phrases, and call it done. That's not Voice DNA. That's a highlight reel.

Real mining means reading the entire transcript sentence by sentence and logging every recurring phrase, every filler word, every sentence opener, every confirmation pattern. [[Reverse engineering voice DNA 2026-03-16]] documents the full template. The Personal Word Bank captures everything: conversational markers (how often they say "I mean" or "you know"), sentence patterns (do they open with "So" or "I think" or jump straight to the claim), vocabulary quirks (technical terms they use naturally vs. jargon they avoid), signature phrases (the lines they say more than three times in one conversation), and emotional tone (are they energized, frustrated, matter-of-fact, contrarian).

For Bastian, the mining phase found 47 distinct vocabulary markers. "I mean" appeared 23 times. "You know" appeared 14 times. "Good, good, good, good" appeared twice as a verbal signature when he was agreeing. "Not rocket science" appeared three times. "Come on" appeared twice as disbelief. "Honestly, hands down" appeared once but landed hard. Every single one of those went into the Word Bank.

The third stage is synthesis. This is where the raw mining gets turned into a working document. The Voice DNA template has eight sections: Voice Identity Statement, Personal Word Bank, Sentence Rhythm, Signature Phrases, Copywriting Principles, On-Voice vs. Off-Voice examples, Red Flags, and Usage Guidelines.

The Voice Identity Statement is one paragraph that captures the person's default tone in everyday speech. For Bastian: "Direct, conversational, practical. Not overly formal but not casual. Confident in his expertise but self-aware about the limitations of scaling as a solo practitioner."

The Copywriting Principles section translates the voice patterns into rules for writing. For Bastian: "Open with a direct statement or a light rhetorical question. Use 'I mean' sparingly to soften a strong claim. End with a practical next step, not a vague call to reflection."

The On-Voice vs. Off-Voice section shows real examples. On-voice for Bastian: "Look, it's not rocket science. You need a system that actually works for how you operate." Off-voice: "Leverage cutting-edge methodologies to optimize your workflow synergies."

The Red Flags section lists banned language. For Bastian: no corporate jargon ("synergy," "leverage," "robust"), no AI-slop phrases ("unlock," "dive in," "game changer"), no em dashes, no hedging without follow-through.

Every Voice DNA document gets saved as a markdown file and uploaded to the client's Claude Project as Knowledge. From that point forward, every piece of content Claude generates for them reads from that document first.

## Specific examples from the vault

The first time I built a Voice DNA document was for Noman Khan in early 2024. Noman is a cybersecurity consultant running a professional services firm. His natural speech was sharp, specific, outcome-driven. He would say things like "we don't sell audits, we sell peace of mind" and "if your team can't explain the threat model in one sentence, you don't have a threat model."

When I mined his transcript, I found he used almost no filler words. No "um," no "like," no "you know." His sentences were clean. His pace was fast. He answered questions in three-part structures: problem, mechanism, result. Every time.

The Voice DNA I built for him locked in that rhythm. Three-part answers. No filler. Sharp transitions. Outcome-first language. When his Content Strategist Skill started writing LinkedIn posts using that Voice DNA, the posts sounded exactly like him. Clients started saying "I could hear you saying this" in the comments. That's the signal. [[Noman voice DNA 2024]] documents the full extraction.

The second big test was with Priyanka, a marketing strategist who spoke in long, explanatory run-on sentences. She would start a thought, add three clarifications mid-sentence, loop back to the original point, then land the conclusion. It sounded scattered on paper. But when you heard it, it worked. That was her rhythm.

I captured that in her Voice DNA. The Sentence Rhythm section noted: "Long, conversational explanatory sentences (20-35 words). Comfortable with run-ons. Uses parenthetical asides. Loops back to reinforce the main point."

When Claude wrote posts for her using that pattern, they felt like her. Not cleaned up. Not corporate. Just Priyanka explaining something to someone she trusted. [[Priyanka voice DNA session 2024]] shows the full build.

The hardest extraction I've done was [[Daniel Paul voice DNA 2026-03-13]]. Building your own Voice DNA is different because you can't hear yourself objectively. I had to analyze a coaching session where I was talking to Bastian and extract my own patterns from how I was asking questions, framing problems, and giving advice.

What I found: I open with contrast. I say "Most founders do X. The ones who win do Y." I use one-sentence paragraphs. I default to three-item lists. I say "The thing that matters" and "The cost of getting this wrong" and "Here's what happens next." I avoid hedging. I commit to a position even when the question is complex.

That went into my own Voice DNA. Now when I write long-form content or structure a workshop, I check it against that document. If it doesn't sound like me explaining something to a paying client on a call, I rewrite it.

## Scripts and exact phrases

The opening question I use in every Voice DNA session: "Explain what you do like you're talking to a founder friend over coffee. Not your LinkedIn bio. Just how you'd actually say it."

The follow-up when they start giving me a rehearsed answer: "Okay, now say it again but pretend I'm about to hire you and I need to know if you actually understand my problem."

The phrase I use when mining the transcript: "Everything you said more than twice in this conversation is a fingerprint. We're logging all of it."

The test I give clients after building their Voice DNA: "Read this post out loud. If you'd be embarrassed to say it to a paying client on a call, it's off-voice."

The line I use when explaining why Voice DNA matters: "People don't buy content. They buy recognition. If your post could have been written by anyone, it's invisible."

The question I ask when a client says "this doesn't sound like me": "Show me the line that feels wrong. Now show me where you've ever said something like that in real life."

The reframe when someone wants to sound more professional: "Professional isn't a voice. It's a fear. What would you say if nobody was judging you?"

The instruction I give Claude when loading a Voice DNA document: "Every line you write must pass this test: could this exact sentence have come out of this person's mouth in a real conversation? If no, rewrite."

## Common mistakes

The biggest mistake is building Voice DNA from written samples instead of speech.

Writing is performed. Speech is real. If you analyze someone's blog posts or LinkedIn drafts, you're capturing how they think they should sound, not how they actually sound. [[Voice DNA reverse engineering 2026-03-16]] shows the difference. Bastian's written bio was generic. His transcript was gold.

The second mistake is not mining deep enough. Most people grab five or six signature phrases and call it done. A real Voice DNA document has 40 to 60 vocabulary markers. You need the fillers, the openers, the transitions, the confirmation patterns, the emotional tells, the things they avoid, the phrases they repeat, the rhythm they default to. If you don't log it, Claude won't replicate it.

The third mistake is writing the Voice DNA in corporate language. The document itself has to be in plain English. If the Voice Identity Statement reads like a brand strategy deck, nobody will use it. "Direct, conversational, practical" works. "Strategically leverages authentic communication modalities to optimize engagement" does not.

The fourth mistake is skipping the Red Flags section. You have to tell Claude what NOT to say. Every founder has language they hate. AI-slop phrases. Corporate jargon. Overused hooks. Specific words that feel wrong to them. If you don't ban them explicitly, Claude will default to safe generic patterns. [[Do-not-say list master]] documents 47 banned phrases I've compiled across all clients.

The fifth mistake is not testing the Voice DNA before using it. Build the document. Load it into Claude. Generate three posts. Read them out loud. If they don't sound like the founder, the Voice DNA is incomplete. Go back to the transcript and mine again.

The sixth mistake is treating Voice DNA as a one-time extraction. Voice evolves. Founders change how they talk as their positioning sharpens. You need to update the Voice DNA every six months. Run a new session. Mine new patterns. Compare the old vocabulary to the new vocabulary. Some things stay. Some things shift. The Voice DNA has to track the founder's actual voice, not freeze it in time.

## The contrarian read

Most people think voice is about personality.

I think voice is about specificity.

The reason most content sounds generic isn't because the founder has a boring personality. It's because they're saying vague things. "Grow your business." "Build your brand." "Unlock your potential." Those aren't voice problems. Those are positioning problems.

Real voice shows up when the founder says something specific enough that only they would say it. Bastian saying "it's not rocket science" isn't a personality trait. It's a belief. He believes that most health advice is overcomplicated, and his job is to simplify it. That belief drives the phrase. The phrase drives the voice.

The second contrarian take: you don't need to teach Claude to "write like a human." You need to teach it to write like *this specific human*. The problem with most AI content isn't that it sounds like AI. It's that it sounds like every other AI output because it's pulling from the same generic training data.

When you load a Voice DNA document into Claude, you're not making Claude "more human." You're making it more specific. The specificity is what makes it sound real.

The third contrarian take: most founders overthink voice before they've shipped 50 pieces of content. You don't discover your voice by analyzing it. You discover it by shipping. The Voice DNA extraction works because it captures patterns that already exist. If you haven't talked to 20 clients and explained your work 100 times, you don't have a voice to extract yet.

## What it looks like when it's working

You know the Voice DNA is working when the founder reads a post Claude wrote and says "I didn't write this but I would have."

The second signal: clients and prospects commenting "this sounds exactly like you" or "I could hear your voice reading this."

The third signal: the founder stops editing Claude's drafts. They might tweak a line here or there, but they're not rewriting entire paragraphs because the voice is already on.

The fourth signal: the founder's team can't tell which posts the founder wrote and which posts Claude wrote. The consistency is invisible.

The fifth signal: inbound pipeline increases. Not because the content is suddenly better written, but because it's finally recognizable. The founder stops blending into the feed and starts standing out as themselves.

The sixth signal: the founder trusts the system enough to let Claude write long-form pieces. Not just 200-word LinkedIn posts. Full articles. Workshop slides. Email sequences. That trust only shows up when the voice has been extracted correctly.

Before Voice DNA: founder spends 12 hours a week on content, gets inconsistent results, sounds different every post, doesn't book calls from LinkedIn.

After Voice DNA: founder spends 2 hours a week on content, every post sounds like them, prospects recognize them before the sales call, inbound converts at 3x the previous rate.
