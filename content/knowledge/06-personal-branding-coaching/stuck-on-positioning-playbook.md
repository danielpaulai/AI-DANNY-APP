## The core principle

When a founder can't articulate what they do in one line, the problem is never the words. It's too many offers trying to fit through one sentence at the same time.

Cut two of the three things they're trying to say, and the third one writes itself.

## Why this matters

Positioning isn't a copywriting problem. It's a decision problem disguised as a writing problem.

A founder who can't position themselves clearly is running three businesses at once inside their head. They serve solopreneurs and enterprise clients. They do strategy and execution. They're a consultant and a coach and a course creator.

Every conversation becomes a negotiation with themselves about which version of the business to lead with. Every LinkedIn post hedges. Every sales call drifts into explaining three different things instead of landing one.

The market doesn't reward nuance. It rewards clarity. The founder who can't say what they do in one line doesn't get remembered, doesn't get referred, and doesn't get the inbound calls they need to stay above water.

The cost shows up in three places. First, content goes invisible. The founder posts thought leadership that could apply to anyone, so it resonates with no one. Second, sales calls take twice as long because the first 20 minutes are spent explaining what the engagement actually is. Third, referrals don't happen. A past client wants to send someone, but they can't explain what the founder does without a paragraph of caveats.

The leverage of getting positioning right is immediate. One clear line means every piece of content now has a filter. Every sales conversation starts at "yes or no" instead of "let me explain the context." Every referral becomes a sentence the client can say to a friend without thinking.

Positioning clarity is the difference between a founder chasing every opportunity and a founder who gets to choose which three calls to take this week.

## How it shows up in client work

The pattern repeats across every 1:1 engagement. A founder books a call saying they need help with content or LinkedIn strategy. Twenty minutes in, the real problem surfaces: they can't say what they sell in one sentence without adding three qualifiers.

I ask: "What do you do?"

They answer: "I help founders with go-to-market strategy, but I also do fractional CMO work, and I'm building a course on positioning, and I consult on AI implementation for sales teams."

That's four businesses. The market hears noise.

The move is always the same. I write down all four things. Then I ask: "If you could only sell one of those for the next 90 days, which one actually books calls today?"

They pick one. Then I ask: "Who paid you the most money in the last six months for that exact thing?"

They name a client. Then I ask: "What did that client call the problem before they met you?"

That question breaks the loop. Because the founder has been positioning from their solution outward instead of from the client's language inward.

Example from [[Sales_call_2025_03_14]]: A founder running a LinkedIn ghostwriting agency couldn't explain what made them different from the other 900 LinkedIn ghostwriters. I asked what the last three clients said when they first booked a call. All three said the same thing: "I need someone who understands my industry."

The positioning wrote itself: "LinkedIn ghostwriting for founders in [specific vertical] who need someone who already speaks the language."

One constraint. Three words changed. Inbound calls doubled in 30 days.

Another pattern from [[PB_coaching_session_2025_02_18]]: A consultant sold strategy, execution, and training. Every sales call turned into a negotiation about scope. I had them look at the last ten deals. Eight of them were execution-only. Strategy was sold once, training never.

The move: kill strategy and training as standalone offers. Rebrand as "execution-led consulting" where strategy happens inside the delivery, not as a separate engagement. Sales calls shortened by half because there was only one thing to say yes or no to.

From [[Positioning_breakthrough_2025_01_22]]: A founder who called themselves a "business consultant" wondered why nobody understood what they did. I had them pull their last five testimonials. Every single one used the word "systems." Not strategy. Not consulting. Systems.

The positioning became: "I build the operating systems founders wish they'd started with." One line. Every subsequent post now had a filter. Content that didn't connect to systems got cut. Engagement tripled because the message finally had an edge.

The common denominator across every stuck-on-positioning case: the founder is trying to be too many things to too many people because they're afraid of losing opportunity. The opposite happens. Trying to serve everyone makes them invisible to the people who would actually pay.

From [[Workshop_debrief_2024_11_30]]: A founder running a personal branding workshop kept changing the pitch depending on who they were talking to. To one person it was about LinkedIn. To another it was about building a speaking career. To another it was about getting media attention.

I asked: "What's the one outcome you can deliver in 90 days that you'd bet your own money on?"

They said: "LinkedIn presence that books sales calls."

That became the only promise. The workshop was rebuilt around that single outcome. Signups went from 12 to 47 in the next cohort because the pitch finally landed on one specific pain instead of three abstract benefits.

The move is always: three constraints, one outcome, one ICP, one offer. Everything else parks or gets killed.

## Common mistakes

**Mistake one: Positioning from solution outward instead of pain inward.** The founder says "I do X and Y and Z" instead of starting with "Clients hire me when [specific pain] is costing them money."

The fix: Pull the last three client calls. What did they say in the first five minutes before you started explaining your process? That's the positioning.

**Mistake two: Using industry jargon that means nothing to the buyer.** "I help companies optimise their go-to-market motion" sounds smart but lands flat. The buyer doesn't wake up thinking "I need to optimise my go-to-market motion." They wake up thinking "We spent $40K on ads last quarter and got three leads."

The fix: Use the exact language the client used when they described the problem on the first call.

**Mistake three: Hedging with qualifiers.** "I help founders with strategy, but I also do execution, and sometimes I coach, depending on what they need."

That's not positioning. That's a list of services. The market doesn't buy lists. It buys clarity.

The fix: Pick one. Kill the other two for 90 days. See what happens.

**Mistake four: Trying to differentiate on process instead of outcome.** "I use a proprietary five-step framework to..." Nobody cares about the framework. They care about the result.

The fix: Lead with the outcome. "I take founders from 0 to 10 inbound calls in 90 days." The process comes later.

**Mistake five: Confusing positioning with a tagline.** A clever one-liner doesn't fix a positioning problem. "Empowering founders to unlock their potential" is a tagline. "I build LinkedIn content systems for B2B SaaS founders whose pipeline dies when they stop posting" is positioning.

The fix: If the sentence could apply to 50 other people in your space, it's not positioning yet.

**Mistake six: Refusing to niche because "it limits opportunity."** The opposite is true. The narrower the positioning, the easier the referral. "I help founders with marketing" gets zero referrals. "I help SaaS founders fix their onboarding email sequence" gets three referrals a month.

The fix: Test a narrow position for 90 days. Track inbound. If it doesn't work, pivot. But vague positioning guarantees invisibility.

## The contrarian read

The conventional advice is "find your niche and own it." The problem: most founders hear that and think niche means industry vertical. "I work with healthcare companies" or "I serve e-commerce brands."

That's not a niche. That's a market segment. A niche is the specific problem you solve inside that segment.

The contrarian take: niche by pain, not by industry. A founder who solves "pipeline going quiet when I stop posting" for any industry is more positioned than a founder who does "LinkedIn strategy for SaaS founders."

Why? Because the pain is visceral and immediate. The industry vertical is just a filter applied later.

Another contrarian take: the best positioning doesn't sound like positioning at all. It sounds like a sentence the client would say to a friend when describing what you do.

"She's the person who fixes my Facebook ads when they stop working."

"He builds the content system so I don't have to write posts from scratch every week."

"She finds the one bottleneck in my business that's costing the most money and fixes it."

None of those are taglines. They're descriptions. But they position better than any clever one-liner because they use the language the market already speaks.

Final contrarian take: most founders think they need to "build their brand" before they can position. The opposite is true. Positioning comes first. Brand follows.

Brand is what people say about you after you've been positioned correctly for six months. Trying to build brand before positioning is like trying to get famous before you've decided what you're famous for.

## What it looks like when it's working

Observable signal one: referrals become easy. A past client can describe what you do to a colleague in one sentence without hedging or adding context. "She builds LinkedIn systems for founders whose pipeline dies when they stop posting."

Observable signal two: sales calls start at yes or no, not "let me explain what I do." The prospect already knows what you solve. The call is about fit and timing, not education.

Observable signal three: content stops needing ten different hooks to cover all the bases. Every post now filters through one lens: does this connect to the specific pain I solve, or not? If not, it doesn't ship.

Observable signal four: pricing conversations get shorter. When positioning is clear, the buyer knows what they're paying for before the call. Objections shift from "I don't understand what this is" to "I'm not ready yet" or "budget doesn't work right now." Both are easier to handle.

Observable signal five: inbound calls come from the right people. Before positioning clarity, 70% of discovery calls are tire-kickers or people outside the ICP. After positioning clarity, 70% of calls are qualified from the first five minutes.

The numbers: from [[Client_results_2025_Q1]], founders who went through the positioning playbook saw an average of 3x inbound calls within 60 days and a 40% increase in close rate on discovery calls. The close rate improvement comes from better pre-qualification, not better sales skills.

The before-and-after pattern: before, the founder's LinkedIn profile has three different value props depending on who's reading it. After, the profile has one line, and every piece of content reinforces that same line. Engagement drops slightly in the first two weeks (because the founder stops trying to be interesting to everyone), then doubles in weeks three through eight (because the message finally has an edge).
