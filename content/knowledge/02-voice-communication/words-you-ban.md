## The core principle

The words you ban define the edge between Daniel Paul writing and AI slop. Every phrase on the blacklist exists because it failed the 14-year-old test, landed like corporate jargon, or showed up in 10,000 other AI-generated LinkedIn posts the same week.

If the reader can smell the AI, you lost them before the second line.

## Why this matters

Generic AI output is now the default. Claude, ChatGPT, and every other LLM ship with the same trained-in corporate voice. They open with "Let's dive in." They close with "Unlock your potential." They use em dashes like punctuation confetti.

The cost of sounding like AI is invisibility. When your hook reads like 500 other posts that week, the algorithm doesn't save you. The reader scrolls past. The client never books the call.

The leverage of getting this right is brutal simplicity. One banned phrase caught early saves 20 minutes of rewriting. One clean post that sounds human books a <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->5K client. Voice isn't decoration. It's the moat.

Every founder who comes to Danny drowning in content theatre is using these exact words. "Let's unpack this." "Game-changer." "Synergy." They sound like everyone because they're using the same tool the same way. The blacklist is the first diagnostic. If the draft is full of slop, the system isn't trained yet.

## How it shows up in client work

The single most common pattern across every founder Danny works with: they open Claude, type a question, get average output, paste it to LinkedIn, wonder why nothing happens. The output feels professional. It uses complete sentences. But it lands flat.

When Danny runs the Invisibility Diagnostic on those posts, they score 0/4 on Voice and 1/4 on POV. The content is "fine." That's the problem. Fine is invisible.

One client, a <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->M ARR SaaS founder, came in with this hook: "In today's fast-paced world, it's crucial to leverage robust solutions that resonate with your audience." Danny counted six blacklist violations in one sentence. "In today's fast-paced world" (instant AI tell). "Crucial" (meaningless intensifier). "Leverage" (banned verb). "Robust" (corporate fluff). "Resonate" (AI comfort phrase). The sentence said nothing.

The rewrite: "Your tool works. Your marketing doesn't. Here's why." Three sentences. Zero slop. That post booked four discovery calls in 48 hours. [[Client_session_2024_11_14]]

Another pattern: founders using "unlock" and "game-changer" because they think those words sell excitement. They don't. They sell sameness. Every AI-generated growth post uses them. Danny's rule: if you wouldn't say it on a real sales call with a paying client, don't type it on LinkedIn.

One coaching client was stuck on this line: "Let's dive deeper into the multifaceted landscape of founder positioning." Danny asked him to read it out loud. The client laughed halfway through. He wouldn't say "multifaceted landscape" to his co-founder. He definitely wouldn't say it to a prospect. The human version: "Most founders can't explain what they do in one sentence. That's the real problem." [[Seven_day_breakthrough_2025_01_22]]

The em dash epidemic shows up everywhere. Clients draft posts with three em dashes per paragraph because Claude defaults to them. Danny's voice rule: no em dashes ever. Use periods or commas. If a thought needs an em dash, it needs to be two sentences. One client went from 12 em dashes across five posts to zero. Engagement doubled. The rhythm changed. Short sentences land harder. [[Voice_cleanup_session_2024_10_03]]

The hashtag trap hits every new client. They stack 8-10 hashtags at the bottom of every post because "that's how LinkedIn works." Danny kills them all. Hashtags on LinkedIn in 2025 are a trust signal that you don't know how the platform works. The algorithm doesn't reward them. Readers skip past them. Zero value, negative positioning. [[LinkedIn_strategy_overhaul_2024_12_01]]

The "Great question!" sycophancy pattern appears in 90% of first drafts. Clients think they need to be friendly. They open every answer with praise. "I love this!" "Such an important topic!" "Great question!" Danny's reframe: respect the reader's time by answering the question. Skip the warmup. Start with the position. One client cut all praise from his comments and DM replies. Response rate went up 40%. People wanted answers, not validation. [[DM_strategy_revision_2025_02_10]]

The hedging-without-committing virus shows up when founders don't trust their own expertise. "It depends." "Every situation is unique." "There are many factors to consider." All true. All useless. Danny's coaching move: say what you'd do, then name the one variable that would change it. That's useful. Hedging without a follow-through is just stalling. [[Sales_discovery_frameworks_2024_09_15]]

One client was writing hooks that opened with "Imagine if..." Danny banned it after the third post. "Imagine if your content actually converted" isn't a hook. It's a lazy thought experiment. The client rewrote: "Your content gets likes. It doesn't book calls. Here's the gap." Same message. Ten times sharper. [[Hook_audit_session_2025_03_01]]

The "Here's a hot take" pattern is pure self-narration. Nobody at a bar says "Here's a hot take" before sharing an opinion. They just say it. The phrase is a tell that the writer doesn't believe the claim is actually hot. Danny's rule: if the take is hot, it doesn't need the disclaimer. Just ship it. [[Content_voice_rules_2024_08_20]]

## Scripts and exact phrases

When catching slop in a client draft: "Read this line out loud. Would you say this on a sales call? If no, rewrite it."

When a client asks why you banned em dashes: "If a thought needs an em dash, it needs to be two sentences. Period."

When a client wants to keep "leverage" in a post: "Swap it for 'use.' If 'use' sounds too simple, the sentence is trying to sound smart instead of being clear."

When a founder hedges with "it depends": "Say what you'd do. Then name the one thing that would change it. That's useful. 'It depends' alone is stalling."

When a client opens with "Great question!": "Cut it. Respect their time. Start with the answer."

When someone writes "Let's dive in": "Just say the thing. No diving. No unpacking. No unlocking. Say it."

When a client uses "game-changer": "Would you call your own product a game-changer on a discovery call? Then don't call it that in a post."

When someone stacks hashtags: "LinkedIn in 2025 doesn't reward hashtags. Readers skip them. Delete all of them."

## Common mistakes

Thinking banned words are "just style." They're not. They're trust signals. When a post sounds like AI, the reader assumes you didn't write it. They're right. The post dies.

Keeping one or two slop phrases "for variety." There's no safe dose. One "unlock" flags the whole post. Cut them all.

Replacing banned phrases with synonyms that mean the same thing. "Leverage" becomes "utilize." Still banned. Use "use."

Defending corporate jargon because "my industry talks like this." Your industry talks like everyone else now. That's the problem. Stand out by sounding human.

Assuming the blacklist only applies to LinkedIn posts. It applies to everything. Sales emails. Discovery call follow-ups. Workshop decks. Client onboarding docs. If it's external, the blacklist runs.

Using em dashes "just once" because the sentence is long. Break it into two sentences. The rhythm improves. The clarity doubles.

Hedging with "it depends" and thinking you sound thoughtful. You sound uncertain. Say what you'd do. Own the position.

Opening with praise because you think it softens the message. It wastes the reader's first three seconds. Start with the answer.

## The contrarian read

Every writing coach says "show, don't tell." Danny says the opposite for founder content: tell, then show. The thesis first. The proof second. Long-form literary writing can afford the buildup. LinkedIn posts can't.

Every LinkedIn "expert" says hashtags boost reach. Danny kills them all. The data doesn't support it. The trust cost is higher than the marginal visibility gain.

Every AI productivity guru says "use AI to write 10X faster." Danny says use AI to write 10X better by training it to reject the 25 phrases that make it sound like AI. Speed without voice is just faster noise.

Most founders think plain English sounds unprofessional. Danny's ICP — <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->00K-$5M founders — pays more for clarity than for vocabulary. The 14-year-old test isn't dumbing down. It's respecting the reader's time.

The conventional wisdom says "be relatable." Danny says be specific. Relatability without specifics is just vague warmth. Names, numbers, dates, and real examples beat empathy theatre every time.
