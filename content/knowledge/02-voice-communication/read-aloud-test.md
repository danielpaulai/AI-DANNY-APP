## The core principle

If you would be embarrassed to say it on a sales call, you cannot ship it on LinkedIn.

## Why this matters

The read-aloud test is the kill switch that catches every piece of AI slop, every generic hook, every corporate buzzword before it leaves your hands.

Most founders type something into Claude, scan it with their eyes, think "yeah that looks fine," and hit publish. They never speak the words out loud.

That's the gap where bad content escapes.

Because written text lies to you. Your brain autocorrects. It fills in tone. It hears your voice reading the words, not a stranger's voice.

But when you force yourself to READ THE WORDS OUT LOUD, your body reacts. Your face tenses. You hear the fakeness. You catch the phrase that sounds like a LinkedIn influencer cosplaying as you.

The cost of skipping this test is compounding invisibility. Every post you ship that doesn't sound like you trains your audience to scroll past your name. Every generic hook conditions them to expect nothing interesting from you.

And the worst part: you don't see the damage immediately. You still get likes. You still get "great post!" comments from other people who are also posting AI slop.

But the qualified buyers stop reading. They pattern-match you as "another AI-generated founder account" and move on.

The read-aloud test protects your positioning. It's the last line of defense between "content that sounds like me" and "content that sounds like everyone else using the same prompt."

## How it shows up in client work

The read-aloud test shows up in three specific patterns across 1:1 coaching sessions.

**Pattern 1: The embarrassment reflex**

A founder brings me a LinkedIn post they're about to ship. I ask them to read it out loud on the Zoom call.

Within three sentences, they pause. Their face changes. They say "oh god, I would never actually say this."

The line is always something like "In today's fast-paced business landscape, leveraging AI can unlock transformative outcomes."

They typed it. Claude generated it. It looked professional on the screen. But the second they heard it in their own voice, their body rejected it.

[[1:1_PB_session_2025-02-18]] — founder brought a post with "game-changing" in the hook. I made him read it aloud. He stopped at the word, laughed, and said "I sound like a LinkedIn bot." Rewrote the hook as "Most founders are typing into Claude and getting Claude back. Here's what I do instead." Shipped that version. Booked two calls that week.

**Pattern 2: The vocal fry test**

I train clients to read their hooks out loud in a deliberately flat, bored voice. If the hook needs your enthusiasm to land, it's a weak hook.

The best hooks create tension or curiosity in the structure of the sentence itself, not in how you perform it.

[[Workshop_2025-03-12_AI_School]] — one attendee read his hook with full vocal energy: "Are you ready to UNLOCK the POWER of AI in your business?" It sounded fine when he performed it.

I made him read it again in a monotone. Dead silence. The sentence had no structural interest. It was just a question that needed hype to work.

We rewrote it as: "I spent $400 on AI tools last month. Used two of them." Same information. No performance required. The gap between $400 and two creates the tension.

He read that version flat. Still interesting. That's the test.

**Pattern 3: The client-facing filter**

The question I ask in every content review session: "Would you send this post privately to your highest-paying client as a message?"

Not "would you post it publicly." Would you DM it to the person who just paid you <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->5K?

If the answer is no, the post doesn't ship.

[[1:1_PB_session_2024-11-08]] — founder wrote a post about "the five pillars of personal brand strategy." Looked polished. Hit all the formatting rules. No AI tells in the copy.

I asked the DM question. He said "No way. This sounds like a blog post. I'd never send this to a client."

We cut four of the five pillars. Kept one. Wrote 1,200 characters on that one pillar using a specific client example. The new version passed the DM test. That's the version that shipped.

## Specific examples from the vault

**Example 1: The "unlock" purge**

[[Voice_audit_2025-01-20]] — I ran a voice audit on a founder's last 20 LinkedIn posts. Nine of them contained the word "unlock" or "unlocking."

"Unlock your potential." "Unlocking growth." "Unlock the power of AI."

I made him read all nine posts out loud in one sitting. By post four, he was laughing. By post seven, he said "I sound like a late-night infomercial."

The word "unlock" had become a crutch. He never noticed it on screen. But the second he heard himself say it nine times in ten minutes, the pattern became obvious.

We added "unlock" to his banned words list. He hasn't used it since. His engagement went up 40% the following month.

**Example 2: The sales call transcript as content test**

[[Content_strategy_session_2024-12-03]] — founder couldn't figure out why his LinkedIn posts weren't converting. He was posting three times a week. Good formatting. No obvious AI tells.

I asked him to send me his last three sales call recordings. I transcribed them. Then I printed out his last ten LinkedIn posts.

We laid them side by side.

On the sales calls: sharp, specific, opinionated. "Most agencies will tell you to post daily. I tell my clients to post twice a week and make every post count. Here's why..."

On LinkedIn: generic, hedged, vague. "Consistency is key in content marketing. Here are some tips to help you stay on track..."

Two completely different people.

I played back a 60-second clip from his best sales call — the moment where he told a prospect "if you're not tracking which posts book calls, you're just posting into the void."

Then I made him read his most recent LinkedIn post out loud.

The gap was immediate. He said "I would never say this shit on a call."

That became the new filter. Every post had to pass this test: "Is this something I would say on a sales call, word for word?"

His next post opened with "If you're not tracking which posts book calls, you're posting into the void." Direct lift from the sales call. Booked three discovery calls that week.

**Example 3: The mom test**

[[Workshop_2025-02-20_Founder_Content]] — I teach a variation of the read-aloud test in workshops: read your post to your mom (or any non-founder family member).

If they don't understand it, your ideal client won't either.

One attendee read his hook: "Leverage AI to synergize your go-to-market motion and unlock scalable growth levers."

His mom's response: "What does that mean?"

He tried to explain. Took him four sentences to say "use AI to get more customers faster."

That became the new hook: "Use AI to get more customers faster. Here's how I did it in 90 days."

The mom test is a forcing function. It kills corporate jargon. It kills abstraction. It kills anything that requires a business degree to parse.

**Example 4: The phone voice note method**

[[1:1_PB_session_2025-03-08]] — founder was stuck writing hooks. Everything he typed felt flat.

I had him open his phone's voice recorder and record himself talking through the post idea like he was explaining it to a friend at a coffee shop.

No script. No editing. Just raw explanation.

He sent me the audio. I transcribed it. Pulled three sentences verbatim.

"Most founders think AI will replace them. I think it'll replace everyone around them. That's why I'm building an AI team, not an AI tool."

That became the post. Word for word. No polishing.

It performed 3x better than any post he'd written at his desk.

The phone voice note method is the read-aloud test in reverse. Instead of reading written text out loud to catch the fake stuff, you speak first and write second.

Your spoken voice is always closer to your real voice than your typed voice.

## Scripts and exact phrases

**"Read this like you're on a sales call."**  
The frame I use in every content review session. Not "read it out loud." Not "read it to yourself." Read it like you're on a Zoom call with a prospect who just asked you a question.

**"If you wouldn't say it to a client, don't say it to the timeline."**  
The positioning filter. Your LinkedIn feed is a sales channel. Every post is a conversation with a potential buyer. If the language wouldn't work in that conversation, it doesn't work on LinkedIn.

**"Your face just changed. What did you hear?"**  
The reaction I call out when a founder reads a line and their expression shifts. They always hear the problem before they can articulate it. Trust the face.

**"Read it flat. No enthusiasm. Does it still work?"**  
The vocal fry test. If the hook needs your energy to land, the structure is weak.

**"Would you send this as a DM to your highest-paying client?"**  
The client-facing filter. If the answer is no, the post doesn't ship.

**"That's a line you typed. Give me the line you'd say."**  
The rewrite prompt when someone reads a sentence that's clearly written-voice, not spoken-voice. I make them close the doc and just tell me what they meant.

**"You just said that in three words. Your post said it in twenty. Use the three."**  
The compression test. Spoken language is tighter. When you read a post out loud and then explain what it means, the explanation is always shorter than the original text.

**"Read the last five posts in a row. Out loud. Does it sound like the same person wrote all five?"**  
The consistency check. Most founders have three different voices across their last ten posts because they're copying different prompts, different styles, different moods. Reading them consecutively makes the inconsistency obvious.

## Common mistakes

**Mistake 1: Reading silently in your head and calling it done**  
Your brain autocorrects when you read silently. You hear what you meant to write, not what you actually wrote. The only way to catch AI slop is to force it through your mouth.

**Mistake 2: Reading out loud but not listening**  
Some founders perform the test mechanically — they read the words, but they're not actually listening to how it sounds. The point isn't just vocalization. It's hearing your own voice say the words and noticing when your body rejects them.

**Mistake 3: Reading only the hook**  
The hook matters, but the body copy matters more. A great hook with a generic body is still a bad post. Read the entire thing out loud, start to finish, before you ship.

**Mistake 4: Reading after you've already published**  
The read-aloud test is a pre-publish filter, not a post-mortem. By the time you realize a post sounds wrong, 5,000 people have already scrolled past it. Do the test before you hit "Post."

**Mistake 5: Outsourcing the test to someone else**  
"I had my VA read it" doesn't count. You need to hear YOUR voice say the words. Your VA's voice tolerance is different. Your ear is the only one that matters.

**Mistake 6: Skipping the test on "short" posts**  
A three-sentence post can still sound like AI. Length doesn't protect you. Read everything out loud.

## The contrarian read

Most content advice tells you to "write like you talk."

That's wrong.

You should write like you talk to a client on a good call — not like you talk to your friend at a bar.

Spoken conversation is messy. It has filler words, false starts, tangents, verbal tics. If you transcribed a casual conversation verbatim and posted it on LinkedIn, it would be unreadable.

The read-aloud test isn't about capturing raw speech. It's about capturing the TONE and CLARITY of your best professional conversation.

The version of your voice that shows up when you're explaining something important to someone who's paying attention.

That voice is tighter than casual speech but looser than formal writing. It's the sweet spot.

The other contrarian take: not every post needs to pass the read-aloud test.

Some content formats are deliberately written-voice. Long-form breakdowns. List posts. Frameworks. These aren't meant to sound like spoken conversation. They're meant to be scanned, not read aloud.

The read-aloud test applies primarily to hooks, stories, and opinion posts — the content types that depend on voice and tone to land.

If you're writing a "7 ways to improve your sales process" post, the read-aloud test is less relevant. That's a reference document. It lives or dies on structure and clarity, not voice.

But if you're writing "Here's why most founders are wrong about pricing," you need to read it out loud. Because that post is selling a point of view, and point of view lives in voice.

## What it looks like when it's working

When the read-aloud test becomes a habit, you start catching problems before they're problems.

You type a sentence. You hear it wrong before you even finish typing. You delete it and start over.

The filter moves upstream. You're no longer catching bad posts at the review stage. You're catching bad LINES at the drafting stage.

Your typing rhythm changes. You write shorter sentences. You cut more. You default to simpler words.

Observable signal: your editing time drops by 50%. When every sentence is written in spoken-voice from the start, you spend less time rewriting.

Another signal: your engagement starts matching your effort. Posts you spent 20 minutes on perform the same as posts you spent 90 minutes on, because the 20-minute posts sound like you and the 90-minute posts sound like a content strategist.

The ultimate signal: people start quoting you back to yourself. In discovery calls. In DMs. In replies.

When someone says "I saw your post about X, and the line you wrote about Y — that's exactly what I needed to hear," that's confirmation the voice is landing.

Before the read-aloud test becomes a habit, nobody quotes you. They say "I saw your post" but they don't remember a single line.

After the test becomes a habit, specific lines stick. Because they sound like something a real person would actually say.
