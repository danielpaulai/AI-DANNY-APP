## The core principle

The first line of a DM is not an introduction. It's proof you did your homework.

Most people open with "Hi, I noticed your profile" or "I'd love to connect." Both are lies. You didn't notice anything specific, and "connect" means nothing. The reader deletes before finishing the sentence.

The only opener that works: name something real they said, built, or shipped, then tell them why it matters to you. No flattery. No pitch. Just signal that you're paying attention.

## Why this matters

LinkedIn is a cold platform pretending to be warm. Every founder gets 15+ connection requests a week from strangers selling something. The default assumption is spam.

If your opener reads like a template, you've already lost. The reader's brain pattern-matches it against every other generic DM they've archived that week. You become invisible before you finish typing.

The cost of getting this wrong is immediate. You burn a lead. You waste the research time. And worse, you train yourself to write like everyone else, which means you stay invisible even when you finally have something worth saying.

The leverage of getting it right is asymmetric. A specific, well-researched opener bypasses the spam filter entirely. It earns you three more sentences of attention. That's enough to book a call if you don't waste it.

The mechanism is simple: specificity signals effort. Effort signals respect. Respect earns attention. Attention converts to replies. You can't skip the first step.

When a founder replies to your DM, it's not because you were clever. It's because you proved you read their work, understood it, and connected it to something they care about. That's the only reason anyone replies to a stranger.

## How it shows up in client work

The pattern I see most often: clients send 50 DMs and get 2 replies. When I ask to see the openers, every single one starts with "I came across your profile" or "I love what you're doing with [company name]."

Both are AI-tells now. Everyone can see them coming. The founder on the receiving end reads it as: "I didn't actually look at your work. I'm using a template. Please delete this."

One client, a positioning consultant, was sending DMs to SaaS founders offering free audits. Her opener was: "I help SaaS founders clarify their messaging. Would you be open to a free 15-minute audit?"

Zero replies in three weeks. Fifty DMs sent.

I asked her to show me the founder she was about to DM next. She pulled up a profile. I asked: "What did this person post about last week?" She didn't know. She'd copied the LinkedIn URL into a spreadsheet but hadn't read a single post.

The fix: spend five minutes per lead. Read their last three posts. Pick one moment where they said something specific about positioning, messaging, or go-to-market confusion. Quote it. Connect it to your offer.

Her new opener: "Saw your post about 'horizontal vs vertical' positioning. You said you're stuck between selling to everyone or just fintech. That's the exact decision I helped [Client Name] make last quarter. Fintech-only 10x'd their inbound. Happy to show you the before/after if you're still wrestling with it."

Three replies in the first ten DMs. Two booked calls. One closed. All from changing the first line.

Cited from [[Business_strategy_session_2_2024_08_11]], where I walked a client through this exact cold DM audit process.

Another pattern: clients who over-personalize. They open with three paragraphs about the founder's journey, their company history, and their mission. It reads like a fan letter.

The founder thinks: "This person wants something from me but is pretending they don't."

Personalization works when it's surgical. One line. One specific thing. Then the turn. The turn is where you connect that specific thing to the outcome you deliver.

Example from a client who coaches B2B sales teams: "Your post about reps losing deals in discovery hit home. We lost $400K last quarter the same way. You said the fix is 'better questions, not better slides.' That's the exact framework I teach. If you want the 7-question sequence I give my reps, happy to send it over."

That's 47 words. No fluff. No background. Just proof of attention, shared pain, and an immediate offer tied to the thing they said.

The reply rate on that opener: 40%. Twenty DMs sent, eight replies, three calls booked. Same client was getting 5% reply rates the week before with generic openers.

Cited from [[Purely_Personal_cohort_1_2025_03_06]], where multiple founders workshopped their DM openers live and I scored them on specificity.

The other mistake: clients who open with a question. "What's your biggest challenge with LinkedIn right now?" or "How are you currently handling content?"

Both sound like survey requests. The founder has no incentive to answer. You're asking them to do work for you before you've given them a reason to care.

The fix: replace the question with a statement. Instead of "What's your biggest challenge with content?" say: "Saw you posted twice in January, then went quiet for six weeks. That's the exact pattern I see in founders who know they should post but hate the process. Built a system that cut my content time from 12 hours to 2. If you're stuck in the same loop, happy to show you how it works."

Statement. Observation. Outcome. Offer. That's the structure. No question required.

One client tested this. She sent 20 DMs with question openers. Four replies, all one-word answers, zero calls. She sent 20 DMs with statement openers. Eleven replies, six calls booked, two closed.

Same offer. Same ICP. Different first line.

## Specific examples from the vault

In [[Weekly_Meeting_2025_10_15]], the team was drafting LinkedIn DM templates using Text Blaze to speed up outreach. The problem: every template started with "Hi [Name], I came across your profile."

I told them to delete the template. Write each DM from scratch. Use Text Blaze only for the closing CTA, not the opener.

The reason: if the opener feels templated, the reader stops reading. You can't automate the proof-of-research step. That's where the signal lives.

The solution we shipped: create a Text Blaze snippet for the CTA only. The opener stays manual. Format: one specific observation from their recent posts, one outcome you delivered for someone in their exact position, one offer tied to the thing they said.

In [[Nas.io document redesign with branding - 6b8ef772]], I was building email and DM systems for the NAS.IO ops team. The challenge: they were sending hundreds of follow-up messages to students who attended sessions, but the openers all sounded like customer support tickets.

Example opener they were using: "Hi [Name], thank you for attending yesterday's session. We wanted to follow up and see if you have any questions."

Zero replies. The message reads like an auto-responder.

The fix: replace "thank you for attending" with a specific moment from the session. What question did they ask in chat? What tool did they say they wanted to try? What problem did they mention they were stuck on?

New opener: "Saw your question in chat about automating LinkedIn posts. You said you're manually scheduling 10x a week. That's exactly what [Tool Name] fixes. If you want the 5-minute setup guide I sent to three other students yesterday, reply and I'll send it over."

That's a real follow-up. It references a real moment. It offers a real next step. Reply rate jumped from 3% to 28% across 200+ follow-up DMs.

In [[March Madness Marathon problems and solutions - 508640a8]], we diagnosed why a promotional campaign for a live event got low attendance. One of the issues: the DMs promoting the event opened with "We're hosting a special marathon session this Sunday."

The problem: no hook. No reason to care. Just an announcement.

The fix: lead with the outcome, not the event. "You've been asking for a session on [specific skill]. We're running a 3-hour deep-dive this Sunday with [Trainer Name] covering exactly that. No recording. Show up or miss it."

Same event. Different opener. Attendance doubled in the final 48 hours.

In [[Writing a bold and catchy email - a8498c36]], I was coaching the team on Taki Moore-style email writing. The principle applies directly to DMs: if the first sentence doesn't create a gap between where the reader is and where they want to be, they stop reading.

Taki's rule: no preamble. No "I hope this finds you well." Start with the tension. The gap. The problem they're already thinking about.

Applied to DMs: "Your last post said you're spending 15 hours a week on content. That's 60 hours a month. I just helped a founder compress that to 8 hours total. If you want to see the workflow, happy to send it over."

That's tension. That's a gap. That's a reason to reply.

## Scripts and exact phrases

**When opening a cold DM based on a recent post:**
"Saw your post about [specific thing they said]. You said [exact quote or paraphrase]. That's the exact problem I solved for [Client Name] last quarter. [One-sentence outcome]. If you're still wrestling with it, happy to show you how we fixed it."

**When opening a warm DM to someone you met at an event:**
"Good to meet you at [Event Name]. You mentioned you're stuck on [specific problem]. I built a [tool/framework/system] that handles that exact thing. If you want the 5-minute walkthrough, let me know and I'll record it."

**When following up after a session or workshop:**
"Saw your question in chat about [specific tool/problem]. You said you're currently [their current manual process]. That's exactly what [Solution Name] automates. If you want the setup guide I'm sending to three other attendees, reply and I'll forward it."

**When reaching out to a founder whose content you've been following:**
"Been reading your posts on [specific topic] for the last month. Your line about [specific quote] landed hard. I'm working on [related thing] and would love 10 minutes to ask you two questions about [specific aspect]. No pitch. Just research."

**When opening a DM to offer a free resource:**
"Your post about [problem] is the exact issue I built [Resource Name] to solve. It's a [format: checklist/template/guide] that walks through [specific outcome]. No opt-in required. If you want it, reply and I'll send the link."

**When opening with a contrarian take on something they said:**
"Read your post about [topic]. You said [their position]. I'd actually push back on that. Here's why: [one-sentence contrarian insight]. If you want the full breakdown, happy to send over the case study where we tested both approaches."

**When reaching out to someone who went quiet on LinkedIn:**
"Noticed you posted 3x in [Month], then went quiet. That's the pattern I see in every founder who knows they should be consistent but hates the process. Built a system that makes posting feel like ops, not content creation. If you're stuck in that loop, happy to show you how it works."

**When following up on a no-reply DM:**
"Sent a note last week about [specific thing]. No reply, which usually means wrong timing or wrong offer. If it's timing, ignore this. If it's the offer, would one question help clarify if this is worth 15 minutes?"

## Common mistakes

**Opening with a compliment.** "I love what you're doing with [company]" or "Your profile stood out to me." Both sound like spam. Compliments are free. Specifics cost time. The reader can tell the difference.

**Asking a vague question.** "What's your biggest challenge right now?" or "How's business?" You're asking the reader to do work before you've earned their attention. Replace the question with an observation + outcome.

**Leading with your credentials.** "I'm a [title] who helps [ICP] do [thing]." Nobody cares who you are until they know why they should. Lead with their problem, not your resume.

**Writing three paragraphs before the offer.** The first line should create tension. The second line should show you've solved it before. The third line should offer the next step. Anything longer than that is wasting attention.

**Using "I noticed" or "I came across."** Both are filler. Cut them. Start with the specific thing. "Saw your post about X" is stronger than "I noticed you posted about X."

**Asking for a call before you've delivered value.** "Would you be open to a quick call?" is the fastest way to get ignored. Replace it with: "If you want [specific thing], reply and I'll send it over." Value first, call second.

**Personalizing the wrong things.** Mentioning their company name, their location, or their job title is not personalization. That's mail-merge. Personalization is quoting something they said and connecting it to an outcome you delivered.

**Hedging the offer.** "If you're interested" or "Let me know if this resonates" or "No pressure." All of it sounds weak. Make the offer direct. "Reply and I'll send it" is stronger than "Let me know if you'd like me to send it."

One client opened every DM with "Hope you're having a great week!" It's filler. It trains the reader that your message is low-priority. Cut it. Start with the thing that matters.

Another client ended every DM with "Looking forward to hearing from you!" It sounds like a form letter. Replace it with a clear next step. "Reply with yes and I'll send the guide by end of day."

## The contrarian read

Most LinkedIn advice says "personalize your outreach." That's not wrong. It's just incomplete.

Personalization without a clear outcome is wasted effort. You can spend 10 minutes researching someone, write a beautiful opener that references their work, and still get ignored if you don't tell them what happens next.

The contrarian position: specificity is not the goal. The goal is earned attention. Specificity is just the tool.

That means you can write a less-personalized DM and still win if the outcome you're offering is clear, immediate, and tied to something they said publicly.

Example: "Saw your post about losing deals in discovery. I built a 7-question framework that closes 40% more deals in the same pipeline. If you want it, reply and I'll send the doc."

Is that deeply personalized? No. It doesn't mention their company, their team size, or their specific challenges. But it's specific enough to prove you read their post, and the outcome is clear enough to justify a reply.

The over-personalization trap is real. Clients spend 20 minutes researching someone, write a 200-word DM that sounds like a biography, and wonder why they didn't reply. The answer: too much input, not enough output. You gave them information, not a reason to act.

The better move: spend 5 minutes. Find one thing. Connect it to one outcome. Make one offer. Send it. Move on.

Another contrarian take: cold DMs work better than warm intros for testing messaging. With a warm intro, the reader is doing you a favor. They're more likely to reply even if your message is weak. That gives you false validation.

With a cold DM, the reader has no obligation. If they reply, it's because your message earned it. That's the real test.

I tell clients: write 10 cold DMs before you ask for a single warm intro. If your cold DMs don't get replies, your warm intros won't convert either. Fix the message first.

## What it looks like when it's working

You send 20 DMs. You get 8 replies within 48 hours. Four of them book calls. Two close.

The replies don't say "Thanks for reaching out." They say "This is exactly what I needed. When can we talk?"

The founders you DM start checking your profile before they reply. You see profile views from every person you messaged. That's the signal your opener worked.

You stop needing to send follow-ups. The first DM earns a reply or it doesn't. When it does, the conversation moves immediately to scheduling.

Your clients start asking you to teach them how you write DMs. That's the real proof. When operators who close deals for a living want to learn your method, you've built something repeatable.

You can open a cold DM thread, read the first message you sent, and feel zero embarrassment. It's specific. It's clear. It's useful. That's the bar.

Before: 50 DMs sent, 3 replies, 0 calls booked, weeks of follow-up.

After: 20 DMs sent, 8 replies, 4 calls booked, 2 closed, no follow-up required.

The difference is the first line.
