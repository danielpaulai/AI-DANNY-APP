## The core principle

DMs are not marketing. DMs are conversations that cost something to start.

Every word you send privately to another human being either earns trust or burns it. There is no neutral. The inbox is not a broadcast channel. It is not a place to test hooks or run A/B tests on strangers. It is the only channel where the reader chose to let you in, and every message is a referendum on whether you should stay.

## Why this matters

Most founders treat DMs like they treat LinkedIn posts: something to batch, something to automate, something to delegate to a VA or a tool. This is how you end up invisible.

When a prospect opens a DM from you, they are deciding whether you are someone worth knowing or someone wasting their time. That decision happens in the first four words. Not the first sentence. The first four words.

If those four words sound like a cold email, you are already deleted. If they sound like a sales pitch wrapped in fake curiosity, you are blocked. If they sound like every other founder who mass-messaged 200 people that week, you are ignored.

The cost of getting this wrong is not just one lost conversation. It is the compounding cost of your name becoming associated with noise. LinkedIn remembers. So do people. Once you are categorized as spam, you cannot undo it with better copy. You are done.

The leverage of getting it right is the opposite. A well-written DM opens a door that no amount of content can. It moves someone from "I see your posts" to "I want to talk to you." It turns a cold audience into a warm conversation. It books calls, closes deals, and builds relationships that last years.

The mechanism is simple: DMs reward specificity, reject templates, and punish anything that feels like marketing. The more personalized the message, the more leverage it has. The more it sounds like something you would say to a friend over coffee, the more likely it lands.

## How it shows up in client work

I see three recurring patterns when I review client DMs.

**Pattern 1: The template trap.** A founder shows me their outreach script. It starts with "Hi [Name], I saw your post about [Topic] and wanted to reach out." The formula is correct. The execution is dead. The problem is not the structure. The problem is that 50 other people sent the exact same message that week, and the prospect can smell it. The tell is always in the specificity. "I saw your post about leadership" is a template. "I saw your post about firing your co-founder and I've been there" is real.

One client, a B2B SaaS founder, was sending 30 DMs a week and getting zero replies. I read the messages. Every single one opened with a compliment about the prospect's company, followed by a pitch for a demo. Perfect formula. Zero results. The fix was brutal: write five DMs instead of 30, and make each one about something the prospect actually said in a post or comment. Within two weeks, three of the five replied. One booked a call. The difference was not volume. It was cost per message. He started treating each DM like it mattered.

Cited in [[Andrealiz_Uy_and_Daniel_Paul_2025_07_08]] and [[Mitchell_(Kondo)_Daniel_Paul_2025_01_29]].

**Pattern 2: The friend-client confusion.** A founder DMs a paying client the same way they DM a prospect. Casual, friendly, open-ended. "Hey, just checking in. How's everything going?" This works for friends. It does not work for clients who are paying you $5,000 a month. They do not need you to check in. They need you to show up with value.

I coached a personal branding client who was losing momentum with her clients. Every week she would send "Just wanted to see how you're doing" messages. The clients would reply with one-liners. The relationship felt warm but the results were stalling. The shift: stop checking in, start delivering. "I pulled three new hook ideas from your last call. Want to run one this week?" The client replies in 60 seconds. The difference is clarity. A client DM should always have a next step.

**Pattern 3: The over-explain.** A founder writes a DM that is three paragraphs long. The first paragraph is context. The second paragraph is the ask. The third paragraph is a sign-off. By paragraph two, the prospect is gone. The rule for DMs is the same as the rule for posts: one sentence per paragraph. If you cannot say it in 40 words, you do not know what you are asking for yet.

One client, a consultant selling <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->5K engagements, was writing DMs that read like cover letters. "I wanted to reach out because I've been following your work for a while and I think there's a potential fit between what you're building and what I do, which is helping founders scale their personal brands without hiring a full marketing team." I cut it to: "You're building something real. Want to talk about how your LinkedIn could pull its weight?" Same message. One-tenth the words. Three times the reply rate.

Cited in [[Diko_Kissimon_One_on_One_Personal_Branding_2024_07_09]].

**Pattern 4: The CTA mismatch.** A founder sends a DM asking for a 30-minute call when the relationship does not justify it yet. This is the fastest way to get ignored. The ask has to match the relationship stage. If you have never talked before, the ask should cost the prospect nothing: "Would it help if I sent you the three-post framework I used to go from 0 to 10 inbound calls in 90 days?" If you have talked once, the ask can be bigger: "Want to jump on a 15-minute call to see if this is worth exploring?" If you have talked three times, the ask can close: "Let's do this. I'll send the proposal by Friday."

I worked with a founder who was DMing warm leads and asking for 45-minute strategy calls. The reply rate was 10%. The fix was not better copy. It was a smaller ask. "I have a two-page doc that shows how we'd approach this. Want me to send it?" Reply rate went to 60%. The strategy call happened after they read the doc.

**Pattern 5: The reply ghost.** A founder gets a reply and then waits three days to respond. This is the DM equivalent of no-showing to a call you booked. The prospect replied because they were interested in that moment. Three days later, they are not. The window for a DM conversation is 24 hours. If you take longer than that, the momentum is dead. I tell clients: if you do not have time to reply same-day, do not send the DM yet.

**Pattern 6: The automation tell.** A founder uses a tool to send 100 DMs a day. The tool works. The DMs send. The replies are zero. The tell is always in the timing. If you send 100 messages in one hour, LinkedIn knows. If every message is identical except for the name, the prospect knows. The fix is not better automation. It is less automation. Send five DMs a day, written by hand, to people you actually researched. The reply rate will be 10x higher than 100 automated messages.

Cited in [[Mitchell_(Kondo)_Daniel_Paul_2025_01_29]].

**Pattern 7: The unearned ask.** A founder DMs a stranger and asks for an intro to their network. This only works if you have already given value first. The sequence is: give value, build trust, then ask. Not the other way around. I see this constantly with founders who want to meet VCs or potential partners. They DM someone they have never talked to and say "Would love an intro to your network." The correct sequence is: DM with value, get a reply, have three conversations, then ask for the intro after you have proven you are worth knowing.

The DM worked because the ask was binary and the next step was frictionless.

## Scripts and exact phrases

**Opening a cold DM to a prospect:**
"I saw your post about [specific thing they said]. I've worked with three founders who had the same problem. Want to see what worked for them?"

**Following up on a no-reply:**
"I know you're busy. If this is not a priority right now, totally cool. Just wanted to close the loop."

**Asking for a call after three DM exchanges:**
"This feels like it's worth 15 minutes on a call. Want to find a time this week?"

**Turning a comment into a DM:**
"That comment you left on my post, there's a longer version of that conversation if you want it. Should I send it?"

**Re-engaging a cold lead:**
"We talked in [Month] and then life got busy. If you're still thinking about this, I have something new that might help. If not, all good."

**Delivering value before asking:**
"I pulled three ideas from your last post that could turn into content. Want me to send them?"

**Closing a warm DM conversation:**
"Let's do this. I'll send the proposal by Friday. If it looks good, we start Monday."

**Checking in with a client (the right way):**
"I have the next three posts ready. Want to review them before I schedule?"

## Common mistakes

**Mistake 1: Starting with a compliment.** "I love your work" is not an opening line. It is filler. The prospect knows you are trying to soften them up. Start with the value or the ask.

**Mistake 2: Asking for time before proving value.** "Can I pick your brain for 15 minutes?" is a red flag. The prospect does not know you yet. Give value first, then ask for time.

**Mistake 3: Writing like you talk to a VA.** "Per my previous message" or "circling back" are corporate tells. Write like you talk to a friend.

**Mistake 4: Over-explaining the context.** The prospect does not need to know how you found them or why you are reaching out. Get to the point in the first sentence.

**Mistake 5: Sending a wall of text.** Anything longer than four sentences is too long. Break it into multiple messages if you have to.

**Mistake 6: Not having a clear next step.** Every DM should end with a question or a clear action. If the prospect has to guess what you want, they will not reply.

## The contrarian read

Most people think DMs are about being friendly. I think DMs are about being useful.

Friendly gets you a reply. Useful gets you a conversation. The difference is specificity. A friendly DM says "I'd love to connect." A useful DM says "I have a three-post framework that solved the exact problem you posted about yesterday. Want it?"

The other contrarian take: automation is not the enemy. Bad automation is. If you can automate the research (finding the right 10 people to message) and personalize the outreach (writing five custom DMs), you get the best of both. The mistake is automating the wrong part. Automate the grunt work. Personalize the message.

## What it looks like when it is working

You send 5 DMs a week and 3 of them reply.

Prospects reply within 24 hours, not after three follow-ups.

Clients respond to your DMs with next steps, not one-word answers.

You book calls from DMs without pitching. The conversation just moves there naturally.

You close deals in the DMs without needing a proposal. The value is so clear that the ask is obvious.

Your DMs feel like texts to a friend, not emails to a stranger.

The signal that it is working: people start DMing you first. That is when you know your public content and your private follow-up are aligned.
