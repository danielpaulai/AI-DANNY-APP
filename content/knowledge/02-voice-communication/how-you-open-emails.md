## The core principle

The first five words of your email are the only ones that matter.

Everything else is negotiable.

## Why this matters

Most founders write emails backwards.

They spend 30 minutes on the body, five seconds on the subject line, and zero time on the opening sentence.

Then they wonder why nobody reads past line two.

The cost of getting this wrong compounds silently.

Your best offer gets ignored because the subject line sounds like spam. Your strongest insight dies in paragraph three because the hook didn't land. Your follow-up gets deleted unread because the opening sentence promised nothing worth trading time for.

The mechanism is simple: your reader makes three binary decisions in three seconds.

Decision one: do I open this? (Subject line)

Decision two: do I keep reading? (First sentence)

Decision three: do I care enough to act? (Everything else)

If you lose at decision one or two, nothing else you wrote matters. The body could be perfect. The offer could be irresistible. The CTA could be clear. But if the reader never got there, you built a house with no door.

The leverage of getting this right is asymmetric. A subject line that earns the open means every word after that costs you nothing extra. An opening sentence that lands means the reader is now bought in, now looking for reasons to stay instead of reasons to leave.

That shift — from looking for an exit to looking for value — is the only thing email marketing actually sells.

## How it shows up in client work

Most of the founders I work with think their email problem is a writing problem.

It's not. It's a framing problem.

They open with context the reader doesn't need yet. "Hi [Name], hope you're doing well. I wanted to reach out because..." By the time they get to the actual point, the reader is already gone.

The pattern I see most often: the founder buries the value proposition in paragraph two or three, after spending paragraph one explaining who they are or why they're writing.

Here's what that looks like in real campaigns I've reviewed.

A client running AI training workshops for solopreneurs was sending follow-up emails that opened with: "Thanks for attending last week's session on Claude Skills. I hope you found it valuable." Dead on arrival. The reader already knows they attended. They don't need a recap. They need a reason to read the next sentence.

We rewrote it to: "The skill you built last Tuesday is sitting in your Claude account doing nothing." Immediate tension. The reader now has a problem they didn't know they had 10 seconds ago, and they keep reading to find out how to fix it.

Another client — consultant selling <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->5K engagements — was opening cold outreach with: "I noticed your company recently raised a Series B and I'd love to discuss how we might work together." Generic. Forgettable. No specificity. No hook.

We rewrote to: "Your last three LinkedIn posts all mention the same problem: you're closing deals but losing them in onboarding." That line pulled from [[Weekly_Meeting_2025_10_08]] where we diagnosed exactly this pattern. The specificity signaled research. The problem signaled relevance. The open rate jumped from 18% to 41%.

The common thread across every workshop, every coaching session, every content review: the founder knows what they want to say, but they open with setup instead of impact.

They write "I wanted to share some thoughts on..." when they should write "You're doing five jobs that one Claude Skill could handle for you."

They write "Following up on our conversation..." when they should write "Here's the exact script we talked about, ready to use today."

The shift I make in every session: cut the first two sentences. Start where the value starts.

In [[Rewriting Claude Dispatch email with Taki Moore style - 93aa57e5]], we worked through exactly this. The original draft opened with "It's Danny. I don't do emergency sessions lightly." Weak. Self-referential. No cost to the reader if they stop reading.

Final version opened with: "You can now text Claude a task from your phone, walk away, and come back to the work done on your computer." Immediate specificity. Immediate outcome. The reader now knows exactly what's in it for them in the first 12 words.

That's the pattern that repeats across every vertical, every offer type, every audience: value first, context second, credentials never.

## Specific examples from the vault

In [[Writing a bold and catchy email - a8498c36]], we built an email for a March Madness Marathon event. The original subject line was: "🚨 I had to call an emergency session. This just dropped and it changes everything."

Emoji. Vague urgency. No specificity. The reader has no idea what "this" is or why they should care.

We tested three alternatives. The one that landed: "I texted Claude from the airport." Seven words. No emoji. Pure specificity. The reader immediately knows this is about Claude, about mobile-first workflows, and about something the sender actually did (not just hyped). Open rate doubled.

The opening sentence in that email originally read: "I just spent the last 48 hours playing with something Anthropic quietly dropped last week and I genuinely couldn't wait until next month's curriculum to show you this."

Too long. Too many qualifiers ("just," "genuinely," "quietly"). Too much setup before the payoff.

Final version: "You can now text Claude a task from your phone, walk away, and come back to the work done on your computer." The value is in the first sentence. The reader knows immediately if this applies to them.

In [[March Madness Marathon problems and solutions - 508640a8]], we debriefed why an event underperformed. One of the diagnoses: the promotional emails started with "The goal of March Madness was to have a much higher attendance..." Context nobody asked for. The reader doesn't care about your internal goals. They care about their outcome.

The corrected approach for future events: "Three hours. Multiple trainers. Everything that's changed in AI and LinkedIn in the last few months, covered in one session built specifically for you." Outcome-first. Specificity-first. The reader knows in 20 words if this is worth their time.

In [[Nas.io document redesign with branding - 6b8ef772]], we rebuilt trainer documentation for AI Business School. The email delivering that doc originally opened with: "Here's the updated session guide for March 2026." Functional. Forgettable. No hook.

Revised to: "The March curriculum is live. Open the doc and you'll see every session title, every tool, every deliverable, color-coded by week." The specificity makes the action clear. The reader knows what to expect before they click.

Every example follows the same structure: cut the preamble, start with the specific outcome or specific tension, give the reader a reason to stay in the first 10 words.

## Scripts and exact phrases

Here are the opening lines that reliably perform across client emails, workshop invites, and cold outreach:

"You're doing five jobs manually that one Claude Skill could handle for you."

"The skill you built last Tuesday is sitting in your Claude account doing nothing."

"Your last three LinkedIn posts all mention the same problem: you're closing deals but losing them in onboarding."

"You can now text Claude a task from your phone, walk away, and come back to the work done on your computer."

"I texted Claude from the airport and landed to a full week's work done."

"Three hours. Multiple trainers. Everything that's changed in AI and LinkedIn in the last few months, covered in one session built specifically for you."

"Here's the exact script we talked about, ready to use today."

"The March curriculum is live. Open the doc and you'll see every session title, every tool, every deliverable, color-coded by week."

Every line starts with outcome, tension, or specificity. Never context. Never credentials. Never "hope this finds you well."

## Common mistakes

Opening with "I wanted to reach out because..." Dead. The reader doesn't care why you wanted to reach out. They care what's in it for them.

Opening with "Hope you're doing well." Generic filler that signals you have nothing urgent to say.

Opening with "Following up on our conversation..." Weak unless you immediately name the specific thing you're following up on. "Following up" by itself is vague. "Here's the pricing breakdown we talked about Friday" is specific.

Burying the value proposition in paragraph two or three. If the reader has to scroll to find out why they should care, they won't scroll.

Using emojis in professional emails. In [[Writing a bold and catchy email - a8498c36]], we explicitly removed the 🚨 emoji from the subject line because it cheapened the message. Emojis signal hype. Plain text signals substance.

Opening with "I'm excited to share..." or "I'd love to discuss..." Self-referential. The reader doesn't care about your excitement. They care about their outcome.

Starting with credentials before value. "As the founder of Purely Personal..." tells the reader nothing about what they get. "You're spending 12 hours a week on content that Claude could compress to 2" tells them everything.

## The contrarian read

Conventional wisdom says emails should "build rapport" before asking for anything.

Wrong.

Rapport is a luxury you earn after you've already proven relevance.

If your opening sentence doesn't immediately signal "this applies to you," the reader deletes the email before rapport has a chance to matter.

The other bad advice: "personalize every email."

Personalization is only valuable if it proves you did research that changes the offer. "I saw you recently posted about X" is generic unless it leads to a specific insight the reader didn't have before.

"Your last three posts mention the same problem" is personalization that works because it names a pattern the reader may not have noticed themselves.

"I noticed your company raised a Series B" is personalization that doesn't work because it's public information that adds no insight.

The third myth: subject lines should create curiosity.

Curiosity without context is just confusion. "You won't believe what happened next" might work for clickbait. It doesn't work when you're asking someone to spend 10 minutes reading your email or 60 minutes on your call.

Better: specificity that makes the reader instantly know if this is relevant. "I texted Claude from the airport" creates curiosity anchored in a concrete outcome. The reader knows this is about mobile AI workflows before they open the email.

## What it looks like when it's working

Open rates above 40% on cold outreach. Above 60% on warm follow-ups. Above 80% on workshop invites to existing clients.

Reply rates above 10% on cold emails (not autoresponders — real replies that move the conversation forward).

The reader responds within 24 hours instead of a week, because the opening line landed hard enough that they couldn't ignore it.

Clients forward your emails to their teams without being asked, because the value was immediately clear and the format was clean enough to share.

You stop hearing "I didn't have time to read the whole thing" because the first sentence made the whole thing worth reading.

Before: emails that sit in the inbox for days, then get archived unread.

After: emails that get opened within an hour, read to the end, and acted on the same day.

The observable signal: you're booking more calls from fewer emails, because every email you send now earns the read instead of hoping for it.
