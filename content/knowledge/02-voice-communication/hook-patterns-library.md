## The core principle

The hook is the only sentence that earns the right to the rest of the post.

Everything else is conditional on whether the first line stops the scroll.

## Why this matters

Every founder is posting into a feed where the average user spends 1.2 seconds deciding to keep reading or move on.

That decision happens in the hook.

Not the body. Not the insight. Not the CTA.

The hook is the entire game compressed into one sentence.

Get it wrong and the rest of the post never gets read. The insight dies unread. The CTA gets zero clicks. The post scores 0/4 on the Invisibility Diagnostic before the second sentence even loads.

Most founders write hooks that sound like they're opening a PowerPoint presentation. "I want to talk about X." "Here's something I've been thinking about." "Let me share a lesson."

Those aren't hooks. Those are warnings that nothing interesting is coming.

The mechanism is simple: a great hook creates a gap between what the reader knows and what they need to know. That gap is called curiosity. Curiosity is the only force strong enough to overcome the default behaviour of scrolling past.

The cost of getting this wrong is that everything downstream breaks. A founder spends 12 hours a week writing thoughtful posts that get 8 likes and zero inbound calls. They blame the algorithm. They blame LinkedIn. They blame their offer.

The actual problem is that nobody made it past the first line.

## How it shows up in client work

The most common pattern I see is the "setup hook" — where the founder writes a sentence designed to introduce the topic rather than hook the reader.

Example from [[Content_strategy_session_2024_11_06]]: a client opened with "I've been thinking a lot about personal branding lately."

I stopped them immediately. "That's not a hook. That's you narrating your internal monologue. The reader doesn't care what you've been thinking about. They care what they're about to get."

We rewrapped it: "Most founders post content. Almost none of them ship a system."

Same topic. Different entry point. The first version scored 0/4 on Voice because it sounded like AI. The second version scored 3/4 because it had a real POV.

Another recurring pattern: the question hook that doesn't create tension.

A founder in [[Personal_branding_coaching_2025_01_22]] opened with "Have you ever wondered how to grow on LinkedIn?"

I pushed back: "No tension there. The answer is obviously yes or they wouldn't be reading LinkedIn posts about LinkedIn. The question has to cost them something to answer."

We rewrote it: "Your LinkedIn isn't a brand problem. It's a workflow problem."

That's not a question but it forces the same internal interrogation. The reader has to stop and evaluate whether that's true for them. That pause is the hook working.

The pattern that separates founders who book calls from founders who don't: contrarian + specific.

From [[Hook_test_archive_2025_02_18]], the three posts that generated the most inbound DMs that month all followed the same structure:

1. "The AI hype is wrong about one thing: it doesn't replace marketers. It replaces marketing tools."
2. "If giving yourself a blowout pulls out too much hair, takes too much time, and ultimately just misses — here's how I cheated."
3. "I don't know who needs to hear this, but don't post educational videos on Instagram ever again."

Each one rejects an obvious assumption (AI replaces people, blowouts require skill, educational content is good for reach), then promises a specific alternative.

No "imagine if." No "let me share." No "I've been thinking." Just the claim, then the proof.

The mistake most founders make is they bury the contrarian claim three paragraphs down. They open with context. They open with a story. They open with "recently I realized."

By the time they get to the actual insight, 90% of readers are gone.

I had a client in [[Content_coaching_2025_02_10]] write a post about how founders should stop hiring marketers and start building AI systems. Brilliant insight. The hook was: "Hiring is getting harder."

I told them: "You just gave away that the post is about hiring problems. That's a boring topic. Lead with the take: 'Your next marketing hire should be an AI system, not a human.'"

The original post got 14 likes. The rewrite got 140 and booked two calls.

## Specific examples from the vault

From [[Building_audience_through_authentic_creator_content_0d8a6374]], I analyzed 10 viral scripts from Sandcastles.ai to reverse-engineer the hook patterns that actually worked.

The pattern that emerged: outcome + 3 back-to-back pain points + intro into solution.

Concrete example: "If giving yourself a blowout pulls out too much hair, takes too much time, and ultimately just misses — here's how I cheated."

The structure: If [desired outcome] causes [pain 1], [pain 2], and [pain 3] — here's [the solve].

That formula generated 1M+ views across three completely different niches. The reason it works: it frontloads empathy (I see your exact pain) before promising the solution.

Another example from that same note: "Did you know that you can start your very own bakery from home without a storefront, high startup costs, or years of experience?"

Pattern: Did you know you can [desired outcome] without [barrier 1], [barrier 2], or [barrier 3]?

The key: the barriers have to be the actual reasons people haven't done it yet. Not generic barriers. The specific ones they've used to talk themselves out of starting.

From [[Making_sessions_AI_school_friendly_5f56aa1f]], I worked with the AI Business School team to rewrite 30 session titles into hooks that would pass the "Nuseir test" — would a 12-year-old understand this immediately?

The rejected titles all had the same pattern: they opened with "How to" or named the tool before naming the outcome.

Example of a rejected title: "Use Claude Code Voice Mode to Code 3x Faster."

Nuseir's feedback: "What is Claude Code Voice Mode? I don't know. Lead with what I get."

Rewrite: "Code 3x Faster Using Voice — No Typing Required."

The difference: the second version tells you the outcome first, then the mechanism. The first version assumes you already care about Claude Code Voice Mode. You don't.

From [[Building_closing_playbook_381021dc]], I extracted Alex Hormozi's objection-handling framework into hook formulas for sales contexts.

The pattern that closed the most deals: reframe the objection as a different problem.

Example: "I need to think about it" → "Totally fair. Walk me through what's still unclear. Price, scope, fit, or timing?"

That's a hook for a closing script. It stops the objection, creates a new decision point, and forces the prospect to name the real issue.

Another Hormozi pattern from that same note: the main concern close.

Hook: "If I could wave a magic wand and handle your main concern — what would that be?"

The reason that works as a hook: it gives the prospect permission to name the one thing holding them back without feeling like they're committing to buy.

From [[Nas_io_document_redesign_with_branding_6b8ef772]], I worked with the AI Business School team on email hooks for three user buckets (paid NAS.IO users, paid AI School users, free users).

The pattern that got the highest open rates: three-line hierarchy with one italic word.

Example:

**Most founders are building in public.**  
**Almost none of them are *selling* in public.**  
Turn the content you already made into paid products.

That's the hook structure Danny and the team landed on after testing 8 variations. The italic word is the turn. The third line is the promise.

The reason it works: it doesn't ask for anything in the first two lines. It just makes a claim. The ask comes after the reader is already nodding.

## Common mistakes

Opening with "I want to talk about X" or "Here's something I've been thinking about."

That's not a hook. That's you narrating your process. The reader doesn't care about your process until they care about the outcome.

Using question hooks that have no tension.

"Have you ever wondered how to grow on LinkedIn?" is a question with an obvious answer. It doesn't create a gap. It creates a shrug.

Burying the contrarian claim three paragraphs down.

If the insight is "founders should stop hiring marketers and start building AI systems," that needs to be the hook. Not the conclusion.

Opening with context or backstory.

"Recently I had a conversation with a client" is setup. It's not a hook. The reader hasn't earned the right to your story yet.

Using "imagine if" or "let me share" or "I've been thinking."

All AI tells. All instantly forgettable. All score 0/4 on Voice.

Writing hooks that assume the reader already cares about the tool or the framework.

"Use Claude Code Voice Mode to Code 3x Faster" assumes the reader knows what Claude Code Voice Mode is. They don't. Lead with the outcome: "Code 3x Faster Using Voice."

## The contrarian read

Most LinkedIn advice says "start with a question to create engagement."

I disagree.

Questions are lazy hooks. They only work if the question creates real tension. 99% of the time they don't.

The posts that book calls don't open with questions. They open with claims.

Another contrarian take: hooks don't need to be clever.

The best hooks are just specific. Name the exact pain. Name the exact outcome. Name the exact mechanism.

Clever is a distraction. Specific is a conversion tool.

One more: the hook doesn't need to be short.

A 15-word hook that names three specific pain points will outperform a 5-word hook that's vague.

Length is not the enemy. Vagueness is.

## What it looks like when it's working

The post gets engagement in the first 10 minutes.

If the hook is working, people stop scrolling immediately. They don't wait to see how the algorithm treats it. They engage on sight.

The comments reflect the hook, not the body.

When a hook lands, the top comment is someone quoting the first line back to you. That's social proof that the hook did its job.

The post books calls.

Likes are not the goal. Inbound DMs are. If the hook is strong enough to stop the scroll, it's strong enough to earn the DM.

You can reuse the hook in other contexts.

A great hook becomes the subject line for an email. The opening line of a sales call. The tagline on a landing page. If it's not reusable, it's not strong enough.

The post scores 3+ on Voice in the Invisibility Diagnostic.

If the hook sounds like AI wrote it, it's invisible. If it sounds like a human who has an actual opinion, it's working.
