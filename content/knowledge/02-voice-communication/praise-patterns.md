## The core principle

Praise is currency. Spend it like you're broke.

The second you open with "Great question!" or "I love this!" you've revealed you have nothing to say yet. Real praise names the specific thing that worked and why it mattered. Everything else is filler.

## Why this matters

Generic praise trains people to ignore you.

When every response opens with "this is such an important topic" or "you're absolutely right," the words become wallpaper. The recipient stops reading after the first line because they've learned nothing real follows.

Specific praise does three things generic praise can't. First, it proves you actually engaged with the work. Second, it tells the person what to do more of. Third, it gives them language to describe their own strength that they didn't have before.

The cost of getting this wrong shows up in two places. In content, opening every post or comment with sycophancy makes you invisible. Every founder on LinkedIn who starts with "Love this!" sounds identical. In coaching or sales calls, leading with praise-as-filler wastes the first 30 seconds of trust the client gave you. They came for a diagnosis. You gave them a compliment they've heard before.

The leverage of getting this right is positioning. When someone reads your feedback and thinks "they saw something I didn't see in my own work," you've earned credibility they can't revoke. That's not praise. That's analysis they paid for without knowing it.

## How it shows up in client work

The pattern repeats across every type of engagement. Founders send LinkedIn posts for review. Workshop participants submit homework. Sales calls open with the prospect explaining their offer. In every case, the instinct is to open with validation before critique.

The instinct is wrong.

In [[Content_strategy_session_2024_11_06]], a client sent a post for review that opened with "Most people think AI will replace them. Smart people know it won't." Generic praise would have been "Great hook!" Specific praise was: "The parallel structure works because it creates an in-group the reader wants to join. The problem is the claim is vague. Replace 'Smart people know it won't' with 'The best founders are using it to replace their 12-hour content week.' Now the reader knows what the in-group actually does."

That's not praise. That's a diagnosis with one piece of working code embedded in it. The client now has language to describe what made the hook almost work, and a concrete fix.

In [[Sales_call_2025_03_14]], a prospect explained their offer on a discovery call. The instinct would have been to say "That sounds like a great program!" Instead: "The transformation you described in the first 30 days is the entire offer. Everything after that is proof. Lead with that and the rest writes itself."

The prospect replied: "I've been explaining this wrong for six months." That's what specific praise unlocks. It tells them what they already have that they didn't know was valuable.

The inverse also shows up. In [[Workshop_session_2024_12_03]], a participant submitted a hook that was genuinely weak. Generic critique would have been "This needs work." Specific critique was: "This hook has no claim. You opened with a question, which means the reader has to do work before they know why they're reading. Replace 'Have you ever wondered why some founders grow faster?' with 'Most founders post daily and stay invisible. The ones who grow post differently, not more.' Now there's a claim."

The participant rewrote it in 10 minutes. Specific praise and specific critique use the same mechanism: name the exact thing that's working or broken, then show the alternative.

In [[Personal_branding_coaching_2025_01_18]], a founder was stuck on positioning. They'd written three versions of their one-liner and couldn't pick. Generic praise would have been "All three are solid!" Specific praise was: "Version two is the only one where I know who you serve and what they get. Versions one and three describe what you do, not what they walk away with. Ship version two."

The founder shipped it that afternoon. Praise that commits to a position gives the recipient permission to move.

The pattern also shows up in comment threads on LinkedIn. Most founders reply to every comment with "Thanks for sharing!" or "Appreciate this!" Those responses are invisible. In [[Content_analysis_2025_02_18]], the highest-performing comments were the ones that either added a new insight or asked a follow-up question that deepened the original post.

Example from a real thread: Original post was about how most founders waste time on content that doesn't book calls. Generic comment: "So true!" Specific comment: "This is why I track pipeline per post, not likes per post. Vanity metrics killed my first business." The specific comment got 14 replies. The generic one got zero.

Praise works the same way. Generic praise is "Great post!" Specific praise is "The line about tracking pipeline instead of likes is the entire framework. That's the new north star."

In [[Seven_day_breakthrough_pattern]] coaching sessions, the default move when a founder makes progress is to say "Amazing work!" The better move is to name the exact moment the shift happened. Example from [[Breakthrough_session_2025_03_20]]: "The second you said 'I help founders build systems that run their marketing' instead of 'I do content strategy,' the offer became clear. That's the line. Use it everywhere."

The founder wrote it at the top of every sales document. Specific praise tells them what to anchor to.

The other place this shows up is in objection handling on sales calls. When a prospect says "I've tried content before and it didn't work," the instinct is to validate their concern before reframing. That's the wrong sequence. In [[Handling_too_expensive]], the move is to reframe first, then validate second.

Example from a real call: Prospect says "I'm worried this won't work for my niche." Generic validation: "I totally understand that concern." Specific reframe: "What didn't work before was probably the execution, not the niche. If your offer is strong and the ICP is narrow, content is the most reliable channel. The question is whether you're willing to do it consistently for 90 days."

That's not praise or validation. That's a diagnosis that costs the prospect something to ignore.

## Specific examples from the vault

In [[Content_strategy_session_2024_11_06]], the client submitted a post that scored 2/4 on the Invisibility Diagnostic. The hook had voice and specificity but no POV or ask. Generic feedback would have been "Solid start, just needs a stronger ending." Specific feedback was: "You opened with 'Most founders waste 12 hours a week on content that doesn't book calls' — that's the claim. But the post doesn't tell me what to do instead. Add a three-step framework in the body or a single CTA at the end. Right now I believe the problem but don't know the solution."

The client added: "Here's what I do instead: 1. Audit every post against pipeline, not likes. 2. Cut anything that didn't book a call. 3. Double down on what did." The rewrite booked two calls in the first 48 hours.

In [[Sales_call_2025_03_14]], the prospect explained a coaching offer that promised "clarity and confidence." Generic praise would have been "That's a great outcome to promise!" Specific feedback was: "Clarity and confidence are feelings, not results. A buyer can't measure them. Replace it with 'In 90 days you'll have a one-line positioning statement that books calls, or we keep working until you do.' Now the promise is falsifiable."

The prospect rewrote the offer page that night. Booked three clients in two weeks. The shift wasn't praise. It was specificity that made the promise real.

In [[Workshop_session_2024_12_03]], a participant submitted a LinkedIn profile headline that read "Helping founders grow." Generic feedback would have been "Good start, but make it more specific." Specific feedback was: "This headline has no ICP and no mechanism. Replace it with 'I help B2B founders build LinkedIn content systems that book 5+ calls a month without hiring a team.' Now I know who you serve, what they get, and how."

The participant changed it before the workshop ended. Three inbound DMs in the next week.

In [[Personal_branding_coaching_2025_01_18]], the founder had written three versions of their offer. Version one: "I help entrepreneurs build their brand." Version two: "I help founders turn their LinkedIn into a client channel in 90 days." Version three: "I teach founders how to create content that converts."

Generic praise: "These are all strong!" Specific praise: "Version two is the only one with a number and a timeframe. The other two describe what you do, not what I get. Ship version two today."

The founder used it in a sales call that afternoon. Closed a $5K engagement.

In [[Hook_test_archive_2025_02_18]], the analysis of 50 top-performing posts revealed a pattern: the hooks that booked calls were the ones that rejected a common assumption in the first line. Example: "Most founders post content. Almost none of them ship a system."

Generic insight: "Contrarian hooks perform well." Specific insight: "The pattern is claim + specificity in the first sentence, then proof in the body. The hooks that don't work open with questions or 'imagine if.' The ones that work open with 'Most X do Y. The best X do Z instead.' That's the template."

That template became the default hook structure for every client in [[Content_strategy_2025_Q1]].

In [[Breakthrough_session_2025_03_20]], the founder had been stuck on pricing for three weeks. They'd written five versions of their offer, all between <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->,500 and $3,000. Generic advice: "Pick the one that feels right." Specific advice: "The price isn't the variable. The promise is. If you promise 'better content,' $3K is expensive. If you promise 'a content system that books 5 calls a month in 90 days or we keep working,' $3K is cheap. Fix the promise first."

The founder repriced at $4,500 with a result-tied guarantee. Closed two clients in the next 10 days.

In [[Objection_handling_2025_01_22]], a client asked how to handle "I need to think about it" on sales calls. Generic advice: "Give them time and follow up." Specific advice: "That phrase almost never means thinking. It means a missing piece. Ask: 'Walk me through what's still unclear. Price, scope, fit, or timing?' Most of the time they name one. Then you handle it on the call."

The client used it on the next call. Closed on the spot.

## Scripts and exact phrases

"The line about [specific thing] is the entire offer. Lead with that."

"This has no claim. You opened with a question, which means the reader has to do work before they know why they're reading."

"Version [number] is the only one where I know who you serve and what they get. Ship that."

"The transformation you described in the first 30 days is the whole program. Everything after that is proof."

"That's not a result. That's a feeling. Replace [vague outcome] with [specific measurable thing]."

"The hook works because [specific mechanism]. The problem is [specific gap]. Replace [weak part] with [working alternative]."

"This post scored [number]/4 on the Invisibility Diagnostic. Here's what's missing: [specific element]. Add [concrete fix]."

"The parallel structure creates an in-group. The claim is vague. Replace [generic line] with [specific alternative that names the action]."

## Common mistakes

Opening with sycophancy before the real feedback. "Great question!" trains the listener to ignore the next sentence because they've learned it's filler. Lead with the position or the diagnosis.

Praising effort instead of outcome. "I can tell you worked hard on this" is meaningless. The recipient already knows they worked hard. Tell them what worked or what didn't.

Hedging specificity with softening language. "This might be just me, but I think maybe the hook could be a bit stronger" wastes 12 words to avoid committing. Say: "The hook has no claim. Add one."

Praising everything equally. If every piece of work gets "This is great!" the recipient learns nothing about what to do more of. Specificity is the mechanism that makes praise useful.

Using praise as a setup for critique. "I love the concept, but…" signals that the praise wasn't real. If the concept is strong, say so and stop. If it's not, skip the praise and name the gap.

Complimenting the person instead of the work. "You're so talented!" doesn't help them replicate the thing that worked. "The specificity in line three is what made this post book calls" does.

## The contrarian read

Most people think praise builds confidence. It doesn't. Specificity builds confidence.

The founder who hears "Great job!" walks away with nothing new. The founder who hears "The line about tracking pipeline instead of likes is the framework — that's the new positioning" walks away with an anchor.

The other contrarian position: withholding praise isn't harsh. It's efficient. If the work isn't ready, saying "This is solid!" wastes both people's time. Saying "This has no ICP — add one in the next draft" moves the work forward.

The third hot take: praise is analysis, not emotion. The goal isn't to make someone feel good. The goal is to tell them what's working so they can do it again on purpose.

## What it looks like when it's working

The recipient quotes your feedback back to you three weeks later as the thing that unlocked the shift.

Your comments on LinkedIn posts get replied to more than generic "Love this!" comments because you added a new angle or asked a question that deepened the original point.

Clients implement the feedback within 24 hours instead of sitting on it because the fix was concrete enough to act on immediately.

Workshop participants cite the specific line you praised as the new standard they're holding their work to.

Sales calls close on the spot after you reframe an objection because the reframe was specific enough to dissolve the concern rather than validate it.
