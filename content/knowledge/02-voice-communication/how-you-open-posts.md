## The core principle

Your first line is the only line that matters.

Everything else — the body, the proof, the CTA — dies if the hook doesn't land. The reader sees your name, your face, and one sentence. That's the decision window. Scroll or stop. There is no third option.

## Why this matters

Most founders write hooks that sound like they're reading from a script at a corporate town hall.

"In today's fast-paced world…" "Imagine if…" "Here's a hot take…"

These are AI tells. They signal "skip this" before the second line even loads.

The cost is invisible but catastrophic. A founder spends 90 minutes writing a post. The body is sharp. The proof is real. The CTA converts. But the hook is generic, so 95% of their audience scrolls past in 0.8 seconds. The post gets 14 likes from the founder's inner circle and zero pipeline movement.

LinkedIn's algorithm amplifies engagement in the first 60 minutes. If the hook doesn't stop the scroll in that window, the post is dead. It doesn't matter how good the rest of it is. The platform buries it. The founder posts again next week with the same result. Six months later they're burned out, blaming "the algorithm" or "content saturation," when the real problem was line one.

The leverage of getting this right is asymmetric. A founder who masters hooks can write mediocre bodies and still book calls. A founder with weak hooks and brilliant bodies gets ignored. The first line is the entire game.

## How it shows up in client work

The pattern I see most often: founders open with a question or an "imagine if" construction because that's what they've been taught.

"What if I told you there's a better way to [do the thing]?"

"Have you ever wondered why [obvious problem]?"

These hooks test well in copywriting courses. They fail on LinkedIn because everyone is using them. The reader's brain has learned to filter them as noise.

I had a client — SaaS founder, strong offer, smart positioning — who was posting 3x a week and getting 8–12 likes per post. I pulled up his last 10 posts. Every single hook opened with a question. [[Weekly_Meeting_2025_08_18]] documents the session where we diagnosed this. His body content was solid. His proof was specific. But every hook sounded like a landing page above the fold.

The reframe I gave him: "Stop asking permission to make a point. Just make the point."

We rewrote his next hook from "What if your sales team could close 30% more deals with one simple change?" to "Your sales team is leaving 30% of revenue on the table because they're asking the wrong question in discovery."

Same promise. Different entry. The post went from 11 likes to 140 and booked two calls.

Another pattern: founders who've worked with ghostwriters or agencies inherit hooks that sound like ad copy.

"Buckle up." "Let's dive in." "This changed everything."

These are instant credibility killers. They position the founder as someone who outsourced their voice to a junior copywriter who learned to write from Gary Vee circa 2019. [[Scraping LinkedIn profile posts with API - e46f9d5c]] shows the audit I ran on a client's profile after they came off a six-month ghostwriting engagement. 14 of 16 posts opened with phrases from the AI-slop list. The comments were full of "great post!" reactions from other people using the same ghostwriter. Zero clients.

I also see founders who open with qualifiers because they're trying to avoid sounding arrogant.

"I'm not an expert, but…" "This might be controversial, but…" "Just my two cents…"

These hedges sound humble. They read as insecure. The reader's brain interprets them as "this person doesn't believe what they're about to say." [[Personal_Branding_session_5_2024_01_05]] includes a breakthrough moment with a client who opened every post with a disclaimer. I asked her: "If you walked into a sales call and opened with 'I'm not sure if this will work for you,' what would happen?" She laughed and said the prospect would leave. Same dynamic on LinkedIn. The hook either earns the reader's attention or it doesn't. Hedging forfeits the round.

The flip side: founders who overcorrect and open with fake controversy.

"Hot take: [thing everyone already agrees with]."

"Unpopular opinion: you should focus on results."

These hooks promise tension and deliver platitudes. The reader feels baited. They don't engage. They scroll faster next time. [[Prioritize Profit Over Vanity Metrics - 2396e8fa]] shows a carousel hook that fell into this trap: "Your 10,000 followers mean nothing if your bank account shows zero." The line itself is fine. But calling it a "hot take" or "unpopular opinion" would've killed it. The hook works because it states a specific tension without announcing that it's supposed to be provocative.

The strongest hook pattern I've seen across clients: open with a specific claim or a specific moment, not a setup for a claim.

One client — personal branding coach for executives — was opening posts with "Let me tell you about the time I…" I told him to cut "let me tell you" and just start with the moment. His next post opened: "I spent $47K on a rebrand that tanked my pipeline in three weeks." That post booked four discovery calls. The body was identical to posts he'd written before. The only change was the hook.

## Specific examples from the vault

The best hook I've seen from a client came from [[Weekly_Meeting_2025_08_18]]: "I got 1,558 likes on a LinkedIn post and booked zero calls."

The post was about vanity metrics versus pipeline. But the hook worked because it named a real number, implied a problem, and made the reader ask "how is that possible?" No setup. No "imagine if." Just the tension, stated plainly.

Another example from the same client: "Most founders post content. Almost none of them ship a system."

That hook worked because it rejected the obvious assumption (posting is the goal) and named a specific alternative (shipping a system). The reader either agrees and keeps reading, or disagrees and comments. Both outcomes are wins. The line earns engagement because it has a position.

[[March Madness Marathon problems and solutions - 508640a8]] documents a session title workshop where we applied the same principle to event promotion. The original title was "March Madness Marathon: Learn AI Skills." Generic. No tension. We rewrote it to "Create 5 Lead-Generating Posts From Any Content in 30 Minutes." The new title wasn't a hook for a post, but it followed hook logic: specific action, specific deliverable, specific timeframe. Attendance doubled.

I had a founder who sold consulting services to mid-market SaaS companies. His posts were getting 20–30 likes but no inbound. I pulled his last 15 hooks. 12 of them opened with "Here's how to [do the thing]." The pattern was invisible to him because he'd written those hooks over six months. To a reader seeing them in a feed, they all looked identical.

We tested a new pattern: open with the outcome of NOT doing the thing, then pivot to the solution in line two.

Old hook: "Here's how to run a discovery call that converts."

New hook: "Your discovery calls are converting at 22% because you're asking 'what's your budget' in the first five minutes."

The new hook worked because it diagnosed a specific mistake before offering a fix. The reader who makes that mistake stops scrolling. The reader who doesn't make that mistake keeps scrolling, which is fine — they weren't the ICP anyway.

[[Nas.io document redesign with branding - 6b8ef772]] shows a workshop brief where we applied hook logic to session titles. The original session title was "AI for Marketing." Too broad. No tension. We rewrote it to "Turn Content You Already Made Into Paid Products." The new title worked as a hook because it named a specific transformation the reader could visualize. Same content. Different entry point. Signups increased 40%.

One pattern that consistently underperforms: opening with a personal story setup.

"Three years ago, I was broke and desperate. Today, I run a seven-figure agency. Here's what changed."

The line-by-line narrative works in long-form content or YouTube videos where the reader has already committed to watching. On LinkedIn, the reader hasn't committed to anything yet. The hook has to earn the next sentence. "Three years ago" doesn't do that. It asks the reader to trust that the story will be worth their time before they've seen any proof.

The reframe: open with the insight or the result, THEN flashback to the story in the body if it adds value.

Better hook: "I doubled my agency revenue by firing 60% of my clients." Now the reader wants to know how. The story can show up in paragraph three as proof.

## Scripts and exact phrases

**Contrarian + specific format:**

"Most [people in your niche] do [common behavior]. Almost none of them [the thing that actually works]."

Example: "Most founders post content. Almost none of them ship a system."

**Outcome-of-inaction format:**

"Your [thing] is [underperforming metric] because you're [specific mistake]."

Example: "Your LinkedIn DMs are converting at 11% because you're pitching in message two."

**The specific moment format:**

"[Concrete event with a number]."

Example: "I spent $47K on a rebrand that tanked my pipeline in three weeks."

**The rejected assumption format:**

"[Thing people believe] isn't the problem. [Actual thing] is."

Example: "Your LinkedIn isn't a brand problem. It's a workflow problem."

**The tension-without-resolution format:**

"[Specific outcome that shouldn't be possible, stated plainly]."

Example: "I got 1,558 likes on a LinkedIn post and booked zero calls."

**The specific number + timeframe format:**

"[Action verb] [specific deliverable] in [specific time]."

Example: "Create 5 lead-generating posts from any content in 30 minutes."

**The diagnostic format:**

"If [observable behavior], then [underlying problem you diagnose]."

Example: "If you're explaining your offer in three sentences, you don't have an offer yet."

**The reversal format:**

"Everyone says [conventional wisdom]. The opposite is true: [your position]."

Example: "Everyone says 'post daily.' The opposite is true: post when you have something worth saying."

## Common mistakes

**Opening with a question instead of a claim.**

The reader's brain treats questions as a request for permission. "Should you care about this?" The answer is "probably not" and they scroll. A claim forces engagement: "Here's what's true. Agree or disagree." Questions are lazy hooks that signal the founder doesn't trust their point.

**Using "imagine if" or "what if" constructions.**

These are 2019 Facebook ad hooks that have been beaten to death. The reader's pattern-matching system flags them as generic copy and scrolls before processing the rest of the sentence. They also delay the actual point by an entire line, which is fatal when you have 0.8 seconds to capture attention.

**Opening with a qualifier or a hedge.**

"I'm not an expert, but…" "This might be controversial, but…" "Just my opinion, but…" These phrases undermine the hook before it's even delivered. They position the founder as unsure. If you're unsure, don't post it. If you're sure, don't apologize for being sure.

**Announcing that you're about to say something interesting.**

"Here's a hot take…" "Buckle up…" "Let's dive in…" These are meta-hooks. They narrate the post instead of starting the post. The reader doesn't care that you think your take is hot. They care whether the take is actually useful. Lead with the take, not the announcement.

**Opening with a setup sentence that goes nowhere.**

"In today's fast-paced world, businesses need to adapt." This is corporate jargon posing as a hook. It says nothing specific. It could apply to any business in any industry in any decade. The reader's brain discards it as filler and moves on.

**Starting with a story without establishing why the reader should care.**

"Three years ago I was sitting in a coffee shop…" The reader doesn't know you yet. They don't know if your story is worth their time. You have to earn the right to tell the story by opening with the insight or the outcome first. Story-first hooks work in long-form where the reader has already committed. They fail on LinkedIn where commitment costs attention.

## The contrarian read

Most people think hooks need to be clever or creative or "thumb-stopping."

That's backwards.

The best hooks are the most boring ones — if you define boring as "direct and specific." A hook that says "Your LinkedIn isn't a brand problem, it's a workflow problem" isn't clever. It's a diagnosis. But it works because it rejects an assumption the reader holds and names a specific alternative.

The founder who chases "creative" hooks ends up writing lines that sound impressive but say nothing. "Unlock your potential." "The future of work is here." "Are you ready to transform your business?" These hooks perform in creative director portfolios. They die on LinkedIn because they don't give the reader a reason to stop.

Another contrarian take: weak hooks are better than no hooks.

I've seen founders spend 30 minutes trying to craft the perfect opening line and end up not posting at all. That's worse than posting with a mediocre hook. A mediocre hook teaches you what doesn't work. No post teaches you nothing. The goal isn't perfection. The goal is iteration speed. Post, measure, adjust.

I also push back on the idea that hooks need to be "scroll-stoppers." That phrase comes from paid ads where you're interrupting someone who wasn't looking for you. On LinkedIn, your audience opted in by connecting with you or following you. The hook's job isn't to interrupt. It's to reward the reader for stopping. Big difference.

The other thing nobody says: hooks work best when they're written last, not first.

Most founders write the hook, then build the body around it. That's why their posts feel forced. The body is trying to justify a hook that was written in a vacuum. I teach clients to write the body first, extract the core insight, then write the hook as a distilled version of that insight. The hook becomes a promise the body keeps, not a thesis the body tries to prove.

## What it looks like when it's working

You know the hook is working when the comments section fills up with two types of responses: "This is exactly what I needed to hear" and "I completely disagree, here's why."

Both are wins. Agreement means you articulated something the reader felt but couldn't name. Disagreement means you stated a position clearly enough to provoke a reaction. What you don't want is silence, or worse — "great post!" comments from people who didn't read past the hook.

Observable signal: profile views spike within 24 hours of posting. The reader saw the hook, clicked through to see who you are, and is now evaluating whether to connect or follow. That's the hook doing its job.

Another signal: you start getting DMs that quote your hook back to you. "I saw your post about LinkedIn being a workflow problem, not a brand problem — can we talk?" That's pipeline movement. The hook moved someone from passive consumer to active buyer.

The before-and-after I see most often: founder goes from 15 likes and zero comments to 80 likes and 12 comments, with 3 of those comments coming from people outside their immediate network. The absolute numbers don't matter. What matters is the ratio shift. More engagement from strangers means the hook is pulling people in who weren't already fans.

Also: you stop needing to write explanatory second sentences. Weak hooks require a second sentence to clarify what the first sentence meant. Strong hooks stand alone. If you can delete line two and the hook still makes sense, you wrote a good hook.

Revenue signal: your sales calls start with "I read your post about [specific hook], and I think we need that." The prospect is pre-sold because the hook diagnosed a problem they have. You're not selling anymore. You're prescribing. That's what a working hook does — it shifts the sales dynamic from "convince me" to "help me."
