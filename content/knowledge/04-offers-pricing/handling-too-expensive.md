## The core principle

"Too expensive" is almost never about the price.

It's about unclear ROI, mismatched positioning, or a decision-maker who wasn't actually qualified to buy in the first place.

## Why this matters

When a prospect says "too expensive," most founders immediately drop their price or add more features for free.

Both moves destroy positioning and train the market to negotiate harder next time.

The real cost shows up three months later when the pipeline is full of price shoppers who ghost after discovery calls.

The founder becomes known as "the person who negotiates," which means every conversation starts with "what's your best price?"

Revenue stalls because every deal requires custom pricing, margin compression, and manual justification of value that should have been obvious from the start.

The leverage of handling price objections correctly is this: you either close the deal at full price by reframing value, or you disqualify fast and move to the next qualified prospect.

Both outcomes save you from the worst case — spending three weeks chasing someone who was never going to pay.

## How it shows up in client work

The pattern I see most often: a founder builds a strong offer, prices it correctly, then caves the moment someone says "I need to think about it" or "that's more than I expected."

The cave happens because the founder doesn't have a script.

They panic, assume the price is wrong, and immediately offer a discount or a payment plan they hadn't planned to offer.

In [[Sales_call_2025_03_14]], a client closed a <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->5K engagement by reframing "too expensive" as "compared to what?"

The prospect said the price was high.

The client asked: "High compared to hiring internally, high compared to not solving this problem, or high compared to another vendor?"

The prospect paused and said: "Actually, hiring someone would be $80K a year minimum."

Deal closed two days later at full price.

Another client in [[Discovery_call_debrief_2024_11_18]] was selling a $6K workshop and kept hearing "we don't have budget."

I asked: "What's the outcome you're promising?"

She said: "Better team alignment."

That's not an outcome. That's a vague process improvement.

We rewrote the promise to "by end of the workshop, your leadership team will have a signed 90-day roadmap with one owner per initiative — or you don't pay."

Price objections dropped by 80% because the ROI was now concrete and time-bound.

In [[Positioning_intensive_2025_01_22]], a founder was charging $3K for a service that saved clients $50K annually in operational costs.

Every prospect said "too expensive."

I asked: "Do they know the $50K number before they hear your price?"

He said no.

We moved the ROI conversation to the discovery call, before pricing was ever mentioned.

He framed it as: "Most clients save between $40K and $60K in the first year. What's your current cost?"

When the prospect said "$55K," the $3K price became a 5% cost of the solution they were already paying for.

Objections disappeared.

In [[Sales_framework_build_2024_09_10]], a client was selling a <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->5K consulting engagement and kept getting "we need to run this by finance."

I asked: "Is finance on the first call?"

He said no.

That's the problem. If the person you're talking to can't approve <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->5K without consulting someone else, you're talking to the wrong person.

We built a pre-qualification question into his discovery form: "What's the approval threshold for external consulting spend?"

If the answer was below <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->5K, the prospect got routed to a different offer or disqualified entirely.

Price objections dropped because he stopped pitching to people who couldn't say yes.

In [[Client_debrief_2025_02_08]], a founder was selling a <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->2K annual retainer and heard "can we start with three months and see how it goes?"

That's not a price objection. That's a commitment objection disguised as a price test.

I coached him to say: "Most clients who start with three months don't renew because we don't have enough runway to show full ROI. The program is designed for 12 months. If you're not sure it'll work, that tells me we haven't aligned on the outcome yet. Let's back up."

Half the prospects dropped off. The other half signed 12-month deals at full price.

## Scripts and exact phrases

**When they say "too expensive":**

"Too expensive compared to what? Hiring internally, not solving this problem, or another vendor?"

**When they say "we don't have budget":**

"Got it. Walk me through what you were planning to spend and what that would solve. Because if it doesn't solve the problem, even a smaller number is wasted money."

**When they say "can we start smaller and scale up?":**

"Most clients who start small don't scale up because we don't have enough runway to show the full ROI. This program is designed for [timeframe]. If you're not sure it'll work, that tells me we haven't aligned on the outcome yet. Let's back up."

**When they say "I need to think about it":**

"Totally fair. Walk me through what's still unclear. Is it price, scope, fit, or timing?"

**When they say "I need to talk to my partner/boss/CFO":**

"No problem. What do they need to hear to say yes? Let's make sure you have everything you need to make that case."

**When they say "we were hoping for something cheaper":**

"Cheaper than what? What were you comparing this to?"

**When they say "that's more than we budgeted":**

"Got it. So it's not that [price] is expensive — it's that it's [difference] more than you planned. Let's figure out if the outcome is worth [difference] more than your original budget."

**When they say "we'll pay half now and half later":**

"I don't offer payment plans because it creates an incentive for me to drag the work out. You pay upfront, I deliver fast. If that doesn't work, we're probably not a fit."

## Common mistakes

**Mistake 1: Apologising for the price.**

"I know it's a lot, but..." immediately signals the price is negotiable.

Never defend the price. State it and wait.

**Mistake 2: Adding more features to justify the price.**

"And we'll also throw in a free audit and extra support hours."

This trains the prospect to ask for more free stuff next time.

If the original scope doesn't justify the price, the positioning is broken, not the price.

**Mistake 3: Offering a discount before they ask.**

"Normally it's <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->0K, but for you I can do $8K."

If you discount unprompted, the prospect assumes the real price is $6K and will negotiate down from there.

**Mistake 4: Blaming the market.**

"Yeah, I know it's tough out there, budgets are tight."

This validates their objection and makes it real.

The correct frame is: "Most of my clients close deals in markets like this because their buyers see the ROI clearly. If you're not seeing the ROI, we need to figure out why."

**Mistake 5: Talking faster and adding more detail when they push back on price.**

The founder panics and starts over-explaining.

The prospect reads this as desperation.

The correct move: pause, acknowledge, and ask a clarifying question.

**Mistake 6: Assuming "too expensive" means the price is actually too high.**

Most of the time, "too expensive" means "I don't understand why this is worth it" or "I'm not the decision-maker and I don't want to admit it."

## The contrarian read

Conventional sales wisdom says: "Handle objections with empathy, acknowledge their concern, and reframe the value."

That's not wrong, but it misses the deeper point.

If you're hearing "too expensive" on most calls, the problem isn't your objection-handling script.

The problem is your positioning, your ICP targeting, or your discovery process.

Here's the uncomfortable truth: a well-positioned offer to a qualified buyer almost never hears "too expensive."

They might hear "I need to think about it" (which is a commitment objection, not a price objection), or "I need to check with someone" (which is a decision-maker objection, not a price objection).

But if someone genuinely understands the ROI and is the right buyer, they don't say "too expensive."

They either say yes or they say no.

So when you hear "too expensive" repeatedly, the fix isn't to get better at handling the objection.

The fix is to rewrite the positioning so the objection doesn't come up in the first place.

That means: lead with the outcome and the ROI number, not the process.

Qualify harder upfront so you're only talking to decision-makers who can approve the spend.

And stop pitching to people who aren't in enough pain to pay.

## What it looks like when it's working

You quote the price, and the prospect either says "let's do it" or asks "what's next?"

No haggling. No "let me think about it." No "can we do a payment plan?"

When price objections do come up, they resolve in one or two questions because the ROI is already clear.

You're turning down prospects who can't meet the price instead of discounting to close them.

Your close rate stays above 50% for qualified discovery calls, and the deals that close stay closed (no refund requests, no scope creep demands for free).

Your average deal size increases year-over-year because you're known for holding the line on pricing, not caving.
