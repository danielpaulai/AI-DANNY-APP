## The core principle

You charge for outcomes, transformations, and working systems that the client owns and operates after you leave.

Not hours. Not deliverables. Not "content creation" or "LinkedIn management" or "AI consulting." You charge for the specific measurable result the client walks away with, and the independence to keep producing that result without you.

## Why this matters

Most founders price like employees disguised as business owners.

They sell time. They sell process. They sell "we'll handle your LinkedIn" or "we'll build you some AI workflows" and then trap the client in a retainer where the founder owns nothing and the dependency never ends.

The client pays forever. The founder stays hired. Nobody wins.

The cost of getting this wrong is a business model that doesn't scale and a positioning problem you can't talk your way out of. If you charge for hours, you compete with everyone else charging for hours. If you charge for "content," you compete with Fiverr and ChatGPT. If you charge for "AI strategy," you compete with every ex-consultant who just read a prompt engineering Twitter thread.

The leverage of getting it right is a category of one. When you charge for the outcome with a number and a timeframe, and the client owns the system that delivers it, you stop competing on price. You compete on conviction. The client either believes the outcome is worth the money or they don't. The sale happens in three sentences or it doesn't happen at all.

Founders who get this right close deals in one call. Founders who don't spend six weeks in email limbo explaining their process to people who will never buy.

## How it shows up in client work

The pattern shows up in every single onboarding call where the prospect asks "what's included?" and I have to reframe the entire conversation.

They want a list. They want hours. They want "10 posts a month" or "3 AI tools" or "weekly check-ins." They're buying inputs because that's what every other vendor trained them to buy.

I stop them and ask one question: "What result are you hiring me to produce?"

Most of them can't answer. They say "better LinkedIn presence" or "more efficient workflows" or "stronger personal brand." All symptoms. None of them outcomes.

So I keep asking. "Better LinkedIn presence that does what? Gets you what? By when?"

Eventually we land on something real. "I want 10 inbound sales calls a month from LinkedIn within 90 days." Now we're talking about an outcome. Now I can price it.

By the end of the conversation we'd repositioned his entire business around three clear tiers: intro program at <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->00, group workshop at <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->,000, high-ticket coaching above that. Each tier promised a specific transformation with a timeline. The pricing followed the promise. The promise came first.

In [[Sandeep_Weekly_Call_2025_11_05]], the client had written a "3-Day Authority Studio" offer at <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->,500. Beautiful deck. Completely undeliverable. I told him on the call: "It's impossible to deliver this in three days." We rebuilt the offer as a Client Attraction System with three tiers (Basic, Gold, Diamond), realistic 10-15 day timelines, and pricing tied to what actually ships: <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->,500 for content, +<!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->,000 for automation, +<!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->,000 for GoHighLevel funnel duplication. The client left with an offer he could actually sell and deliver.

The mistake almost everyone makes is starting with the price and reverse-engineering the deliverables. "I want to charge $5K, what can I throw in the stack to justify it?"

Wrong order.

Start with the outcome. "You'll have a working content system that compresses your weekly marketing from 12 hours to 2, and the system stays with you after I leave." Now ask: what's that worth? What does the client currently pay for 12 hours a week of their own time? What's the cost of not having that system for another six months?

That's the price. Then you work backward to figure out what needs to ship to deliver that outcome.

In [[100M offer examples for knowledge base - 4a68c8ca]], I studied Hormozi's offer stacking methodology across 10 different business models. The pattern is always the same. The offer leads with the outcome and a guarantee ("Double your take-home pay in 18 months or we refund our fee and pay you $50K for wasted time"). Then the stack exists to prove you can actually deliver that outcome. The stack is evidence. The price negotiates itself once the outcome is clear.

Every client who's stuck on pricing is stuck because they don't have a clear outcome yet. Fix the outcome first. The pricing conversation takes 30 seconds after that.

## Scripts and exact phrases

**When a prospect asks "What's included?"**
"Walk me through what result you're hiring me to produce. What do you need to have at the end that you don't have now?"

**When they say "I'm not sure about the price"**
"That's fair. Let me ask you this: what's it costing you right now to not have this system? What's six more months of doing it manually worth?"

**When they want to negotiate**
"I don't negotiate on the outcome. If you don't believe the result is worth the price, we shouldn't work together. But I can show you a smaller version of the same outcome if budget is tight."

**When they ask "How many hours does this take?"**
"I don't sell hours. I sell the result. Whether it takes me 10 hours or 100 hours, you're paying for the system that works, not the time I spent building it."

**When they ask "What if it doesn't work?"**
"Then I keep working until it does, or I refund you. The guarantee is tied to the outcome, not the process."

**When they compare you to a cheaper option**
"You're not comparing me to them. You're comparing the outcome I deliver to the outcome they deliver. If they can get you the same result, hire them. If they can't, the price difference doesn't matter."

**When they say "I need to think about it"**
"Totally fair. Walk me through what's still unclear. Is it the outcome, the timeline, the price, or the fit?"

**When writing the offer**
"You'll have [specific deliverable] that produces [specific outcome with number] within [specific timeframe], or [specific guarantee]."

## Common mistakes

**Mistake 1: Pricing by competitor comparison**

Founders look at what everyone else charges and price somewhere in the middle. This is backwards. Your price should reflect the outcome, not the market. If you can deliver a result worth $50K and everyone else charges $5K because they don't deliver that result, charge $50K. If you can't defend the outcome, lower the outcome—don't lower the price.

**Mistake 2: Stacking deliverables to justify the price**

"For <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->0K you get 20 posts, 10 DM templates, 5 email sequences, 3 lead magnets, and a partridge in a pear tree." This is how vendors think. Clients don't care about the stack. They care about the result. Lead with the result. Use the stack as proof you can deliver it.

**Mistake 3: Selling process instead of outcome**

"We start with a discovery call, then we do research, then we build your strategy, then we implement..." Nobody cares about your process. They care about what they get at the end. Skip the process. Name the result. If they ask how you do it, answer then.

**Mistake 4: Charging hourly or monthly without tying it to an outcome**

Retainers without outcomes are traps. "We'll manage your LinkedIn for <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->K a month" is a vendor relationship, not a transformation. The client pays forever and owns nothing. Flip it. "We'll build you a content system that runs your LinkedIn in two hours a week, and the system stays with you after we leave. <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->0K one-time." Now the client is buying an asset, not renting your labor.

**Mistake 5: Building the offer before the outcome is clear**

Most founders start designing the offer stack before they know what outcome they're selling. This produces Frankenstein offers that sound impressive but don't convert. Do the outcome first. Get the client to say "yes, that's exactly what I need" before you build anything.

**Mistake 6: Pricing too low because you're scared**

Founders underprice because they don't believe their own outcome yet. If you can't defend the price on a call without flinching, you don't believe the result is real. Fix your conviction before you fix your pricing. Test the outcome on one client. Prove it works. Then charge what it's worth.

## The contrarian read

Most pricing advice tells you to "know your worth" or "charge what you're worth." This is garbage advice.

You're not charging for your worth. You're charging for the client's outcome. Your worth is irrelevant. The client doesn't care how much you've studied or how hard you work or how many tools you've mastered. They care about the result.

The contrarian truth: price has nothing to do with you. Price is a function of the client's alternative. What does it cost them to keep doing it themselves? What does it cost them to hire someone else? What does it cost them to do nothing?

If doing it themselves costs 12 hours a week and their time is worth <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->00/hour, that's $96K a year. You're charging <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->0K to give them their time back. You're not expensive. You're 90% cheaper than the status quo.

If hiring someone else costs $5K a month on a retainer and they own nothing at the end, and you charge <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->5K one-time for a system they own forever, you're not more expensive. You're offering a better deal.

If doing nothing costs them six months of slow growth and they miss their funding round because their LinkedIn presence wasn't strong enough, what's that worth? Probably more than whatever you're charging.

The second contrarian truth: the best clients never negotiate. They either believe the outcome is worth the price or they don't. The clients who negotiate are the clients who don't believe the result is real yet. Don't convince them. Let them go. The sale happens in the first three minutes or it never happens.

The third contrarian truth: you can't charge premium prices for average outcomes. The outcome has to be non-negotiable. "You'll have this specific result by this specific date or I keep working for free" is a premium offer. "I'll try my best and we'll see what happens" is a $500 offer.

## What it looks like when it's working

You know the pricing is right when the prospect either says yes in the first 10 minutes or says no immediately. No "I need to think about it." No "Can you send me a proposal?" No six-week email thread.

You know the offer is right when you can explain it in three sentences and the client says "That's exactly what I need."

You know the outcome is clear when the client asks zero questions about process and only asks "When do we start?"

The numbers: clients close in one call. Average deal size is $5K-<!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->5K for a one-time system build. No retainers unless tied to a specific milestone outcome. Client owns the system and can run it without you after 90 days.

The before-after: before, founders spend three months in sales conversations and close 10% of prospects at <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->K average deal size. After, they close 40% of prospects in one call at <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->0K average deal size. The shift isn't better sales skills. The shift is better outcomes.

Observable signals: the prospect says "This is cheaper than I expected" even though you raised your prices. That's the signal. It means the outcome is so clear that the price feels like a bargain.

The wrong signal: the prospect says "That's expensive" and then asks for a discount. That's a red flag. It means they don't believe the outcome is real. Let them go.
