## The core principle

Price is a positioning statement, not a cost calculation.

Every number you charge tells the market who you are, what you believe your work is worth, and which clients you want to attract. The only way to know if that message landed is to test it, measure what happened, and adjust. Everything else is guessing.

## Why this matters

Most founders price by looking left and right at competitors, adding 10%, and hoping that feels premium enough. That approach optimises for safety, not for revenue. It trains you to justify your price instead of owning it.

The cost is invisible at first. You take on clients who negotiate, who question every line item, who ghost after the first call because the price "felt high" but you never learn why. Your calendar fills with discovery calls that go nowhere. Your close rate hovers at 20% and you blame your positioning, your copy, your funnel. But the real issue is the price. It's either too low and attracting people who can't afford what comes next, or it's unmoored from a real promise and the buyer doesn't see the value.

The leverage comes when you treat price as a variable you control and measure. You charge <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->K for a thing, track what happens, then charge $5K for the same thing with a clearer guarantee and see if the close rate changes. You learn that some price points attract hagglers, others attract serious buyers. You learn that adding a higher tier makes the mid-tier look reasonable. You stop guessing and start knowing.

When price becomes testable, your entire offer stack becomes testable. You find out what promises are worth paying for and what bonuses are just noise. You stop leaving money on the table because you were afraid to ask.

## How it shows up in client work

I've run dozens of pricing experiments across my own offers and client engagements. The pattern that shows up most often: founders underprice because they're scared of hearing no, then they overprice the wrong thing and wonder why nobody buys.

The first major test I ran was on workshop pricing in late 2023. I was charging €497 for a 90-minute AI content workshop. Close rate was around 40%. I raised it to €997 with no other changes. Close rate dropped to 25%, but revenue per session doubled. The math worked, but the real insight came from the no's. The people who said no at €997 weren't serious buyers. They wanted tips, not transformation. The people who said yes at €997 wanted a working system by end of session and they held me to it. That client quality difference was worth more than the revenue bump. [[Workshop_pricing_test_2023_11_15]]

The next test: I bundled three 90-minute workshops into a €2,497 package and added a 30-day Slack support window. Close rate went to 60%. Why? Because the package solved a different problem. Single workshops attract people who want to learn. Packages attract people who want to implement. The buyer intent shifted, so the price resistance disappeared. [[Three_workshop_bundle_test_2024_02_08]]

In early 2024 I tested a <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->4,000 done-with-you personal brand build for a client in cybersecurity consulting. The offer was: we build your voice DNA, your content system, your ICP brief, and five custom Claude Skills over 8 weeks. The client said yes in 20 minutes. I realised I'd underpriced. I ran the same offer at <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->5,000 for the next prospect. They also said yes. The lesson: when you're solving a <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->00K revenue problem, a <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->5K price is not the objection. Lack of belief in the outcome is the objection. ## Scripts and exact phrases

When a client asks "why does this cost $X?" I say: "Because that's what it costs to deliver the outcome we just agreed on. If the outcome isn't worth $X to you, we shouldn't work together."

When someone says "that's more than I expected," I say: "What were you expecting? And what outcome were you expecting for that price?" Most of the time they realise they were comparing my offer to something that doesn't deliver the same result.

When testing a new price, I say it with full confidence on the call: "The investment for this is $X. Does that work for your budget?" No hedging. No "I know it's a lot but..." If I don't believe the price, the client won't either.

When I raise prices between engagements, I don't announce it. I just quote the new price to the next prospect. If they ask why it's higher than what someone else paid, I say: "That was the price when I had capacity. Right now the demand is higher and the outcome I deliver is worth more based on the results I'm seeing."

When pricing tiers, I name them by outcome, not by feature count. The low tier is "DIY," the mid tier is "Guided Build," the high tier is "Done With You." Each name tells you what you're actually buying.

When someone asks "can you discount this?" I say: "No, but I can remove scope to bring the price down. What part of the outcome are you willing to give up?" That question forces them to see the price is tied to the result, not arbitrary.

When introducing a guarantee, I tie it to an observable outcome: "If we don't hit [specific number] by [specific date], we'll work another 30 days at no cost." That removes price objection because the risk is now on me.

When a client closes at a new higher price, I note exactly what I said, how I positioned it, and what objections they raised (if any). That becomes the script for the next pitch at that price.

## Common mistakes

Testing price without testing promise clarity. If your offer says "I'll help you grow your LinkedIn," a $500 price and a $5,000 price will both get rejected. The issue isn't the number, it's the vagueness. Fix the promise first. Make it specific, measurable, time-bound. Then test price.

Dropping price when the real issue is positioning. Founders hear "that's too expensive" and panic. They lower the price, close the next client, and think the problem is solved. But now they're working with a client who couldn't afford the original price, which usually means they can't afford the scope of work either. The engagement goes sideways. Better move: hold the price, reframe the outcome, close a better client.

Treating pricing as a one-time decision instead of a variable. You pick a number, build a landing page around it, and never touch it again. That's leaving money on the table. Price should be tested quarterly at minimum. As your skill improves, your results improve, and your price should track that.

Adding features to justify price instead of sharpening the outcome. Client says your <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->0K offer feels high. You panic and add three more bonuses, two more coaching calls, a Slack channel. The price stays <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->0K but now you're doing 30% more work. Wrong move. The client didn't object to the price because of missing features. They objected because they don't believe the outcome is worth <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->0K. Sharpen the guarantee or the promise. Don't pile on more deliverables.

Pricing based on hours instead of value. You calculate what your time is worth, multiply by estimated hours, add a margin. That's cost-plus pricing and it caps your upside. A <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->0K engagement that takes you 10 hours is better than a <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->0K engagement that takes you 40 hours. You should be pricing on the value of the outcome to the client, not the time it takes you to deliver it.

Using tiered pricing where the tiers are separated by features, not outcomes. Your low tier has 2 calls, your mid tier has 4 calls, your high tier has 6 calls. Nobody knows what to pick because the difference is arbitrary. Better: low tier is "DIY with a template," mid tier is "guided build with coaching," high tier is "done with you." Now each tier is solving for a different buyer intent.

Anchoring your price to what competitors charge instead of what you deliver. You see three competitors at $3K, so you price at $3,500 to look premium. But your offer is better, your guarantee is stronger, your results are faster. You should be at $7K. Competitive pricing only makes sense when you're selling the same commoditized thing everyone else is selling. If you're not, ignore them.

## The contrarian read

The most common pricing advice is "charge what you're worth." That's terrible advice. You're worth what the market will pay, and the market doesn't care about your feelings.

Better advice: charge what the outcome is worth to the buyer. If your offer solves a <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->00K revenue problem, charging $5K is underpriced. If your offer solves a $500 headache, charging $5K is overpriced. The value is not in your time, your expertise, or your effort. The value is in the result.

Another piece of standard advice: "test pricing slowly." Bullshit. If you're scared to double your price overnight, you don't believe in your offer. I've doubled workshop prices between one booking and the next and the sky didn't fall. The people who said no at the higher price were never serious buyers. The people who said yes were better clients. Fast tests teach you faster.

Third contrarian take: discounting to close a deal is not a strategy, it's a signal that your offer isn't tight. If you have to lower the price to get a yes, the buyer doesn't believe in the outcome. That's a positioning problem, not a pricing problem. Fix the promise or walk away. Discounting trains clients to negotiate and attracts people who will be difficult to work with.

Fourth: tiered pricing is overrated unless the tiers reflect real buyer intent. Most founders build three tiers because that's what SaaS companies do. But SaaS tiers work because each tier unlocks a different use case (solo user vs. team vs. enterprise). Service business tiers rarely map that cleanly. If your tiers are just "more calls" vs. "fewer calls," you're making the decision harder, not easier. One strong offer with a clear outcome beats three medium offers every time.

Fifth: the "charge more and work with fewer clients" advice assumes you have enough demand to be selective. Most founders don't. If your calendar isn't full at your current price, raising prices won't magically attract better clients. It will just give you an empty calendar. You need to fix your pipeline first, then raise prices.

## What it looks like when it's working

You quote a price and the client says yes within 48 hours, no negotiation. When that happens three engagements in a row, you're underpriced. Raise it.

Your discovery calls close at 40%+ and the no's are fast. The people who can't afford it or don't see the value self-select out in the first 10 minutes. You're not wasting time on 60-minute calls that go nowhere.

You test a new price tier or a new offer structure and you can measure the difference within two weeks. You're not guessing if it worked. You know because you tracked close rate, revenue per deal, and client quality.

Your highest-priced offer sells better than your lowest-priced offer. That's the signal that your positioning is tight and you're attracting buyers who want the outcome, not the discount.

You stop hearing "that's expensive" and start hearing "that's exactly what I need." When price objections disappear, it means your promise clarity is dialled in and the buyer believes you can deliver what you're selling.

You're booking discovery calls with people who already decided they want to work with you and the call is just confirming fit and logistics. When that happens, pricing becomes a non-issue because the decision was made before the call started.

The average contract value of new clients is growing quarter over quarter without you consciously trying to raise prices. That means your positioning, your results, and your reputation are compounding and the market is rewarding it.
