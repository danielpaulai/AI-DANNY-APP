## The core principle

Price is the last thing you decide, not the first.

The price negotiates itself once the outcome is clear, the guarantee removes the risk, and the buyer understands exactly what happens in week one.

## Why this matters

Most founders price based on what they think they can get away with, what competitors charge, or what feels safe.

This creates three catastrophic problems.

First, the price lands like a number instead of a decision. The buyer hears "<!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->5K" and has no frame for whether that's cheap or expensive because you haven't anchored it against anything real.

Second, you spend sales calls defending the price instead of reinforcing the outcome. Every objection becomes "that's too much" instead of "I'm not sure this solves my problem."

Third, you attract price shoppers instead of outcome buyers. The person who leads with "what's your price" is the person who will never see the value no matter how much proof you show them.

The cost of getting pricing wrong isn't losing one deal. It's training the market to see you as expensive, creating a pipeline full of unqualified leads, and spending every sales call justifying your worth instead of diagnosing their problem.

The leverage of getting it right is the opposite: buyers who say yes faster, sales calls that feel like strategy sessions instead of negotiations, and a reputation as the person who delivers results, not the person who charges the least.

## How it shows up in client work

The single most common pricing mistake I see is revealing the number before establishing the promise.

A client comes to me and says "I charge $5K for a LinkedIn personal brand package." I ask what the promise is and they say "I'll optimize their profile and write posts for them."

That's a deliverable, not a promise. A promise has a number, a timeframe, and an outcome the buyer can measure. "In 90 days you'll have a content system that books 5+ inbound calls a month, or we keep working until you do" — that's a promise.

Once the promise is clear, the price stops being a question. If I promise 5 inbound calls a month and your average deal size is <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->0K, the package is worth $50K a month in pipeline. Charging $5K for that is a joke. Charging <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->5K is cheap.

The second pattern is pricing without a guarantee that scares the seller.

I worked with a workshop facilitator who was charging <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->,999 for a 90-minute session with no refund policy. She was closing 1 in 10 pitches. I told her to add this guarantee: "If you don't walk out with a working asset you can use Monday morning, I refund your money on the spot before you leave the room."

She was terrified. "What if they ask for a refund?" I said, "Then you didn't deliver. You shouldn't keep their money."

She added the guarantee. Her close rate went to 6 in 10. Not because the workshop changed — because the guarantee removed the buyer's risk and transferred it to her, where it belonged. [[Creating compelling offers with strong promises and guarantees - 1b2a681a]]

The third pattern is pricing that doesn't anchor on the smallest commitment.

A founder told me his offer was "<!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->,999 pay-in-full or <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->,999 VIP." I asked what his payment plan was. He didn't have one. I said, "What's the weekly price?"

We broke it down: <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->,999 divided by 8 weeks is <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->49/week. That's the lead. "For less than what you'd spend on one dinner with a client, you get a complete AI content system that saves you 10 hours a week."

The anchor isn't the total price. It's the smallest true thing. [[AICEO offer optimization and member conversion strategy - 449aafe2]]

The fourth pattern is high-ticket sellers hiding the price before calls.

This one is tactical. Most high-ticket offers don't reveal pricing on the landing page because they want to force a conversation. The logic is: if prospects see <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->5K on the website, they'll self-disqualify before learning the value.

The problem with this logic is it attracts the wrong people. The person who books a discovery call without knowing the price range is either desperate, confused, or a tire-kicker. The qualified buyer wants to know if they're in the right ballpark before spending 30 minutes on a call.

The answer isn't to hide the price. It's to reveal it in context. "Investment starts at <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->5K" tells them the floor without giving them a reason to say no before the conversation. [[Ticket Pricing Strategies for High and Low-Ticket Items - a8b0534b]]

The fifth pattern is pricing that doesn't reflect speed.

A client told me she charges <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->0K for a 90-day engagement. I asked what the client gets in week one. She said, "A strategy document."

That's not a deliverable. That's homework. I told her to rebuild the offer so the client walks out of the first session with something they can use immediately — a content calendar, a voice DNA document, a working Claude Skill, anything tangible.

She rebuilt it. Week one now delivers a complete positioning brief and a 30-day content calendar. The price stayed at <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->0K but her close rate doubled because the buyer could see ROI in the first 7 days instead of waiting 90.

The sixth pattern is pricing consultants who charge by the hour.

I worked with a cybersecurity consultant who was billing $300/hour for transformation projects that took 40+ hours. His positioning was "I'll help you figure out what to do." The problem: his buyers couldn't budget for "figuring it out."

I told him to productize. Instead of "$300/hour," the offer became "$60K for a 30-day Security Revenue Diagnostic. You get a complete roadmap with prioritized initiatives, ROI projections, and a 90-day implementation plan. Fixed scope. Fixed price. Fixed timeline."

He closed his next three pitches at $60K+ because the buyer knew exactly what they were paying for and exactly what they'd get. [[Noman]]

The seventh pattern is founders who underprice because they're scared to lose the deal.

A founder told me he was charging $5K for a service that took him 60 hours to deliver. I did the math. $5K divided by 60 hours is $83/hour. He could make more working at McDonald's.

I asked why he priced it there. He said, "I'm afraid if I charge more, they'll say no."

I said, "They're already saying no. You're just saying no to yourself first."

We repriced the offer at <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->5K with the same scope. He lost the next two pitches but closed the third. Net result: he made the same revenue in one engagement that previously took three, and he had bandwidth to take on two more clients that month. [[Guide_Cohort_Meeting_2023_12_21]]

The third example is the reactivation pricing play.

A founder had a list of 50 past prospects who had said no to his <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->0K offer six months earlier. I told him to reactivate them with a new payment option: "I just added a <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->49/week entry point. Same program. Lower barrier."

She was terrified. "What if they abuse it?" I said, "Then you'll learn to qualify better on the front end."

She added it. Her close rate went from 30% to 60%. Not a single client asked for the extension. The guarantee removed the buyer's fear of wasting money, and that was worth more than the refund protection.

## Common mistakes

**Mistake 1: Leading with the price instead of the promise.**

The buyer hears "<!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->5K" and has no frame for whether that's cheap or expensive. The price should land after the outcome is clear, not before.

**Mistake 2: Pricing by the hour instead of by the outcome.**

$300/hour is a cost. "You'll have a complete roadmap in 30 days for $60K" is an investment. Buyers budget for investments. They resent costs.

**Mistake 3: Adding guarantees that protect the seller, not the buyer.**

"If you're not satisfied, we'll discuss a refund" is a weasel clause. A real guarantee names the outcome and what happens if it doesn't land.

**Mistake 4: Hiding the price before discovery calls.**

This attracts tire-kickers and wastes time on unqualified leads. Revealing "Investment starts at <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->5K" on the landing page filters the pipeline without giving qualified buyers a reason to self-disqualify.

**Mistake 5: Pricing pay-in-full only with no payment plan.**

Most buyers don't object to the total price — they object to the cash flow hit. Offering weekly or monthly payment options removes the objection without discounting the value.

**Mistake 6: Underpricing because you're scared to lose the deal.**

If you price at $5K because you're afraid <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->5K will scare them off, you're saying no to yourself before the buyer says anything. Price for the outcome. Let the buyer decide if it's worth it.

## The contrarian read

Conventional wisdom says you should price competitively — look at what others charge and slot yourself somewhere in the middle.

That's backwards.

Pricing competitively trains the market to see you as interchangeable. If your price is based on what competitors charge, you're admitting you offer the same thing they do.

The contrarian move is to price based on the outcome, not the market. If the outcome is "5 inbound calls a month" and the buyer's average deal size is <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->0K, the offer is worth $50K a month in pipeline. Charging <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->5K for that is cheap even if competitors charge $5K.

The second contrarian move is to make the guarantee scarier for you than the buyer.

Most guarantees are designed to protect the seller. "If you're not satisfied, we'll discuss it" gives the seller an out. A real guarantee names the outcome and what happens if it doesn't land: "If you don't see measurable ROI in 90 days, I'll work for free until you do."

That guarantee terrifies the seller. It should. If you're not confident you can deliver the outcome, you shouldn't be selling the offer.

The third contrarian move is to reveal the price early, not late.

High-ticket sellers hide the price because they're afraid of scaring off prospects before the value conversation. The problem: hiding the price attracts the wrong prospects. The person who books a call without knowing the price range is either desperate or a tire-kicker.

Revealing "Investment starts at <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->5K" on the landing page filters the pipeline. Qualified buyers know they're in the right ballpark. Unqualified buyers self-select out before wasting your time.

## What it looks like when it's working

When pricing is working, the buyer asks about payment options, not whether the price is justified.

The question shifts from "Is this worth <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->5K?" to "Can I pay weekly instead of upfront?"

That shift means the buyer already decided the outcome is worth the investment. They're just negotiating the payment structure, not the value.

The second signal is your close rate goes up without discounting.

If you're closing 3 in 10 pitches at $5K and you reprice the same offer at <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->5K with a stronger promise and guarantee, your close rate should stay flat or improve. If it drops, the problem isn't the price — it's the promise or the qualification.

The third signal is buyers who close faster.

When the promise is clear, the guarantee removes risk, and the price is anchored on the smallest commitment, most buyers decide in one call. If you're doing three-call closes on a <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->5K offer, something in the pricing architecture is unclear.

The fourth signal is you stop defending the price on sales calls.

When pricing is working, the conversation is about fit, not cost. You spend the call diagnosing whether the buyer has the problem your offer solves, not justifying why your price is fair.

The fifth signal is past no's convert when you change the payment structure.

If you reactivate old leads with a new weekly pricing option and 10-25% of them book calls, your original price wasn't the problem. The payment structure was. Most buyers don't object to the total investment — they object to the cash flow hit.
