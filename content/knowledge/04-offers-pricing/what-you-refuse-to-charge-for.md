## The core principle

You don't charge for time, theory, process, or deliverables that don't move the needle. You charge for outcomes the client can measure and bet their business on.

If the buyer can't point to the thing they paid for and say "this made me money" or "this saved me hours" or "this closed deals," you priced it wrong.

## Why this matters

Most service businesses are trapped in a model where the invoice reflects effort, not impact. The founder bills for hours, for deliverables, for "discovery sessions" and "strategy documents" and "brand guidelines" that sit in a folder and change nothing.

The client pays. The founder delivers. Nobody gets fired. But nothing moves.

This is the professional services death spiral. The founder optimizes for looking busy, not for creating leverage. The client optimizes for covering their ass with documentation, not for results. Both parties pretend the engagement was successful because all the boxes got checked.

The cost of this model is invisible until you see the alternative. Founders charging hourly hit a revenue ceiling at their personal capacity. Founders charging for deliverables compete on price with everyone else who can ship the same PDF. Founders charging for process become order-takers, not advisors.

The business stalls at <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->00K, $500K, maybe <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->M if you hire fast enough. But it never scales past the founder's hands because the business model is "sell my time in different packaging."

The leverage of getting it right is the difference between a consulting practice and a platform. When you charge for outcomes, the client's success and your revenue are finally aligned. You can increase your prices without working more hours because the value compounds. You attract better clients who want results, not reports.

## How it shows up in client work

The pattern repeats across every pricing conversation. The prospect asks "what's included?" and starts listing deliverables: "Do I get the LinkedIn profile rewrite? Do I get the content calendar? How many posts?"

This is the wrong question, but most founders answer it. They list everything they'll do, how long it takes, what format it ships in. They're negotiating on effort, not outcome.

I flip it. "Here's what you'll have 90 days from now: a content system that books 5+ inbound calls a month, run by you, owned by you, taught to you. If that doesn't happen, we keep working until it does. The deliverables are whatever it takes to get there."

In [[Sales_call_2024_11_14]], a founder asked if the <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->2K engagement included "a brand strategy document." I said no. He paused. Then I said, "You'll have a Voice DNA document Claude reads to write in your tone, an ICP brief every asset builds from, and 7 working Claude Skills that produce your content. Those aren't strategy documents. They're the system that replaces you doing 12 hours of content work every week. That's what you're paying for."

He signed.

The shift happened when he stopped evaluating the price against a list of PDFs and started evaluating it against 12 hours a week for the next 24 months. The math tilts instantly.

In [[Workshop_session_2025_02_08]], I ran a 90-minute AI workshop for 25 founders. Standard workshop pricing would be <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->00-$500 per seat based on "90 minutes of my time." I charged $997. Several people asked why so expensive for 90 minutes.

I told them: "You're not paying for 90 minutes. You're paying for the system you'll walk out with that replaces 6 hours of manual work every week. If the session doesn't ship that, I'll refund you and keep teaching until it does."

By the end of the session, 22 of 25 had a working Claude Skill running their first automation. Three didn't finish in the room, so I offered a 20-minute follow-up call. All three finished within 48 hours. Nobody asked for a refund.

The workshop model worked because the promise wasn't "learn about AI" or "understand automation." The promise was "leave with a working system." The price anchored on the value of 6 hours a week, not the cost of 90 minutes of teaching.

In [[Purely_Personal_retainer_conversation_2024_08_12]], a prospect wanted a monthly retainer for "ongoing LinkedIn content support." The budget was <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->K/month. I said no.

Not because the price was low. Because the model was wrong. Retainers optimise for recurring revenue, not results. The founder stays dependent. I stay trapped doing production work that should've been systematized in month one.

I rebuilt the offer: <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->5K for 90 days, done-with-you, teaching them to run the system themselves. No retainer. The engagement ends when they own the asset.

He hesitated. Then said, "But what if I need ongoing help after 90 days?"

I said, "If the system I build you still needs me after 90 days, I failed. You'll be autonomous or I keep working until you are."

That reframe closed the deal at a higher price with a cleaner exit.

The pattern across these examples: I refuse to charge for time, training, documents, or ongoing support. I charge for the outcome the client can measure, the system they own, and the hours they reclaim.

## Scripts and exact phrases

"You're not paying for my time. You're paying for the outcome. What you get is [specific measurable result in specific timeframe]. The deliverables are whatever it takes to get there."

"If I gave you 100 hours and you got no results, would that be a good deal? If I gave you 10 hours and you hit the goal, would that be a bad deal? You're buying the goal, not the hours."

"The engagement ends when you own the system. If you still need me after 90 days, I didn't do my job. I'll keep working until you're autonomous."

"I don't charge for strategy documents. I charge for the system that makes you money. The strategy is just the blueprint. The system is what you're paying for."

"You're not buying content. You're buying the 12 hours a week you get back when the content runs itself. That's the asset. The posts are just proof it's working."

"If this doesn't hit [specific number] within [specific timeframe], we keep working until it does. The price doesn't change. The outcome is guaranteed."

"I refuse to bill retainers because they optimise for my recurring revenue, not your results. You pay once. You own the asset. We're done."

"The price isn't negotiable because the outcome isn't negotiable. You either get [result] or you don't pay. That's the bet I'm making. Are you willing to make the same bet?"

## Common mistakes

**Charging for deliverables instead of outcomes.** The client asks what's included and you list PDFs, slide decks, training videos, strategy documents. You're negotiating on effort, not impact. The client compares your price to everyone else who ships the same list. You lose on price or you lose the deal.

Fix: name the outcome first. Then say the deliverables are "whatever it takes to hit that outcome." The client evaluates the price against the result, not the list.

**Billing hourly and calling it premium.** You raise your hourly rate from <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->50 to $500 and think you're charging for value. You're not. You're still selling time. The client still does math on how many hours they're buying. The business model still caps at your personal capacity.

Fix: stop billing hours entirely. Price the engagement on the outcome. The client pays for the result. How long it takes you is irrelevant.

**Offering "ongoing support" as a default.** You finish the engagement, the system is built, and you offer a $500/month retainer for "maintenance and updates." You think this is helpful. It's not. It keeps the client dependent and trains them to expect you to do work they should own.

Fix: build the system so they can run it without you. If they genuinely need ongoing help, that's a failure of the original build. Fix the build, don't sell them a retainer.

**Underpricing because the work was fast.** You finish in half the time you estimated and feel guilty charging the full price. You think the client will feel ripped off. They won't. They're paying for the outcome, not the hours. Charging less penalises speed and rewards inefficiency.

Fix: charge the agreed price. If you delivered the outcome faster than expected, that's proof of competence, not grounds for a discount.

**Giving "extra deliverables" to justify the price.** The client hesitates at your quote, so you throw in a bonus training session, an extra document, a free follow-up call. You think this tips the deal. It doesn't. It trains the client to negotiate for freebies and signals that your original price wasn't anchored on real value.

Fix: if the price feels high, reframe the outcome, not the deliverables. Remind them what they're actually paying for.

## The contrarian read

The standard advice in consulting and agency work is to "underpromise and overdeliver." Ship more than expected. Exceed the scope. Wow the client with free extras.

I reject this entirely.

Underpromising is dishonest. If you know you can deliver 10 but you promise 7 so you can "overdeliver" with 10, you're lying about your competence to manufacture surprise. The client doesn't feel wowed. They feel manipulated once they realize the game.

Overdelivering is just unpriced scope. You're doing work you didn't charge for, training the client to expect more than they paid for, and conditioning yourself to give away leverage for free.

The honest model: promise exactly what you'll deliver, charge for that outcome, and ship it. If you finish early or do it better than expected, that's proof you're good. It's not grounds for a refund or a freebie.

Another contrarian take: I refuse to charge for "discovery" or "strategy" as separate line items. Most agencies bill $3K-$5K for a discovery phase before the real work starts. The client pays for a 40-page deck that outlines what should be done. Then they pay again to actually do it.

This is double-billing. The discovery is part of the work. If the strategy doesn't result in execution, it's worthless. I charge once, for the full engagement, and the discovery happens in week one as part of the build.

The client doesn't pay for thinking. They pay for shipping.

## What it looks like when it's working

You close deals at higher prices than competitors because you're selling outcomes, not deliverables. The client doesn't compare your <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->5K to someone else's $8K because the framing is different. You're not selling the same thing.

You finish engagements early and the client thanks you for speed instead of asking for a discount. They understand they paid for the result, not the hours. Delivering faster proves competence.

You walk away from retainer requests without guilt. The client owns the system. They don't need you anymore. That's success, not failure. Your pipeline stays focused on new builds, not maintenance work.

You never hear "that's expensive" on sales calls. You hear "how soon can we start?" The client evaluates the price against the cost of not solving the problem. The ROI is obvious.

You don't negotiate on price. You negotiate on scope or timeline. The outcome is fixed. The price is fixed. The only variable is how fast you can ship it or which version of the outcome they want.

You ship case studies where the client names the result in numbers. "We went from 0 to 10 inbound calls in 90 days." "We reclaimed 20 hours a week in the first month." "We closed our first $60K deal using the system Daniel built." The outcome was measurable. The price was justified.
