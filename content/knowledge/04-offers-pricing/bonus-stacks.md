## The core principle

A bonus isn't extra value you throw in at the end. It's a strategic reframe that makes the price irrelevant.

The stack isn't about listing things. It's about positioning one offer against another in the buyer's mind so that saying no costs more than saying yes.

## Why this matters

Most founders price low because they're scared of the objection. Then they wonder why clients don't show up to calls, ghost after purchase, or treat the engagement like a commodity.

The mechanism is simple: when the buyer perceives low value, they assign low priority. When they perceive massive value, they protect the investment.

Bonus stacking solves three problems at once. First, it gives you room to discount without devaluing the core offer. Second, it lets you address objections before they're voiced by pre-loading solutions into the stack. Third, it creates urgency through conditional triggers (all buyers get X, but only pay-in-full buyers get Y).

The cost of getting this wrong is immediate. You close fewer deals because the offer feels thin. You attract price shoppers instead of outcome buyers. You leave money on the table because you never tested whether the market would pay 3x more for the same core deliverable wrapped in a stronger stack.

The leverage of getting it right is exponential. You close faster, at higher price points, with better clients who actually implement. The stack does the selling. You just present it and shut up.

## How it shows up in client work

Every founder I work with makes the same mistake on their first pass at an offer: they list what they deliver, add a price, and wonder why the close rate is 30%.

The pattern repeats. They come in with a $5K offer that includes "strategy session, weekly calls, access to me." No stack. No bonuses. No reframe.

I ask: what does the buyer walk out with on Day 1? Silence. What do they get if they pay in full today versus instalments? Silence. What's the thing you'd charge <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->K for separately but you're throwing in here to prove the offer works? Silence.

That's when we rebuild the offer as a stack. Not a list of services. A stack of outcomes, each with its own perceived value, its own name, its own reason for inclusion.

[[100M offer examples for knowledge base]] shows this in action across 10 industries. The pattern is always the same: 12-16 core components with specific deliverables and value justifications, plus 12-14 bonuses tiered by trigger condition. The value-to-price ratio ends up 13:1 to 19:1.

One client ran a <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->0K consulting engagement with three deliverables and no bonuses. Close rate was 25%. We rebuilt the stack: same three core deliverables, but now each one had a name, a dollar value, and a walk-out proof point. Then we added 8 bonuses: 4 for all buyers, 2 unlocked at milestone, 2 exclusive to pay-in-full. The offer went to <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->5K. Close rate jumped to 65%.

The psychology shift was instant. Before, buyers were evaluating whether the service was worth <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->0K. After, they were evaluating whether to lose access to $47K of perceived value by walking away.

Another pattern from [[Offer Creation]]: founders confuse bonuses with features. A feature is "weekly calls." A bonus is "Weekly Priority Access — you get a standing 30-minute slot every Tuesday where we handle your biggest blocker, no wait time. Value: <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->,200/month." Same thing, different frame. The second version sells.

The Hormozi framework I use with every client maps obstacles to bonuses. If the buyer's scared of wasting time, the bonus is "Done-for-You Setup in 48 Hours — we build your first system before the kickoff call so you walk in with a working asset." If they're scared of tech, the bonus is "Lifetime Tool Access — we maintain your Claude account, update your skills quarterly, and onboard your team at no extra cost."

[[Making sessions AI school friendly]] shows this at scale. When Danny rebuilt the April 2026 schedule for NAS AI School, every session title had to answer three questions: what is this about, what do I take away, what is the outcome. That's the same logic that drives bonus naming. "Bonus: Access to our Slack" is worthless. "Bonus: Priority Support Channel — post your question, get an answer from me or my team within 2 hours, Monday-Friday, for 90 days" is a stack item.

The contrarian move I teach: remove a bonus if you can't name its dollar value and justify why it's included. Weak bonuses dilute the stack. A 6-item stack where each item feels like it should cost <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->K is stronger than a 12-item stack where 6 items feel likefreebies.

One founder pitched a <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->5K engagement with 14 bonuses. I cut 8. The remaining 6 were renamed, dollar-valued, and reframed as obstacle-to-solution pairs. The offer went to $30K and closed faster than the original <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->5K version.

## Scripts and exact phrases

"This offer includes [core deliverable]. That's worth $X. But here's what makes this different: [list the stack]."

"Most people charge $X just for [one component of your stack]. You're getting that plus [five more things] for $Y."

"There are three tiers to this stack. Everyone gets [list baseline bonuses]. If you hit [milestone], you unlock [list milestone bonuses]. If you pay in full today, you get [list pay-in-full exclusives]."

"The total value here is $X. I'm pricing this at $Y because [reason tied to your positioning]. If we unbundled this, you'd pay $X just for [one component]."

"Here's what you walk out with on Day 1: [name the fast win]. That alone justifies the price. Everything else is leverage."

"Bonus 1: [Name] — [what it is], [what it solves], [dollar value]. Bonus 2: [repeat structure]."

"Some of these bonuses are worth more than the core offer. I'm including them because [strategic reason: proves the system works, removes a common blocker, sets you up for long-term success]."

"This is a pay-in-full exclusive. You won't see this on the instalment plan because [reason: I can only offer this to buyers who commit fully upfront]."

## Common mistakes

**Listing features instead of outcomes.** "You get weekly calls" isn't a bonus. "You get Weekly Priority Access — 30-minute standing slot, no wait time, <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->,200/month value" is a bonus. Name it. Value it. Frame it as a solution.

**Stacking weak bonuses that dilute the offer.** If you can't justify why the bonus is worth $500-plus, cut it. A 6-item stack where every item feels premium beats a 12-item stack where half the items feel like freebies.

**No dollar values.** If the bonus doesn't have a dollar value next to it, the buyer assigns zero value. Always name the price you'd charge for that bonus if sold separately.

**No trigger conditions.** All buyers get the same stack. Wrong. Tier the bonuses: baseline for everyone, milestone unlocks for progress, pay-in-full exclusives for commitment. This creates urgency and increases average order value.

**Bonuses that don't address objections.** Every bonus should pre-handle a fear. Scared of wasting time? Bonus: Done-for-You Setup in 48 Hours. Scared of tech? Bonus: Lifetime Tool Access and Team Onboarding. Scared of no results? Bonus: Performance Guarantee — we keep working until you hit [specific outcome].

**Adding bonuses after the buyer asks for a discount.** The stack is part of the offer, not a negotiation tactic. If you pull bonuses out mid-conversation, the buyer learns to haggle. Present the full stack upfront, then remove bonuses if they ask for a lower price. "I can do $8K instead of <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->0K, but we'd need to remove these three bonuses. You'd lose [specific outcomes]. Still make sense?"

## The contrarian read

Most pricing advice says "add value until the price feels small." I disagree. Add value until the buyer can't calculate ROI anymore.

When the stack is simple, the buyer does math. "<!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->0K for three calls and a PDF? That's $3,333 per call. Is one call worth $3,333?" They're evaluating the transaction.

When the stack is 12 components with 8 bonuses, each with its own dollar value and justification, the buyer stops doing math. The cognitive load is too high. They start evaluating the relationship. "Do I trust this person to deliver what they're promising?" That's a better question for you. Trust closes faster than math.

The second contrarian move: bonus stacks work better for high-ticket than low-ticket. At $997, a 6-item stack feels like overkill. At <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->0K, a 6-item stack feels like minimum viability. At $50K, a 12-item stack with milestone unlocks and pay-in-full exclusives is expected.

The third: you don't need to deliver all the bonuses at once. Some are Day 1. Some unlock at Week 4. Some are post-engagement. The stack isn't a delivery schedule. It's a value perception tool. As long as the buyer knows they're getting it, the timing doesn't matter.

## What it looks like when it's working

Close rate jumps from 30-40% to 60-70% without changing the core deliverable. The only thing that changed was how the offer was framed.

Buyers stop negotiating price. They start asking "when can we start" and "what do I need to do to unlock the pay-in-full bonuses."

You hear phrases like "this feels like a no-brainer" and "I'd be stupid to walk away from this." That's the stack working. The buyer can't see a world where saying no makes sense.

Average order value increases because more buyers choose pay-in-full to access the exclusives. A <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->0K offer with instalment options but <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->5K worth of pay-in-full bonuses converts 40% of buyers to full payment.

Refund requests drop. When the buyer perceives massive value, they show up to calls, implement the work, and protect the investment. Buyers who pay <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->K and get <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->K of value ghost. Buyers who pay <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->0K and perceive $40K of value become testimonials.

You can test price increases without losing deals. If the stack justifies <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->5K but you're charging <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->0K, you have $5K of room to test. Raise the price by <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->K, keep the stack the same, and measure close rate. If it holds, raise again. The stack absorbs the price increase.
