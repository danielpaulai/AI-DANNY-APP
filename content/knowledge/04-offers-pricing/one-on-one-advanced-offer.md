## The core principle

The advanced tier is not a better version of the standard offer. It's a different buyer self-selecting into a higher commitment level because they value speed, proximity, and implementation support over autonomy.

Most founders build pricing tiers by adding features. VIP gets three extra modules. Premium unlocks a bonus course. This creates a value ladder based on content volume, which trains the buyer to comparison-shop and negotiate down.

The advanced tier in Purely Personal's model is built on a different axis: implementation speed and direct access. Same curriculum. Same end result. Different path to get there. The buyer who chooses advanced is saying "I will pay more to compress the timeline, to have you in the room when I'm stuck, and to skip the trial-and-error phase."

This is not about extracting more money from the same buyer. It's about serving a different psychological profile: the operator who knows that speed has a dollar value and that unblocking one decision on a live call is worth more than ten hours of recorded lessons.

## Why this matters

When pricing is only about content volume, the founder traps themselves in a feature arms race. Every competitor can add another module, another bonus, another template. The buyer becomes trained to ask "what else do I get?" and the founder becomes trained to give more for less.

This collapses margins and commoditizes the offer. The buyer who would have paid $3,000 for proximity now pays <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->,999 for content, and the founder ends up serving twice as many clients at half the leverage.

The mechanism that makes advanced pricing work is **self-selection based on readiness and operating speed**. The buyer who chooses standard is often earlier-stage, more price-sensitive, or wants to move at their own pace. The buyer who chooses advanced is typically already profitable, already running lean, and views their own time as the constraint. They are not paying for more. They are paying to move faster and to de-risk implementation.

The cost of getting this wrong is serving everyone at the standard tier and wondering why margins are thin and why every onboarding call feels like it starts from zero. The founder ends up doing high-touch work at low-touch pricing because they never gave the ready buyer a way to pay for speed.

The leverage of getting it right is that the advanced buyer becomes the easiest client to serve. They show up prepared. They execute between sessions. They ask sharp questions. They close faster. They refer other advanced buyers. This creates a flywheel where the most profitable clients are also the lowest-friction clients.

## How it shows up in client work

The advanced tier became necessary after watching three months of 1:1 personal brand coaching sessions where half the clients were moving at double speed and asking for additional implementation support between calls, while the other half needed the full session just to finish the homework from the previous week. Both groups were paying the same price.

The fast-moving clients were consistently asking: "Can I book an extra call this week to review my hook library before I publish?" or "Can you look at my Voice DNA draft tonight so I can finalize it tomorrow?" These were not scope-creep requests. These were buyers signaling that speed had value to them and they were willing to pay for it.

The solution was to split the offer. Standard stayed as-is: bi-weekly calls, async Slack support, self-paced execution. Advanced added: weekly calls instead of bi-weekly, same-day Slack response SLA, one 1:1 setup call in the first week to pre-build the Voice DNA and ICP brief before the first group session, and a private Notion workspace with session recordings and assets delivered within 24 hours instead of end-of-week.

Same curriculum. Same end-state deliverables. Different intensity and proximity.

In [[Personal_brand_VIP_client_case_2024_09]] a founder running a <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->M consulting business joined at the advanced tier specifically because he needed his LinkedIn profile rebuilt in two weeks before a major conference where he was keynoting. Standard tier would have taken six weeks to get to profile optimization. Advanced tier meant we did the setup call on Monday, rebuilt the profile by Wednesday, and had the new hook library live by Friday. He paid <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->,000 more than standard and considered it cheap because the alternative was showing up to a 500-person audience with a profile that didn't reflect his expertise.

In [[Seven_day_breakthrough_pattern]] the advanced tier client who broke through on positioning did so because we held three live working sessions in one week instead of waiting two weeks between calls. The standard client would have spiraled for another ten days. The advanced client paid for proximity and got the breakthrough in seven days instead of thirty.

The pattern that repeated across multiple advanced clients: they already knew their business model worked. They were not testing. They were scaling. The constraint was execution speed, not figuring out what to do. Standard tier served the "I need to figure this out" buyer. Advanced tier served the "I know what to do, I need it done faster and I need you in the room when I hit a decision point" buyer.

Another signal: advanced clients almost never ghost between sessions. Standard clients sometimes disappear for two weeks and come back apologizing. Advanced clients show up to every call with the work done because they are paying for speed and they know delays cost them real money. The higher price creates higher commitment, which creates faster results, which justifies the higher price.

The advanced tier also became the pathway to doing fewer total clients at higher revenue. Serving 10 standard clients at <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->,999 each is <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->9,990 and requires 20 calls per month. Serving 6 advanced clients at <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->,999 each is <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->7,994 and requires 24 calls per month, but the calls are sharper, the execution is faster, and the referrals are stronger because the results are better.

## Common mistakes

Pricing advanced as "standard plus bonuses" instead of "standard at higher speed with higher proximity." The buyer comparison-shops and negotiates down. The founder ends up discounting or adding more to justify the price.

Not making the setup call mandatory for advanced. If the setup call is optional, the founder gives away the leverage without capturing the value. The setup call is what makes advanced worth the premium because it compresses the first three weeks into one week.

Offering advanced as "unlimited access" instead of "weekly calls and same-day Slack SLA." Unlimited sounds generous but creates scope creep and trains the client to over-rely on the founder. Weekly calls with same-day Slack is bounded, specific, and still feels premium.

Positioning advanced as "for bigger businesses" instead of "for faster timelines." The buyer self-selects based on company size, which correlates weakly with willingness to pay. The buyer who self-selects based on timeline urgency pays without negotiation because they understand the cost of delay.

Not splitting the tier until the founder is already doing high-touch work at low-touch pricing. The time to introduce advanced is when the founder notices they are giving some clients extra calls, faster turnarounds, or same-day reviews without charging for it. That is the signal that advanced should exist.

Offering too many tiers. Standard and advanced is clean. Standard, premium, and elite creates decision paralysis and makes every conversation a pricing negotiation instead of a commitment conversation.

## The contrarian read

Most pricing advice says to build a value ladder and move clients up the ladder over time. Start them at $500. Upsell to <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->,500. Upsell again to $3,000. This trains the buyer to expect that paying more means getting more and creates a never-ending feature arms race.

The contrarian position: price for commitment level at point of entry. The buyer who is ready to move fast and pay for proximity should pay that price on day one. The buyer who is not ready should start at standard and stay there. Upsells are a symptom of underpricing the ready buyer.

The other contrarian take: advanced should be the default recommendation in sales calls, not the upgrade. Most founders pitch standard and mention advanced as an option. This positions advanced as unnecessary. The better move: pitch advanced first, let the buyer self-select down to standard if speed is not a constraint. The buyer who chooses standard after hearing advanced knows they are choosing slower execution and cannot later complain about the pace.

The third contrarian position: advanced should have fewer total clients than standard, not more. If the founder fills advanced slots first and leaves standard slots open, advanced becomes the scarce tier and standard becomes the fallback. This inverts the typical pricing dynamic where premium is the upsell and standard is the default.

Advanced clients close in one call. Standard clients take two or three.

Advanced clients complete onboarding in one week. Standard clients take three.

Advanced clients publish their first post in seven days. Standard clients take 21.

Advanced clients refer other founders within 90 days. Standard clients refer occasionally or not at all.

Advanced revenue per client is <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->,999. Standard revenue per client is <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->,999. Advanced requires four fewer clients to hit the same monthly revenue target.

The founder's calendar shows 4 advanced clients and 6 standard clients instead of 10 standard clients. Total revenue is the same. Total delivery hours are fewer because advanced clients execute faster and need less hand-holding.

Slack messages from advanced clients are sharp, specific questions that take 60 seconds to answer. Slack messages from standard clients are open-ended "what should I do?" questions that take 10 minutes to unpack.

The founder's pipeline skews toward advanced buyers over time because advanced clients refer other advanced buyers. The business becomes more profitable without increasing pricing or reducing scope.

The most reliable signal: the founder stops feeling like they are giving away free high-touch support to low-touch clients because the high-touch clients are paying for high-touch and the low-touch clients are getting exactly what they paid for.
