## The core principle

Payment plans are a positioning tool disguised as a pricing mechanism, and the decision to offer one reveals everything about what you think your offer is worth and who you think can afford it.

## Why this matters

The payment plan question shows up in every high-ticket sales conversation, and how you handle it determines whether you're running a premium business or a volume business wearing a premium price tag.

When you offer payment plans by default, you're training the market to expect them. You're also absorbing all the risk — the client gets the full value upfront, you get paid over time, and if they ghost after month two, you've delivered <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->0K of work for $3K of cash.

The cost of getting this wrong compounds. Payment plans extend your cash conversion cycle. They create operational overhead tracking who paid what when. They attract buyers who are chronically underfunded and will blame you when the business doesn't print money fast enough to cover the installments. And most dangerous: they train you to pitch to the payment plan buyer instead of the full-fee buyer, which means you're optimizing for the wrong ICP.

The leverage of getting it right is immediate. Full-fee clients pay faster, implement harder, and ghost less. They self-select as serious. And you're building a business where cash comes in when value ships, not six months later when half the clients have stopped paying.

## How it shows up in client work

The payment plan question almost always surfaces in one of three moments: on the discovery call when price comes up, in the proposal follow-up when they're stalling, or as a negotiation tactic after you've closed them emotionally but before they've wired the money.

The client who asks "do you offer payment plans?" before you've even finished explaining the offer is telling you they're shopping on monthly budget, not outcome value. This is the buyer who's comparing you to a $500/month VA and wondering why you cost 10x more. You can close them, but they'll be hard to onboard, slow to implement, and the first to complain when results take work.

The client who says "this sounds perfect but I need to break it into three payments" after the call has already decided to buy. They're not negotiating price — they're negotiating cash flow. This is the buyer you CAN offer a plan to, but you charge for it. Taki Moore's rule: **if the value is there and they want it, they'll find the money.** If they can't find it, the real objection isn't the payment structure, it's the belief that your offer will deliver.

The pattern I've seen across 1:1 coaching clients is that founders who default to payment plans attract founders who default to payment plans. It's positioning contagion. If you're nervous about charging <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->0K upfront, your prospects smell it, and they'll push for $3K x 3 because they know you'll cave. [[Client_Catch-up_2024_11_06]] shows this dynamic clearly — Fahad's client asked for a payment plan not because she couldn't afford it, but because Fahad introduced it as an option before she even asked.

The Taki Moore framework from [[Creating compelling offers with strong promises and guarantees - 1b2a681a]] is explicit: payment plans should never be advertised upfront. They're a tool you deploy AFTER the client has committed emotionally and you've confirmed they're the right fit, and even then, you charge a premium for the flexibility. The standard Taki structure: offer two options during the close — pay in full and get a bonus (an extra strategy session, lifetime access, a speed-to-value deliverable), or split into 2-3 payments at a 10-15% markup.

The mistake most founders make is positioning the payment plan as the generous move. It's not. The generous move is delivering an outcome so clear that the client WANTS to pay in full because they know it's worth it.

## Scripts and exact phrases

When they ask on the discovery call: "We don't advertise payment plans because the clients who get the best results pay in full and implement immediately. That said, if the fit is right and you're committed, we can structure it. But let's confirm this is the right move first — walk me through what happens in your business if we DON'T work together."

When they ask after they've committed emotionally: "Here's what I can do. You can pay in full today and I'll throw in [specific bonus with dollar value], or we can split it into two payments 30 days apart, but the price goes to [10% higher]. Either way works, but the full-fee clients tend to move faster because they're all-in from day one."

When they say "I need to think about it" and you suspect it's a cash flow issue: "Totally fair. Just so I understand — is this a 'not sure if it's the right move' question, or a 'I want to do this but need to move some money around' question? Because those are two different conversations."

When they say "do you offer monthly payments?": "Not for this offer, no. The reason is simple: the value ships complete in session one. You walk out owning the full system. Financing something you get immediately doesn't make sense for either of us. That said, if cash flow is tight this month, we can push the start date 30 days and lock in your spot. Does that work better?"

When they ghost after asking about payment plans: (don't chase — send one final message) "Hey [name], circling back on this. If the payment structure is the only thing holding you back, let's get on a 10-minute call and figure it out. If it's something else, just let me know and I'll close the loop."

## Common mistakes

**Advertising payment plans on the sales page.** The moment you list "3 payments of $X" next to the price, you've told every buyer that's the expected path. Even the clients who WOULD pay in full will now choose the plan because you normalized it.

**Offering payment plans to unqualified leads.** If they haven't implemented your free content, if they're shopping on price, if they're asking "do you offer payment plans?" before they've asked "what's the outcome?" — they're not your client. A payment plan won't fix that. It'll just extend the pain.

**Not charging a premium for payment plans.** If paying in full and paying over time cost the same, you're incentivizing the behavior that hurts your cash flow. The 10-15% markup isn't a penalty — it's the cost of you carrying the risk.

**Using payment plans to close weak offers.** If you're pitching payment plans to make a <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->0K offer feel more affordable, the real problem is that the <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->0K offer doesn't have <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->0K of perceived value. Fix the offer, not the payment structure. [[Creating compelling offers with strong promises and guarantees - 1b2a681a]] makes this explicit: "Simple, small promises that are easy to believe and easy to keep."

**Treating payment plans as a negotiation tool.** The client who negotiates you down to a payment plan will negotiate you on everything else — scope, timeline, deliverables. You're setting a precedent. Stand firm on the structure unless they're otherwise a perfect fit.

**Not having a collections process.** The client who misses payment two will blame you for not reminding them. Build the follow-up system BEFORE you offer the plan, or don't offer the plan at all.

## The contrarian read

Most business advice says "remove friction" and "make it easy to say yes." Payment plans are the logical extension of that philosophy.

I'd argue the opposite: INCREASE friction for the wrong buyers.

The founders who build $500K+ businesses with premium offers are the ones who make it HARD to work with them. Not hard as in bureaucratic. Hard as in: you have to be serious, you have to be funded, you have to be ready to move. Payment plans are a signal that you're optimizing for volume, not value.

Alex Hormozi's model is extreme on this — "<!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->00M Offers" is built on the idea that the offer should be SO good that price becomes irrelevant. He doesn't talk about payment plans because when you're selling a clear outcome with a believable mechanism, the buyer finds the money.

The counter-argument is: "but Danny, some of my best clients needed a payment plan to get started, and they went on to become case studies." Fair. That's the 10%. The other 90% became operational drag.

The real contrarian play: charge MORE for payment plans, not less, and position them as the "I need extra time to get budget approved" option, not the "I can't afford this" option. That reframe alone filters the right 10% from the wrong 90%.

## What it looks like when it's working

You close 8 out of 10 deals at full fee. The two who ask for payment plans are funded founders who just want to spread the outlay across two months for accounting reasons, and you charge them 10% more with no guilt.

Your onboarding rate is 95%+. Clients who pay in full show up to session one with the pre-work done, the Loom recorded, the examples ready. Clients who negotiate a payment plan show up asking if they can push the start date.

Your cash conversion cycle is under 7 days. You close, they wire, you onboard. No 60-day lag waiting for the third installment to clear.

You stop hearing "I need to think about it" after revealing the price. When the offer is clear and the outcome is believable, the payment structure is a logistics question, not a decision point.

The signal it's NOT working: you're chasing late payments, clients are asking to pause mid-engagement, or you're running sales calls where the first question is "do you take payments?" instead of "what's the outcome?"
