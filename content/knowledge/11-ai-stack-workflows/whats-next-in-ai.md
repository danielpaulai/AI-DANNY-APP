## The core principle

I don't track AI to collect hot takes or flex cutting-edge knowledge for its own sake.

I track what matters for the work: tools that compress workflows, models that make AI actually sound human, and patterns that separate theatre from revenue.

## Why this matters

Most founders waste hours a week reading AI newsletters that tell them about features they'll never use.

They sign up for every new model release. They watch demo videos. They bookmark "Top 10 AI Tools" threads. And at the end of the month their marketing workflow is still manual, their content still sounds generic, and their pipeline is still empty.

The cost of chasing shiny objects is the business work that doesn't ship.

The opportunity cost is knowing what to ignore. Every hour spent testing a tool that doesn't move revenue is an hour not spent on the thing only you can do: selling, building relationships, holding the vision.

What I watch in AI is filtered through one question: does this compress time or improve outcomes for the founder running the business?

If it doesn't, I don't care how impressive the benchmark is.

## How it shows up in client work

When clients ask "should I be using X new tool," the answer is almost never yes on day one.

The pattern I see: founders read about a new model or tool, get excited, spend three hours testing it, realize their existing stack already does 90% of what they need, and then feel behind because they're not "keeping up."

I had a client in [[Noman]] work who wanted to test every new model the week it dropped. DeepSeek came out, he spent two days comparing outputs to Claude. Gemini 2.0 launched, another day gone. The actual work — building his consulting practice, booking discovery calls, shipping content — kept getting delayed.

The reframe: you're not paid to be an early adopter. You're paid to close deals and serve clients. Use the AI that already works and only switch when the new one solves a problem the old one can't.

Another client in [[Nuri Personal branding]] was tracking AI startups obsessively because he wanted to position himself as a thought leader in the space. He'd write posts about Lovable, Replica, every trending AI company. Good content. Zero leads.

The issue wasn't the topics. It was that he was writing **about** AI instead of **using** AI to ship the work that books calls. His audience didn't care what he knew about startups. They cared whether he could help them scale their marketing with AI.

The shift: stop writing about tools, start showing outcomes. "I used AI to cut a founder's content workflow from 12 hours to 2" beats "Here are 5 AI startups to watch" every time.

I track tools and models that land in three buckets: **workflow compression** (does it save meaningful time), **output quality** (does it make the work sound human and useful), and **revenue proximity** (does it move someone closer to a booked call).

Most new releases fail all three tests.

## Specific examples from the vault

The clearest example of tracking what matters versus chasing hype is the DeepSeek conversation in [[Comparing ChatGPT and DeepSeek Key Differences for Business - d471ccfe]].

A client sent me a LinkedIn post comparing ChatGPT and DeepSeek with the hook "Perhaps the Most Important thing you'll read in 2024."

Classic AI hype. Big claim, no specifics, no business outcome.

I rewrote it to focus on the actual decision a founder needs to make: cost, privacy, use case. The rewrite led with "$94.5M Cost Gap Reshaping AI Choice" and broke down when you'd pick one over the other based on your workflow, not based on which one has more parameters.

The pattern: most AI content is "look at this cool thing." Useful AI content is "here's when you'd use this and when you wouldn't."

Another example from [[AI writing detection words and phrases - b2fb04ad]]: a client asked for a list of words that immediately flag content as AI-written. The request came from a real problem — his posts were getting called out as generic, and he didn't know why.

I gave him 25 words (delve, tapestry, realm, leverage, robust, seamless, etc.) plus the sentence structures that scream AI: "In today's fast-paced world..." / "It's important to note that..." / "Let's dive deep into..."

But the bigger insight wasn't the word list. It was the principle: **AI tells happen when you skip the editing pass.** Most founders generate content, skim it, post it. They never read it out loud. They never ask "would I say this to a client on a call?"

That's the tracking that matters. Not "what's the newest model" but "what are the patterns that separate good AI output from slop, and how do I teach clients to spot them?"

In [[Practical Insights on Using AI for Writing - 3e6aa4da]], Tyler Cowen's approach to using AI is the model I track most closely: he uses AI as secondary literature. He's prepping for a podcast on British historians, so instead of buying 30 books, he interrogates Claude about Richard II and Henry V. He rereads Shakespeare and asks AI "what are the puzzles in chapter 2?"

The insight: **AI is better as a thinking partner than as a content generator.** Most people use it to write the thing. Tyler uses it to understand the thing, then writes it himself.

That's the shift I'm watching: founders who treat AI as a team (Content Strategist, Editor, Analyst, Prospector) versus founders who treat it as a magic box that spits out finished drafts.

In [[Building nasops transcript-based documentation and email generation app - 78e52862]], I'm tracking how transcript-to-documentation tools can compress ops work. The use case: a school company runs training sessions, and at the end of each session they need to send participants a summary, a worksheet, and implementation steps.

Most companies do this manually. Someone watches the recording, takes notes, writes the doc, formats it, sends it. Six hours of work per session.

The AI-native version: paste the transcript, the system generates the doc in Danny's (or the ops lead's) voice, includes the key points and action steps, and ships it within 20 minutes of the session ending.

That's the kind of tooling I track: not "cool demo" but "this replaces six hours of manual work with 20 minutes of review."

## Scripts and exact phrases

When a client asks "should I try X new tool," I ask:

"What problem are you solving that your current stack can't handle?"

If they can't answer that in one sentence, they don't need the new tool.

When a client says "I need to keep up with AI or I'll fall behind," I say:

"You're not paid to keep up with AI. You're paid to close deals. Use the AI that works and only switch when the old one breaks."

When a client is comparing models (ChatGPT vs Claude vs Gemini vs DeepSeek), I ask:

"Which one writes in your voice with the least editing? That's the one you use."

When a founder is posting about AI trends instead of using AI to ship work, I reframe:

"Stop writing about tools. Start showing outcomes. '12 hours to 2' beats '5 tools to watch' every time."

When someone asks what AI features to track, I say:

"Track three things: does it save time, does it sound human, does it move someone closer to booking a call. Everything else is noise."

## Common mistakes

The biggest mistake: **tracking everything.**

Founders subscribe to five AI newsletters, follow 20 AI accounts, read every release note, test every new model. They spend 10 hours a month learning about AI and zero hours shipping work.

The fix: pick one model (Claude or ChatGPT), one use case (content or sales or ops), and go deep. Ignore everything else for 90 days.

Second mistake: **chasing benchmarks instead of outcomes.**

A client will say "this new model scores higher on reasoning tasks" and I'll ask "did it write a better LinkedIn post than the one you're already using?" The answer is almost always no.

AI companies optimize for benchmarks. You should optimize for "does my client book a call after reading this."

Third mistake: **testing tools before defining the workflow.**

A founder will say "I'm trying this new AI writing tool" and I'll ask "what's your content workflow right now?" They don't have one. They're jumping to tools before they've defined what success looks like.

The sequence should be: **outcome → workflow → tool.** Not tool → hope it creates a workflow → maybe get an outcome.

Fourth mistake: **treating AI models like team members without training them.**

Most founders type a question into ChatGPT, get a generic answer, and assume that's all it can do. They never build a Voice DNA document. They never write an ICP brief. They never create custom instructions.

The models are as good as the context you give them. If you're getting generic output, you're giving them generic input.

Fifth mistake: **using AI to write instead of using AI to think.**

Tyler Cowen's insight again: most people use AI to generate the final draft. The better use is to ask it questions, use it as secondary literature, then write the thing yourself.

The output sounds human because a human wrote it. The AI just helped you think faster.

## The contrarian read

Most AI tracking content says "here's what's coming next, get ready."

I think that's backwards.

**The best AI strategy is to ignore 90% of releases and go deep on the 10% that solves your specific problem.**

Most founders don't need the newest model. They need to use the current model better. They don't need more tools. They need to build a workflow around the tools they have.

The AI hype cycle convinces you that you're falling behind if you're not testing every new release. That's a trap. You're falling behind if you're spending hours testing tools instead of booking calls.

Another contrarian take: **AI won't replace you, but founders who use AI well will replace founders who don't.**

The threat isn't the technology. The threat is the founder who figured out how to compress 12 hours of marketing work into 2 while you're still doing it manually.

That's the race. Not "who knows the most about AI" but "who ships the fastest with AI."

## What it looks like when it's working

You're using one or two models consistently instead of testing five.

You have a Voice DNA document and an ICP brief that every AI skill reads from.

Your content workflow went from 12 hours a week to 2, and the output quality stayed the same or got better.

You stopped reading AI newsletters and started shipping AI-assisted work.

Your LinkedIn posts score 3+ on the Invisibility Diagnostic without you having to think about it because the AI knows your voice.

You book the same number of calls (or more) while spending half the time on content.

You can explain your AI stack in two sentences: "I use Claude for content and sales, and I use X for Y." No overwhelming tool list, no chasing every new release.
