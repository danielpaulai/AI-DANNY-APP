## The core principle

You cannot build a Voice DNA document by asking someone to describe how they write.

You build it by recording them speaking naturally, then extracting the patterns they use without knowing they're using them.

## Why this matters

Every founder who asks for help with content says the same thing: "I want it to sound like me."

Then they sit down to write and it sounds like corporate LinkedIn.

The gap isn't effort. It's awareness.

People do not hear their own voice. They hear the performance version they think they should sound like.

This is why AI-generated content fails the smell test even when the founder writes the prompts themselves. They're feeding the AI their performed voice, not their real one.

A Voice DNA document solves this by holding up a mirror. It shows the founder exactly how they actually sound when they're not performing.

The cost of skipping this step is immediate. Content that doesn't pass the "would you say this on a real call" test gets ignored. Engagement drops. Inbound dries up. The founder posts three times, gets mediocre results, and stops.

The mechanism that makes Voice DNA work is specificity. Not "they speak casually." Not "they use simple language." The actual phrases. The actual sentence rhythm. The actual vocabulary they reach for when explaining something they care about.

When you capture that and feed it into Claude, the output changes. Posts that took 90 minutes to write now take 15. The founder reads it back and doesn't edit it. That's the signal.

## How it shows up in client work

The most common way this surfaces is during onboarding calls. [[Onboarding_call_Bastian_2026_03_13]] is the clearest example.

Bastian is a doctor turned health entrepreneur. Smart. Experienced. Clear on his positioning. But when I asked him to write a LinkedIn post about his offer, he froze.

Not because he didn't know what to say. Because he was trying to write it the way he thought LinkedIn posts should sound.

I recorded a 45-minute conversation instead. Didn't tell him it was for voice extraction. Just asked him to explain his business, his clients, his results.

He used "I mean" 47 times. "You know" 23 times. "Come on" as an expression of disbelief. "Not rocket science" three times. "Blah blah blah" twice.

Those aren't filler words. They're his rhythm.

When I fed that transcript into the Voice DNA extraction prompt, the output gave Claude everything it needed. Bastian's posts now sound exactly like him talking on a call. Zero edits needed.

The pattern repeats across every client. [[Voice_DNA_Noman_2024_11_18]] was similar. Noman speaks in long, run-on sentences when he's explaining something he's confident about. Short, choppy confirmations when he's checking for alignment.

His signature phrase is "Does that make sense?" Used 14 times in one conversation.

That phrase now appears in every piece of content his AI writes for him. Not because I told Claude to use it. Because the Voice DNA document taught Claude that Noman checks for understanding constantly.

The breakthrough moment in these sessions is always the same. The founder reads the Voice DNA document and says "This is weird. I didn't know I said that."

That's the point. You don't know. That's why we record it.

The clients who skip this step and go straight to "just write me a LinkedIn post" get generic AI output. The ones who do the voice extraction work first get content that books calls.

[[Challenge_Voice_DNA_Session_2026_02_24]] shows how this works at scale. During the 10x With AI Challenge, I built an entire session around voice extraction.

Participants installed Wispr Flow. Recorded themselves answering five questions. Fed the transcript into Claude. Built their Voice DNA in 90 minutes.

The five questions were deliberate:

1. How I explain what I do (forces natural positioning language)
2. The one thing I say over and over (captures conviction)
3. A specific win I talk about most (surfaces proof patterns)
4. How I describe my ideal client (reveals empathy language)
5. The belief I have that most people don't (uncovers contrarian positioning)

Those five questions produce 90% of the vocabulary, rhythm, and conviction markers that make someone sound like themselves.

The participants who followed the process shipped posts the same day that outperformed everything they'd written manually in the previous month.

The ones who skipped the recording step and tried to write the Voice DNA document from memory got mediocre results.

## Scripts and exact phrases

The five-question extraction script from [[Challenge_Voice_DNA_Session_2026_02_24]]:

"Record yourself answering these five questions as if you're explaining them to a friend over coffee. No script. No editing. Just talk."

**Question 1: How I explain what I do**

"You know how [describe the person's situation before they meet you]? Well, I help them [describe the transformation]. The way it works is [explain your mechanism in one sentence]."

**Question 2: The one thing I say over and over**

"If there's one belief or principle you repeat in every conversation with clients, what is it? Say it exactly how you'd say it to them."

**Question 3: A specific win or result I talk about most**

"Think of one client result you bring up constantly. Not your biggest win ever — the one you actually tell people about. Describe what happened."

**Question 4: How I describe my ideal client**

"Don't give me demographics. Tell me: what do you notice about the people you help? What frustrates you on their behalf? How would you describe them to a friend?"

**Question 5: The belief I have that most people don't**

"What do you believe about your industry, AI, business, or your work that most people think is wrong? Say it like you're defending it."

The master extraction prompt I use after recording:

"You are analyzing a transcript to build a Voice DNA document. Extract these patterns:

1. **Personal Word Bank** — list every distinctive word or phrase used more than once
2. **Sentence Rhythm** — note average sentence length, common openers, punctuation patterns
3. **Core Beliefs** — capture any strong conviction statements
4. **Energy Signature** — describe the overall tone (confident, questioning, direct, etc.)
5. **Story Triggers** — note any repeated examples or analogies
6. **Voice Formula** — write one paragraph describing how this person naturally speaks

Use only direct quotes as evidence. No inference."

The reframe I use when clients resist recording: "You're not trying to capture how you write. You're capturing how you think out loud. Those are different."

## Common mistakes

**Mistake 1: Writing the Voice DNA document instead of speaking it**

This shows up in every single onboarding call. The founder asks "Can I just write down my voice instead of recording?"

No.

Written voice is performed. Spoken voice is real. Claude needs the real one.

The tell is always the same. When someone writes their voice description, it's full of adjectives. "I'm conversational and approachable with a touch of humor."

That's useless. Claude can't operationalize "approachable."

When someone speaks for 30 minutes and I extract the patterns, I get: "Uses 'actually' to signal disagreement. Opens 40% of sentences with 'So.' Averages 18 words per sentence. Says 'Does that make sense?' after explaining anything complex."

That's usable.

**Mistake 2: Answering the five questions in writing**

I see this constantly. The founder treats the five questions like a form to fill out.

They write careful, edited answers. Submit them. Ask me to build the Voice DNA.

The output is generic because the input was performed.

The fix: force them to record. No exceptions.

**Mistake 3: Recording a monologue instead of a conversation**

Some founders record themselves giving a speech to the camera.

That's not natural voice. That's presentation voice.

The Voice DNA document needs conversational patterns. Filler words. Self-corrections. The messy stuff.

The best extractions come from coaching calls or peer conversations. Not solo recordings.

**Mistake 4: Skipping the vocabulary mining step**

The temptation is to read the transcript once and write a summary description.

That misses the specific words.

I use a literal word frequency tool first. Pull every word used more than three times. Then manually filter for the distinctive ones.

"Actually" isn't distinctive. "Not rocket science" is.

**Mistake 5: Building Voice DNA from LinkedIn posts instead of transcripts**

This one kills me. A founder sends me 20 of their best posts and says "Build my voice from these."

Those posts are already edited. Already filtered. Already generic.

Real voice lives in unedited speech. Not published content.

**Mistake 6: Adding aspirational language to the Voice DNA**

The founder reads the extraction and says "I wish I sounded more confident. Can you add that?"

No.

The Voice DNA documents reality, not aspiration. If you want to sound more confident, practice sounding more confident in real conversations first. Then re-record.

## The contrarian read

Most AI voice training focuses on style guides and tone documents.

"Write in a friendly, conversational tone. Use short sentences. Avoid jargon."

That's not voice. That's formatting rules.

Real voice is involuntary. It's the patterns you use without thinking.

The reason most founders fail at training AI to sound like them is they're trying to teach Claude what they think they sound like instead of what they actually sound like.

Voice DNA inverts this. It extracts first, describes second.

The other contrarian take: length matters.

Everyone wants to build a one-page voice guide. "Keep it simple so Claude reads it."

Wrong metric.

Claude can read 200,000 tokens of context. The constraint isn't length. It's specificity.

A 5,000-word Voice DNA document with 50 direct transcript quotes outperforms a 500-word generic style guide every time.

The final contrarian position: most people don't need Voice DNA for everything.

If you're writing internal documentation or process guides, generic voice is fine. Clear and functional beats on-brand.

Voice DNA is for external content that needs to build trust. LinkedIn posts. Sales emails. Landing pages. Anywhere the reader needs to feel like they're hearing from a real person.

## What it looks like when it's working

The signal is immediate. The founder reads the Claude output and doesn't edit it.

Not "I'd change one word." Not "Close but needs a tweak." Zero edits.

That's the standard.

When Voice DNA is working, content production time drops by 70-80%. A post that took 90 minutes takes 15.

The founder ships more. Engagement holds or climbs because the posts still sound human.

Inbound increases because people feel like they're reading something real, not AI slop.

The metric I track: how many posts does the founder publish without editing in the first two weeks after building Voice DNA?

Target: 8 out of 10.

If they're editing more than 20%, the Voice DNA document isn't specific enough. Re-record and extract again.

The other signal: clients comment "This sounds exactly like you."

That's proof the AI passed the Turing test for that specific founder's voice.
