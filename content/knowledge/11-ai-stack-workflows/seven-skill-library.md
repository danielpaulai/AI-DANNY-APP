## The core principle

Most founders think AI is a tool you open when you need help. One big chat box. One generic assistant. Type a question, get an answer, close the tab.

That's the problem.

AI isn't a tool. It's a team. And like any team, it only performs when each member has a specific role, a clear mandate, and the context to do their job without re-explaining everything daily.

The 7-skill Claude library is the operating system that replaces the chaos of "what should I ask Claude today" with a structured team of specialists who each own one part of your marketing operation.

## Why this matters

Every founder I work with starts in the same place. They've heard AI can help. They open Claude. They type "write me a LinkedIn post about leadership." They get something generic. They edit it for 20 minutes. They post it. Nobody books a call.

Then they try again the next week. Same process. Same mediocre output. Same wasted time.

The pattern I see across hundreds of conversations: the bottleneck isn't the AI's capability. It's the founder's mental load of constantly re-explaining who they are, who they serve, what they believe, and what they want the output to do.

When you treat Claude like a single tool, you're asking one underpaid junior marketer to do seven jobs simultaneously. Write the content. Edit the content. Design the carousel. Analyze last month's performance. Research prospect companies. Write the cold DM. Prep for the sales call.

That person would quit in a week. So would the AI.

The cost of getting this wrong is measurable. A founder spending 12 hours a week on marketing content who's using Claude as a single tool might compress that to 10 hours. Better than nothing. Not the transformation they were promised.

When we rebuild that same founder's workflow with a 7-skill team architecture, the 12 hours becomes 2. Same output quality. Often better. Because each specialist has the full context to do their ONE job excellently, and the jobs stack into a workflow that doesn't require the founder to be present for every step.

The leverage is in the specialization. One Content Strategist who owns your editorial calendar and never forgets what you published last month. One Writer who sounds exactly like you and doesn't need to be reminded every time. One Designer who knows your brand colors and never puts a gradient where it doesn't belong. One Editor who catches the AI tells before you post. One Analyst who tracks what's working without you asking. One Prospector who builds target lists. One DM Writer who turns those lists into conversations.

Seven specialists. One system. The founder shows up for strategy and review, not execution.

## How it shows up in client work

The pattern repeats across every client engagement. Founder opens the first call with some version of "I've been using ChatGPT but it's not giving me what I need." When I ask to see their setup, it's always the same thing. No custom instructions. No saved contexts. They're typing their entire business model into the prompt box every single time they want a post written.

One client in late 2024—SaaS founder, $800K ARR, selling project management software to construction companies—was spending 14 hours a week on LinkedIn content. He had ChatGPT Plus. He'd open it Monday morning, paste his offer into the chat, write a post about feature releases, edit for an hour, post it, and close the tab. Tuesday he'd do it again. Zero memory. Zero consistency. Zero pipeline impact.

We built him a 5-skill system in one 90-minute session. Not seven skills initially—we started with the essential five: Strategist, Writer, Editor, Designer, Analyst. By the end of the call he had five separate Claude projects, each with its own Voice DNA document, ICP brief, brand guidelines, and role-specific mandate.

The Strategist owned the calendar. Every Monday it would surface three content themes based on what performed last month, what the sales team was hearing on calls, and what prospects were asking in DMs. The Writer turned those themes into posts in his voice—first-person stories about jobsite problems, not SaaS feature lists. The Editor scored every draft on the Invisibility Diagnostic before it shipped. The Designer turned the best posts into carousels with his brand colors. The Analyst tracked which posts led to profile visits, which led to DMs, which led to calls.

Within 8 weeks his content workflow dropped to 2.5 hours a week. More importantly: he booked 6 inbound discovery calls directly from LinkedIn in the same period. Before the system, he'd had 1 inbound call in 6 months. [[Client_call_SaaS_founder_construction_2024_11_18]]

The breakthrough wasn't the technology. It was the specialization.

Another client—fractional CMO, $300K/year business, selling to B2B tech companies—had the opposite problem. She was great at strategy but terrible at execution. She'd plan a brilliant content calendar in Notion every month, then ship maybe 40% of it because the execution overwhelmed her. Writing took too long. Editing took too long. Turning text into visuals felt like learning Photoshop from scratch every time.

We built her a 7-skill system including the Prospector and DM Writer. The Strategist planned her calendar. The Writer drafted everything. The Editor tightened it. The Designer made carousels. The Prospector built lists of companies hiring CMOs or launching new products (her ICP signals). The DM Writer turned those lists into personalized outreach. The Analyst tracked what was working.

She went from 40% execution to 95% in the first month. The content quality improved because each skill had one job. The outreach happened consistently because the Prospector fed the DM Writer every week without her needing to think about it. She closed two clients in Q1 2025 directly from the outreach system. [[Client_call_fractional_CMO_2025_01_22]]

The pattern I see when this works: the founder stops doing execution and starts doing strategy. They show up Monday morning, review what the Strategist suggests, approve the themes, and walk away. The Writer drafts. The Editor scores. The Designer builds. By Wednesday the founder reviews the final assets, makes minor tweaks, and ships. Friday the Analyst sends a performance summary. The founder adjusts strategy based on data, not feelings.

The pattern I see when it doesn't work: the founder tries to use all seven skills in one conversation. They ping-pong between "write me a post" and "now edit it" and "now design a carousel" in the same chat thread. The context gets muddy. The outputs get generic. They give up and go back to doing it manually.

The system only works when each skill lives in its own project with its own context documents. Separation is the feature, not a bug.

## Specific examples from the vault

A client running a personal branding consultancy hit a wall in December 2024. She'd been posting consistently for 18 months. Good engagement. No pipeline. She asked me to audit her content strategy. I read 30 of her posts. They all sounded the same. Not because they were bad—they were well-written. But they had no POV. No edge. No asking.

We rebuilt her system with a Content Strategist who had one job: ensure every post passed a 3/4 on the Invisibility Diagnostic before it shipped. Voice, POV, Specificity, Asking. If a post scored below 3, the Strategist flagged it and explained why. The Writer redrafted until it passed.

First post we tested: "Personal branding isn't about being visible. It's about being remembered." Scored 1/4. Generic claim. No specifics. No ask. The Strategist flagged it. The Writer redrafted: "You posted 147 times last year. How many people messaged you to work together? If the answer is less than 10, your content isn't the problem. Your positioning is." Scored 4/4. Sharp POV. Specific number. Clear ask (rethink your positioning).

She shipped the new version. 47 comments. 8 DMs. 2 discovery calls booked within 72 hours. [[Client_call_personal_branding_consultant_2024_12_09]]

Another example from March 2025. A solo consultant selling AI implementation to mid-market companies was drowning in prospect research. He'd spend 3 hours every Monday building a list of 20 companies to reach out to, then another 2 hours on Tuesday writing personalized DMs. By Wednesday he was burned out and the outreach died.

We built him a Prospector skill and a DM Writer skill. The Prospector had clear instructions: find companies with 50-500 employees, recently hired a CTO or Head of Engineering, posted a job listing mentioning AI or automation in the last 60 days. The DM Writer had a template library: opener that referenced the specific job post, two-line credibility builder, one question about their AI roadmap, no pitch.

First week he ran it: Prospector returned 28 companies. DM Writer drafted 28 messages. He reviewed them in 30 minutes, adjusted 4, sent all 28. 9 replies. 3 calls booked. One closed for $45K in 90 days. [[Client_call_AI_consultant_2025_03_18]]

The Prospector didn't do anything a human couldn't do. It just did it in 15 minutes instead of 3 hours, and it never forgot the criteria. The DM Writer didn't write anything magical. It just applied the same proven template to every prospect without getting tired or cutting corners on message #23.

A client building a LinkedIn agency had the opposite problem. Too much content. She'd batch-write 20 posts every Sunday, schedule them in Buffer, and call it done. The posts performed okay. But they didn't convert because they weren't tied to anything. Random topics. Random hooks. No throughline.

We rebuilt her system with a Strategist and an Analyst working together. The Strategist planned content in 2-week sprints tied to specific business goals. Sprint 1: drive profile visits from agency owners. Sprint 2: drive DM conversations about content strategy. Sprint 3: drive discovery call bookings. The Analyst tracked which posts contributed to each goal and fed that data back to the Strategist.

After 6 weeks the pattern was clear. Posts about content strategy got likes but didn't book calls. Posts about pipeline problems booked calls but got fewer likes. She adjusted: 60% pipeline content, 40% content strategy content. Calls booked doubled. [[Client_call_LinkedIn_agency_2025_02_11]]

The Analyst didn't tell her anything shocking. It just made the invisible visible. Most founders have no idea which content is actually working because they're tracking vanity metrics (likes, comments) instead of business metrics (profile visits from ICP, DMs with buying intent, calls booked).

## Scripts and exact phrases

When introducing the 7-skill concept to a new client, I start with this frame:

"Right now you're asking Claude to be your entire marketing team in one conversation. That's like hiring one person to be your strategist, writer, editor, designer, analyst, and salesperson simultaneously. They'd quit in a week. So does the AI—it just stops being useful and you blame the tool."

When a client pushes back with "but I only need help with writing," I say:

"Writing is the last 20% of the job. The first 80% is knowing what to write, who it's for, whether it's any good, and what to do with it after you ship. If you're doing that 80% manually, AI is saving you 10 minutes and costing you 10 hours."

The setup prompt I give every client for their Content Strategist:

"You are the Content Strategist for [Name]. Your job is to plan what content ships, when, and why. You own the editorial calendar. You know what performed last month. You know what the sales team is hearing. You know the ICP's current pain points. Every week you suggest 3-5 content themes tied to business goals. You never suggest content for content's sake."

The setup prompt for the Writer:

"You are the Writer. You only write. You don't strategize, you don't edit, you don't design. The Strategist tells you what to write. You write it in [Name]'s voice using the Voice DNA document. One sentence per paragraph. No em dashes. No AI-slop phrases. You pass the draft to the Editor when it's done."

The setup prompt for the Editor:

"You are the Editor. You score every draft on the Invisibility Diagnostic: Voice, POV, Specificity, Asking. Each gets 0-4. If the total score is below 3, you reject the draft and explain why. If it passes, you tighten the copy and pass it to the Designer."

The exact question I ask when a client says their Writer "isn't sounding like them":

"Show me the Voice DNA document you're using. If you don't have one, that's the problem. The Writer can't sound like you if it doesn't know what you sound like."

The test I run when a client says their system "isn't working":

"Pull up the last 5 posts you shipped. Tell me which skill wrote each one, which skill edited each one, and what the Analyst said about performance. If you can't answer those questions, you don't have a system. You have a folder of documents you're not using."

## Common mistakes

The most common mistake is building the skills but not using them. Founder spends 90 minutes setting up seven Claude projects with full context documents, then goes back to typing into ChatGPT the next day because it's "faster." It's not faster. It feels faster because you're avoiding the discipline of working through a system.

Second most common: using multiple skills in one chat thread. Founder opens the Writer project and says "write me a post, now edit it, now turn it into a carousel, now tell me if it's any good." The Writer wasn't designed to edit or design or analyze. You just confused the specialist by asking them to do four jobs. Use the Writer to write, then hand the draft to the Editor in a separate project.

Third: not updating the context documents. Founder builds the system in January. Changes their ICP in March. Changes their offer in May. Never updates the Voice DNA or ICP brief the skills are reading from. By June the Writer is still writing for the old audience and the founder blames the tool.

Fourth: building skills they don't need. Founder builds a Designer skill, then realizes they never post carousels. Or builds a DM Writer skill when they're not doing outbound. Or builds an Analyst skill when they're not tracking anything. Start with the 3-5 skills you'll actually use weekly. Add more when the system is working.

Fifth: not reading what the skills output before shipping. Founder asks the Writer for a post, copies it straight to LinkedIn without reading it, then complains when it bombs. The Writer isn't perfect. The Editor exists for a reason. The system works when you review and approve at each handoff, not when you blindly trust it.

Sixth: treating the skills like magic. Founder thinks "I set this up, now content happens automatically." No. The system compresses 12 hours of work into 2, but you're still doing those 2 hours. Strategy still requires your brain. Review still requires your judgment. The skills are specialists, not replacements.

## The contrarian read

Most AI productivity advice tells you to "just use ChatGPT for everything" or "build one perfect prompt." Both are wrong.

The "use ChatGPT for everything" crowd treats AI like a search engine. Type a question, get an answer, move on. That works for one-off tasks. It doesn't work for recurring workflows where context matters. You can't build a content system on top of a tool that forgets who you are every time you close the tab.

The "one perfect prompt" crowd thinks if they just nail the 2,000-word super-prompt, all their problems disappear. I've seen these prompts. They're 3,000 words of instructions trying to teach Claude to be a strategist, writer, editor, designer, and analyst in one breath. The prompt works for exactly one use case, then breaks the moment the founder's needs shift.

The contrarian position: specialization beats generalization, even with AI. Seven focused skills outperform one mega-prompt every time. Not because the technology is different. Because the human workflow is clearer.

When you ask a single AI instance to do seven jobs, you're context-switching constantly. "Write this. Now edit it. Now analyze it. Now design it." The AI doesn't know which hat it's wearing at any moment. The outputs get confused. You get frustrated. You quit.

When you separate the jobs into seven specialists, each conversation has one purpose. The Strategist strategizes. The Writer writes. The Editor edits. No confusion. No context-switching. Clean handoffs. Predictable outputs.

The other contrarian take: most founders over-index on the Writer and ignore everything else. They think content marketing is about writing posts. It's not. It's about knowing what to write, writing it in a voice that converts, ensuring it's not generic garbage, making it visual, tracking what works, and turning attention into conversations.

Writing is 20% of the system. The other 80% is what the six other skills handle.

## What it looks like when it's working

You open your Content Strategist project every Monday morning. It surfaces three themes based on last month's data: what got engagement, what got DMs, what the sales team heard on calls. You pick one theme. You approve the angle. You close the project.

You open your Writer project. You paste the theme. The Writer drafts three posts in your voice. You read them. Two are good. One is generic. You tell the Writer why the third one failed. It redrafts. Now all three are good. You close the project.

You open your Editor project. You paste the three drafts. The Editor scores each one. Two score 4/4. One scores 3/4 (good voice and POV, weak on asking). You adjust the CTA on the weak one. Now all three score 4/4. You close the project.

You open your Designer project. You paste the best post. The Designer turns it into a three-slide carousel in your brand colors. You approve it. You close the project.

You ship the post. You ship the carousel. You move on to the actual CEO work. Wednesday you check LinkedIn. The post has 23 comments. 5 profile visits from ICP companies. 2 DMs. You reply to the DMs using the DM Writer. One books a call.

Friday your Analyst project sends you the week summary. Post A: 890 impressions, 47 engagements, 3 profile visits, 1 DM. Post B: 1,240 impressions, 62 engagements, 8 profile visits, 3 DMs, 1 call booked. Post C: 670 impressions, 31 engagements, 2 profile visits, 0 DMs. Pattern: posts about customer problems outperform posts about product features 2:1.

You adjust next week's strategy based on the data. You don't guess. You don't go by feelings. You let the Analyst tell you what's working.

The whole workflow takes 2 hours. Content ships consistently. Quality stays high. Pipeline moves. You're not drowning. You're not heroically grinding through 14-hour content days. You're running a system.
