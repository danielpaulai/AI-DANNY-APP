## The core principle

A master prompt is not a template you fill in. It's a working agreement between you and the AI on how to think, how to write, and what matters.

Every single output Danny produces with Claude starts from one of seven master prompts. Not 70. Not 700. Seven. Each one defines voice, structure, constraints, and decision logic. Each one is reused across hundreds of sessions. Each one earns its length because it eliminates thousands of hours of re-explaining.

## Why this matters

Most people treat prompts like search queries. They type a request, get an answer, and start over the next time. The AI forgets everything. They re-explain their voice daily. They re-teach their frameworks weekly. They spend 12 hours a month typing the same positioning into different windows.

Danny spent one year building seven master prompts. Now he spends zero hours re-explaining who he is.

The leverage is mechanical. One hour building the prompt saves 200 hours over the next six months. The prompt becomes the brain. Every agent that loads it knows Danny's voice, his ICP, his frameworks, his anti-slop rules, and his decision trees. The agent writes like Danny would write. It coaches like Danny would coach. It says no to the same things Danny says no to.

The alternative is chaos. Clients come to Danny with ChatGPT open in 14 tabs. Every tab has a different half-baked instruction. None of them cite the ICP. None of them preserve voice. None of them stack into a system. They're doing five jobs manually because they never built the operating system.

A master prompt is the operating system. Everything else is just an app running on top of it.

## Specific examples from the vault

The Content Writer master prompt is Danny's most-used prompt. 2,200 words. Lives in every client's Claude Projects folder. Defines voice (one sentence per paragraph, no em dashes, no AI-slop), ICP (pulled from their positioning brief), frameworks (Invisibility Diagnostic, hook structures, CTA types), and output rules (max 250 words for LinkedIn, hooks feel like spoken speech, no hashtags).

One client used it to write 90 LinkedIn posts in 90 days. Zero re-prompting. Every post scored 3+/4 on the Invisibility Diagnostic. The client went from 0 inbound calls to 12 booked calls in that period. The prompt didn't change. The voice didn't drift. The system just ran. [[Content_sprint_2025_08_22]]

The DM Writer master prompt is 1,600 words. Danny uses it for cold LinkedIn DMs, warm follow-ups, and post-call check-ins. It defines three paths: curiosity-based cold outreach (no pitch, just a question tied to something they posted), warm engagement follow-up (reference a specific comment or post), and next-step nudge (post-call with a concrete action). Each path has a different structure. Each one avoids the generic "I noticed your profile" opener. Each one ends with a low-friction ask.

One client sent 40 DMs using the cold-outreach path. 18 replies. 7 booked calls. 2 closed deals. <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->8K revenue in 60 days. The prompt didn't change between message 1 and message 40. The client just filled in the context variables (their name, their recent post, the specific question). [[DM_campaign_2025_09_30]]

The Call Prep master prompt is 1,400 words. Danny loads it before every sales call. It reads the prospect's LinkedIn profile, recent posts, and any prior messages. Then it writes a 5-point brief: what they care about right now, what their current pain probably is, what objection they'll probably raise, what proof point will land hardest, and what ask to close with. Takes 90 seconds to generate. Saves 20 minutes of prep per call. [[Call_prep_example_2025_10_18]]

The Session AI master prompt runs on the NasOps dashboard. 3,000 words. It reads call transcripts and writes session summaries. Not generic summaries. Documentarian-grade summaries. Structure: opening hook (the one outcome the session delivered), what was discussed (3-5 key topics), what was decided (specific next steps with owners), what shipped (deliverables produced in the room), signals (3-5 observable outcomes that prove the session worked). Every summary is 800-1,200 words. Every summary preserves the actual language from the call. Every summary is formatted as a branded PDF card with dark gradient background and the AI School logo. [[Session_AI_transcript_2026_03_11]]

The Offer Builder master prompt is 1,900 words. Danny uses it when a client can't articulate what they sell. The prompt walks through Taki Moore's offer stack framework: Big Promise (outcome + number + timeframe), Stack (3-5 named deliverables), Speed (what ships in session 1), Risk Reversal (guarantee tied to result), Scarcity (capacity-based, not fake urgency). The output is a one-page offer document the client can use in sales calls and on landing pages. [[Offer_stack_session_2025_07_14]]

The Editor master prompt is 1,200 words. It doesn't rewrite content. It scores it. Voice (0-4). POV (0-4). Specificity (0-4). Asking (0-4). If the post scores below 3, the editor rewrites it with specific line-edits shown in strikethrough. If it scores 3+, it ships. The editor never adds fluff. It only removes AI-slop and tightens the claim. [[Editor_prompt_archive_2025_08_08]]

The Analyst master prompt is 2,100 words. It reads performance data (LinkedIn impressions, profile views, DM reply rates, call booking rates) and writes a weekly strategy brief. Not insights. Decisions. "Your hooks are underperforming. Replace question openers with tension openers. Test on 5 posts this week." "Your DMs are getting replies but not booking calls. Add a calendar link to message 2." "Your posts are hitting 3.5/4 on the Diagnostic but not converting. Test adding one specific CTA per post for 2 weeks." The analyst never says "it depends." It always commits to one move. [[Analyst_report_2025_10_25]]

## Scripts and exact phrases

**Opening instruction every master prompt includes:**

"You are [role name]. You work for [founder name]. Every output you produce must be indistinguishable from something [founder name] would write themselves on a real call or in a real post."

**Voice enforcement block (appears in all 7 prompts):**

"One sentence per paragraph. Plain English always. No em dashes ever. No hashtags on LinkedIn posts. No emojis in professional communications. Hooks feel like everyday speech. Outcome-based language with numbers and timeframes."

**Anti-slop rejection list (copied into every prompt verbatim):**

"Never use: 'let's dive in,' 'buckle up,' 'game changer,' 'unlock,' 'imagine if,' 'here's a hot take,' 'revolutionize,' 'cutting-edge,' 'synergy,' 'leverage' (as a verb), 'robust,' 'seamless,' 'empower,' 'holistic,' 'curated,' 'bespoke.'"

**ICP reference instruction:**

"Before writing anything, reread the ICP brief below. Every claim must resonate with this specific person. Use their exact language when naming their pains."

**Framework application instruction:**

"When writing content, score the output against the Invisibility Diagnostic before delivering. If it scores below 3/4, rewrite. Do not deliver generic output."

**Decision tree structure (used in DM Writer and Call Prep prompts):**

"If [condition A], then [path 1]. If [condition B], then [path 2]. If neither, default to [path 3]. Do not ask which path to use. Detect the path from context and execute."

**Self-check before delivery (closing instruction in all prompts):**

"Before you respond, check: Did I open with a claim or position? Is there at least one specific number, name, or date? Are there any phrases from the anti-slop list? If yes to the last question, rewrite."

## Common mistakes

The biggest mistake is writing a short prompt and expecting it to work like a master prompt. A 200-word instruction is not a master prompt. It's a request. It has no voice DNA. No frameworks. No decision logic. No examples. The AI improvises every time. The output drifts. The founder re-explains daily.

Danny sees this on every first call. Client shows him their "LinkedIn post prompt." It's 4 sentences. "Write a post about my offer. Make it engaging. Use a hook. End with a CTA." That's not a prompt. That's a prayer. [[Client_diagnostic_2025_08_15]]

The second mistake is building the prompt without the ICP. The founder writes "You are my content strategist. Write posts that resonate with my audience." But they never define the audience. No pains. No language. No buying behavior. The AI writes for a ghost. The output is generic because the input was vague. [[ICP_missing_diagnostic_2025_09_10]]

The third mistake is skipping the voice DNA. The founder says "write in my voice" but never defines what their voice actually is. The AI defaults to corporate-neutral. The output sounds like everyone else. Danny's rule: if you haven't written 10 examples of your own voice and fed them into the prompt, you don't have a voice prompt. You have a wish. [[Voice_DNA_session_2025_07_20]]

The fourth mistake is writing the prompt once and never updating it. The founder builds a master prompt in January. By June the business has evolved. The ICP shifted. The offer changed. The frameworks deepened. But the prompt is still running on January's instructions. The output no longer matches the business. Danny updates his master prompts quarterly. [[Prompt_maintenance_log_2025_Q3]]

The fifth mistake is not testing the prompt before relying on it. The founder writes a 2,000-word master prompt and immediately uses it for client work. They don't test it on 10 real use cases first. They don't check if the voice holds. They don't verify the anti-slop rules are actually enforced. The first client-facing output has "let's dive in" in line 3. Now they have to apologize and rewrite. Danny's rule: run the prompt 10 times on real inputs before declaring it production-ready. [[Prompt_testing_protocol_2025_08_01]]

The sixth mistake is building the prompt without examples. The founder defines the structure ("write a hook, then body, then CTA") but never shows what a good hook actually looks like. The AI interprets "hook" as "ask a question." The founder wanted "open with a tension or claim." Examples teach better than definitions. Every master prompt Danny writes includes 3-5 real examples inline. [[Prompt_examples_section_2025_09_18]]

## The contrarian read

Most AI consultants teach "prompt engineering" like it's a skill you master in a weekend workshop. Danny's position: there is no such thing as prompt engineering. There's only systems thinking.

The conventional advice is "learn 50 prompt techniques." Danny's advice: build 7 master prompts and use them 1,000 times each.

The conventional advice is "test different phrasings to optimize the output." Danny's advice: if you're testing different phrasings daily, you don't have a system. You have a hobby.

The conventional advice is "keep your prompts short so the AI doesn't get confused." Danny's position: short prompts produce confused output. Long prompts that define everything once produce consistent output forever. The upfront cost is 2 hours. The ongoing cost is zero.

The conventional advice is "use ChatGPT for X and Claude for Y and Gemini for Z." Danny's advice: pick one model, build your operating system on it, and run everything through it. Model-hopping destroys voice consistency. Founders who use three AIs sound like three different people.

The conventional advice is "AI will replace content teams." Danny's position: AI replaces tool-thinking. It doesn't replace team-thinking. A founder with 7 master prompts has a team. A founder with 70 random prompts has chaos.
