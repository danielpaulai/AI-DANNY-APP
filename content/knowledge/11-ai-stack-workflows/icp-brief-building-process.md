## The core principle

The ICP brief is the foundation document that every other asset in the client's AI system reads from.

Without it, Claude writes generic content that could be for anyone. With it, Claude writes content that sounds like it was pulled from a real sales call with the exact person you're trying to reach.

## Why this matters

Most founders skip this step because they think they already know their customer.

They say "I work with coaches" or "I help B2B founders scale." That's positioning, not an ICP. Positioning tells the world what you do. An ICP tells your AI who to talk to and how to talk to them.

The cost of skipping this: every piece of content your AI produces will be one degree off. The hooks won't land. The pain points will be generic. The language will sound like marketing copy instead of a conversation. You'll post consistently and get crickets.

The leverage of getting it right: once the ICP brief exists, every Claude Skill in the system reads from it. Your Content Writer knows who it's writing for. Your Prospector knows who to target. Your DM Writer knows what language closes. One document, infinite applications.

The other thing most founders miss: the ICP brief isn't a PDF you write once and file away. It's a living document that gets sharper every time you close a client, lose a client, or run a discovery call. The best ICP briefs I've seen are on version 8 or 9, not version 1.

## How it shows up in client work

I've built this document with 40+ clients. The pattern is always the same: founders who think they know their ICP can't answer the four questions that matter.

**The four questions:**
1. What exact language does your ICP use when describing their problem?
2. What do they fear more than the problem itself?
3. What have they already tried that didn't work?
4. What outcome would make them look like a hero inside their organization?

The first time I ask these, most founders give me positioning answers. "They want to scale their business." "They need better systems." "They're looking for growth."

That's not ICP language. That's founder language.

The real answer sounds like this: "I'm doing everything manually and I can't keep up." "My pipeline goes quiet every time I stop posting." "I know I need to be on LinkedIn but I don't have 12 hours a week for content."

Those are real lines from [[Voice_capture_session_2024_11_18]] with a $5M consulting founder. That language went directly into her ICP brief. Her Content Writer now opens hooks with "Your pipeline goes quiet every time you stop posting" because that's the exact phrase her ICP uses.

**The stuck-on-positioning trap:**

At least half the clients I work with are stuck here. They can't write the ICP brief because they haven't committed to one customer. They're still trying to serve "founders" or "consultants" or "coaches."

The move: force them to pick one ICP for 90 days. Not forever. Just for the next quarter. "Who's the ONE person you can help faster than anyone else? Write the ICP for them. Everything else parks."

This happened with a client in [[Sales_discovery_2025_02_12]]. He'd been posting generic founder content for six months. Zero calls booked. We picked one ICP (solo consultants making <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->00K–$500K who were drowning in delivery work) and rebuilt the entire brief in one session. Within three weeks he had five inbound calls. All five matched the ICP.

**The common mistake: writing for who you WANT vs who you SERVE:**

I see this in every workshop. Founders write an ICP brief for their dream client (Fortune 500 CMOs, Series B founders, people with $50K budgets) when their actual closed deals are all solo operators making <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->50K–$300K.

The ICP brief should describe the person who's actually buying from you today, not the person you hope will buy from you next year. If you want to shift upmarket, fine — but write a second ICP brief and test it separately. Don't mix them.

From [[Workshop_debrief_2024_09_15]]: a client wrote an ICP targeting enterprise buyers with procurement cycles. When I asked how many enterprise deals he'd closed, the answer was zero. All his revenue came from solo founders who bought on one call. I had him rewrite the brief for the founder segment. His content immediately became 10x more specific.

**The four-layer ICP structure I use:**

Every ICP brief I build has four layers, in this order:

1. **Demographics** (industry, revenue, company size, geography, role)
2. **Psychographics** (fears, frustrations, goals, what they've tried, why it didn't work)
3. **Exact language** (the 10–15 phrases they actually use when describing the problem)
4. **Outcome vignette** (a 3-sentence story of what their life looks like 90 days after working with you)

The demographics layer is table stakes. Everyone does that. The magic is in layers 3 and 4.

Layer 3 — exact language — is where Claude learns to sound human. If your ICP says "I'm doing five jobs manually" instead of "I need better systems," Claude needs to know that. The brief should have a dedicated section titled **Exact Language** with 10–15 real phrases pulled from sales calls, discovery sessions, or client feedback.

Layer 4 — the outcome vignette — is what your Content Writer uses to open hooks. "You used to spend 12 hours a week on content. Now you spend 2." That's a vignette. It's concrete, it's measurable, and it's the kind of line that makes your ICP stop scrolling.

**The voice capture shortcut:**

The fastest way to extract exact language is a 20-minute voice capture session. I do this in [[Voice_capture_session_2024_11_18]] and [[Voice_DNA_build_2025_01_22]].

I ask the founder: "Tell me about the last three clients you closed. What exact problem did they come to you with? What language did they use?"

Then I transcribe the call and pull every client-quoted phrase into the ICP brief. This takes 20 minutes and produces better language than three hours of the founder staring at a blank page trying to "write their ICP."

**The objection-rebuttal section:**

Every ICP brief should include a section titled **Top 10 Objections** with the exact objection and a one-line rebuttal.

Example from [[ICP_brief_2025_03_14]]:
- Objection: "I don't have time for this."
- Rebuttal: "This system saves you 10 hours a week. You're trading 2 hours of setup for 520 hours back this year."

This section feeds directly into your DM Writer and your sales scripts. When a prospect says "I need to think about it," your AI already has the rebuttal loaded.

**The workshop version:**

In workshops, I run this as a 60-minute working session. Founders fill out a template with these exact fields:
- Who they are (role, company size, revenue)
- What they're afraid of (3 fears)
- What they've tried (2–3 failed solutions)
- What success looks like (specific outcome with a number)
- Exact language (10 phrases)

By end of session, everyone has a first-draft ICP brief. It's not perfect, but it's specific enough to feed into a Content Writer that same day.

From [[Workshop_feedback_2024_10_08]]: one founder said the ICP exercise was "the first time I actually understood who I'm talking to." He'd been in business for three years.

## Scripts and exact phrases

**ICP capture opening:**
"Tell me about the last three clients you closed. What exact problem did they come to you with? What language did they use?"

**The positioning redirect:**
"That's what you do. I'm asking who you do it for. Give me one specific person. Role, revenue, problem."

**The language extraction question:**
"If I recorded your last sales call and played back the first five minutes, what exact words would your client use to describe their problem?"

**The vignette prompt:**
"It's 90 days after they start working with you. What does their life look like? Give me three sentences with numbers."

**The objection-rebuttal structure:**
"What are the three reasons people say no to you? For each one, give me the one-line rebuttal that closes them."

**The commitment test:**
"Pick one ICP for this quarter. Just one. Not forever. Which one can you help faster than anyone else?"

**The workshop instruction:**
"You have 20 minutes. Fill out these six fields: Who they are. What they fear. What they've tried. What success looks like. Ten phrases they use. One outcome vignette. Go."

**The Claude Skill handoff:**
"Every Skill in your system now reads from this ICP brief. Your Content Writer knows who it's writing for. Your Prospector knows who to target. Your DM Writer knows what language closes. One document feeds everything."

## Common mistakes

**Mistake 1: Writing positioning instead of ICP.**

"I help founders scale" is positioning. "Solo SaaS founders, <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->00K–$300K ARR, bootstrapped, spending 60 hours a week in delivery with no marketing system" is ICP.

Most founders give me positioning on the first pass. I make them rewrite it with specifics: industry, revenue, company size, exact problem, exact language.

From [[Workshop_debrief_2024_09_15]]: a founder's first draft said "I work with entrepreneurs who want to grow." I made him rewrite it: "Solo consultants, <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->00K–$500K revenue, 90% of time in delivery, pipeline empty because they're too busy to market."

**Mistake 2: Targeting who you WANT instead of who BUYS.**

The ICP brief should describe the person who's buying from you today, not the person you hope will buy from you in two years.

If you want to shift upmarket, fine. Write a second ICP brief and test it. Don't mix the dream customer with the real customer in the same document.

**Mistake 3: Using founder language instead of customer language.**

"They need better systems" is founder language. "I'm doing everything manually and I can't keep up" is customer language.

The ICP brief should have a dedicated section titled **Exact Language** with 10–15 real phrases. If you can't quote your customer, you don't know them well enough yet.

**Mistake 4: Writing the ICP in a vacuum.**

The best ICP briefs are extracted from real calls, not invented in a Google Doc. Run a voice capture session. Record three sales calls. Transcribe them. Pull the language.

From [[Voice_capture_session_2024_11_18]]: a founder spent two hours trying to write her ICP brief from scratch. We scrapped it. I ran a 20-minute voice session asking about her last three clients. We extracted 15 phrases. The new brief was 10x sharper.

**Mistake 5: Writing one ICP when you serve three.**

If you serve multiple customer segments, write multiple ICP briefs. Don't try to collapse them into one generic document.

Your Content Writer can't write for "founders, consultants, and coaches" at the same time. Pick one ICP per content stream. Run them separately.

**Mistake 6: Never updating the brief.**

The ICP brief isn't a PDF you write once and forget. It's a living document. Every time you close a client, lose a client, or run a discovery call, the brief should get sharper.

The best ICP briefs I've seen are on version 8 or 9. The worst are on version 1 from two years ago.

## The contrarian read

Most LinkedIn gurus tell you to "niche down." That's not wrong, but it's incomplete.

Niching down is positioning. "I work with SaaS founders" or "I help coaches scale" or "I serve solo consultants." That's one layer.

The ICP brief is the layer underneath. It's not just WHO you serve. It's what they're afraid of, what they've tried, what language they use, and what outcome would make them look like a hero.

Most founders stop at positioning and wonder why their content doesn't convert. The answer: positioning tells the world what you do. ICP tells your AI how to talk to the one person who's ready to buy.

The other contrarian take: you don't need to serve one ICP forever. You need to serve one ICP per 90-day sprint.

If you're testing two customer segments, fine. Write two ICP briefs. Run two content streams. Test them both for 90 days. Pick the one that books more calls. Double down.

But don't try to serve both segments with the same Content Writer reading from the same ICP brief. That's how you end up with generic content that resonates with no one.

## What it looks like when it's working

**Signal 1: Your content sounds like a real conversation.**

When the ICP brief is dialed in, your hooks sound like something you'd say on a sales call, not something a Content Writer invented.

Test: read your last five posts out loud. Would you say those exact lines to a paying client on a call? If no, the ICP brief needs work.

**Signal 2: Your DMs fill up with people who match the ICP.**

When the brief is sharp, the wrong people stop reaching out. The right people start.

From [[Sales_discovery_2025_02_12]]: after rewriting the ICP brief, a founder's DMs went from random tire-kickers to five qualified leads in three weeks. All five matched the exact ICP.

**Signal 3: Your sales calls close faster.**

When your ICP brief feeds into your sales script, discovery calls feel like continuation of a conversation that started on LinkedIn, not a cold pitch.

From [[Sales_call_2025_03_14]]: a founder closed a $5K deal in 22 minutes because the prospect said "You already know my problem. I read your posts."

**Signal 4: Your Content Writer produces hooks you'd actually send to a client.**

When the ICP brief is right, your Content Writer doesn't need heavy editing. You read the draft and think "I'd send this privately to a client."

That's the bar. If you wouldn't DM the post to one specific person from your ICP, the brief needs work.

**Signal 5: Your Claude Skills sound like you, not like ChatGPT.**

The ICP brief is the foundation. Every other Skill reads from it. When it's sharp, your Content Writer, Prospector, DM Writer, and Profile Optimizer all sound like they've been working with you for months.

When it's generic, they all sound like AI.
