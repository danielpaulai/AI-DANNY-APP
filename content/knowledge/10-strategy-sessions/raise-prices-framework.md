## The core principle

If you are afraid to say the price, the problem is not the number. The problem is the promise.

When a founder hesitates to raise prices, they are always solving the wrong problem. They think they need to justify the increase. They think they need better sales skills. They think they need more proof. None of that is true. What they need is a promise that makes the price irrelevant.

Price is a signal of value. If the value is unclear, any price feels high. If the value is specific and desirable and easy to believe, the price conversation becomes a formality.

## Why this matters

Most founders are trapped in a race to the bottom they did not choose. They set their price based on what they think the market will tolerate, then spend the rest of their business justifying why they are worth it. That is backwards.

The cost of underpricing is not just money. It is positioning. When you charge less than the value you create, the market reads you as less capable. Clients treat you as replaceable. You attract buyers who negotiate hard and pay slowly and demand revisions. You build a pipeline full of people who can barely afford you, which means every conversation feels like convincing them you are worth the risk.

Underpricing also kills margin. When margin is thin, you cannot invest in the assets that would make your business stronger. You cannot hire the team. You cannot take the time to build the systems. You stay trapped in delivery mode because you need every client just to stay afloat.

The leverage of getting pricing right is existential. A founder who raises prices and holds the line earns back their positioning, their calendar, and their confidence. They start attracting clients who can afford to pay and who expect results instead of excuses. They build a business with margin. Margin buys options. Options buy freedom.

## How it shows up in client work

The most common pattern is the founder who knows they are undercharging but cannot bring themselves to change the number. They have been charging $3K for a service that takes 40 hours and they know it should be $8K. But they are terrified that if they raise it, nobody will buy.

The conversation always starts the same way. They ask me how to justify the increase to their existing clients. I tell them we are not justifying anything. We are rebuilding the promise.

In one session with a LinkedIn ghostwriter charging <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->,500 a month, I asked what the client was actually buying. She said "four posts a week, one carousel, two newsletters." I said that is not what they are buying. Nobody wakes up wanting four posts. They want pipeline. So what is the real promise?

She hesitated. Then she said "more inbound leads." I asked how many. She said "I don't know, it depends." I said that is why you cannot raise prices. You are selling inputs, not outcomes. Inputs have a market rate. Outcomes command a premium. [[Sales_call_2024_08_19]]

We rebuilt the offer. The new promise was "10 qualified inbound DMs a month or I write until you get them." The price went to $4,500. She closed three clients in two weeks. The objection rate did not change. The quality of buyer did.

Another client was a brand strategist charging $5K for a positioning package. She wanted to go to <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->2K but was stuck. I asked her what the client was left with after the engagement. She said "a positioning document, a messaging house, and a brand story." I said nobody wants documents. What do they actually want?

She said "clarity." I said that is still too vague. Clarity about what? She thought for a minute. Then she said "they want to know what to say when someone asks what they do, and they want it to land." Bingo.

We restructured the deliverable. Instead of three documents, she now delivered one outcome: a 60-second positioning script that the founder could say on a sales call and have the prospect lean in. That was the promise. The <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->2K price became a non-issue. [[Positioning_call_2024_11_03]]

The pattern repeats. Founders get stuck on price because they are selling the wrong thing. They are selling their time, their process, their deliverables. None of those things justify a premium. The only thing that justifies a premium is a result the buyer cannot get anywhere else.

A workshop client was charging $997 for a 90-minute AI training. He wanted to charge <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->,997 but felt like he needed to add more content. I told him content is free. Every founder can watch a YouTube video. What they cannot do is walk out of a 90-minute session with a working system they can use on Monday.

We rebuilt the workshop around that promise. Instead of teaching AI tools, he built their first automated workflow live in the room. By the end of the session, they had a working email drafter, a working content repurposer, or a working client onboarding sequence. The workshop went to <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->,997. Nobody balked. [[Workshop_pricing_2025_01_20]]

The other common pattern is the founder who raises prices but immediately caves when the prospect hesitates. They panic and offer a discount. Or they offer to split the price across more deliverables. Or they apologize and say "I can work with you on that."

That is a confidence problem, not a pricing problem. If you do not believe the price, the prospect will not either.

I ran a live pricing workshop where a consultant said she wanted to charge <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->5K for a three-month engagement but kept getting "that is too expensive." I asked her to pitch me. She said "It is <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->5K for three months of consulting, but we can structure it however works for you."

I stopped her. The moment you say "we can structure it however works for you," you have told the prospect the price is negotiable. That is a signal. Signals matter more than words.

I had her redo it. This time she said "<!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->5K. Three months. Here is what ships in month one." No hedge. No apology. No offer to customize. The prospect either buys or they do not. That is the frame. [[Pricing_objections_workshop_2025_02_10]]

The third pattern is the founder who raises prices and then tries to justify them with more deliverables. They add bonuses. They add extra calls. They add a bonus module. That is the wrong move. More deliverables do not make a higher price feel justified. A stronger promise does.

A client was selling a <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->0K consulting package and wanted to go to <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->0K. She asked if she should add more check-ins. I said no. Adding more inputs does not double the value. Doubling the outcome does.

We did not add anything. We changed the promise from "I will help you build your marketing strategy" to "You will have five qualified sales calls booked in the first 30 days or I keep working until you do." The price doubled. The deliverables stayed the same. The objection rate dropped. [[Offer_rebuild_2024_09_15]]

## Specific examples from the vault

The clearest example is a founder who came to me charging <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->,500 for a personal branding package. She wanted to raise it to $5K but could not close anyone at that price. She kept getting "I need to think about it."

I asked her to walk me through the last sales call. She said "I explained the deliverables, showed them examples of past client work, and then told them the price. They said it was more than they expected."

I said the problem is not the price. The problem is the order. You are selling the deliverables before you have sold the outcome. That is backwards.

I had her redo the call structure. Open with the outcome. "In 90 days you will have a LinkedIn profile that books three inbound calls a month. That is the promise." Then ask if that outcome is worth $5K to them. If yes, then show the deliverables. If no, the call is over.

She ran the next call that way. The prospect said yes before she even explained what was included. She closed at $5K. [[Sales_structure_2024_07_22]]

Another example from a workshop. A founder was selling a $3K offer and wanted to go to $8K. He asked if he should add a bonus. I said no. Instead, I had him cut a deliverable.

He was selling five one-hour strategy sessions plus a written plan. I told him to drop the plan. Nobody wants a plan. They want the first action taken. So instead of five sessions and a plan, he sold three sessions and one implemented system. The price went to $8K. Nobody complained. The perceived value went up because the promise got clearer. [[Workshop_offer_2025_03_02]]

A third example is a client who was afraid to raise prices because her current clients could not afford the increase. She was charging <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->K a month for LinkedIn ghostwriting and wanted to go to $4K. She thought she would lose everyone.

I told her she would not lose everyone. She would lose the wrong people. The clients who could barely afford <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->K were never going to be great clients. They negotiated on scope. They asked for revisions. They paid late. Losing them was the goal.

She raised prices. Four clients left. Three new clients signed at $4K in the first month. Her revenue went up and her workload went down. [[Client_transition_2024_10_14]]

The pattern that shows up over and over is the founder who thinks they need to ease into a price increase. They want to raise $500 this quarter, then another $500 next quarter. I tell them that is the wrong move. Incremental increases train the market to expect negotiation.

If the new price is <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->0K, charge <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->0K today. Not $8K with a plan to go to <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->0K later. The market does not care about your internal pricing roadmap. They care about whether the promise is worth the price right now. [[Pricing_rollout_2025_01_08]]

## Scripts and exact phrases

"The price is $X. Here is what you get in the first 30 days."

No justification. No apology. Just the number and the first outcome.

"If I can deliver [specific result] in [specific timeframe], is $X a fair price?"

This is the qualifying question. If they say no, the call is over. If they say yes, the rest of the conversation is just logistics.

"I used to charge $Y. That was underpriced. The new price is $X because the promise is now [specific outcome]."

This is the script for transitioning existing clients or reactivating old leads. You are not justifying the increase. You are stating that the old price reflected an old promise and the new price reflects a new one.

"This is not for everyone. If budget is tight, I can refer you to someone else. But if you want [specific outcome], this is the investment."

This is the disqualification script. It gives them permission to walk away and it positions the price as non-negotiable.

"The price is $X. The payment plan is [structure]. Which one works for you?"

This is the closing question. You are not asking if they want to buy. You are asking how they want to pay. The frame assumes the sale.

"Most people think they need [deliverable]. What they actually need is [outcome]. That is what I sell."

This is the reframe for prospects who are focused on inputs instead of outcomes.

"I do not discount. If the price does not work, I can recommend someone at a lower price point."

This is the objection handler for "can you do it for less?" The answer is no, and you give them an out.

## Common mistakes

The first mistake is raising prices without changing the promise. If the promise is the same and the price goes up, you just made the offer worse. The prospect is paying more for the same thing. That is a bad trade.

The second mistake is apologizing for the price. The moment you say "I know it is a lot, but..." you have told the prospect the price is too high. They will believe you.

The third mistake is adding more deliverables to justify a higher price. More is not better. More is just more. A stronger outcome justifies a higher price. More check-ins do not.

The fourth mistake is negotiating before the prospect asks. Founders preemptively offer payment plans or discounts because they are afraid of losing the deal. That is a signal that the price is soft. The prospect will push.

The fifth mistake is anchoring on hourly rate. If you charge <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->00 an hour and the project takes 40 hours, you think the price should be $8K. That is wrong. The price should be whatever the outcome is worth to the buyer. Hourly rate is an input cost. Inputs do not set price. Outcomes do.

The sixth mistake is running a "soft launch" at the new price with the plan to go higher later. That trains the market to expect increases and it makes every client wonder if they overpaid.

## The contrarian read

The conventional wisdom is that you need social proof, testimonials, and case studies before you can raise prices. That is wrong.

You can charge a premium on day one if the promise is clear and the guarantee removes the risk. Social proof helps, but it is not the bottleneck. The bottleneck is whether the prospect believes you can deliver the outcome you promised.

Another piece of conventional wisdom is that you should raise prices gradually so you do not shock the market. I disagree. If the old price was wrong, fix it now. Incremental increases drag out the pain and train prospects to wait for the next discount.

The most damaging conventional wisdom is that you need to be the best in your category before you can charge premium prices. That is backwards. Premium pricing is what creates the margin to become the best. If you stay cheap, you stay stuck. You cannot invest in the systems, the team, or the positioning that would make you premium-worthy.

Premium pricing is not the reward for being great. It is the requirement.

## What it looks like when it is working

You stop getting "I need to think about it" as an objection. When the promise is clear and the guarantee is strong, the prospect either buys or they self-disqualify. Hesitation means something is unclear.

You start closing faster. When the price is justified by the outcome, the sales cycle compresses. There is less back-and-forth. Less negotiation. The prospect knows what they are buying and they either want it or they do not.

You start attracting better clients. Premium pricing filters out the tire-kickers and the budget shoppers. The clients who sign are serious. They expect results. They do not ask for discounts. They pay on time.

Your close rate does not drop. Most founders think raising prices will cut their close rate in half. It does not. The close rate on qualified prospects stays roughly the same. What changes is the quality of the pipeline.

You get paid what the work is worth. Not what the market thinks it should cost. Not what you think you can get away with. What the outcome is actually worth to the buyer.
