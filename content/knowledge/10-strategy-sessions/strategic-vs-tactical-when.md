## The core principle

Strategy is the category. Tactics are the execution.

Most founders get this backwards. They open a call asking "Should I post daily on LinkedIn?" when they haven't answered "Who am I talking to and what do I want them to do?" They build features before they know what problem they're solving. They hire before they know what role the business actually needs.

The question isn't whether you need strategy or tactics. You need both. The question is: what level is the actual problem at?

## Why this matters

Getting the level wrong costs months.

A founder comes to a call stuck on "My content isn't working." They want better hooks. They want a new post format. They want to know if video performs better than carousels.

Those are tactical questions. But when you dig, the real issue is positioning. They can't say what they do in one line. They have three ICPs. They're selling to everyone and no one.

No hook fixes that. You can teach them ten frameworks, they'll still sound generic because the strategy layer is broken.

The opposite happens too. A founder spends three months "refining their positioning" when the real problem is they haven't shipped anything. They're stuck in strategy paralysis. The business doesn't need another brand workshop. It needs five LinkedIn posts this week and three outbound DMs.

The cost of misdiagnosis is time. Time you don't get back. Time the business bleeds while you solve the wrong problem.

The mechanism is simple: strategy sets direction, tactics cover ground. If the direction is wrong, speed makes it worse. If the direction is right but you're not moving, nothing happens.

## How it shows up in client work

This shows up in every single 1:1 call.

The pattern: founder comes in with a tactical problem. "My posts get likes but no DMs." "I need better cold email copy." "Should I launch on Product Hunt?"

First move: name the level. Ask one question that surfaces whether this is a strategy or a tactics issue.

"Who exactly are you trying to reach with this post?" If they can't answer in one sentence, it's not a hooks problem. It's a positioning problem. Strategy layer.

"What happens after someone replies to that cold email?" If the answer is "I don't know" or "I figure it out," it's not a copy problem. It's an offer problem. Strategy layer.

"What does success on Product Hunt actually give your business?" If the answer is "visibility" or "traffic," and they can't connect that to revenue in three steps, it's not a launch tactics problem. It's a business model problem. Strategy layer.

The diagnostic comes down to one test: if I give you the tactical answer right now, can you execute it today? If yes, it's a tactics problem. If no, something strategic is missing.

Example: "Should I use video or text posts?" If the founder knows their ICP, knows their message, knows what they're asking the reader to do, they can test it today. Tactics problem. Answer: test both, measure replies, double down.

But if they don't know who they're talking to? Video vs text doesn't matter. Strategy problem. Park the question. Fix positioning first.

From [[Karen_Strategy_call_2026_01_13]]: Founder came in with a classic tactics question: "Should I hire a VA to help with content?" I asked "What's the content doing for the business right now?" She said "Not much." I asked "Why not?" She said "I'm not sure who I'm talking to." Hiring a VA to make more of what isn't working is expensive theatre. We didn't hire anyone. We fixed the ICP and the messaging. Three months later she asked the same question and the answer was different: yes, hire, because now you know what good looks like.

The other pattern: founders who never leave strategy.

They refine their positioning for six months. They rewrite their About page twelve times. They do brand audits, hire consultants, buy courses, and never ship.

From [[Daniela_Strategy_session_2026_01_17]]: Client had been "working on her brand" for eight months. She had three versions of her positioning doc. She had hired two brand strategists before me. She'd done workshops, exercises, Vision boards, the full circus.

I asked her to show me what she'd shipped in the last 90 days. Nothing. She was stuck in strategy theatre.

The move: I told her to park strategy entirely. Pick one offer. Pick one person it's for. Write five LinkedIn posts this week about problems that person has. DM ten people. Do that for 30 days. Identity emerges from the work, not the planning.

She resisted. "But I don't know my brand yet." I said: "Your brand is what people say about you after they work with you. You can't plan that. You earn it by doing the work."

She shipped. Thirty days later, she knew exactly who she was because the market had told her. Three clients in 90 days. The brand clarity followed the work, not the other way around.

## Scripts and exact phrases

When a founder brings a tactical question and the real issue is strategic:

"That's a great question, but I'm going to push back on it. Here's why: [name the missing strategic piece]. Let's handle that first, then the tactical answer becomes obvious."

When a founder is stuck in strategy theatre and needs to ship:

"I hear you. But here's what I'd do: park strategy for 30 days. Pick one thing. Ship it. The clarity you're looking for comes from the work, not from more planning."

When diagnosing the level on a call:

"Before we answer that, help me understand: [ask the one question that surfaces strategy or tactics]. If it's X, this is a tactics problem. If it's Y, we need to go one level up."

When a founder asks "Should I...?" about a tactic:

"Maybe. Depends. What's the business goal behind this? If we do this and it works, what changes?" (If they can't answer, it's not a tactics problem.)

When closing a strategy session:

"Here's what we're NOT doing next: [list the tactics they wanted to jump to]. Here's what we ARE doing: [name the one strategic move]. Once that's handled, those tactics will be ten times easier."

When a tactic IS the right answer:

"Yes. Do that. Here's exactly how. [Give the specific script/process/tool]. Test it this week. Report back Friday."

## Common mistakes

**Mistake 1: Answering the question the founder asks instead of the question they need answered.**

Founder asks "What's the best time to post on LinkedIn?" You give them the data: Tuesday mornings, 8-10am. They go post at 8am Tuesday. Nothing happens. Why? Because posting time wasn't the problem. The posts were generic. They didn't know who they were talking to. You solved a tactics problem that didn't matter because the strategy was missing.

The fix: name the level FIRST, then answer.

**Mistake 2: Forcing strategy when the founder just needs to ship.**

Founder comes in, ready to execute, asks "Should I send 10 cold DMs today or write a LinkedIn post?" You say "Let's step back and think about your overall go-to-market strategy." They glaze over. You lose them.

They didn't need strategy. They needed permission and a script. The strategy was already handled (they know their ICP, they know their offer). They just wanted to know which channel to hit today.

The fix: if the strategic foundation is solid, give the tactical answer and get out of the way.

**Mistake 3: Treating every call like a strategy session.**

Not every call needs frameworks. Sometimes the founder just needs you to say "Yes, that works, go do it" or "No, that's a distraction, kill it." Overcomplicating simple decisions is expensive.

**Mistake 4: Building tactics in isolation from business goals.**

Founder wants to "grow their email list." You help them build a lead magnet, set up a funnel, run ads. List grows. Revenue doesn't. Why? Because you never asked what the email list was supposed to DO for the business. Was it meant to nurture cold leads? Replace a sales call? Sell a course? Without the strategy layer, the tactic becomes theatre.

**Mistake 5: Confusing activity with progress.**

Posting daily on LinkedIn feels like strategy execution. It's not. It's tactics. If the positioning is wrong, posting daily just scales the wrong message. More activity doesn't fix a strategy problem. It amplifies it.

**Mistake 6: Never moving from strategy to tactics.**

The founder who's been "working on their brand" for six months, attending workshops, refining messaging, and never shipping. Strategy becomes procrastination. The brand doesn't exist until it's in the market.

## The contrarian read

Most people think strategy is big and tactics are small.

Wrong.

Strategy is fast. Tactics are slow.

Defining your ICP takes one session. Writing 100 posts for that ICP takes three months.

Choosing your offer takes an afternoon. Building a sales funnel takes weeks.

Deciding to kill a channel takes five minutes. Ramping up a new channel takes 90 days.

The hard part isn't strategy. The hard part is committing to one strategy long enough to execute the tactics that make it real.

Here's the other contrarian take: most "strategic" advice is just expensive tactics disguised as vision.

Brand positioning workshops. Vision boards. "North Star" documents. OKRs for solopreneurs. These are tactical outputs pretending to be strategic. They're theatre.

Real strategy is boring. It's answering three questions:

1. Who am I serving?
2. What do I want them to do?
3. What's the one thing I'm betting on to make that happen?

That's it. Everything else is tactics or distraction.

If your "strategy session" takes longer than two hours, you're doing it wrong.

## What it looks like when it's working

You're on a call with a founder. They ask a tactical question. You name the level in 30 seconds. If it's strategy, you stop them, handle the strategy, and the tactics become obvious. If it's tactics, you give the specific answer and move on.

No wasted time. No strategy theatre. No solving the wrong problem.

The founder leaves with one clear move. Not twelve. One.

And that move is at the right level. If they're stuck on positioning, they're not building lead magnets. If positioning is handled, they're not rewriting their About page for the fifth time.

Observable signals:

- Founder can articulate their ICP in one sentence without hesitation.
- Founder can name what they're asking their content/outreach/sales process to DO.
- Founder ships weekly because the strategy is clear enough that tactics don't require constant re-decision.
- Revenue moves because the tactics are aligned to a strategy that actually connects to business outcomes.

From client work: when strategy is handled, tactics conversations take 10 minutes. When strategy is missing, tactics conversations spiral for an hour and produce nothing actionable.

The test: if you can't execute the answer from the call THIS WEEK, the call was at the wrong level.
