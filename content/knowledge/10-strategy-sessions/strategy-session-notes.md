## The core principle

Session notes are real-time operating records, not summaries written after the fact.

They capture what happened, what was said, what changed, and what ships next — written live while the session unfolds, not reconstructed from memory two hours later when half the decisions are already forgotten.

## Why this matters

Bad session notes kill momentum.

When you reconstruct a call from memory, you lose the exact language the client used. You lose the moment they shifted. You lose the specific objection they raised before they said yes. You lose the next action they committed to in the room.

Three days later when you're writing the follow-up email, you're guessing. The client notices. They feel like you weren't listening. Trust drops.

Good session notes preserve the operating state of the engagement. You can pick up exactly where you left off. You can quote the client's exact words back to them. You can hold them to the commitment they made in minute 47 of the call. You can reference the specific pain point that made them book in the first place.

The leverage is in the details. Names. Numbers. Direct quotes. The specific phrase that landed. The exact deliverable they asked for. The reason they said no to the first version of the offer. The timeline they committed to. The obstacle that showed up mid-session.

Without those details, every session becomes generic. With them, every session becomes a specific, citable, actionable record you can build the next move from.

## How it shows up in client work

Every call gets documented live in a structured format: participant list, transcript extract, key decisions, next actions, direct quotes.

The format stays consistent across hundreds of sessions because consistency makes the notes searchable. When a client says "Remember that thing we talked about in October?" you can find it in under 30 seconds because every October session follows the same structure.

For workshop builds, the notes capture more than just what was taught. They capture attendance, engagement signals, which examples landed, which sections ran long, what questions came up in chat, what broke technically. For 1:1 coaching calls, the notes prioritize direct quotes. When a client is stuck on positioning, the note captures their exact fumbling attempt to describe what they do — not a cleaned-up summary. That fumble IS the positioning problem. ## Common mistakes

Writing summaries instead of records. A summary says "We discussed pricing." A record says "Client balked at <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->5K, said yes at <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->8K with 90-day guarantee, signed in the room."

Cleaning up the client's language. When a client says "I don't know, like, we help people with, um, strategy stuff I guess?" that's the note. Rewriting it as "They provide strategic consulting services" erases the positioning problem.

Skipping the participant list. Six months later when you need to loop someone into a follow-up, you can't remember if they were on the original call. The participant list preserves who was in the room.

Leaving out the interruptions and constraints. "Danny had kidney stones but ran the rehearsal anyway" explains why the slides were incomplete. Without that context, the incomplete slides look like poor prep.

Not citing the source of decisions. "Alex suggested the Growth Track" is useful. "We added a Growth Track" is not. When someone challenges the decision later, you need to know who made the call.

Writing notes in narrative prose instead of structured sections. Narrative is hard to search. Structure is easy to search. Consistent structure across 200 notes means you can find the pricing conversation from any call in under 30 seconds.

## The contrarian read

Most people think session notes are for the client. They're not. They're for you.

The client was on the call. They heard what was said. The note is so YOU can reload the context three weeks later when you're writing the follow-up and can't remember if they said $50K or <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->5K.

Most people think good notes require a notetaker or a VA or Otter.ai. They don't. Good notes require a format and the discipline to fill it in live while the session runs.

Most people think long notes are better. They're not. The best notes are short, structured, and searchable. A 200-word note with 4 direct quotes and 3 next actions beats a 2,000-word narrative summary every time.

The real purpose of session notes is to preserve your own operating state so you can pick up where you left off without re-explaining the context to yourself. If the note makes the client feel heard, that's a bonus. But the primary user is you.

## What it looks like when it's working

You can read a note from six months ago and remember the exact state of the engagement in under 60 seconds.

You can quote the client's exact words back to them in a follow-up email without checking the transcript.

You can hand a note to another team member and they can run the next session without asking you a single clarifying question.

You can search "positioning fumble" across 200 notes and find every session where a client struggled to articulate what they do.

You can read the "next actions" section from the last call and know exactly what ships today.

The client feels heard because you're citing their exact language, their exact objection, their exact commitment from the call three weeks ago.

Other team members can read your notes and learn how you work, how you diagnose problems, how you structure a session, how you close a sale.

Your notes become the source of truth for the engagement, the project, the workshop series, the curriculum build.
