## The core principle

When a client can't name their ICP in one sharp sentence, they don't have a positioning problem — they have a revenue problem waiting to happen.

## Why this matters

A broad ICP isn't neutral. It actively destroys the business.

Every dollar spent on content that tries to speak to "anyone who needs this" is a dollar lighting itself on fire. Every sales call with someone outside the bullseye is an hour the founder will never get back. Every piece of positioning that hedges — "we help coaches and consultants and entrepreneurs" — trains the market to scroll past.

The cost shows up in three places. First, the founder burns out. They're writing content for five different audiences, running five different value propositions, testing five different hooks. None of them land because none of them are specific enough to make one person stop and say "this is exactly me."

Second, the sales cycle stretches. A vague ICP means vague messaging. Vague messaging means the prospect has to do translation work in their head to figure out if this applies to them. Most won't bother. The ones who book a call spend the first 20 minutes asking clarifying questions instead of buying.

Third, the founder becomes a order-taker instead of a specialist. When you serve everyone, you command average rates. When you serve one person better than anyone else on the planet, you can charge what the outcome is worth.

The leverage of getting this right is the opposite of all three. Content that speaks to one person resonates with hundreds who match that profile. Sales calls with the right ICP close faster because the prospect already knows you built this for them. Pricing conversations shift from "why does this cost so much" to "when can we start."

## How it shows up in client work

I see the broad ICP pattern in 80% of first calls. The founder opens with some version of: "I help people grow their business" or "I work with anyone who wants to get better at X."

The diagnostic is simple. I ask: "Who's your last three paying clients?" They name them. Then I ask: "What do those three have in common?" Nine times out of ten, the answer is "not much."

That's the problem. Not the lack of clients. The lack of a pattern in who's buying.

One client I worked with in late 2024 was a leadership coach. Her initial ICP: "executives who want to lead better." When I pushed, she listed: a VP at a SaaS company, a solo consultant, and a mid-level manager at a nonprofit. Three completely different people with three completely different problems.

We spent the first 30 minutes of the call forcing constraints. I asked: "If you could only work with one of those three types for the next 12 months, which one would make you the most money?" She picked the VP. Then: "Why that one?" Answer: "They have budget, they make decisions fast, and the results are visible."

That's the real ICP. Not the one you wish you had. The one that actually pays and actually gets results. [[Subra_Mukherjee_One_on_One_Personal_Branding_2024_10_25]]

Another pattern: the founder who's afraid to narrow because they think they'll lose opportunities. Dr. Subra came in saying she worked with "professionals who want to learn mind mapping." The word "professionals" is a red flag. It means nothing.

I asked her to name the last ten people who actually paid. She listed them. Eight were mid-career professionals stuck in corporate jobs who wanted a side business. Two were retirees.

I told her: cut the retirees. Not because they're bad people. Because they're a different business. Mid-career professionals buy differently, consume differently, refer differently. Trying to serve both means your messaging appeals to neither.

She resisted for 20 minutes. Then she admitted: "The retirees take twice as long to close and they haggle on price." That's the signal. Your real ICP is the one who buys fast, pays what you ask, and gets results that make you look good. [[Subra_Mukherjee_One_on_One_Personal_Branding_2024_10_25]]

The Founder OS workshop in November 2025 had a live example of this playing out. One attendee was building an AI productivity course. His ICP: "anyone who wants to use AI better."

I stopped him. "That's not an ICP. That's a Google search query."

We did the constraint exercise in real time. I asked: "Who's the one person who, if they got results, would tell ten other people exactly like them?" He thought for a minute. Then: "Solo founders running service businesses who are drowning in admin work."

That's an ICP. It's specific enough that you can picture the person. It's narrow enough that you can build a solution they'll pay for. It's connected enough that one success story creates ten referrals. [[Founder_OS_AI_Masterclass_2nd_Session_2025_11_22]]

The pattern I see in every narrowing conversation: the founder fights it until they see the math. A broad ICP means you're competing with everyone. A narrow ICP means you're the only option for that exact person.

One founder I worked with was building an AI consulting offer. Initial ICP: "businesses that want to implement AI." We spent 45 minutes narrowing. Final ICP: "Solo founders running <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->00K-$500K service businesses who are the face of their brand and spending 12+ hours a week on content."

That's not narrower because it sounds good. It's narrower because every word is a filter that increases close rate. "Solo founders" means one decision-maker, no procurement, fast deals. "<!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->00K-$500K" means they have budget but not a marketing team. "Face of their brand" means they care about voice and POV. "12+ hours a week on content" means they feel the pain daily.

The result: his close rate went from 20% to 60% in 90 days. Not because his offer changed. Because he stopped talking to people who were never going to buy. [[Purely_Personal_Pre_work_2024_11_20]]

Another version of this: the founder who narrows to the wrong ICP. They pick the avatar they *want* to work with instead of the one who actually buys.

One client came in saying his ICP was "enterprise CMOs." I asked him to show me his last five closed deals. None of them were enterprise CMOs. They were all solo founders or small team leads.

The enterprise CMOs sounded sexier. They had bigger titles, bigger budgets, bigger logos. But they also had six-month sales cycles, procurement processes, and legal reviews. The solo founders bought in one call and paid upfront.

I told him: your real ICP is the one who's already buying. Build for them first. Once you have case studies and recurring revenue, you can move upmarket. But if you optimize for the sexy ICP who never buys, you go broke before you get there. [[Chandu_first_meeting_2024_06_03]]

That's three problems in one sentence: "educator" is a role not a position, "mind mapping" is a tool not an outcome, and "professionals" is everyone and no one.

The breakthrough came when I asked her to describe the last person who paid and got great results. She described a woman in her 40s, stuck in a corporate job, trying to start a side coaching business, overwhelmed by information and unable to focus.

I said: "That's your ICP. Write that exact description at the top of every piece of content you create. If it doesn't speak to her, delete it."

Her response: "But won't I lose all the other people?"

My answer: "You're already losing them. Broad messaging doesn't attract more people. It attracts no one." [[Subra_Mukherjee_One_on_One_Personal_Branding_2024_10_25]]

"Name your last five paying clients. What do they have in common? If the answer is 'not much,' you don't have an ICP yet."

"Your ICP isn't who you want to work with. It's who already buys, pays what you ask, and gets results fast."

## Common mistakes

**Mistake 1: Confusing ICP with total addressable market.** The founder says "my ICP is small business owners" because that's who could theoretically buy. But ICP isn't TAM. ICP is the one person you can serve better than anyone else. Small business owners is a TAM. "Solo founders running <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->00K-$500K service businesses" is an ICP.

**Mistake 2: Narrowing to the sexy avatar instead of the real buyer.** The founder picks "enterprise CMOs" because it sounds impressive. But the last five deals were all solo founders. Your ICP is who's actually buying, not who you wish was buying.

**Mistake 3: Thinking narrow means small.** The founder resists narrowing because they think they're limiting their revenue. The opposite is true. Narrow positioning lets you charge more, close faster, and build a category of one. Broad positioning makes you a commodity competing on price.

**Mistake 4: Defining the ICP by demographics instead of behavior.** "My ICP is 35-50 year old professionals in tech" is demographics. "My ICP is solo founders who post on LinkedIn 3x a week but hate every minute of it" is behavior. Demographics don't buy. People with specific pains and frustrations buy.

**Mistake 5: Trying to serve two ICPs at once.** The founder says "I work with corporate executives and solo founders." Those are two completely different businesses. Different pains, different buying cycles, different pricing models, different content strategies. Pick one. Build for them. Then expand.

**Mistake 6: Narrowing but not committing.** The founder writes a narrow ICP in their positioning doc, then writes broad content because they're afraid to exclude anyone. The ICP only works if every piece of content, every hook, every sales page speaks directly to that one person.

## The contrarian read

The conventional wisdom is "niche down so you stand out." That's not wrong, but it misses the actual mechanism.

You don't narrow to stand out. You narrow to stop burning money on people who were never going to buy.

Every founder I've worked with who resisted narrowing had the same fear: "If I narrow, I'll miss opportunities." The reality: you're already missing opportunities by being so broad that no one knows what you do.

The second contrarian take: narrowing isn't about exclusion. It's about efficiency.

A broad ICP means you're writing five different value propositions, testing five different hooks, running five different campaigns. A narrow ICP means you write one thing that works, test one hook that lands, and scale what's already converting.

The third take: your ICP should make you slightly uncomfortable.

If your ICP feels safe and inclusive, it's too broad. The right ICP makes you nervous because you're leaving people out. That discomfort is the signal you've actually committed to a position.

Fourth: the best ICP is fractal.

The solo founder running a <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->00K service business today becomes the agency owner with ten team members in two years. If you narrow to solo founders and serve them well, you don't lose them when they scale — they bring you with them. Narrow at the entry point, grow with them over time.

## What it looks like when it's working

The founder can describe their ICP in one sentence without hedging. No "mostly" or "usually" or "we work with a range of." Just: "I work with solo founders running <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->00K-$500K service businesses who are the face of their brand."

Content starts landing. Hooks that used to get 10 likes now get 50. Comments shift from "interesting" to "this is exactly me." DMs start with "I feel like you wrote this for me."

Sales calls get shorter. The prospect already knows the offer is built for them. The first 20 minutes aren't spent explaining what you do — they're spent confirming fit and closing.

Close rate goes up. In one case I worked with, narrowing the ICP took close rate from 20% to 60% in 90 days. Same offer, same pricing, same sales process. The only difference: every conversation was with someone who was already 80% sold before they booked the call.

Referrals become predictable. When your ICP is narrow and connected, one client tells ten others exactly like them. Broad ICP means every referral is random. Narrow ICP means every referral looks like your best client.

The founder stops second-guessing. No more "should I post this?" or "will this alienate people?" Every piece of content either serves the ICP or it doesn't. The decision is binary.
