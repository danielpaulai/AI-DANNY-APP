## The core principle

Walk away before the money is wasted, not after.

The best projects are the ones you don't start. The second-best are the ones you kill before they kill you.

## Why this matters

Most founders confuse motion with progress.

They see a shiny new app idea, a potential partnership, a "we should totally build this" moment, and they start moving. They prototype. They hire. They book calls. They invest weeks into something that should have died in the first conversation.

The cost is invisible until it's catastrophic. Six weeks vanish. A developer burns through retainer hours. A client relationship sours because you oversold a half-baked concept. Your own time gets swallowed by a project that was never going to make money.

The leverage of killing early is enormous. One hour of ruthless pre-mortem saves twenty hours of recovery. One "no" protects your entire month. One hard stop at the right moment keeps your pipeline clean, your focus sharp, and your business from drowning in half-finished commitments.

The problem is most people don't have the criteria. They can't tell the difference between a real problem worth solving and a vanity project dressed up as strategy. They can't separate "this feels exciting" from "this will actually close." They can't name the moment when they should stop.

This category exists to give you the criteria.

## How it shows up in client work

The pattern repeats across dozens of calls: the client comes in excited about a new project, I ask three questions, and the project is dead within ten minutes.

Not because the idea is bad. Because the conditions for success aren't there.

I saw this with Maven Course planning in [[Maven_Run_through_2024_11_15]]. Ross had been chasing a travel brand partnership for weeks. He'd already delivered one video that flopped. They'd promised more work. They'd teased a Guatemala tourism board connection. He was burning hours on follow-ups and feeling like he was about to break through.

The red flags were everywhere. The video they asked him to post didn't match his style. They made him post at midnight instead of his peak time. They wouldn't commit to pricing until after the "test." They kept saying "we'll see" instead of "here's the next step."

I didn't have to tell Ross to walk away. I just had to show him he was already walking toward nothing.

The kill signal was simple: they were using him to test ideas, not to execute a plan. No real brief. No locked budget. No timeline. Just "do this and maybe we'll talk about the next thing."

That's not a client. That's a time drain dressed up as an opportunity.

Same pattern with Maria in [[Maria_Ilieva_One_on_One_Personal_Branding_(_advanced)_2024_09_05]]. She came in struggling to define her ICP because she didn't have a business yet. She was trying to build a personal brand without knowing what she was selling. She was about to get fired and wanted to start "softly contacting people" about freelance work.

The mistake most coaches make here is they'd help her write better LinkedIn posts. I stopped the whole thing.

If you don't know what you're selling, you can't build a brand. If you can't name your offer, posting content is theatre. The project isn't "build Maria's brand." The project is "help Maria figure out what she does."

We ran the Ikigai GPT. We extracted her actual expertise (consumer insights, shopper research, competitor strategy). We named the one thing people already ask her for. Then we could talk about positioning.

The kill decision was killing the "let's work on LinkedIn" project and replacing it with "let's define the offer first."

In [[Weekly_Meeting_Mehsum_2025_04_21]], Mehsum came in wanting feedback on a video. The video was fine. The real issue was he wanted to add a "join the community" CTA at the end. I asked what the community was. He said "we're not ready for that yet."

That's the kill signal right there. If you're not ready to deliver on the thing you're asking people to sign up for, don't ask.

The instinct is to build momentum. Get the signups. Figure it out later. That instinct destroys credibility faster than doing nothing. People remember when you overpromise. They forget when you underpromise.

We cut the community line from the video. The video still worked. The ask was clearer. The follow-through was real.

The NasOps project in [[Building nasops transcript-based documentation and email generation app - 78e52862]] is the opposite pattern: a project that should have been killed but wasn't. Eight sessions deep. Sixteen pages of features. PDF exports. AI session summaries. Weekly metric tracking. Challenge progress tables. Voyage AI embeddings for feedback clustering.

The build was technically flawless. The problem was never defined.

The user wanted "an app that looks exactly like this" and attached a screenshot. The goal was vague: "make sure this app is actually something we want to build for nas.io." The use cases were broad: transcript-to-doc generation, Taki Moore-style email writing, campaign cadence automation.

That's not a brief. That's a wishlist.

A real brief has one sentence: "This app solves [specific problem] for [specific person] and ships [specific outcome] in [specific timeframe]." If you can't write that sentence, you don't have a project. You have motion.

The kill criteria I use with every new project:

1. Can the client name the one person this is for?
2. Can they describe the before-and-after in numbers?
3. Can they commit to using the first version within 7 days of delivery?
4. Do they already have 3+ examples of the problem costing them money or time?
5. Is this solving a workflow problem or a "wouldn't it be cool if" problem?

If the answer to any of those is no, the project gets killed or scoped down to a proof-of-concept. No exceptions.

The LPG system in [[LPG distribution inventory and billing management system - 3a9ec055]] passed every test. The client had 8 product types, manual stock tracking, paper-based invoicing, and a debt collection process that required physical TT slips. They could name the exact cost: hours per week on manual admin, delayed payments from missing invoices, cylinder stock they couldn't track.

That project shipped. Because the conditions were there.

The EngagementFlow AI project in [[App development problem identification - d1955b05]] is the cleanest example of a project that survived the kill test. Noman and Priyanka had one specific problem: the knowledge transfer gap between sales and customer success. They could describe the cost: senior leaders spending hours writing SOWs manually, no peer review mechanism, no audit trail from promise to delivery.

Twenty-six features got built. The platform went live. Because the problem was real, the buyer was clear, and the outcome was measurable.

## Specific examples from the vault

Irving's session in [[Irving_Waisman_One_on_One_Personal_Branding_(_advanced)_2025_04_29]] is the moment when you realize the client isn't ready for the project they think they're here for.

He came in saying he'd "fallen off the bandwagon" with the course. Lost motivation. Couldn't find the point. I asked why. He said he wanted to quit his job, move across Canada to be with family, break up with his girlfriend, then move to Thailand and live off content creation for a while.

That's not a positioning problem. That's a life decision.

The mistake most coaches make is they keep coaching. They'd say "let's work on your content strategy for Thailand" or "let's define your niche before you move." That's solving the wrong problem. The real problem is Irving doesn't know if he's going yet.

The kill decision was killing the "build Irving's LinkedIn brand" project and replacing it with "help Irving close the chapters he needs to close first."

I asked him: what are the three things you'd do in the next three months to make Thailand happen? He said tell people at work he's leaving, break up with his girlfriend, and start closing chapters. Those are the real projects. Content strategy is step 47.

From [[Maven_Run_through_2024_11_15]], the travel brand partnership died in one exchange:

Ross: "They said either way we'd still like to work with you, so send us some ideas."

Me: "And what did they say when you sent the ideas?"

Ross: "They said 'we'll see.'"

That's the kill signal. "We'll see" is not a client commitment. It's a polite rejection dressed up as interest.

The question I asked Ross: "Are they using you to test ideas or to execute a plan?" If they're testing, you're free labour. If they're executing, you're a vendor with a scope.

The Nas.io document redesign project in [[Nas.io document redesign with branding - 6b8ef772]] shows the kill decision applied to deliverables within a project. Danny was rebuilding a trainer guide, writing session titles, and producing email strategy docs. He kept asking me to resume tasks without restating context.

That's a sign the project is too big. If the scope requires constant context-switching, it's three projects pretending to be one.

The kill move was breaking it into discrete workstreams: curriculum doc, title-writing rules, email strategy, challenge forms. Each one got its own deliverable. Each one could be shipped and used independently.

No sprawl. No "let's also add this while we're here." Just ship the thing, confirm it works, move to the next.

## Scripts and exact phrases

"If you can't name the one person this is for, we're not building it yet."

"What's the cost of not solving this? If the answer is 'it would just be nice,' that's not a project."

"Can you use the first version within 7 days of delivery? If no, we're building the wrong thing."

"Is this solving a workflow problem or a 'wouldn't it be cool if' problem? Only workflow problems get built."

"What's the before-and-after in numbers? If you can't answer that, we don't have a brief."

"Are they using you to test ideas or to execute a plan? Testing is free labour. Execution is a vendor relationship with a scope."

"Let's kill this version and scope it down to the smallest thing that proves the concept. If it works, we expand. If it doesn't, we saved 80% of the time."

"What are you trying to learn from this project? If the answer is 'whether this is a good idea,' that's research, not a build. Research takes one call, not six weeks."

## Common mistakes

**Mistake 1: Confusing excitement with viability**

The client comes in energized about a new idea. They've been thinking about it for weeks. They've told their team. They've sketched wireframes. The energy feels like validation.

It's not. Excitement is a starting point, not proof. The question isn't "does this feel good." The question is "will this close deals in 90 days."

**Mistake 2: Starting before the conditions are in place**

I see this constantly in app builds. The client wants to start coding before they've validated the problem. They want to design the UI before they've talked to 5 users. They want to hire a team before they've scoped the MVP.

Starting too early doesn't save time. It creates debt. You build the wrong thing, then rebuild it, then rebuild it again. Three times the cost, zero learning.

**Mistake 3: Treating "maybe" as "yes"**

"They said they're interested" is not a commitment. "They want to see a proposal" is not a green light. "They'll let us know" is not a timeline.

If the client or partner can't commit to a next step with a date, you don't have a deal. You have a conversation.

**Mistake 4: Solving the surface problem instead of the real one**

Maria wanted help with LinkedIn. The real problem was she didn't know what she was selling. Irving wanted content strategy. The real problem was he hadn't decided if he was moving to Thailand.

If you solve the surface problem, you waste time and the client stays stuck. If you name the real problem, you either solve it or you kill the project and replace it with the right one.

**Mistake 5: Keeping a project alive because you've already invested time**

Sunk cost fallacy kills more projects than bad ideas. You've already spent three weeks on the prototype. You've already had four client calls. You've already pitched it internally.

None of that matters. The only question is: if you knew then what you know now, would you start this project today? If the answer is no, kill it.

## The contrarian read

Most business advice says "don't quit too early" and "persistence beats talent." That's true for the right projects. It's poison for the wrong ones.

The conventional wisdom is you need to push through the hard part. You need to test longer. You need to give it more time. You need to see if it works.

I think that's backwards. The hard part isn't the sign you should keep going. It's the sign you should check if you're solving the right problem.

If a project feels like pulling a truck uphill, you're probably pulling the wrong truck. The right projects have friction, but they don't feel like drowning. They feel like sprinting uphill: hard, but forward.

The other contrarian take: killing a project isn't failure. It's discipline.

Founders who can't kill projects end up with ten half-finished commitments and no wins. Founders who kill fast end up with three completed projects that actually ship.

The scorecard isn't how many projects you started. It's how many you finished. Killing the wrong ones is how you finish the right ones.

## What it looks like when it's working

You have a one-page kill checklist. Five questions. If the answer to any question is no, the project parks or dies.

You run every new idea through the checklist within 24 hours of the initial conversation. Not next week. Not after the prototype. Within 24 hours.

Your project list shrinks by 60%. Your shipping rate doubles.

You stop saying "let me think about it" and start saying "this doesn't pass the test yet, here's why." The client either fixes the conditions or they move on. Either outcome is better than six weeks of maybes.

When someone pitches you a partnership, you ask: "What's the specific next step with a date?" If they can't answer, you don't follow up. You let it die.

You stop confusing motion with progress. You stop booking calls to "explore ideas." You book calls to decide yes or no on a specific question by end of session.

Your calendar clears. Your focus sharpens. The projects you do start actually finish.
