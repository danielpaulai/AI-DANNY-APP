## The core principle

The best measure of a business isn't revenue or profit margin. It's how much you're brave enough to bet on yourself this year.

Reinvestment rate is the percentage of profit that goes back into building the machine instead of sitting in your pocket. A founder running at 10% reinvestment is playing defense. A founder running at 60% is playing offense.

## Why this matters

Most founders optimize for the wrong number.

They look at profit and feel safe. They look at cash reserves and think they're winning. But the real question isn't "how much did I keep?" It's "how much did I put back in to make next year bigger?"

Every dollar you don't reinvest is a decision to stay the same size. That's fine if you're done growing. It's fatal if you're not.

The cost of under-investing shows up three ways. First, you stay stuck at the same revenue ceiling year after year because you never built the systems to break through it. Second, you burn out trying to manually scale what should have been automated or delegated six months ago. Third, competitors who ARE reinvesting pass you while you're congratulating yourself on profitability.

The founders who win long-term aren't the ones with the fattest bank accounts in year two. They're the ones who kept 30% for themselves, put 60% back into the business, and came out the other side with a machine that prints money without them in it.

Reinvestment is the only real moat in a commoditized world. Your product can be copied. Your pricing can be undercut. Your team can be poached. But a founder who systematically reinvests in brand, systems, and leverage compounds at a rate nobody can match.

The mechanism is simple: profit buys optionality. Reinvestment buys leverage. Optionality feels safe but scales linearly. Leverage feels risky but scales exponentially.

When you choose to take home 80% and reinvest 20%, you're choosing linear growth. When you flip that ratio, you're choosing exponential growth. Most founders never make that choice consciously. They drift into whatever feels comfortable and wonder why the business plateaus.

## Common mistakes

**Mistake 1: Waiting until revenue is "high enough" to reinvest.** Founders say "I'll invest in help once I hit $500K." But you hit $500K BECAUSE you invested in help at $300K. The investment creates the growth, not the other way around.

**Mistake 2: Confusing profit with cash to spend.** Profit is the scoreboard. It's not your salary. Most of it should go back into building the next version of the business. Founders who treat profit as take-home pay plateau fast.

**Mistake 3: Reinvesting in the wrong things.** Not all reinvestment is equal. Spending $50K on a rebrand when you don't have a content system is dumb. Spending $50K on an AI content system when you're manually writing every post is smart. Reinvest in leverage, not vanity.

**Mistake 4: Pulling everything out in the first profitable year.** The business JUST started working and you drain it? That's when it needs capital most. Leave the money in. Let it compound.

**Mistake 5: Treating reinvestment as an expense instead of an investment.** Founders say "I can't afford to hire someone." But hiring isn't a cost. It's an investment that buys you time, capacity, and leverage. If the math doesn't work, the hire is wrong or the business model is broken. Fix one of those instead of staying stuck.

**Mistake 6: Only reinvesting the "leftovers."** If reinvestment happens AFTER you pay yourself, it never happens. Flip the order. Reinvest first. Pay yourself second. The business grows on what it gets fed, not on what's left over after you've eaten.

## The contrarian read

Most business advice says "be profitable as fast as possible." I say the opposite.

Profitability is a milestone, not a strategy. The goal isn't to be profitable. The goal is to build a business that scales without you in it. That requires aggressive reinvestment, even if it delays profitability.

Amazon didn't become Amazon by optimizing for profit in year three. They reinvested everything for 15 years and became the most valuable company on earth. Most founders don't have 15 years of runway, but the principle holds: growth compounds faster than savings.

The conventional advice is "don't spend money you don't have." I say spend money you DO have on things that will make you more money. That's not reckless. That's how leverage works.

Founders who optimize for take-home pay in years 1-3 build lifestyle businesses. Founders who optimize for reinvestment in years 1-3 build assets. Both are valid. Just be honest about which one you're building.

The other contrarian take: reinvestment rate matters more than revenue. A <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->00K business reinvesting 60% will outgrow a $500K business reinvesting 10% within two years. The $500K founder is playing defense. The <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->00K founder is playing offense.

## What it looks like when it's working

Revenue grows year-over-year without you working more hours.

You have systems, people, or AI handling work you used to do manually.

Your take-home pay as a PERCENTAGE drops but the absolute DOLLAR amount stays flat or grows because revenue is scaling.

You're hiring before you "need" to because you see the constraint coming six months out.

Your cost per acquisition drops because you invested in brand and content systems that do the selling before the sales call.

You're saying no to clients because you're at capacity, not because you're too scared to hire.

You have 3-6 months of runway in the business account, not in your personal account.

When someone asks what you're investing in this year, you have a clear answer with a dollar amount attached.
