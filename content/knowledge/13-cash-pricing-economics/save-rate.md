## The core principle

Most founders think about what they charge. Almost none think about what they keep.

Save rate is the percentage of revenue that stays in the business after all costs — not just COGS, not just overhead, but everything. It's the actual margin that determines whether you can scale, whether you can invest, whether you can survive a bad quarter.

## Why this matters

Revenue is a vanity metric if the save rate is broken.

I've seen founders celebrate hitting $50K months while keeping 8%. That's $4,000. After paying themselves, there's nothing left to hire, nothing left to improve the product, nothing left to weather a slow month.

The mechanism is brutal: low save rate means every dollar of new revenue costs almost a dollar to generate. You're running on a treadmill. High save rate means every dollar of new revenue creates leverage. You can reinvest, you can build systems, you can compound.

The cost of getting this wrong shows up three ways. First, you can't hire. Every new team member eats the entire margin, so you stay stuck doing everything yourself. Second, you can't invest in marketing. If your save rate is 15% and your CAC is 20% of revenue, the math doesn't work. Third, you can't survive mistakes. One bad month and you're scrambling to cover payroll.

The founders who suffer most are service businesses with high labour costs and low pricing. Agencies charging $3K/month with two full-time team members assigned to each client. Coaching businesses with course platforms, email tools, Zoom accounts, design subscriptions, and a VA eating 60% of revenue before the founder takes a dollar.

The leverage of getting it right is the inverse. A founder keeping 60% of revenue can hire strategically, invest in tools that actually multiply output, and build a cash buffer that lets them make decisions from strength instead of desperation.

## How it shows up in client work

The save rate conversation happens in two places: pricing calls and operations audits.

On pricing calls, the founder tells me their offer. I ask what it costs to deliver. They list the obvious things — software, team time, maybe some ads. Then I ask about the hidden costs. The Zoom account. The CRM. The scheduler. The two hours a week they spend on admin that should be automated. The VA they hired because they were drowning.

Most founders haven't added it up. When we do the math, they realize they're keeping 20-30% of revenue. Sometimes less.

One client, a LinkedIn consultant charging <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->,500/month per client, thought she was doing well. She had eight clients, <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->0K/month. Then we broke it down. Two VAs at <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->,200 each. $600 in tools. $500 in ads. $300 in miscellaneous. That's $3,800 in fixed costs. Then delivery time: 10 hours per client per month at a <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->50/hour value. That's <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->2,000 in her time. Total cost: <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->5,800. She was keeping $4,200 on <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->0K revenue. 21% save rate.

She couldn't hire anyone. She couldn't scale. She was trapped. [[Client_call_LinkedIn_consultant_2024_09_18]]

Another pattern: founders who overpay for tools they don't use. One founder had 11 active SaaS subscriptions totaling $847/month. I asked him to name what each one does. He could only describe four. We cut seven immediately, saved $520/month, and his save rate jumped 5 percentage points without changing a single other thing. [[Operations_audit_SaaS_bloat_2024_11_22]]

The biggest trap is productized services with too much custom work. A founder selling "LinkedIn content packages" at <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->,500/month would include "up to 3 revisions" and "custom graphics for every post." Clients would request 5+ revisions. Graphics would take 2-3 hours per post. She was spending 15 hours per client, making the effective hourly rate <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->00, and after tools and a designer, keeping maybe 35%.

We rebuilt the offer. Fixed scope: 4 posts, 1 revision round, templates instead of custom graphics. Kept the price. Delivery time dropped to 4 hours per client. Save rate went from 35% to 68%. Same revenue, double the margin, and suddenly she could hire. [[Productized_service_rebuild_2024_10_14]]

The contrarian move is sometimes raising the price does more for save rate than cutting costs. A founder charging $997 for a workshop was keeping 40% after venue, materials, and promotion. We doubled the price to <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->,997. Costs stayed flat. Revenue per attendee doubled. Save rate jumped to 65%. The math worked because the costs didn't scale with price. The service business that discovered retainers: A copywriter charged per project. <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->,500 for a sales page. $800 for a landing page. We moved her to retainers: $3,000/month, two deliverables per month, fixed scope. Sales call became a 20-minute intro. Onboarding was automated. Scope was defined in the contract. ## Scripts and exact phrases

"What's your all-in cost to deliver this?" (Not just direct costs. Everything. Tools, time, admin, support, revisions, mistakes, refunds.)

"If you hired someone to replace you on this client, what would you pay them, and would there be any margin left?" (Forces the hourly math.)

"What are you paying for that you haven't used in 60 days?" (SaaS audit opener.)

"This offer is a margin killer. You're keeping 18%. You need 50% minimum or the business doesn't scale." (Direct on broken economics.)

"Let's lock the scope. One deliverable, one revision, two weeks max. Anything outside that is a new project." (Productized service boundary.)

"You can't cost-cut your way to a good save rate. You need to charge more or deliver faster." (When a founder is slashing everything.)

"Your save rate is 25%. That means for every <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->0K you earn, you're keeping <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->,500. Can you hire on that? Can you invest? Can you grow?" (The reality check.)

"Save rate below 40% means you're an employee in your own business. Above 60% means you're an owner." (The threshold.)

## Common mistakes

**Mistake 1: Not tracking it at all.** Most founders can tell you their revenue. Almost none can tell you their save rate. They don't track costs beyond the obvious ones. Tools, team, ads maybe. But not their own time. Not the hours spent on admin. Not the invisible drag of scope creep. You can't improve what you don't measure. [[Operations_audit_framework_2024_10_01]]

**Mistake 2: Valuing their time at zero.** "I don't count my hours because I'm the founder." Wrong. If you're spending 20 hours delivering a <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->K project and you can't hire someone to do it for less than <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->,500, your margin is $500. That's the real number. Pretending your time is free makes every offer look profitable when it's not. [[Founder_time_valuation_discussion_2024_11_10]]

**Mistake 3: Offering unlimited revisions or vague scope.** "We'll work with you until you're happy." That's not an offer, that's a commitment to unpredictable costs. One client will be happy in two hours. Another will request changes for three months. Your average save rate might be 40%, but the worst client is keeping you at 5%. Cut the scope or lose the client. [[Scope_creep_client_firing_2024_09_05]]

**Mistake 4: Adding team before fixing the margin.** "I need to hire someone to scale." Not if your save rate is 25%. Hiring on a broken margin just accelerates the cash burn. Fix the economics first. Get to 50%+ save rate. Then hire. The new person will be additive instead of dilutive. [[Hiring_decision_framework_2024_12_08]]

**Mistake 5: Keeping tools "just in case."** The $47/month CRM you haven't logged into in three months. The design tool you replaced but never cancelled. The webinar platform you used once. These add up. One founder was bleeding $600/month on dormant tools. That's $7,200/year. On <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->00K revenue, that's 7 points of margin. [[SaaS_subscription_audit_2024_11_15]]

**Mistake 6: Pricing based on market rates instead of margin.** "Everyone charges $3K for this, so I do too." If your costs are higher than theirs, you're subsidizing the market. Price for your margin, not for the competition. If you need 60% save rate and your costs are <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->K to deliver, you need to charge $5K minimum, not $3K. [[Pricing_psychology_margin_first_2025_01_22]]

## The contrarian read

Conventional wisdom says cut costs to improve margins. Fire the VA. Use free tools. Do everything yourself.

I say the opposite: invest in leverage, raise prices, and ruthlessly cut scope.

Cutting costs has a floor. You can only cut so much before the business breaks. But raising prices has no ceiling. And cutting scope — delivering less but delivering it better — is the unlock most founders never consider.

The founders keeping 70%+ save rates aren't the cheapest operators. They're the ones charging premium prices for tightly-scoped offers delivered fast with systems, not labour. They've automated admin, templatized delivery, and fired clients who didn't fit the model.

The other contrarian take: a 90% save rate on $5K/month is worse than a 50% save rate on $50K/month. Save rate without volume is a hobby. The goal isn't the highest percentage. The goal is the highest dollar amount kept. A founder keeping 50% of <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->00K is keeping $50K. A founder keeping 80% of <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->0K is keeping $8K. Revenue still matters.

The best operators optimize for both: high save rate AND high revenue. They charge premium prices, deliver fast, and scale with systems instead of people.

## What it looks like when it's working

Observable signals:

You can name your all-in cost per client or per project without opening a spreadsheet. You track it monthly. You know the number.

Your save rate is above 50%. Ideally above 60%. That means for every <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->0K earned, you're keeping $6K+. You can hire. You can invest. You can weather mistakes.

You've cut or automated at least three recurring costs in the last six months. You're not paying for tools you don't use. You're not overpaying for labour you could replace with a $50/month AI tool.

Your pricing is tied to your margin, not the market. If your costs go up, your price goes up. If delivery gets faster, your price stays the same and your save rate improves.

You can describe your offer scope in three sentences. There's no "we'll figure it out as we go." There's no "unlimited revisions." Every engagement has a clear start, clear end, and clear deliverables.

When a low-margin client shows up, you either charge them more or you don't take them. You've fired at least one client in the last year because the economics didn't work.

Before/after in numbers:

A founder keeping 22% of $30K/month is keeping $6,600. After rebuilding pricing, tightening scope, and cutting three unused tools, they're keeping 58% of $35K/month. That's <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->0,300. Same founder, same time spent, triple the dollars kept. That's the unlock. [[Save_rate_transformation_case_study_2025_02_15]]
