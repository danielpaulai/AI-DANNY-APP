## The core principle

Recurring revenue is a trap most service businesses walk into eyes wide open because they've been sold the SaaS dream without understanding they're not selling SaaS.

A subscription makes sense when the customer's problem recurs monthly and your solution solves it monthly without you showing up.

## Why this matters

The subscription model became gospel because every founder read the same Stripe Atlas blog post and decided monthly recurring revenue was the only legitimate business model.

What they missed: subscriptions work when the infrastructure runs without you, the customer gets continuous value without additional labour from your team, and the churn math supports the model.

Most service businesses fail all three tests.

The founder sells a workshop, delivers it once, then invoices monthly for "ongoing support" that nobody asked for and the client resents paying for.

Or they build a personal brand system, hand it to the client, then charge <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->,000/month to "maintain" a system that requires zero maintenance because it lives in the client's Claude account.

The cost of getting this wrong is predictable: 40-60% annual churn, constant firefighting to "prove value" every month, pricing conversations that never end, and a business model that punishes you for doing good work because the better the system you build, the less the client needs you next month.

The leverage of getting it right is the inverse: you charge what the work is worth upfront, the client owns the system, you're compensated for the transformation not the minutes, and you can take on fewer clients at higher fees because you're not trapped servicing 30 retainers.

## How it shows up in client work

The pattern I see most often: founder launches with project pricing, books 3-4 clients at $8K-<!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->5K each, delivers exceptional outcomes, then convinces themselves they need recurring revenue to "scale."

They restructure everything into a <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->,500/month retainer.

Six months later they're managing 12 clients, doing more work than before, and making less money because half the clients churned and the other half negotiated down.

The mistake isn't the subscription itself—it's applying subscription pricing to project-shaped work.

I worked with a founder in [[Personal_brand_coaching_session_2024_09_18]] who had built a phenomenal AI content system for her clients.

She charged <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->,500/month "for ongoing support and updates."

When I asked what the support actually included, she listed: "monthly strategy calls, content reviews, and keeping the Claude skills updated."

I asked how often clients actually used the monthly call.

Maybe 40% of them, she said.

I asked how often the skills needed updating.

Once every three months, maybe.

I asked what would break if she stopped charging monthly.

Nothing, she admitted.

The system lived in the client's Claude account, they owned it, they could run it without her.

She was charging <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->8,000 a year for work that required maybe $3,000 worth of her time.

The client was paying for a safety blanket, not a service.

She restructured to project pricing: <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->2,000 upfront for the build, $3,000 for a quarterly review if the client wanted one.

Revenue went up 40% in 90 days because she could take on more clients, close them faster, and stop playing customer success manager to people who didn't need managing.

The opposite pattern shows up too: founders who should offer subscriptions but refuse because they've internalized "retainers are bad."

I coached a founder in [[Sales_call_2025_01_22]] who ran LinkedIn ads for professional services firms.

He charged $8,000 for a 90-day campaign build.

The client would launch, see results, then ask him to keep managing the campaigns.

He'd say no, they should run it themselves now.

They'd run it badly, stop getting results, and blame him.

His problem wasn't the pricing model—it was that his service was genuinely recurring.

The client's problem (they need qualified leads every month) recurs monthly, and his solution (managing the ad campaigns) solves it monthly without requiring him to rebuild anything.

That's textbook subscription economics.

We restructured to $8,000 for the build, then <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->,500/month for ongoing management with a 90-day minimum.

Churn dropped to near zero because the value was obvious every month and clients who churned did it because the campaigns stopped working, not because they resented the fee.

The diagnostic I use: if the client could fire you today and the system would keep working without degradation for 6+ months, it's project work.

If the client firing you today means the system stops delivering value within 30 days, it's subscription work.

## Scripts and exact phrases

**When a client asks for a retainer and the work is project-shaped:**

"I'd actually push back on that. What you're buying is the system—the asset that keeps working after I'm gone. If you're paying me monthly, you're either paying for work that doesn't exist or you're paying me to own something you should own. Let's price this as the transformation, not the time."

**When you're tempted to add a recurring fee but can't name the recurring work:**

"What breaks if they stop paying me next month? If the answer is 'nothing,' I'm taxing them for my insecurity, not solving a recurring problem."

**When a client says 'I want ongoing support' but doesn't define it:**

"Walk me through what ongoing support actually means to you. Monthly calls? System updates? Content reviews? Let's name the specific work, estimate the hours, and decide if that's better as a subscription or as on-demand support billed separately."

**When restructuring a retainer client to project pricing:**

"Here's what I'm seeing: you built a system that runs without you, but you're charging monthly to babysit it. What if we restructured this: you charge the full value upfront for building the system, then offer quarterly reviews at $X if they want strategy help, but the system is theirs and you're not on the hook every month."

**When a client genuinely needs a subscription but fears retainers:**

"This isn't a retainer for the sake of recurring revenue. Your problem recurs monthly—you need leads, you need the campaigns managed, you need the system monitored. The subscription reflects the fact that I'm solving a recurring problem, not just billing you because SaaS founders told me to."

**When pricing a workshop or one-time engagement:**

"You're not buying access to me forever. You're buying the outcome of this session: the frameworks, the system, the breakthrough. If you want me back in three months, we can talk about a follow-up, but this is a one-time transformation, priced as a one-time fee."

## Common mistakes

**Mistake 1: Adding a subscription because you read it's how you "scale."**

You don't scale by invoicing the same 10 clients every month for work that doesn't recur.

You scale by building better systems, charging more upfront, and taking on more clients without increasing ongoing obligations.

**Mistake 2: Offering "ongoing support" without defining what it includes.**

Vague retainers breed resentment.

The client thinks they're buying unlimited access, you think you're billing for light maintenance, and six months in you're both unhappy.

Name the work, estimate the hours, and price it honestly.

**Mistake 3: Keeping a client on retainer because you're afraid they'll leave.**

If the only reason they're paying you monthly is inertia, they will leave.

Probably right after they've paid you just enough to feel resentful but not enough to feel committed.

**Mistake 4: Underpricing the project to make the subscription look better.**

Charging $3,000 for the build and <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->,500/month ongoing doesn't make you a SaaS founder.

It makes you someone who's hiding the real price across 12 invoices because you're afraid to ask for <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->1,000 upfront.

**Mistake 5: Refusing to offer subscriptions when the work genuinely recurs.**

If you're managing ad campaigns, monitoring systems, or delivering a service that requires monthly labour and solves a monthly problem, charge monthly.

Don't martyr yourself to "project-only" pricing because you read one thread about retainer traps.

## The contrarian read

The SaaS playbook ruined service pricing.

Every founder now thinks recurring revenue is the holy grail, so they force-fit subscription models onto work that isn't subscription-shaped.

The result: a generation of service businesses with 50% churn, constant scope creep, and founders who spend more time justifying the monthly invoice than delivering the work.

Here's the truth most won't say: recurring revenue is only valuable if the customer's problem recurs and your solution requires recurring work to solve it.

If you're building a system the client owns, charging monthly is either laziness (you didn't price the project correctly upfront) or fear (you're worried they won't pay <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->5,000 once, so you're spreading it across 12 months and calling it a retainer).

The highest-leverage move most founders can make is killing their retainers and repricing everything as project work.

Charge what the transformation is worth, deliver it, hand it over, and walk away.

If the client wants you back in three months, they'll pay you again because the first project was worth it, not because they're trapped in a recurring billing cycle they resent.

## What it looks like when it's working

**Project pricing done right:**

You close a <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->2,000 engagement in one conversation because the client sees the value, pays upfront, and owns the outcome.

You deliver over 30-60 days, hand over the system, and move on to the next client.

The client doesn't churn because there's nothing to churn from—they bought an asset, they own it, it works.

Six months later they refer you to two colleagues because the system is still running and they remember you as the person who solved their problem, not the vendor they're stuck paying every month.

**Subscription pricing done right:**

The client pays <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->,500/month because you're managing their ad campaigns, and if you stopped, the leads would stop within 30 days.

Churn is near zero because the value is obvious every month: campaigns run, leads show up, client is happy.

You're not justifying the invoice—you're delivering a service that solves a recurring problem with recurring work.

The relationship is clean: they're paying for work that happens, not for access to you or a safety blanket they never use.

**The hybrid model done right:**

You charge <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->0,000 for the build, then offer quarterly reviews at <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->,000 each if the client wants ongoing strategy.

Half your clients take the quarterly reviews, half don't.

The ones who don't are still happy because they own the system and it works without you.

The ones who do are happy because they're paying for real value (your strategic input) at a cadence that matches their actual need, not an arbitrary monthly billing cycle.
