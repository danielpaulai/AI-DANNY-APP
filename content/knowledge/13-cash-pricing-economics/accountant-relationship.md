## The core principle

You outsource what accountants are qualified to do. You keep what only you can do.

The line is sharp: they file the VAT, you approve the transactions. They handle compliance, you handle categorisation. They tell you what the law requires, you decide what the business needs.

## Why this matters

Most founders treat their accountant like a black box. Documents go in. Silence happens. A bill arrives. Maybe a question six weeks later when the tax office sends a letter.

This costs time. It costs money. It costs control.

The founder who doesn't understand what their accountant actually does will overpay for services they don't need and underpay for services they desperately do. They'll send documents late, categorise expenses wrong, and then wonder why the accountant charges €200 to fix what should have been a 10-minute job.

The leverage is in the handoff. If you know exactly what you're responsible for and exactly what they're responsible for, the relationship runs like infrastructure. Monthly. Predictable. Boring in the best way.

The cost of getting this wrong is a tax audit you weren't prepared for, a VAT bill you didn't budget for, or a compliance issue that freezes your ability to invoice clients. All preventable. All caused by not knowing where your job ends and theirs begins.

The cost of getting this right is near-zero friction. You categorise expenses as they happen. You confirm the month by the 15th. They file the VAT. The machine runs.

## How it shows up in client work

The pattern I see most often: founders who think their accountant should "just handle everything."

They don't log into the platform for weeks. They send receipts in bulk at month-end. They assume someone else is tracking what needs to be tracked. Then they're surprised when the accountant bills them for cleanup work or asks for documents they thought were already submitted.

The fix is always the same: define the weekly workflow.

In [[Introduction_-_Limited_Company_(Oy)_(Daniel_Paul_&_Jani_Salminen)_2025_05_05]], Jani from Ukko walked me through the exact handoff for a Finnish Oy. His explanation was clear: "Every bank transaction needs to have a corresponding voucher in bookkeeping and vice versa. You have until the 15th to complete the processing. Once you confirm the month, it's a signal to our team that everything is done on your end and we can file the VAT return."

That's the line. I categorise and confirm. They file and submit.

Before that call, I thought the accountant was supposed to log in, see the bank transactions, and figure it out. Wrong. My job is to assign every transaction to an expense or income category. Their job is to take that confirmed data and handle the legal filing.

The same pattern showed up in [[LSCS_Automation_2024_05_07]] when Fahad and I were building automation for Priyanka's London School of Cybersecurity. She was running multiple entities (LSCS, Sinevis) and wasn't clear on which costs belonged where. The accountant kept asking for clarifications because expenses were landing in the wrong company's books.

The solution wasn't hiring a better accountant. It was building a system where every invoice got tagged with the correct entity before it ever reached the accountant. If the founder doesn't know which entity an expense belongs to, the accountant can't file it correctly. That's founder work, not accountant work.

Another client ran a cohort-based training business. She had trainer costs, branding coach costs, infrastructure costs, and back-end support costs. All legitimate. All deductible. None of them categorised.

In [[Business_ideation_first_session_2024_02_23]] and [[Business_clarification_2024_02_17]], we worked through her P&L structure. She'd been treating "other costs" as a catch-all bucket. Her accountant couldn't parse it. When tax season came, she had to manually reconstruct what each line item meant. Cost her a week and an extra €500 in accountant hours.

The fix: break "other costs" into specific categories (back-end support, infrastructure, merch). Name them clearly. Assign them consistently. Now the accountant sees "Zoom subscription - Infrastructure" instead of "Other - €50" and knows exactly how to file it.

The best client relationship I've seen was with a founder who set a recurring Friday task: "Log into Ukko. Assign every transaction from the week. Confirm if the month has closed." Took 20 minutes. The accountant never had to chase him. VAT was filed on time every month. No surprise bills. No cleanup work.

The worst relationship I've seen was a founder who hired an accountant, gave them access to the bank, and then ignored every email. The accountant would send questions like "What is this €1,200 charge?" and get no response for three weeks. Eventually the accountant started categorising things as "miscellaneous" because they had no other option. Tax audit flagged it. Cost the founder €8,000 in back taxes and penalties.

That audit was avoidable. All it required was answering the accountant's questions within 48 hours and tagging expenses correctly from the start.

The other mistake I see: founders who think their accountant should advise on business structure, cashflow strategy, and pricing models.

That's not their job. Their job is compliance. They tell you what the law requires. They don't tell you whether to bill monthly or quarterly, whether to raise your prices, or whether to take a dividend or a salary. Those are business decisions, not accounting decisions.

In [[Building nasops transcript-based documentation and email generation app - 78e52862]], I was working with the ops team at NAS.IO. Alex (COO) kept asking me questions about VAT registration thresholds, entity structure for international contractors, and cashflow forecasting. I told him: "Ask your accountant about the VAT threshold. Ask your CFO or finance advisor about cashflow. The accountant will tell you the legal answer. The finance advisor will tell you the strategic answer."

The accountant is infrastructure. The finance advisor is strategy. Don't confuse the two.

## Specific examples from the vault

The clearest example of this principle in action came from [[Introduction_-_Limited_Company_(Oy)_(Daniel_Paul_&_Jani_Salminen)_2025_05_05]].

Jani walked me through the Ukko platform for my Finnish Oy. He explained: "Now that the month of April has ended, we will be looking into things that were paid in April and you have until the 15th to complete the processing. Once you are done, you can then confirm the month. Once you have confirmed the month, it is a signal to our team that everything is done on your end and we can then carry on to filing the VAT return to the tax office."

He then showed me the two-part workflow. First, the bank side: every transaction shows up under "Account Transactions." Second, the bookkeeping side: every transaction needs a corresponding voucher under "Incomes" or "Expenses."

He demonstrated: "You can either start by adding an expense directly on the expenses page. We'll always need three things for each expense, namely the attachment, the entry details, and the corresponding bank transaction. You could do it in reverse order, so you could start instead from the bank transaction itself."

He clicked on a transaction, clicked "Assign," selected the amount, clicked "Create new expense." The system created a draft voucher with the bank transaction already linked. Then he said: "The bank transaction and the bookkeeping voucher have now been linked to each other. If we now were to go back, we would see that this bank transaction is now assigned. The voucher itself, as we didn't complete it, is going to be incomplete. That would be found under your expenses page."

That ten-minute explanation saved me hours. I knew exactly what my weekly task was: log in, assign transactions, upload receipts, confirm the month by the 15th. Everything after that was their job.

In [[LSCS_Automation_2024_05_07]], Priyanka was running two entities: LSCS (training business) and Sinevis (consulting). She had trainer costs, infrastructure costs, and marketing costs. Some expenses belonged to LSCS, some to Sinevis, some split between both.

Her accountant kept asking: "Which entity does this belong to?" She didn't know. Every month ended with a 30-minute call where they reconstructed her P&L together.

Fahad (the automation specialist on the call) and I built a simple Airtable workflow. Every invoice came in tagged with LSCS, Sinevis, or Split. If Split, she entered the percentage. The system auto-generated two separate line items for the accountant. No more reconstruction calls. The accountant got clean data every month.

That's founder work. The accountant can't decide which entity an expense belongs to. Only the founder knows that.

In [[Business_clarification_2024_02_17]], Priyanka and I were working through her cohort financials. She had "other costs" as a catch-all line item for back-end support, Zoom subscriptions, and merchandise.

I asked: "What's in other costs?"

She said: "Just back-end stuff. Zoom. Some merch."

I said: "Your accountant can't file 'back-end stuff.' Break it into specific categories. Back-end support. Infrastructure. Merch. Name them clearly."

She pushed back: "Does it really matter?"

I said: "It matters when you get audited and the tax office asks what 'other costs' means. It matters when you're applying for a business loan and the bank wants to see a clean P&L. It matters when you're trying to forecast cashflow and you don't know what's recurring vs one-time."

She rebuilt the P&L. Three categories. Clean labels. The accountant never asked for clarification again.

That's the founder's job: knowing what the expense is for and naming it correctly. The accountant's job: filing it correctly once it's named.

## Scripts and exact phrases

"I categorise and confirm. You file and submit."

"What's the deadline for me to confirm the month?" (Use this exact question in your first call with any new accountant.)

"I need to know: what am I responsible for, what are you responsible for, and where do we hand off?"

"Can you walk me through the platform once so I know exactly what my weekly workflow looks like?"

"I'll send receipts within 48 hours of the expense. If I'm late, send me a reminder."

"If you need clarification on a transaction, email me immediately. I'll respond same-day."

"I don't need tax strategy advice. I need compliance handled correctly. If I need strategy, I'll hire a CFO or finance advisor."

"Let's set a recurring monthly check-in where you tell me if anything is missing or miscategorised."

## Common mistakes

**Mistake 1: Treating the accountant like a financial advisor.**

They're compliance specialists, not strategists. They'll tell you what you're legally required to do. They won't tell you whether to raise prices, take a dividend, or restructure your offers. That's not their job.

If you need business advice, hire a fractional CFO or finance advisor. If you need compliance handled, use your accountant. Don't ask one person to do both jobs.

**Mistake 2: Sending documents in bulk at month-end.**

This guarantees cleanup fees. The accountant has to reconstruct what happened instead of processing clean data. It also guarantees delays. If something is miscategorised, you won't find out until the 20th when the VAT filing is already late.

Send receipts within 48 hours. Categorise expenses as they happen. Confirm the month by the 15th. This is the workflow.

**Mistake 3: Not logging into the platform for weeks.**

If you're not looking at the data, you don't know what's incomplete. The accountant can't file a VAT return on incomplete data. They'll send you reminder emails. You'll ignore them. The deadline passes. You get fined.

Set a recurring weekly task: "Log in. Assign transactions. Upload receipts." 20 minutes. Do it Friday afternoon. This prevents every late fee you'll ever pay.

**Mistake 4: Using vague category names like "other costs" or "miscellaneous."**

The accountant can't file this. The tax office can't audit this. You can't forecast this. Name every category specifically. If it's Zoom, call it "Zoom - Infrastructure." If it's trainer fees, call it "Trainer Fees - Cohort 3."

Clear labels = clean books = no cleanup fees.

**Mistake 5: Not knowing which entity an expense belongs to.**

If you run multiple entities (Oy, sole proprietorship, multiple companies), you need to know which entity every expense belongs to. The accountant can't decide this for you. If you send them an invoice without specifying, they'll file it wherever and you'll fix it at tax season.

Tag every invoice with the entity name before you send it to the accountant.

**Mistake 6: Assuming the accountant will chase you.**

Good accountants don't chase. They send one reminder. If you don't respond, they file what they have or they stop working. Either way, you lose.

Respond to every accountant email within 48 hours. If they're asking a question, they need the answer to do their job. If you don't answer, their job doesn't get done.

## The contrarian read

Most founders think hiring a more expensive accountant will solve their problems.

It won't.

The bottleneck isn't the accountant's expertise. It's the quality of the data you're giving them.

If you send messy data to a €500/month accountant, you'll get messy books. If you send clean data to a €150/month accountant, you'll get clean books. The difference isn't the accountant. It's you.

The other contrarian take: most founders overpay for services they don't need and underpay for services they desperately do.

Example: paying €300/month for "full-service bookkeeping" when all you need is VAT filing. Or paying €150/month for compliance and then trying to get strategic cashflow advice from the same person.

Match the service to the need. If you're a solo founder with one entity and predictable expenses, you need basic compliance. That's €100-€200/month. If you're running multiple entities with international contractors and complex revenue splits, you need a finance team. That's €1,000+/month.

Don't pay for the finance team when you only need compliance. Don't pay for compliance and then expect finance strategy.

## What it looks like when it's working

You log in every Friday. Takes 20 minutes. You assign every transaction from the week. You upload the receipts. If the month has closed, you confirm it by the 15th.

The accountant files the VAT by the 20th. No emails. No questions. No cleanup fees.

You check your dashboard once a month. Everything is categorised. Every transaction is assigned. Your P&L is clean. Your cashflow is predictable.

When tax season comes, the accountant sends you a summary. You review it. You approve it. Done.

No surprises. No audits. No late fees. No "we need to talk about Q3" emails in December.

The signal it's working: your accountant never chases you, you never chase them, and the monthly bill is always the same.
