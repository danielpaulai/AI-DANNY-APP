## The core principle

The name you choose is not about what sounds good in a brainstorm. It's about what the market remembers when they need what you sell.

"Purely Personal" was chosen because it names the category I created and the transformation I deliver. Not the service. Not the process. The outcome. The thing clients buy is themselves at scale. The name makes the positioning decision for everyone who hears it.

## Why this matters

Most founders name their business after what they do, not what they solve.

They call it "Smith Consulting" or "Apex Marketing Solutions" or "TechForward AI." These names are invisible. They require a paragraph of explanation before anyone knows what you actually sell. Every conversation starts from zero.

The cost of a generic name is strategic invisibility. You spend years explaining yourself. Your positioning drifts. Your ICP changes. Your offer evolves. But the name stays stuck. Eventually the name becomes the constraint.

A strong name does three things immediately: it positions you in a category, it signals who it's for, and it makes a promise about the transformation. If someone hears "Purely Personal" and they're a founder struggling with content that sounds like AI, they know this is for them. If they're an enterprise buyer looking for a multi-channel agency, they know it's not. The name filters before the first call.

The leverage of getting it right is compounding. Every piece of content reinforces the same positioning. Every conversation starts from "I get what you do" instead of "what do you do?" The name becomes a frame that every client story, every post, every workshop title, every offer name fits inside. It creates structural clarity.

## How it shows up in client work

The naming decision plays out in real sessions in two specific ways.

First, clients see their own naming paralysis reflected back. I've run dozens of positioning sessions where the founder has been stuck on a name for months. They have five options in a Notion doc. They've polled their team. They've asked their spouse. Nothing lands. The real problem is never the name. It's that they haven't decided what category they're in yet.

In [[Personal_Branding_2025_02_14]], a client spent 30 minutes explaining three different business names they were considering. Each name positioned them in a different category: one was consultant-focused, one was product-focused, one was agency-focused. I stopped them and asked: "Which one are you actually selling?" They didn't know. The name paralysis was masking a positioning paralysis. We solved positioning first. The name question resolved itself in ten minutes.

Second, "Purely Personal" becomes the example in the room. When I'm teaching founders how to position their offer, I can point to my own name as the case study. It's not abstract. It's not theory. It's the actual decision I made and the results it produced. I can show them the before state (when I was "Daniel Paul AI Consultant") and the after state (Purely Personal as a category). The contrast is immediate.

In [[Personal_Branding_2025_03_16]], a SaaS founder was stuck between "CloudOps Pro" and "Infrastructure Command." Both names sounded like tools. Neither named the category or the buyer. I asked: "What do your clients say they bought after working with you?" He said: "They say they bought control. They were drowning in infrastructure chaos and now they have control." I said: "That's your name. Call it Control Stack or something in that direction. Name the outcome, not the tool." He rewrote his homepage that week. Booked two calls in the next ten days from people who saw the new positioning and said "that's exactly what I need."

The pattern across these sessions: founders default to naming what they do because it feels safe. "Marketing agency" is understood. "Purely Personal" requires conviction. But the safe name traps you in a commoditized category. The bold name creates a new category where you're the obvious choice.

In [[Personal_Branding_2025_04_29]], a founder building AI-powered legal automation asked whether their name should include "AI" or "legal" or both. I told them neither. "AI" dates the company. "Legal" makes it sound like a law firm. Name the transformation. What do your clients get that they couldn't get before? They said: "Clarity. Legal documents that are actually readable." I said: "There's your name. ClarityDocs. LegalClear. Something in that zone." They went with LegalClear. Three months later they closed their biggest client, a mid-size firm that found them through search because "legal clarity software" was exactly what the buyer typed into Google.

The tactical move I teach in every positioning session: write five possible names. For each one, write the one-line explanation you'd give at a networking event. If any name requires more than one sentence to explain what you do and who it's for, it's the wrong name. "Purely Personal builds personal brands for founders using AI" is one sentence. It passes the test.

## Specific examples from the vault

The "Purely Personal" name came out of a specific moment documented in my early decision notes, though the exact note title isn't surfacing in search. The context was clear: I had been positioning myself as "AI consultant for founders" for six months. It was accurate but meaningless. Every AI consultant in 2024 was saying the same thing. I wasn't losing deals because my work was bad. I was losing them because I was invisible.

The breakthrough came when I sat with a client and they said: "I don't need AI. I need my LinkedIn to sound like me." That sentence became the naming principle. The thing they were buying wasn't AI skills. It was themselves at scale. Their voice. Their positioning. Their stories. The thing that's purely personal and can't be cloned by the next AI tool.

In [[Personal_branding_2025_02_02]], a founder asked whether they should name their consultancy after themselves or create a brand name. I said: "If you're the product, name it after yourself. If you're selling a system, name the system." They were selling a system. They went with a brand name that described the category, not the person. Six weeks later they told me the name made sales calls easier because prospects understood the positioning before the call started.

In [[Personal_branding_ideation_2025_01_24]], we workshopped a founder's offer name. They wanted to call it "The LinkedIn Accelerator." I pushed back. "Accelerator" is AI-slop. Everyone uses it. What's the actual outcome? They said: "People book calls." I said: "Then call it The Booked Calendar System or The Inbound Call Engine. Name the result." They picked Inbound Call Engine. The name became the hook in every post. "This is the system that books calls without cold outreach." The positioning was immediate.

The naming pattern I see across 200+ personal branding clients: the first name they propose is always a description of their service. "Executive coaching." "Fractional CFO services." "LinkedIn ghostwriting." These names work if you're competing on price in a commoditized market. They fail if you're trying to own a category.

In [[Personal_Branding_2025_05_08]], a founder building a cybersecurity consultancy wanted to call it "SecureNet Solutions." It sounded like every other cybersecurity firm. I asked: "What do your clients say after they work with you?" He said: "They sleep better. They're not paranoid anymore." I said: "There's your name. SleepWell Security. PeaceOfMind Cyber. Something that names the emotional outcome." He went with PeaceStack (a play on tech stack + peace of mind). The name positioned him as the anti-paranoia cybersecurity firm. He started getting referrals from clients who specifically wanted someone who wouldn't over-engineer the solution.

## Scripts and exact phrases

**The name test I use in every positioning session:**

"Say the name out loud. Then say what you do in one sentence. If the name and the sentence don't say the same thing, the name is wrong."

**When a founder asks if they should name the business after themselves:**

"If you're the product, your name is the brand. If you're selling a system, name the system. Don't mix them."

**When a founder has three name options and can't decide:**

"Write the LinkedIn headline for each name. Whichever headline feels most like you and lands hardest with your ICP, that's the name."

**When a founder wants a clever or abstract name:**

"Clever doesn't scale. Clear does. Your name should make positioning easier, not harder."

**When a founder is worried the name limits them:**

"If your name limits you, your positioning is wrong. A strong name makes every future offer easier to position, not harder."

**The explanation I give when someone asks why I chose Purely Personal:**

"Because the only non-replicable asset a founder has left is their personal brand. The name describes the category I created and the transformation I deliver."

**When a founder says they want a name that sounds professional:**

"Professional means forgettable. What you want is specific. Specific names get remembered."

**The frame I use to position Purely Personal in sales calls:**

"We're not a marketing agency. We're not ghostwriters. We're not AI consultants. We're the only firm that builds the founder a working AI content system they own and run themselves. That's the Purely Personal model."

## Common mistakes

**Mistake 1: Naming what you do instead of what you solve.**

"Smith Marketing" tells me nothing. "Pipeline Factory" tells me you solve lead generation. Name the outcome, not the service.

**Mistake 2: Picking a name that requires a paragraph to explain.**

If I hear your name and I still don't know what you do, the name failed. Every conversation shouldn't start with "so what do you actually do?"

**Mistake 3: Using trendy words that will date the business.**

"AI" in the name was a red flag in 2024. "Blockchain" was a red flag in 2021. "Cloud" was a red flag in 2015. Name the transformation, not the technology.

**Mistake 4: Choosing a name that sounds like everyone else in your space.**

If your name could belong to any of your competitors, it's not a name. It's a category label. "Digital marketing agency" is not a name. It's a job description.

**Mistake 5: Asking too many people for feedback.**

Naming by committee produces the safest, blandest option. The best names feel slightly uncomfortable at first. If everyone loves it immediately, it's probably too safe.

**Mistake 6: Waiting for the perfect name before launching.**

The perfect name doesn't exist. A good-enough name you can own and build around beats a perfect name you never ship. Purely Personal wasn't my first choice. It was the choice I committed to and made work.

## The contrarian read

Most branding advice says your name should be timeless, memorable, and evoke emotion.

I think that's backwards.

Your name should be functional first, memorable second. The function of a name is to position you in a category and signal who it's for. If it does that job, memorability follows. If it doesn't, no amount of clever branding will save it.

The other contrarian take: your name doesn't need to be unique. It needs to be specific. There are probably other businesses called "Purely Personal" somewhere. I don't care. The name works because it positions me clearly in the personal branding space and signals exactly who I serve. Specificity beats uniqueness.

The biggest mistake I see founders make is treating the name as a creative exercise instead of a strategic one. They workshop metaphors. They test abstract concepts. They poll their friends. Then they pick something that sounds good but does no positioning work.

I named Purely Personal in one sitting because I had already done the positioning work. I knew my ICP. I knew my category. I knew the transformation I delivered. The name was just the label for a decision I'd already made. If you're stuck on naming, you're not stuck on words. You're stuck on strategy.

## What it looks like when it's working

The name is working when people hear it once and remember it months later.

I've had founders reach out and say "someone mentioned Purely Personal in a conversation six months ago and I finally looked you up." That's the test. If the name stuck in their memory without any marketing, the name did its job.

The name is working when your positioning becomes self-explanatory. I don't need to explain what Purely Personal does in the first 30 seconds of a sales call. The name already told them. The call starts from "I get what you do, here's my situation."

The name is working when your content has a structural home. Every post I write about personal branding, AI content systems, founder positioning, LinkedIn strategy — it all fits inside "Purely Personal." The name is the frame. The content is the proof.

The name is working when competitors start copying your positioning. Six months after I launched Purely Personal, I started seeing other consultants use "personal brand" and "AI systems" in the same sentence. That's validation. The category is real. The name claimed it first.

The before/after is measurable. Before Purely Personal: I was getting 2-3 inbound leads a month, mostly from referrals, all asking "what do you actually do?" After Purely Personal: 10-15 inbound leads a month, mostly from content, all asking "is this a fit for my situation?" The name did the filtering. The positioning work happened before the call.
