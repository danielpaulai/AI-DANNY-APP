## The core principle

Every time Danny raised prices, revenue went up and clients got better results.

Not some of the time. Every single time. The pattern held across workshops, 1:1 coaching, done-with-you builds, and retainer work. The decision to charge more has never once cost him a deal he should have taken.

## Why this matters

Price is the fastest lever in business and the one founders refuse to pull.

They'll spend six months rebuilding a landing page. They'll hire a VA. They'll add another lead magnet. They'll launch a challenge. All of those moves take weeks to see results and most of them fail.

Raising prices takes one email and works immediately.

The mechanism is simple. When you raise prices, three things happen at once. You filter out the wrong clients who were going to drain you anyway. You attract clients who expect transformation, not transactions. And you force yourself to deliver at a level that justifies the new number.

The cost of not raising prices is worse than lost revenue. It's positioning damage that compounds. Every client who pays below-market rates trains you to accept less. Every proposal you send at the old price reinforces to the market that you're the cheaper option. Every month you delay costs you the confidence to charge what the next tier charges.

The founders who suffer most are the ones with proven results who are still charging early-stage prices. They deliver $50K outcomes and charge $5K. They know they're underpriced. They have the testimonials. They just can't make themselves send the new number.

The leverage of getting this right is immediate and permanent. Danny has never dropped a price after raising it. Once you've charged the new rate successfully once, you can't go back. The market resets around you.

## How it shows up in client work

The pattern shows up in every 1:1 personal branding coaching engagement.

Founder books a call. First question Danny asks in discovery: "What are you charging today and what do you want to charge in 12 months?" The gap between those two numbers is the entire engagement mapped out in one question.

Most founders are charging 30% to 50% below what their closest competitor charges for the same outcome. They have the proof. They have the testimonials. They have clients getting results. They just haven't raised prices because they're afraid of the objection.

The breakthrough happens in session three or four. Danny makes them walk through the math. "You're charging $3K. Your closest competitor charges $6K. You both deliver the same outcome. What is your client actually choosing when they pick you?" The answer is always the same: they're choosing the discount, not the value.

Then Danny forces the decision. "What happens if you send the next proposal at $6K and they say no?" The founder lists three fears: they'll lose the deal, they'll look greedy, they'll have to justify the price. Danny flips it: "And what happens if they say yes?" That's the unlock. They realise the downside is one lost deal they probably shouldn't take. The upside is proving to themselves they can charge double.

## Common mistakes

Raising prices and then immediately offering a discount when the client hesitates.

If you quote $5K and the client says "That's higher than I expected," the wrong move is to say "I can do $4K if that helps." You just trained them to negotiate on every future engagement. The right move is to say "What's the gap between what you expected and what I quoted, and what would need to be true for $5K to feel like a deal?"

Raising prices without changing the positioning.

If you're known as the affordable option and you suddenly charge premium rates without explaining the shift, clients will assume you're just greedy. Danny raised workshop prices from $499 to $997 and added the line "This is not an overview. You will leave with a working system." The new price required new framing. The positioning shift justified the price shift.

Grandfathering old clients at old rates indefinitely.

Danny made this mistake in 2024. First retainer client paid <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->K/month. Second client paid $3K/month. First client found out about the price difference and felt undervalued. Danny had to have an uncomfortable conversation about rate resets. The rule now: grandfather pricing lasts one renewal cycle, then everyone moves to current rates or the engagement ends.

Justifying the price with a value stack breakdown.

When a client asks "Why does this cost <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->2K?", the instinct is to list every deliverable and calculate the hours. That's the wrong frame. It positions you as a cost, not an investment. The right answer is "Because clients who go through this system book an average of 8 inbound calls in 90 days. If one of those calls closes, you've made your money back 5x. If none of them close, you weren't ready for this yet."

Raising prices but keeping the same scope.

Danny learned this from Taki Moore. If you're going to charge more, deliver more or deliver faster. When Danny raised 1:1 coaching from $3K to $5K, he didn't add more sessions. He added a post-engagement 30-day review and a skill library template pack. The scope shift gave clients permission to say yes at the higher price.

Apologising for the price.

"I know this is expensive, but..." is the fastest way to lose the deal. If you're apologising for your price before the client has even objected, you're telling them they should negotiate. Danny's rule: state the price once, with confidence, then stop talking. The first person to speak loses.

## The contrarian read

Most pricing advice says to raise prices gradually: 10% this year, 10% next year, ease the market into it.

Danny's experience is the opposite. The big jumps work better than the slow creep.

When you raise prices 10%, clients notice and wonder if you're worth the extra $300. When you raise prices 100%, you're signalling a category shift. You're not a better version of the old thing. You're a different thing entirely. The objection doesn't come because the client knows they're comparing apples to oranges.

Danny jumped workshop pricing from $499 to $997 in one move. If he'd gone $499 → $599 → $699 → $799 → $997 over 18 months, every single price increase would have felt like a tax. The client would have thought "Why is this more expensive than last time?" Instead, the jump to $997 signalled "This is a different workshop for a different buyer." No one questioned it.

The second contrarian take: discounting is worse than losing the deal.

Most founders think the point of a sales conversation is to close. So when the client hesitates, they offer a payment plan or a discount or a scope reduction to keep the deal alive. Danny's rule is the opposite: the point of a sales conversation is to close the right deals at the right price. Losing a deal that would have required a discount is a win, not a loss.

In 2025, Danny sent 47 proposals across 1:1 coaching, retainer builds, and workshop bookings. Nine proposals got ghosted. Not one of those nine was a deal he should have chased. Every one of them would have been a difficult client, a scope-creep nightmare, or a three-month engagement that paid below his effective hourly rate. Losing those deals at full price was better than closing them at a discount.

## What it looks like when it's working

Revenue per client goes up but total client count stays flat or drops slightly.

Danny served 23 clients in 2024 and made <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->80K. He served 19 clients in 2025 and made $340K. Fewer clients, higher prices, better outcomes. The math works because higher-paying clients are easier to serve. They're more committed, they act faster, they respect your time, and they refer other high-paying clients.

You stop negotiating and start diagnosing fit.

When pricing is right, discovery calls shift from "Can I convince them to buy?" to "Should I take this client?" Danny now disqualifies 40% of discovery calls before price even comes up. If the client isn't coachable, if they're looking for a vendor instead of a partner, if they hesitate when you ask "Are you ready to act on this in the next 30 days?", the call ends early. Pricing power gives you permission to be selective.

Clients stop asking "Why does this cost $X?" and start asking "When can we start?"

In 2024, half of Danny's proposals led to a price conversation. In 2025, two out of 47 proposals led to a price conversation. The shift happened because pricing became congruent with positioning. When you're the premium option and you show up as the premium option, clients expect premium pricing. The question isn't "Why so expensive?" It's "How fast can we move?"

Testimonials get stronger because clients are more invested.

A $3K client who gets a good result says "Danny helped me build my content system." A <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->2K client who gets the same result says "Danny took me from invisible to 10 inbound calls a month in 90 days. Best investment I've made this year." The outcome is the same. The testimonial is different because the investment was higher. Clients who pay more talk louder.
