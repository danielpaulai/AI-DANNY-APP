## The core principle

Teaching at scale is not about reach. It's about proving the system works before you sell it to one more person.

## Why this matters

Most founders who teach do it backwards. They build the course, market it, sell it, then scramble to deliver something that doesn't break under the weight of 50 paying students asking questions you didn't anticipate.

The Maven course decision was the opposite. It was a deliberate choice to test a content system, a marketing approach, and a pricing model with real money and real consequences before calling it a product.

Teaching at scale forces honesty. You can't fake your way through a live cohort. You can't ship broken slides to 30 people who paid <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->,599 and expect them to stay quiet. Every gap in your thinking becomes visible. Every assumption you made about "this will be easy to explain" gets tested in real time with people who have no context for your shortcuts.

The leverage is obvious: one session taught to 20 people replaces 20 one-on-one calls. But the real value is what you learn. A workshop exposes which parts of your methodology actually land and which parts only make sense inside your head. It shows you which objections come up every single time and which ones you invented. It tells you what people are willing to pay for and what they'll ignore even when you give it away for free.

The cost of skipping this step is invisible until it's too late. You build a course on assumptions, sell it to 100 people, and realize halfway through that the promise doesn't match the delivery. Now you're stuck. You either refund everyone and lose credibility, or you finish the cohort knowing it's not good enough and hope nobody talks about it publicly.

Teaching at scale is the last real test before you productize. It's the difference between "I think this works" and "I've shipped this to 30 paying clients and it works."

## How it shows up in client work

The Maven course shows up in every conversation I have with a founder who wants to build a course, run a workshop, or scale their expertise beyond 1:1 work.

The first question is always: have you taught this live to paying clients? Not free webinars. Not YouTube videos. Not LinkedIn posts. Paying clients who showed up expecting a result and would ask for a refund if you didn't deliver.

Most of them say no. They have an outline. They have slides. They have a Notion doc full of "modules" that nobody has ever seen. They want to pre-record it, put it on a platform, and charge $997. I tell them the same thing every time: teach it live first. Sell 10 spots. Deliver it. Fix what breaks. Then decide if you want to scale it.

The Maven course was that test for me. I'd been running AI workshops for founders since early 2024. By August I had taught maybe 50 sessions across different formats. I knew the content worked. I knew the outcomes were real. But I didn't know if the model was scalable.

The decision to run it through Maven came from a conversation with Priyanka in [[Maven_update_2024_05_06]]. We were trying to figure out how to move from one-off workshops to a repeatable offer. The bootcamp model at LSCS had failed because we tried to sell <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->,599 without the social proof to back it up. The price wasn't wrong. The positioning was.

Maven solved that. It gave us a platform with built-in credibility. It gave us a cohort model that forced live delivery. It gave us a forcing function: you ship the course by this date or it doesn't happen.

The price was <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->,599. I'd never charged that much for a single workshop before. The highest I'd gone was maybe $500 for a two-hour session. But the promise was different. This wasn't "learn AI tools." This was "build your entire marketing system in Claude and walk away owning it."

The first cohort sold 12 spots. Not 30. Not 50. Twelve. Enough to make it worth running, not enough to feel like I'd figured it out.

I spent three months rebuilding the curriculum. The sessions were originally designed as standalone workshops. Each one had to become a chapter in a 4-week build sequence. I had to map dependencies: which skills did they need to build first before the next session made sense? What could I assume they'd remember from week 1 when we hit week 3?

[[Maven_Revamp_2025_03_11]] and [[Maven_Revamp_2_2025_03_12]] document the rebuild. I rewrote every session from scratch. I cut everything that didn't directly contribute to the end deliverable: a Claude Projects setup with 7 working skills and a 90-day content calendar.

The feedback from the first cohort told me three things:

1. The system worked. People shipped LinkedIn posts in their own voice by session 2.
2. The pacing was wrong. Week 1 was too slow. Week 3 was too dense.
3. The promise needed to be sharper. "Build a content system" is vague. "Go from 12 hours a week on LinkedIn to 2" is a number.

I ran it again. Sold 18 spots. Fixed the pacing. Tightened the promise. Added more live walkthroughs and fewer slide decks.

By the third run I had the full model: 4 live sessions, 90-minute walkthroughs, homework assignments with deadlines, a Slack channel for questions, and a final deliverable that every student shipped by week 4.

The Maven course became the proof case for Purely Personal. When a founder asks "can AI actually do this?" I send them a link to the Maven testimonials. When someone says "I don't think AI can write in my voice," I show them the week 2 outputs from past students.

It also became a filter. The people who sign up for a <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->,599 course are different from the people who download a free checklist. They have budget. They have urgency. They're willing to do the work. That's the ICP for Purely Personal: founders who will pay for a system, not a tool.

The decision to teach at scale wasn't about scaling revenue. It was about validating the model under real conditions. You can fake a landing page. You can fake a pitch deck. You can't fake 30 people asking why their Claude Skill isn't working and needing an answer in the next 10 minutes.

## Specific examples from the vault

The clearest example of why the Maven course mattered is in [[Lscs_bootcamp_2024_09_05]]. Priyanka and I were trying to figure out how to sell a <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->,599 cybersecurity bootcamp. The content was strong. DJ was a solid trainer. But we had no social proof and no platform credibility.

I told her: "If you're selling 1599, you either need to be known, have the social proof, or have the track record in the field. Which one do we have?"

She said the track record. But that wasn't enough. A <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->,599 offer without a sales process, without testimonials, without a visible funnel, is a $5K-<!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->0K monthly ad spend just to break even. We didn't have that budget.

The lesson: price without proof is friction. Maven gave me the proof. The platform, the cohort model, the testimonials from graduates. That's what made the <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->,599 price defensible.

Another example: [[Maven_2024_11_22]] was a dry run with Psy Trainer to walk through the entire session arc. I recorded myself teaching session 1 to a fake cohort. The goal was to catch the moments where I assumed too much, where the pacing lagged, where the technical setup would break.

Two things broke immediately:

1. The opening hook was weak. I started with "let me show you Claude Projects." Nobody cares about the tool. They care about the outcome. I rewrote it to: "By end of today you'll have a Claude Skill that writes LinkedIn posts in your voice. Here's what that looks like."

2. The live demo took 18 minutes. Way too long. People zone out after 7. I cut it to 9 minutes and broke the rest into homework.

That dry run saved the first cohort. If I'd shipped that weak hook to paying students on day 1, half of them would have mentally checked out.

[[Maven_Final_Marketing_2024_10_17]] documents the landing page copy for cohort 2. The promise had shifted from "learn AI for marketing" to "build your LinkedIn content system in 4 weeks." That shift came directly from the feedback in cohort 1. Nobody remembered the tools. They remembered the outcome: "I can finally post consistently without it taking my whole weekend."

The contrarian part: most people would have taken that feedback and made the course longer. More modules. More tools. More templates. I did the opposite. I cut two entire sessions and consolidated everything into the 7 core skills. Shorter course, sharper outcome.

## Common mistakes

**Mistake 1: Building the entire course before teaching it once.**

Founders spend months building slides, recording videos, writing workbooks. Then they launch and realize the first module doesn't land. Now they're stuck with 47 pre-recorded videos that don't work. Teach it live. Break it. Fix it. Then record.

**Mistake 2: Pricing based on hours instead of outcomes.**

"It's a 6-week course so it should cost $X." Wrong. The price is the value of the outcome, not the length of the curriculum. A 90-minute workshop that saves a founder 10 hours a week is worth more than a 6-week course that teaches them theory.

**Mistake 3: Selling to the wrong platform.**

Instagram ads for a <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->,599 B2B course is a <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->0K/month burn with no conversions. The Maven course worked because the platform pre-qualified the audience. If you don't have platform credibility, find a partner who does or sell direct through your own audience.

**Mistake 4: Skipping the dry run.**

The first time you teach the session is not the time to discover your demo doesn't work, your hook is weak, or your pacing is off. Run it with a friend. Record it. Watch it back. Fix it before you charge money.

**Mistake 5: Teaching theory instead of building in the room.**

Most courses are "here's how it works." The Maven course was "here's your Claude Skill. You're building it right now. I'll help if you get stuck." People remember what they built, not what you explained.

**Mistake 6: Not collecting feedback immediately.**

I sent a feedback form after every session. Not at the end of the course. After every session. That's how I knew week 3 was too dense. Waiting until the end means you can't fix it for the people who are in the room right now.

## The contrarian read

The conventional wisdom is that courses need to be evergreen, pre-recorded, and passive income. You build it once, sell it forever, scale to infinity.

That's a trap.

Pre-recorded courses have a 3-7% completion rate. Nobody finishes them. The people who do finish don't implement. The ones who implement don't get results because there's no accountability.

Live cohorts are harder. You have to show up. You have to answer questions. You can't automate your way out of delivery. But that's the point.

The Maven course worked because it was live. Students had to show up or fall behind. I had to show up or lose credibility. The forcing function made it real.

The other contrarian take: charging <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->,599 for a 4-week course is not expensive. It's cheap if the outcome is worth <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->0K. Most founders under-price their courses because they compare themselves to Udemy. Wrong comparison. Compare yourself to the cost of hiring someone to do this work for them.

A marketing agency charges $5K/month minimum for content. If the Maven course replaces that and the system stays with the founder, <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->,599 is a steal.

The last contrarian read: teaching at scale doesn't mean teaching to 1,000 people. It means teaching to 10-30 people in a way that's repeatable. Bigger cohorts aren't better. They're noisier. You lose the ability to fix problems in real time.

I capped Maven at 30. That's the right size. Small enough to know everyone's name. Big enough to make it financially worth running.

## What it looks like when it's working

You know the Maven course model is working when:

1. **Students ship the deliverable by week 4.** Not "I'll finish it later." They ship. In the room. With feedback.

2. **The feedback is specific, not vague.** "Week 3 was too dense" is useful. "This was great" is not. Working courses generate specific feedback because people are actually using the system.

3. **You can teach the same session to cohort 3 without rewriting it.** The first cohort is R&D. The second is refinement. The third should be repeatable. If you're still rewriting everything by cohort 3, the model isn't stable.

4. **Students post about it before you ask them to.** Real results generate organic testimonials. If nobody is posting wins without you prompting them, the course isn't delivering.

5. **The testimonials name numbers.** "This changed my business" is generic. "I went from 12 hours a week on LinkedIn to 2" is proof.

6. **You can walk away and the model works without you.** This was the goal for Maven by cohort 5. Train another facilitator, hand them the materials, and the results stay consistent. That's when it's a real product.

The Maven course hit 4 out of 6 by cohort 3. Still not perfect. Still not fully systematized. But good enough to prove the model works and sharp enough to sell to the next 30 people without changing the core promise.
