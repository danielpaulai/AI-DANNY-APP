## The core principle

The only metrics that matter are the ones that predict revenue.

Everything else is vanity theatre.

## Why this matters

Most founders drown in data they can't use.

LinkedIn gives you 47 different numbers on a post.

Impressions, reach, engagement rate, profile views, reactions, comments, shares, follower growth.

None of them tell you if a client is about to book a call.

The cost of optimizing for the wrong signals is silence where it counts.

You post consistently. You hit 10,000 impressions. You get 47 likes. Zero DMs. Zero calls. Zero clients.

You feel productive because the numbers went up. The business stayed flat.

The leverage of watching the right signals is surgical precision.

You know which posts move buyers. You know which comments are from tire-kickers versus decision-makers. You know when someone is three posts away from booking.

You stop chasing vanity. You start building pipeline.

## How it shows up in client work

The pattern that shows up across every 1:1 is the same: founders confuse activity with progress.

They watch follower count like it's revenue.

They celebrate a viral post that brought zero qualified leads.

They burn hours on content that feels good to post but costs nothing to ignore.

In [[Jose_Henriquez_One_on_One_Personal_Branding_2024_12_18]], Jose had the inverse problem.

He was getting engagement but holding back on the real story.

I told him: "Your genuineness, if it translates to LinkedIn, is going to blow up. People just want to see that."

The signal I was watching wasn't his engagement rate.

It was whether his posts made people lean in or scroll past.

The test: would someone DM this person after reading that post?

If no, the post is invisible no matter how many likes it got.

In [[Cindy_business_ideation_2025_01_10]], Cindy had the opposite problem.

She was commenting consistently, building real relationships, being sincere in every reply.

Her commenting was one of the reasons I reached out.

But her profile views weren't converting.

The signal that mattered: who viewed her profile after she commented, and did they book a call?

Not how many people liked her posts.

In the Purely Personal cohort ([[Purely_Personal_cohort_1_2025_03_06]]), the entire group was stuck on lead magnet impressions.

Vanessa said: "I'm just waiting on feedback with impressions as low as they are."

The mistake: optimizing the lead magnet before knowing if the audience even wanted it.

The signal to watch first: how many people asked a question in the comments that the lead magnet would answer.

If nobody's asking, posting the lead magnet won't fix it.

In [[Live_Accelerator_Sessions_(90_minutes_each)_2025_12_15]], the cohort was posting but not moving the business forward.

I made them commit: post within one hour and paste the link in WhatsApp.

The signal wasn't the post itself.

It was: did they ship it? Did they ship it on time? Did the group engage?

That's the real metric. Execution velocity, not vanity reach.

The pattern across all of these: founders watch the numbers LinkedIn gives them instead of the numbers that predict cash.

But "someone told me it hit them hard."

Not because it was genius. Because it was relatable.

People DM'd saying "I've been there. Number 10 is me."

## Common mistakes

### Optimizing for total reach instead of qualified reach

Founders celebrate hitting 50,000 impressions.

Great.

Who were those 50,000 people? If 49,000 are outside your ICP, the post failed.

Better to reach 500 qualified buyers than 50,000 random scrollers.

### Watching follower count like it's revenue

Adding 100 followers feels productive.

It's not.

If those 100 people never comment, never DM, never buy, they're dead weight.

Better to have 300 followers who engage than 3,000 who ghost.

### Celebrating viral posts that don't convert

A post goes viral. 200 comments. 500 likes.

Zero DMs. Zero calls.

That's content theatre. It feels good. It costs nothing. It pays nothing.

The mistake: thinking virality equals value.

Virality without conversion is just noise.

### Posting lead magnets before testing demand

Founders spend 20 hours building a lead magnet, post it, and wonder why nobody downloads it.

The signal to watch first: are people asking questions in your comments that this lead magnet would answer?

If no, they don't want it yet. Don't post it.

### Confusing activity with progress

Posting daily feels productive.

It's not progress unless those posts are moving buyers closer to booking.

The signal: are the same 10 people commenting every week, or are new qualified buyers showing up?

If it's the same 10, you're not growing pipeline. You're performing for an audience that already likes you.

## The contrarian read

Most LinkedIn advice tells you to optimize for engagement.

I say optimize for silence.

The best posts don't get 200 comments from random people.

They get 3 DMs from qualified buyers who say "Let's talk."

I'd rather have a post with 20 likes and 3 booked calls than a post with 500 likes and zero calls.

Most founders think more is better.

More followers. More impressions. More engagement.

I think less is better. Fewer, better people. Fewer, better conversations.

The other contrarian read: comments from non-buyers are worthless.

Most founders celebrate every comment like it's validation.

I ignore comments from people who will never buy.

If someone who's not in my ICP leaves a thoughtful comment, I thank them and move on.

I don't optimize my content for them.

I optimize for the silent lurker who's been reading for three weeks and is about to book.

## What it looks like when it's working

You post once a week. You get 15 likes. You get 2 DMs from qualified buyers.

That's working.

You post daily. You get 200 likes. You get zero DMs.

That's not working.

The observable signal: your DMs fill with "I've been following you for a while. Can we talk?"

Not "Great post!" or "So true!"

But "I need help with [the exact thing you solve]. What's your calendar link?"

Another signal: the same qualified buyers engage with your content week over week.

You recognize their names. They comment on every post. They DM you with real questions.

That's an audience warming up.

Another signal: you can predict which posts will book calls before you publish them.

You know your ICP well enough to say "This one will resonate. This one will get likes but no action."

That's mastery.

The ultimate signal: your pipeline doesn't go quiet when you stop posting.

You've built enough trust that people book calls three weeks after reading your last post.

That's a real audience.
