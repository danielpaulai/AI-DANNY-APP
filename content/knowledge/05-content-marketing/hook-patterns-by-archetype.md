## The core principle

The hook is the only thing that determines whether your content gets read or gets scrolled past. Everything else is negotiable.

Most founders treat the hook as decoration. They write the body first, then bolt on an opening line. That's backwards. The hook IS the strategy. It decides who stops, who keeps reading, and whether the reader feels something cost them something to ignore.

## Why this matters

A weak hook doesn't just cost you one reader. It trains the algorithm that your content isn't worth surfacing. LinkedIn's feed prioritizes posts that hold attention in the first 3 seconds. A generic hook means your best thinking dies at 12 impressions.

The cost compounds. Every founder I work with who says "my content gets likes but nobody books a call" is running one of three hook mistakes: question hooks that cost nothing to answer, setup hooks that take 4 lines to land, or educational hooks that attract learners instead of buyers.

The mechanism is simple. People scroll to escape boredom or find proof their current thinking is right. A strong hook creates a gap between what they believe and what you're about to say. That gap is tension. Tension holds attention. Attention converts to pipeline when the rest of the post delivers proof.

Getting the hook wrong means you're building an audience that will never buy. Educational hooks attract people who want free content. Aspirational hooks attract people who want motivation. Neither group has a credit card out. The hook determines the buyer, not just the reader.

## How it shows up in client work

Every founder I onboard sends me their last 10 posts. I score each one using the Invisibility Diagnostic. The hook almost always scores 0 or 1 out of 4. Same patterns everywhere: "Have you ever wondered…" / "Let me tell you about…" / "Here's why X matters."

The first move is identifying their natural archetype. Some founders are contrarians. Some are storytellers. Some are framework builders. Forcing a storyteller to write contrarian hooks produces AI slop. The goal is finding the archetype that matches how they actually talk on client calls.

One client ran a consulting firm. Every post opened with a question. "Are you struggling with X?" or "What if you could Y?" Zero pipeline in 6 months. I pulled a recording from a real sales call. He opened every call the same way: "Most people think X. That's wrong. Here's what actually happens."

We rebuilt his hook library around that pattern. "Most founders build content calendars. Almost none of them work." / "Your pricing isn't a revenue problem. It's a positioning problem." / "The AI hype is wrong about one thing: it doesn't replace marketers. It replaces marketing tools."

Three weeks. Four inbound calls. Same ICP. Same offer. Different hook archetype.

Another founder sold AI implementation to mid-market companies. She opened every post with a case study setup: "I helped a client save 40 hours a month" or "Here's how we automated their workflow." Dead engagement. The content was solid. The hook told the wrong story.

I watched her run a discovery call. She didn't open with a case study. She opened with a specific painful moment: "You're probably spending 12 hours a week doing something that takes Claude 12 minutes." That's a pain-first hook, not a case study hook.

We rebuilt the feed. "You're paying someone $60K a year to do work Claude does for <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->0 a month" / "Every time you re-explain yourself to a tool, you're losing $500 in opportunity cost" / "If you're copy-pasting between three tools to get one answer, you're running a broken system."

Booked two $35K deals in 8 weeks. Same expertise. Different archetype.

The pattern I see most often: founders write the hook they think will get engagement, not the hook that matches how they actually close deals. A sales call is a hook followed by proof. If your best sales calls open with "here's the one thing everyone gets wrong," your posts should too. If your best calls open with "let me tell you what happened to a client last week," lead with story. Match the archetype to the close.

One more: a founder running workshops. Every post opened with a promise. "Join my workshop to learn X" / "In this session you'll discover Y." Zero signups. I asked him how he filled the room when he ran in-person events. He said "I'd stand up and say: most people leave workshops with notes. You'll leave with a working system."

That's outcome-contrast. Not promise. We rebuilt every hook around that structure. "Most founders post content. Almost none of them ship a system" / "Everyone talks about personal brand. Nobody teaches you how to make it a revenue channel" / "Workshops that give you a PDF are a waste of time. Here's what you should walk out with instead."

Next workshop: 40 signups in 10 days. He didn't change the offer. He changed the hook archetype to match how he actually sold the room when he was in it.

The diagnostic I run: show me your last 10 posts, then show me a recording of your last 3 sales calls. If the hook style doesn't match the call open, we're rebuilding the feed. The hook isn't about what works in theory. It's about what already works when you're closing.

## Specific examples from the vault

A cybersecurity consultant came in with a feed full of educational hooks. "5 ways to secure your network" / "Here's what you need to know about compliance." Getting likes. Getting saves. Zero calls. I asked what he said on sales calls when a prospect asked why they should work with him. He said: "Most companies think compliance is about passing audits. It's not. It's about proving to your customers you're not a liability."

That's a contrarian-reframe hook. Not educational. We rebuilt the feed. "Your security posture isn't protecting you. It's costing you deals" / "Every RFP you lose to 'security concerns' is a positioning problem, not a tech problem" / "Compliance doesn't make you secure. It makes you auditable. Those aren't the same thing."

Booked 3 discovery calls in 2 weeks. One closed at $85K. Cited from [[Cybersecurity_client_onboarding_2024_10_03]].

A founder selling AI automation kept opening with framework hooks. "Here's my 5-step system for X" / "Use the ABC method to Y." Looked smart. Felt like a teacher. Nobody was buying. I asked him what he said when a client asked "why is this hard?" He said: "Because you're doing five jobs that should be one. That's not a skill issue. That's a system issue."

That's a diagnostic hook. We rebuilt around that. "If you're re-explaining yourself to Claude every morning, you don't have an AI problem. You have a workflow problem" / "Your team isn't slow. Your tools are making them slow" / "You're not bad at delegation. You're delegating to the wrong layer."

Two <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->5K deals in 6 weeks. Cited from [[AI_automation_client_2025_02_10]].

A coach running a personal branding program opened every post with a question. "What would change if you had 10K followers?" / "Are you ready to scale your brand?" Zero signups. I asked how she closed clients on calls. She said: "I tell them the number one thing holding them back isn't their content. It's that they're trying to sound like everyone else."

That's a belief-shift hook. We rebuilt. "Your personal brand isn't a content problem. It's an identity problem" / "The reason nobody remembers you isn't your posting frequency. It's that you sound like everyone else" / "Followers don't buy. People who trust your POV do."

Next cohort: full at 12 clients in 3 weeks. Cited from [[Personal_brand_coach_client_2024_12_08]].

The consistent pattern: the hook that books calls is the one the founder already uses when they're selling. The problem is they don't recognize it as a hook. They think it's just "how I explain things." That's the hook. Write that.

## Scripts and exact phrases

"Most people think X. That's wrong. Here's what actually happens."

"You're probably spending [time/money] on [thing] that takes [tool/person] [much less] to do."

"Every time you [painful repeated action], you're losing [specific cost]."

"If you're [specific struggle], you don't have a [surface problem]. You have a [real problem]."

"Your [thing] isn't a [what they think] problem. It's a [what it actually is] problem."

"The reason [symptom] isn't [common excuse]. It's that [actual cause]."

"[Thing everyone does] doesn't [promise]. It [actual result]. Those aren't the same thing."

"Most [ICP] [action]. Almost none of them [desired outcome]."

## Common mistakes

**Opening with a question.** "Have you ever wondered why X?" Yes. Everyone has. That's not tension. That's a writing class exercise. Questions work when they cost something to answer wrong. "Are you charging $5K for work that should cost $50K?" lands because saying yes hurts. "Have you thought about pricing?" costs nothing.

**Using "imagine if" as a setup.** "Imagine if you could 10x your output." Nobody imagines. They scroll. Aspiration without proof is motivational content. Motivational content builds an audience that never buys.

**Leading with "let me tell you" or "here's why."** That's not a hook. That's narration. The reader doesn't care what you're about to do. They care whether the first sentence creates a gap they need to close.

**Burying the claim in line 3.** LinkedIn shows the first 2 lines without expanding. If the hook takes 4 lines to land, it never lands. The tension has to hit in sentence one or it doesn't hit at all.

**Writing the hook after the body.** The hook determines the body. If you write the body first, the hook becomes decoration. That's backwards. Start with the claim. Build the post to prove it.

**Using the same archetype for every post.** I see founders lock into one pattern and run it into the ground. 12 posts in a row that open with "Most people think X." The pattern stops working when it becomes predictable. Rotate between 3 archetypes minimum. Same voice, different entry point.

## The contrarian read

Most advice says "hook in the first 3 seconds." That's not wrong but it's incomplete. The real filter is: does this hook pre-qualify the buyer?

A viral hook that attracts 10,000 people who will never pay you is worse than a specific hook that attracts 100 people who have the problem you solve. Optimizing for reach is how you end up with 50K followers and zero revenue.

The best hook isn't the one that stops the most people. It's the one that stops the right people and repels everyone else. "Most founders waste time on content that doesn't book calls" doesn't land for someone who just wants engagement. Perfect. They're not the buyer.

Another hot take: story hooks are overrated unless you're selling to consumers. B2B buyers don't have time for a 6-paragraph setup. They want the claim in sentence one and proof in sentence two. Save the story for the middle if you need it. Don't bury the lead.

The hardest pill: your best hook is probably the thing you're afraid to say. The opinion you hold back because it'll piss someone off. That's the one that cuts through. Safe hooks produce safe results. If nobody disagrees with your opening line, it's not a hook. It's a platitude.

## What it looks like when it's working

The hook gets quoted back to you on sales calls. A prospect says "I saw your post about X being a Y problem, not a Z problem. That's exactly where I'm stuck." That's the signal. The hook didn't just get read. It changed how they saw the problem.

Comments shift from "great post" to "this is the thing I've been trying to explain to my team for six months." When the hook lands, people don't just engage. They feel seen. That's what converts.

You start getting DMs that open with "I wasn't going to reach out but your post about X made me realize I've been thinking about this wrong." That's pipeline. The hook did the qualification work before the conversation started.

Engagement drops on some posts and spikes on others. That's good. It means the hook is polarizing. The posts that get 40 likes and 8 comments from your ICP are worth more than the posts that get 400 likes from randoms.

You can write a new post in 10 minutes because the hook writes the rest. Once the claim is clear, the body is just proof. If the post is taking 45 minutes, the hook is wrong. Fix the hook. The rest writes itself.
