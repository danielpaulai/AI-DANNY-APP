## The core principle

Cold DMs are not cold outreach. They're the natural next step after someone already knows you exist.

The prospect saw your post. They read your comment on someone else's thread. They saved your carousel. They visited your profile twice. Now you're in their inbox with a message that feels like the conversation already started. That's the difference between a cold DM and spam.

## Why this matters

Most founders treat LinkedIn DMs like email campaigns. They blast templated messages to strangers who never heard of them. They wonder why 95% get ignored and 5% get blocked.

The cost of getting this wrong is permanent. You burn leads before they become leads. You train your ICP to associate your name with generic pitch messages. You waste the only channel left where decision-makers actually read their messages.

The opportunity cost is bigger. A well-timed DM to someone who already saw your content converts at 40-60%. A cold blast to someone who doesn't know you converts at 2%. Same effort. 20x difference in outcome.

LinkedIn rewards attention before action. The founder who posts consistently for 90 days then sends 10 thoughtful DMs books more calls than the founder who sends 1,000 templated DMs to cold prospects. The platform itself is built this way. Sending too many messages to people who don't respond triggers spam filters. Sending fewer messages to people who engage with your content gets prioritised in their inbox.

The mechanism is simple. A DM to someone who already consumed your content costs them nothing to read. A DM to someone who never heard of you costs them mental energy to decide if you're worth their time. Most people don't have that energy. So they ghost.

## How it shows up in client work

The biggest pattern across clients is they confuse activity with progress. They send 50 DMs a week and get 3 replies. They call that "pipeline generation." It's not. It's noise.

The reframe that lands: stop sending DMs to strangers. Start sending DMs to people who already gave you attention. That shifts everything.

When I work with a founder on DM strategy, the first move is always the same. We pull their LinkedIn analytics and map their engaged audience. Who commented on their last 10 posts? Who viewed their profile this week? Who saved their content? That's the DM list. Not a purchased database. Not a cold scrape. The people who already raised their hand.

One client ran a 90-day content sprint, posted 3x a week, built a library of 40 posts. Zero DMs sent during that period. At day 91, we pulled the list of everyone who engaged with at least 3 posts. 200 people. He sent personalised DMs to 50 of them over two weeks. 23 replies. 11 booked calls. 4 closed. That's an 8% close rate from DMs. Industry average is 0.5-2%.

The difference was the opening line. Instead of "Hey, I help founders scale their LinkedIn," he wrote "Saw you commented on my post about founder-led content systems. Curious what part landed for you?" That's not a pitch. That's a question someone who read your work would actually answer.

Another client was stuck in the "spray and pray" trap. 200 DMs a week, 5% reply rate, zero calls. The message was a three-paragraph pitch about his consulting services. I asked him to show me the last 10 people he messaged. None of them had engaged with his content. None of them followed him. Most of them had never visited his profile. He was cold-messaging strangers and calling it LinkedIn outreach.

We killed the volume. Cut it to 10 DMs a week. Only to people who had interacted with his posts in the last 30 days. Changed the opening from a pitch to a callback. "You saved my post on AI workflows last week. Building something in that space or just researching?" Reply rate jumped to 40%. Three calls booked in week one.

The pattern that emerges: the DM is not the start of the relationship. The content is. The DM is the handshake after someone already knows your face.

One founder asked me why his DMs weren't converting even though he was following up on engagement. I read his messages. Every single one was a soft pitch disguised as a question. "Saw you liked my post. Would love to learn more about your business and see if we're a fit." That's still a pitch. It still triggers the spam filter in the prospect's brain.

The rewrite: "You liked my post about [specific topic]. What's your take on [specific aspect of that topic]?" No mention of services. No "would love to chat." Just a real question that shows you actually read their profile and care about their answer. That gets replies. The pitch comes later, if at all.

The common mistake founders make is rushing to the call. They think the DM's job is to book a meeting. It's not. The DM's job is to start a conversation. The conversation's job is to surface whether there's a real fit. The meeting books itself once that's clear.

One client was messaging founders who commented on his posts with "Thanks for the comment! Want to jump on a quick call?" Zero conversions. The reframe: "Thanks for weighing in. What's the biggest challenge you're seeing with [topic from the post]?" Three back-and-forth messages later, the prospect asks "Do you help with this?" Now the call books naturally because they asked for it.

## Scripts and exact phrases

Opening a DM after someone engaged with your content:
"Saw you [specific action: commented / saved / shared] my post on [specific topic]. What part resonated?"

Opening a DM after a profile view:
"Noticed you checked out my profile. Curious what brought you by?"

Following up on a comment thread:
"Your comment on [topic] got me thinking. Have you tried [specific approach]?"

Transitioning from conversation to discovery:
"Sounds like [their challenge] is costing you [time / money / opportunity]. Worth a 20-minute call to map out what a solution looks like?"

Handling "too busy" in DMs:
"Totally get it. No pressure. If [specific pain point] becomes urgent, I'm here."

Handling "need to think about it" in DMs:
"Makes sense. What's the piece you're still working through?"

Soft close in DMs (after 3+ message exchange):
"Based on what you've shared, this sounds like a fit. Want to schedule 20 minutes to walk through exactly how this would work for you?"

## Common mistakes

Pitching before building rapport. The DM opens with "I help [ICP] achieve [outcome]." That's a cold pitch. The prospect doesn't care what you help people do until they know you understand their specific situation.

Messaging people who never engaged. If they didn't interact with your content, they're not warm. They're cold. Cold DMs on LinkedIn have a 1-2% reply rate. Warm DMs (to people who engaged) have a 30-50% reply rate. Don't waste the effort.

Writing long paragraphs. The message looks like a sales email. It triggers the same mental "delete" reflex. Keep the first message to 1-2 sentences. Get a reply first. Build from there.

Asking for a call too early. The prospect doesn't know you well enough yet. They feel pressured. They ghost. Have a real conversation first. Let them surface their problem. Then offer the call as a solution.

Using generic templates. "Hi [First Name], I saw we're both in [industry]..." Every founder on LinkedIn gets this message 10 times a week. It's invisible. Write something only you could write based on their specific activity.

Sending connection requests with a pitch in the note. LinkedIn warns the prospect you're trying to sell them before they even accept. Accept rate drops to 10-20%. Send the request blank. DM them after they accept.

## The contrarian read

Most LinkedIn advice says to send 50-100 DMs a week. It's wrong. Volume strategy works for low-ticket B2C offers. It fails for high-ticket B2B. Decision-makers ignore volume. They respond to relevance.

The better strategy: 10 DMs a week. Only to people who engaged with your content in the last 30 days. Personalised to their specific action. That's 40 DMs a month. If you're posting consistently, 30-50% of them reply. That's 12-20 conversations. 3-5 of those book calls. 1-2 close. That's <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->0K-$50K in pipeline from 40 messages.

The math works because the inputs are different. Cold outreach optimises for volume because conversion rates are 1-2%. Warm outreach optimises for quality because conversion rates are 30-50%. You can send 1,000 cold DMs and book 10 calls, or you can send 40 warm DMs and book 10 calls. Same outcome. One wastes 960 messages.

The second contrarian take: most founders think the DM should book the call. It shouldn't. The DM should start the conversation. The conversation should surface the pain. The pain should make the call obvious. When you try to book the call in the first message, you're forcing a yes/no decision before the prospect knows why they'd say yes. That's why they ghost.

The third: you don't need a CRM to manage DMs. You need a spreadsheet. Name, date of first message, what they engaged with, where the conversation is. That's it. If you're sending 10 thoughtful DMs a week, you don't need automation. You need attention.

## What it looks like when it's working

Reply rates hit 40-60%. Not 5%. Not 10%. Real conversations.

The prospect replies within 24 hours without you following up. They're not ignoring you. They're engaging.

Half the conversations turn into multi-message threads. You're not getting one-word replies. You're getting real back-and-forth.

Calls book naturally. The prospect asks if you do calls. You don't have to pitch the call. They request it.

The pipeline fills without cold outreach. Your DMs are feeding discovery calls, not replacing them.

You're sending fewer messages but booking more calls. 10 DMs a week outperforms 100 because you're messaging the right people.
