## The core principle

You cannot build a personal brand on LinkedIn by only sharing other people's content.

Original content is the only thing that positions you as the operator, the voice, the authority that clients book calls with.

## Why this matters

Every repost is a signal that you don't have enough to say.

It tells your audience: "I'm a curator, not a creator. I'm here to amplify, not originate."

That positioning is fatal if you're selling expertise, coaching, consulting, or any high-ticket service where your personal judgment is the product.

The mechanism is simple. LinkedIn's algorithm rewards original posts with reach. Reposts get a fraction of the impressions. Your audience sees you less. You build less authority. Your pipeline goes quiet.

The cost of getting this wrong compounds weekly. A founder who reposts 80% of the time and posts original content 20% of the time is invisible to their ICP. They're active, but they're not building a brand. They're building a habit of showing up without saying anything.

The founder who flips that ratio—80% original, 20% reposts—owns the conversation. Their feed becomes a library of their thinking. When a prospect scrolls their profile, they see a body of work, not a feed of other people's ideas.

The leverage is extreme. One original post that lands can book three calls. One repost with commentary can remind your network you exist. But ten reposts in a row make you forgettable.

## How it shows up in client work

The pattern I see most often: founders who post daily but wonder why they're not booking calls.

I pull up their profile. The feed is 70% reposts, 20% generic advice, 10% personal stories.

Nothing sticks. Nothing positions them as different. Every post could have been written by someone else.

I worked with a founder running a $300K consulting practice [[Scale_your_Business_to_6_Figures_2025_12_19]]. She was posting five times a week. Mostly reposts of other consultants' frameworks. Some commentary added. Solid engagement—50 to 100 likes per post.

Zero inbound calls in three months.

The fix: flip the ratio. Original content 80% of the time. Reposts only when adding a contrarian take or a specific client example.

Within six weeks, she booked four inbound discovery calls. The posts that landed were all original. The reposts? Crickets.

Another pattern: founders who treat LinkedIn like Twitter. They repost news. They repost memes. They repost inspirational quotes.

None of it builds authority. All of it trains the audience to scroll past.

I had a client in the AI consulting space [[Live_Accelerator_Sessions_(90_minutes_each)_2025_12_15]]. He was posting daily. Half his feed was reposting other people's hot takes on AI tools. The other half was original posts, but vague—"AI is changing everything" style content.

His engagement was decent. His booked calls: zero.

The shift: stop reposting entirely for 30 days. Post only original content tied to a specific client problem he'd solved that week.

First week: two posts. Both flopped. 15 likes total.

Second week: three posts. One hit 200+ impressions. One comment turned into a DM that turned into a <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->5K deal.

The mechanism is always the same. Original content forces you to have a point of view. Reposts let you hide behind someone else's.

Another client ran a personal branding agency [[Everyone_social_2023_09_22]]. She was active on LinkedIn. Posting daily. But most of her posts were reposts of her clients' success stories.

The intent was right—social proof. But the execution was wrong. She was promoting her clients more than herself.

Her profile looked like a case study library, not a personal brand.

The fix: same content, different structure. Turn the client wins into original posts where she's the narrator. "Here's what I told this founder to do. Here's the result. Here's why it worked."

Engagement stayed flat. But the calls tripled. Because now she was the expert, not the amplifier.

I see this pattern in every cohort I run. The founders who repost constantly are the ones who struggle to articulate what they do.

They know their work is valuable. But they haven't done the reps to distill it into a point of view.

Reposting is a crutch. It fills the feed without forcing you to think.

The founders who ship original content weekly are the ones who close deals. Because they've done the work to say something specific.

One more pattern worth naming: the repost-with-commentary trap.

Founders think adding two lines to someone else's post makes it original. It doesn't.

LinkedIn counts it as a repost. The algorithm treats it as a repost. Your audience scrolls past it.

I had a client who spent 30 minutes a day finding posts to repost and adding commentary [[Building_nasops_transcript-based_documentation_and_email_generation_app_-_78e52862]]. He thought he was adding value. He was just diluting his brand.

The fix: take the same 30 minutes and write one original post. Use the other person's post as a prompt, not a platform.

Engagement went up. Calls went up. Authority went up.

He pushed back. "But I want to support my community."

I said: "Support them in the comments. Not in your feed. Your feed is your storefront. Every post is a signal of what you stand for. Reposts say you don't have enough of your own ideas."

He tested it. Went cold turkey on reposts for 14 days. Posted only original content—client stories, contrarian takes, frameworks he used in real sessions.

By the end, he'd booked two discovery calls and had three prospects reach out via DM.

The shift wasn't in the volume. It was in the positioning. Original content positioned him as the expert. Reposts positioned him as a fan.

In another call, a founder running a marketing agency asked why his engagement was high but his pipeline was empty [[Everyone_social_2023_09_22]].

I pulled up his profile. Every other post was a repost. The original posts were generic—"5 ways to improve your LinkedIn" lists.

I told him: "You're not saying anything only you could say. Every post could have been written by any marketer."

He didn't believe me. So I ran the Invisibility Diagnostic on five of his posts. All scored 1 or 2 out of 4.

The rewrite: take one client win from the past month. Write it as a story. Name the specific problem, the specific solution, the specific result. Post that.

First attempt scored a 3. Second attempt scored a 4. That post booked a call within 48 hours.

I said: "That's your entire content strategy. Write that 12 different ways."

He did. Stopped reposting AI news. Started posting original takes on AI-as-team. Engagement dropped slightly. Booked calls tripled.

The pattern repeats: reposting keeps you visible. Original content makes you valuable.

In a workshop on LinkedIn strategy, one founder asked if there was ever a time to repost [[Scale_your_Business_to_6_Figures_2025_12_19]].

I said: "Yes. When you have something specific to add that shifts the conversation. Otherwise, skip it."

She asked for an example. I said: "If someone posts 'AI will replace marketers,' repost it if you can say 'No, AI replaces marketing tools. Marketers who use AI will replace marketers who don't.' That's a contrarian reframe. That's worth reposting."

She tested it. Reposted once in three weeks. Added a sharp contrarian take. Got more engagement on that repost than her previous five original posts combined.

But here's the key: the repost worked because she'd spent three weeks building authority with original content first. The repost was the exception, not the rule.

## Common mistakes

**Mistake 1: Reposting to stay visible.**

Founders think activity equals presence. It doesn't. Reposting keeps you in the feed, but it doesn't build authority. You're just noise.

**Mistake 2: Adding two lines of commentary and calling it original.**

LinkedIn treats repost-with-commentary as a repost. The algorithm penalizes it. Your audience scrolls past it. If you're going to add commentary, write a full original post and reference the other person's take without reposting.

**Mistake 3: Reposting to "support the community."**

Support them in the comments. Not in your feed. Your feed is your storefront. Every post is a positioning signal. Reposts say "I'm a fan, not an expert."

**Mistake 4: Using reposts to fill the calendar.**

If you don't have an original post to write, don't post. One strong original post per week beats seven reposts.

**Mistake 5: Reposting because you're scared to have an opinion.**

This is the real issue most of the time. Reposting feels safe. You can't be wrong if you're just amplifying someone else. But safe doesn't book calls. Specific does.

**Mistake 6: Believing engagement on reposts means they're working.**

Engagement on reposts is empty calories. It doesn't position you. It doesn't build your brand. It doesn't book calls. The founders who track pipeline, not likes, stop reposting fast.

## The contrarian read

Here's where I disagree with most LinkedIn advice: I don't think you should repost at all.

Most LinkedIn gurus will tell you to repost strategically. Add commentary. Tag the person. Build relationships.

I say: skip it entirely for 90 days. See what happens.

The reason: reposting is a drug. It's easy. It fills the calendar. It gets some likes. It feels like you're building a brand.

But it's not. You're building a habit of showing up without saying anything.

The founders who break through on LinkedIn are the ones who go all-in on original content for a sustained period. Three months minimum. No reposts. No shortcuts. Just original posts, client stories, contrarian takes, and frameworks.

By the end, they have a body of work. A feed that reads like a library of their thinking. A profile that books calls.

The ones who keep reposting? Still invisible.

Another contrarian take: reposting trains your audience to expect less from you.

Every time you repost, you're saying "I don't have enough to say this week." Do that enough times, and your audience stops expecting substance. They scroll past your name.

When you finally post something original, it lands flat. Because you've already trained them that your posts aren't worth stopping for.

The fix: never repost. Build the expectation that every post from you is original, specific, and worth reading. That expectation compounds.

## What it looks like when it's working

Your feed reads like a book, not a feed reader.

When a prospect scrolls your profile, they see 12 weeks of original content. Every post sounds like you. Every post ties to a client problem you've solved. Every post has a point of view.

They message you before they even see your offer.

Your engagement stays consistent or grows week over week. Not because you're gaming the algorithm, but because your audience knows every post will be specific and useful.

You book 2 to 4 inbound calls per month from LinkedIn without paid ads, without cold outreach. Just from the feed.

Your original posts get shared more than reposts ever did. Because people share insights, not amplifications.

Your network starts tagging you in conversations. "Ask [Your Name], they wrote about this last week." You become the go-to voice in your space.

You stop caring about vanity metrics. You track pipeline instead. And pipeline moves.
