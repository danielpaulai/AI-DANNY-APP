## The core principle

Post when you can sustain it, not when someone told you to.

The moment you commit to a cadence you cannot maintain, you have already started losing.

## Why this matters

Most founders treat posting cadence like a badge. Three times a week. Daily. Twice daily. They announce it publicly, hold it for two weeks, then disappear for a month.

The cost is trust.

Every time a founder goes dark after promising consistency, their audience learns not to expect them. The algorithm learns not to surface them. The founder learns they cannot be trusted to keep their own word.

The leverage of getting this right is invisible until you have it. When a founder posts consistently for 90 days—whatever their cadence—three things happen that do not happen with sporadic posting.

First, the algorithm starts working for them instead of against them. LinkedIn's distribution model rewards patterns. If you post Tuesdays and Thursdays at 8am for 12 weeks, the platform learns to surface your content to your audience at that time. If you post randomly, the platform has no pattern to optimize around.

Second, the audience stops scrolling past. When someone sees you every Tuesday, they start looking for you on Tuesday. When you show up sporadically, they forget you exist between posts.

Third, the founder builds a different relationship with content. It stops being a performance and becomes a system. The mental load drops. The fear drops. It becomes Tuesday morning, time to write.

The mistake most founders make is optimizing for volume before they have proven they can ship at all. They see someone posting daily and assume that is the standard. They do not see the 18 months that person spent posting twice a week before scaling up.

Cadence is not a strategy. It is a constraint. The strategy is: what can I sustain for 90 days without burning out, without the content dropping in quality, and without needing willpower to show up?

That number is almost always lower than the founder wants it to be. And that is exactly why it works.

## How it shows up in client work

I had a founder in March 2025 who opened our first session by saying she was committing to daily posts. She had posted three times in the previous month.

I asked her why daily. She said "because that's what works."

I asked her to show me the last time she posted daily for 30 consecutive days. She could not.

We moved her to twice a week. Tuesday and Thursday. Same time. She argued that it was not enough. I told her the goal was not volume, it was proof she could sustain a rhythm.

Six weeks later she had posted 12 times. No gaps. The engagement per post was 40% higher than her previous sporadic content because the algorithm had learned to surface her. She booked three calls directly from those 12 posts.

She wanted to scale to three times a week. I told her no. Stay at two until it feels easy.

That pattern shows up in almost every 1:1 coaching engagement. The founder wants more. I want sustainable.

Another client, a solo consultant running a <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->00K business, told me he could do three posts a week. I asked him to walk me through his Monday. Calls from 9am to 3pm. Admin and proposal work until 6pm. Family time after.

I asked him where the 90 minutes of content work was going to come from. He said he would find it.

I said no. We are building this assuming you will not find it. If the only way your content system works is by finding time you do not currently have, the system does not work.

We moved him to one post per week. Friday mornings. Two hours blocked. Coffee, Claude open, one post written, edited, scheduled.

He has not missed a Friday in 16 weeks. His pipeline is fuller than it has ever been. Not because he is posting more. Because he is showing up.

The clients who succeed are the ones who accept the constraint and work within it. The ones who fail are the ones who keep chasing a cadence they saw someone else execute.

I had a client in November 2024 who was posting daily for two weeks, then going dark for three weeks, then posting daily again. Classic boom-bust cycle. Her engagement was low because the algorithm had no idea what to do with her.

I asked her what was happening in the three-week gaps. She said she got busy with client work and did not have time.

I asked her how long it took to write a post when she was in flow. She said 30 minutes.

I asked her how long it took when she was not in flow. She said two hours and she usually gave up.

The problem was not time. The problem was she was trying to write from scratch every time. No backlog. No system. Just willpower and a blank page.

We built her a content bank. 20 hooks. 10 frameworks. 5 client stories. All written in one two-hour session. Then we moved her to twice a week using only that material.

She posted twice a week for 12 weeks. Never missed. The content was not groundbreaking. It was consistent. Her engagement tripled because the algorithm started trusting her.

## Common mistakes

The first mistake is announcing the cadence publicly before proving it privately. Founders post "I am committing to daily content" and then miss day three. The announcement becomes a public failure instead of a private test.

The second mistake is optimizing for volume before optimizing for quality. A weak post every day is worse than a strong post once a week. Volume without substance trains the audience to scroll past.

The third mistake is treating cadence as fixed. Life changes. Client load changes. Energy changes. A cadence that worked in January might not work in July. The founders who succeed adjust without guilt.

The fourth mistake is using someone else's cadence as the benchmark. Gary Vee posts 50 times a day because he has a team of 12 people producing content. You do not. Comparing yourself to him is comparing your Tuesday morning to his content factory.

The fifth mistake is posting to post. If the content does not move someone closer to booking a call, it is content theatre. Cadence for the sake of cadence is vanity.

The sixth mistake is not building a backlog. If you are writing every post from scratch, you will burn out. The founders who sustain cadence have 10 to 20 posts in the bank before they commit to a rhythm.

## The contrarian read

Daily posting is overrated.

The advice to "post every day" comes from people who have teams, ghostwriters, or 10 years of reps. For a solo founder with a full client load, daily posting is a recipe for burnout and weak content.

The better play is fewer posts that actually matter. One post per week that books a call is worth more than seven posts that get likes and do nothing.

LinkedIn rewards consistency, not volume. Twice a week for 90 days beats daily for three weeks then silence.

The other contrarian read: cadence is not the variable that matters most. Voice matters more. POV matters more. Specificity matters more. A founder with a sharp voice posting once a week will outperform a generic founder posting daily.

Most founders optimize for the wrong thing. They think the problem is frequency when the problem is substance. Fix the substance first. Then scale the frequency.

## What it looks like when it is working

The founder posts on the same days every week without checking their calendar. It is Tuesday, so they post. It is Thursday, so they post. No decision fatigue.

The content quality stays consistent. Post 1 and post 40 are the same standard. No drop-off.

The engagement per post is stable or climbing. The algorithm has learned the pattern and is distributing the content predictably.

The founder books calls. Not from every post, but consistently over 90 days. One call every two weeks is enough to fill a pipeline.

The founder is not stressed about content. It is a system, not a performance. The mental load has dropped.

The audience knows when to expect the founder. They look for the Tuesday post. They comment. They share. The relationship is built.

The founder has a backlog. They are not scrambling on Tuesday morning. They have three posts written and ready. If they get sick or a client emergency hits, they do not go dark.

When the founder decides to scale from twice a week to three times a week, they test it for four weeks before committing. If the quality drops, they scale back without guilt.
