## The core principle

Most founders treat LinkedIn posts like essays: they build an argument, then ask for engagement at the end. This is backwards.

A post is not a blog. It's a pattern. The structure IS the message. When you write to a repeatable framework, the reader's brain follows without friction because it recognises the shape before it processes the words.

## Why this matters

Structure is the difference between a post that gets read and a post that gets scrolled past in 0.3 seconds.

The average LinkedIn user scrolls their feed at 300+ posts per hour. That's one post every 12 seconds. Your hook has 1.2 seconds to stop the thumb. Your body has 6 seconds to earn the click to "see more." Your close has 4 seconds to convert attention into action.

Without structure, you're asking the reader to do cognitive work. With structure, the reader glides. They know what comes next. The pattern pulls them through.

The founders who post consistently but get no calls almost always have the same problem: they write well, but they don't write to a structure. Every post is a fresh invention. The reader arrives, sees a wall of text, and leaves. No hook. No rhythm. No payoff.

The cost of bad structure is invisible pipeline. The post gets likes from people who skim. It never books a call because it never asked for one in a way that felt inevitable.

## How it shows up in client work

The most common pattern I see: a founder writes 8 sentences of setup, then drops a list of 5 bullet points, then ends with "What do you think?"

This structure doesn't work because it front-loads friction. The reader has to wade through context before they understand why they should care. By sentence 3 they're gone.

The fix: invert it. Hook with outcome. Proof in one line. Then the list. Then the specific ask.

I ran this exact rewrite with a client in [[Personal_Branding_Session_3_2024_03_05]]. She had been posting "educational" content for 6 weeks. High engagement on likes. Zero calls. The posts all opened with "Let me tell you about X." By the time she got to the value, the reader had scrolled.

We rebuilt every post to the **save-bait structure**: outcome in the hook, 3 back-to-back pain points, intro into solution, body as step-by-step framework, CTA as comment trigger. First post using this structure: 47 saves, 3 DM inquiries, 1 booked call within 48 hours.

The structure changed nothing about her expertise. It changed everything about how the reader experienced it.

Another client had the opposite problem. He would open strong, but the body was formless. One paragraph of insight, then 4 examples, then a tangent about a book he read, then "Let me know your thoughts." No throughline. The reader couldn't follow.

I introduced him to the **step-by-step structure** from the vault research in [[Creating viral educational video series - 50c9b77d]]:
1. Hook: state the dream result
2. Authority gap: state the results you've generated and why people should trust you
3. Introduce the framework name or acronym
4. Share the value: the actual step-by-step process
5. Add a CTA

He used this structure for 3 posts in a row. The CTA in post 3 was "Comment FRAMEWORK and I'll send you the full breakdown." 28 comments. 11 became discovery calls. 2 closed within 30 days.

The structure didn't make him smarter. It made his intelligence accessible.

I also see this in workshop content. In [[Building nasops transcript-based documentation and email generation app - 78e52862]], the client needed post templates for their operations team to use when promoting internal sessions. The original drafts were generic: "Join us for a session on X. Learn about Y. Sign up here."

We rebuilt them using the **3-line hierarchy** from the promotional banner format:
- Setup line (the tension or question)
- Bold punch line with one italic highlight word (the promise)
- Concrete subtitle (the specific outcome)

Example: "Most founders post content. Almost none of them ship a *system*. Turn Content You Already Made Into Paid Products."

This structure works because it moves from relatable problem to uncommon solution to specific proof in 3 beats. The reader feels the gap, sees the bridge, wants to cross.

## Specific examples from the vault

The **save-bait framework** appears in [[LinkedIn Video Frameworks for 1M Impressions - df081e1d]] as a proven repeatable template:

**Hook:** "LinkedIn Videos got my student 1,000,000 impressions. You can do it, too."

**Body:** 6 frameworks presented as named systems (SLAY, AIDA, SCOPE, SMART, PAS, 3E), each with bullet structure using arrows, each delivering one idea per line.

**CTA:** "Comment VIDEO below to get my complete framework guide with: Detailed prompts, Implementation tips, Fine tuning tips."

**Secondary ask:** "Save for later. Share with someone who needs this."

This structure converts because it follows the **outcome → proof → access** pattern. The reader sees the result in the hook, believes it's possible because of the frameworks, and takes action to get the full resource.

Another example from [[Building audience through authentic creator content - 0d8a6374]] shows the **viral hook structure**:

"Outcome + 3 back-to-back pain points + intro into solution."

Real script: "If giving yourself a blowout pulls out too much hair, takes too much time, and ultimately just misses. Here's how I cheated."

This structure works because it names 3 specific failures the reader has experienced, then promises a workaround. The reader self-selects: if all 3 pain points land, they keep reading. If none do, they scroll. No wasted attention.

A third example from the same note: the **comparison structure**:

"Most people: X. Smart people: Y."

Real script: "Most successful creators prioritize conversation over performance."

This structure works because it reframes the obvious belief as the wrong move, then positions the reader as someone who can make the smarter choice. It's contrarian without being combative.

The vault research in [[Creating viral educational video series - 50c9b77d]] identified **7 hook categories that consistently generate 1M+ views**:
1. Curiosity gaps ("Did you know you can start a bakery without a storefront, high startup costs, or years of experience?")
2. Empathy hooks ("I don't know who needs to hear this, but don't post educational videos on Instagram ever again.")
3. Statistical shock ("82% of people experience imposter syndrome.")
4. Pattern interrupts ("The most successful creators prioritize conversation over performance.")
5. Bold statements ("Your LinkedIn isn't a brand problem. It's a workflow problem.")
6. Questions ("If you want a dog who will quietly sit and wait instead of jumping up, getting distracted, or demand barking, you're going to love this behavior.")
7. Age-specific targeting ("37 pages of notes later, I noticed all the fastest-growing creators have this one thing in common.")

Each hook type pairs with a specific body structure. Curiosity gaps pair with step-by-step reveals. Empathy hooks pair with problem-agitate-solution. Statistical shock pairs with case study proof.

## Scripts and exact phrases

**Opening hooks that stop the scroll:**

"I don't know who needs to hear this, but [contrarian statement]."

"[Specific result] got my [client/student] [specific number]. You can do it, too."

"Most founders [common behaviour]. Almost none of them [uncommon behaviour]."

"If [pain point 1], [pain point 2], and [pain point 3]. Here's how I [solved it]."

"Did you know you can [unexpected outcome] without [assumed requirement 1], [assumed requirement 2], or [assumed requirement 3]?"

**Body transitions that hold attention:**

"Here's the thing most people miss:"

"The pattern I see across [number]+ clients:"

"Study and use [framework name] if you want [specific outcome]:"

"There are [number] types of [category]. Here's how to tell which one you are:"

**CTA lines that convert scroll into action:**

"Comment [keyword] and I'll send you [specific resource]."

"Save this post. You'll need it when [specific situation]."

"The next time [trigger event], come back to this."

"DM me [keyword] if you want the [outcome] without [common objection]."

"If you want [outcome], [specific first step]. Everything else follows."

## Common mistakes

**Mistake 1: No hook at all.** The post opens with context or background. "I've been thinking about X lately..." The reader has no reason to stay. The fix: lead with outcome or pain. Context comes later, if at all.

**Mistake 2: Hook that's a question.** "Have you ever wondered why some founders succeed and others don't?" This doesn't stop the scroll. It sounds like every other post. The fix: make a claim. "Most founders fail because they optimize for the wrong thing."

**Mistake 3: Body is a wall of text.** 8 sentences in one paragraph. The reader sees it and leaves before reading word one. The fix: one sentence per paragraph. Blank line between. Let the eye breathe.

**Mistake 4: Lists with no rhythm.** Bullet points that vary in length. Some are 3 words, some are 15. The reader loses the beat. The fix: force every bullet to the same structure. "Verb + object + outcome" for all 5 lines.

**Mistake 5: CTA that doesn't cost anything.** "Let me know your thoughts." "What do you think?" These CTAs get ignored because they require the reader to invent the response. The fix: make the CTA specific and low-friction. "Comment YES if you want the PDF." The reader knows exactly what to type.

**Mistake 6: No framework name.** The post delivers value but doesn't package it. The reader finishes and forgets. The fix: name the system. "This is the 3E Framework: Engage, Educate, Entertain." Now the reader can reference it later and remember where they learned it.

## The contrarian read

Most "viral content" advice tells you to write like you're performing. Big energy. Hype. Manufactured urgency.

I push the opposite: write like you're explaining something obvious to a friend who just asked a real question.

The posts that book calls don't feel like content. They feel like the answer the reader was looking for when they opened LinkedIn that morning.

This is why "save-bait" works and "engagement-bait" doesn't. Save-bait gives the reader a resource they'll return to. Engagement-bait asks them to perform for you. One serves. One extracts.

The other contrarian belief: most founders think structure makes their writing robotic. They resist templates because they want to "sound like themselves."

But structure is what lets voice through. When the reader isn't doing cognitive work to follow the shape, they actually hear the tone. Structure is the infrastructure. Voice is the content that runs on it.

## What it looks like when it's working

You post using a repeatable structure for 3 weeks. By week 4, your audience starts commenting "This is the format I always look for from you."

The DMs shift from "Great post" to "Can we talk about how this applies to my business?"

Your saved count becomes higher than your like count on posts over 10 lines. That's the signal. Saves mean the reader wants to return. Likes mean they scrolled past but tapped the heart.

You start getting referenced. Someone tags you in a comment: "This is what @yourname was talking about with the [framework name]."

The biggest signal: you book a discovery call, and the prospect opens with "I've been reading your posts for 6 weeks. I know exactly what I need." They've already sold themselves. The call is a formality.
