## The core principle

Content that doesn't pass 3 out of 4 on the Invisibility Diagnostic is invisible.

Not "underperforming." Not "could be better." Invisible. It scrolls past unread, gets liked by no one who matters, and generates zero business movement.

The Diagnostic scores four elements: Voice (does this sound like the person or like AI?), POV (is there a real point of view or just facts?), Specificity (names, numbers, dates, places, or vague generalities?), and Asking (is the reader being asked to do something, choose something, change something?). Each scores 0-4. If a piece lands below 3, it's invisible. Rewrite or don't post.

This isn't theory. This came from analyzing 2,000+ LinkedIn, X, and Instagram posts across founders, coaches, and consultants. The pattern was absolute: posts that scored 3+ booked calls. Posts that scored below 3 got likes from peers and zero client movement.

## Why this matters

Most founders are posting invisible content and calling it marketing.

They're writing five posts a week, getting 200 likes, feeling productive, and wondering why their pipeline is empty. The LinkedIn algorithm shows them engagement. Their peers comment. They hit "schedule" and feel like they did the work.

But their ICP scrolled past. Didn't save. Didn't DM. Didn't book.

The cost of invisible content isn't the time spent writing the post. It's the three months of posting consistently with zero inbound while the founder burns through runway and tells themselves "content takes time." The real cost is the missed client who would have booked if the founder had posted something specific, opinionated, and useful instead of generic and safe.

The leverage is the opposite. When a founder starts scoring their drafts before posting, the difference shows up in 72 hours. Not vanity metrics. Real signals: saves, DMs, profile views from the right titles, booked calls. One founder went from 12 posts with zero inbound to 3 posts that generated 11 discovery calls in two weeks. Same posting frequency. Different scoring standard.

The mechanism is this: human attention is the scarcest resource on LinkedIn. The feed is infinite. The reader gives you 1.2 seconds. If the first line doesn't land with specificity, voice, and tension, they scroll. If it lands but the body is vague, they forget you. If it lands, the body delivers, but there's no ask, they nod and move on. All four elements have to score. That's why 3/4 is the floor, not the ceiling.

When founders don't apply this, they suffer quietly. They post for six months, build an audience of other founders who like their content, and book zero high-ticket clients. They hire a ghostwriter who writes in "professional LinkedIn voice" and wonder why it feels off-brand. They try AI tools that generate average hooks and wonder why the output doesn't convert. The diagnostic would have caught all of it before it shipped.

## How it shows up in client work

The single most common pattern in 1:1 coaching is the founder who says "I'm posting consistently but nobody's booking calls."

I ask to see their last 10 posts. Eight of them score 1/4 or 2/4. The voice is generic ("In today's business landscape"). The POV is absent ("Here are 5 productivity tips"). The specifics are missing ("I recently worked with a client"). The ask is nonexistent (post ends with a period, no CTA, no question, no choice).

The founder genuinely believes they're doing content marketing. They're not. They're doing content theatre. The Diagnostic catches it in 60 seconds. [[Personal_branding_coaching_session_2024_09_12]] documents a session where a founder had posted 47 times in 90 days with zero inbound. We scored the last 20. Sixteen scored below 3/4. The issue wasn't consistency. It was invisibility.

Another pattern: founders who hire ghostwriters or use AI tools without a scoring system. The ghostwriter writes in "LinkedIn voice" — polished, professional, grammatically perfect, and entirely forgettable. Or the founder feeds ChatGPT a prompt and ships the output without editing. Both produce content that scores 2/4: decent POV, zero voice, weak specifics, no ask. The Diagnostic would have flagged it before it posted.

I've had clients send me hooks they're proud of. "Does this work?" they ask. I score it: Voice 2/4 (sounds like a TED talk, not them), POV 3/4 (there's a claim), Specificity 1/4 (no names, no numbers, no proof), Asking 2/4 (weak question at the end). Total: 8/16. Below the 12/16 threshold. "Rewrite or don't post," I say. They rewrite. The rewrite scores 14/16. It ships. It books a call within 48 hours. [[Content_review_session_2024_10_08]] documents this exact sequence.

The third pattern: founders who get this intellectually but don't apply it to their own drafts. They'll score someone else's post accurately. Then they write their own, skip the scoring step, and ship something that would have scored 2/4. I built a rule: if you're about to post, run the Diagnostic first. If it's below 3/4, you're not allowed to hit "schedule." This single gate has saved more client pipelines than any other intervention.

The fourth pattern: founders who score well on Voice and POV but fail on Specificity and Asking. Their posts sound like them. They have strong opinions. But they stay abstract. "Most founders struggle with marketing" — who specifically? "I've helped clients solve this" — which clients, what result, what timeframe? The post gets likes from other founders who relate to the vague claim. It gets ignored by the ICP who needs proof. [[Content_audit_2024_11_15]] shows a founder with 23 posts in this exact pattern. We added one specific example with a name and a number to each post. Inbound doubled in three weeks.

The fifth pattern: founders who think the ask is "drop a comment" or "share if you agree." Those aren't asks. They're engagement bait. The real ask is: save this, DM me, book a call, choose between A and B, disagree publicly, or sit with the tension I just created. [[LinkedIn_strategy_session_2025_01_22]] documents a session where we replaced "what do you think?" with "DM me if you're stuck here and I'll send you the exact script I used." The founder went from 3 comments per post to 9 DMs per post. Same content. Different ask.

The most brutal version of this pattern: the founder who's been posting for 18 months, has 8,000 followers, gets 400 likes per post, and has booked zero clients. I run the Diagnostic on their top 30 posts by engagement. Twenty-seven score below 3/4. The 400 likes are coming from other founders, not buyers. The content is peer-validated but ICP-invisible. We rewrite the next 5 posts to pass the Diagnostic. The engagement drops to 150 likes. But 6 ICPs DM. Three book calls. One closes at <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->8K. The founder finally understands: engagement is not the goal. Movement is. [[Personal_branding_breakthrough_2025_02_03]] documents this exact case.

## Scripts and exact phrases

**When scoring Voice:** "Read this out loud. Would you say this sentence on a 1:1 call with a paying client? If no, rewrite it."

**When scoring POV:** "What's the one belief here that half your ICP would disagree with? If there isn't one, you don't have a POV yet."

**When scoring Specificity:** "Name the client. Name the number. Name the date. If you can't, you're still abstract."

**When scoring Asking:** "What do you want the reader to do after reading this? If the answer is 'I don't know' or 'engage with it,' you don't have an ask."

**When a founder says 'but it got 400 likes':** "From who? Show me the profiles. If they're all founders, not buyers, the engagement is peer validation. That doesn't pay your rent."

**When a founder resists rewriting:** "You can post invisible content consistently or visible content occasionally. Pick one. Both require work. Only one books calls."

**When a founder says 'this feels too specific':** "Specific is the only way to be remembered. Vague is the only way to be forgotten. You're not writing for everyone. You're writing for one person who needs this exact thing today."

**When a founder says 'I don't want to share client details':** "Then use your own numbers. 'I went from X to Y in Z timeframe' works. But you need the X, the Y, and the Z. No placeholders."

## Common mistakes

The first mistake: scoring the post after it's live. The Diagnostic is a pre-publish gate, not a post-mortem. If you're analyzing why a post underperformed after the fact, you already failed. Score before you schedule.

The second mistake: scoring only the hook. Founders will rewrite the opening line until it's sharp, then ship a body that's vague and generic. The Diagnostic applies to the entire post. If the hook scores 4/4 but the body scores 1/4, the average is 2.5/4. Still invisible.

The third mistake: confusing engagement with scoring. A post that gets 500 likes can still score 2/4 if it's peer-validated but ICP-invisible. Likes from other founders who relate to the vague claim don't book you clients. Saves and DMs from ICPs do. [[Engagement_vs_conversion_audit_2024_12_20]] documents a founder with 600 likes per post and zero inbound. The posts scored an average of 2.3/4.

The fourth mistake: using the Diagnostic once and assuming you've "got it." Scoring is a skill. It improves with reps. The first 20 posts you score will feel slow and uncertain. After 50, it's automatic. After 100, you can score in 15 seconds while writing the draft.

The fifth mistake: gaming the score instead of improving the content. Founders will add a fake number to hit Specificity or tack on "what do you think?" to hit Asking. The Diagnostic isn't a checklist to hack. It's a lens to see whether the content is actually useful. If you're adding specifics just to pass the score, the reader will feel it. [[Content_manipulation_vs_improvement_2025_01_27]] documents a founder who added "I worked with 3 clients last month" to every post without naming the clients or the results. The posts scored higher but still didn't convert because the specificity was fake.

The sixth mistake: not scoring competitor content. The fastest way to calibrate your scoring is to analyze 20 posts from founders in your space who are booking clients. Score their top posts. You'll see the pattern: they consistently score 3/4 or higher. Then score your own. If yours are below 3/4 and theirs aren't, you know exactly what to fix.

## The contrarian read

Most content advice says "post consistently." The Diagnostic says "post less, score higher."

If you're posting five times a week and three of those posts score below 3/4, you're training the algorithm to show you to the wrong audience. You're teaching your followers to scroll past your content. You're spending 10 hours a week producing invisible work.

The contrarian move: post once a week, score it at 3.5/4 or higher, and ship only that. One high-scoring post per week will generate more pipeline than five invisible posts. I've tested this with 12 clients. Every single one saw better results with lower frequency and higher scoring. [[High_frequency_vs_high_score_test_2024_11_29]] documents the cohort data.

Another contrarian read: AI-generated content can score 4/4 if you edit it properly. The issue isn't that AI writes bad content. The issue is that most founders don't score AI output before posting it. AI writes at 2/4 by default: decent structure, zero voice, weak specifics, no real ask. But if you feed AI a high-scoring example, give it your Voice DNA, and edit the output using the Diagnostic, you can ship AI content that converts. The tool isn't the problem. The lack of a scoring standard is. [[AI_content_scoring_workflow_2025_02_15]] documents the exact process.

The third contrarian read: the Invisibility Diagnostic works better than any LinkedIn algorithm hack. Founders obsess over "post at 8am" or "use carousel posts" or "tag 3 people." None of that matters if the content itself is invisible. I've had clients post at 2am, use text-only format, tag no one, and generate 10 inbound calls in a week because the post scored 4/4. The algorithm rewards content that people save, share, and engage with deeply. The Diagnostic predicts that better than any posting tactic. [[Algorithm_hacks_vs_diagnostic_2025_03_01]] documents six clients who tested this head-to-head.
