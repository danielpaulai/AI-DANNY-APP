## The core principle

A story post isn't a list with a narrative frame. It's an experience the reader walks through with you, where the destination only matters because they felt the journey.

## Why this matters

Most LinkedIn posts that claim to be "stories" are just chronological bullet points with a setup sentence. "Here's what happened. Then this. Then that. Lesson learned." The reader scrolls past because there's no texture, no stakes, no moment where they forget they're reading a post and start living inside the scene.

The difference between a story that lands and one that dies in the feed comes down to specificity and structure. A real story has a person, a place, a problem, and a point where something changes. Without those four elements, you're writing a recap, not a story.

The mechanism is emotional memory. Facts fade. Stories stick. When a founder reads about "the importance of pricing confidence," they nod and move on. When they read about the client call where you told someone to charge <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->5K instead of $8K and watched them physically recoil, then say yes, then close the deal two days later — that scene replays in their head the next time they're about to lowball their own offer.

The cost of getting this wrong is invisibility. Every day, thousands of founders post "lessons learned" threads that score 0/4 on the Invisibility Diagnostic. No voice. No POV. No specificity. No ask. They wonder why LinkedIn feels like shouting into a void. The answer is they never invited anyone into a real moment.

The leverage of getting it right is that one story post can do more for your positioning than twenty advice posts. Because advice is generic. Stories are yours. Nobody else can tell the story of the call where your client said "I need to think about it" and you responded "Walk me through what's still unclear — price, scope, fit, or timing?" and the room went quiet, then they named the objection, then you closed right there. That's a story. It's also a sales script. It's also a positioning claim. All in one post.

## How it shows up in client work

The most common stuck point in 1:1 coaching is when a founder knows they should "share stories" but every draft they write feels either too vulnerable or too boring. They write three paragraphs about their morning routine and ask if it's "personal enough." Or they write a 1,200-word saga about their entrepreneurial journey and wonder why nobody engaged.

The pattern I see across every client who struggles with story posts is the same: they're writing about themselves, not about a specific moment that changed something. The fix is always the same question: "What's one thing that happened this week that surprised you, made you rethink something, or proved a belief you already had?"

When a founder answers that question out loud on a call, the story is already there. The version they write for LinkedIn is worse because they edit out the texture. They replace "I told him to charge <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->5K" with "I encouraged a pricing adjustment." They replace "He went quiet for six seconds" with "He considered the offer." All the life bleeds out.

In [[Workshop_3_-_Content_Strategy_2025_09_11]] I walked Marco, Mario, and Daniel through the pillar framework and content rules. The section on personal brand balance landed hard: publish 95% wins and milestones, 5% challenges framed as hero's journey. That ratio exists because vulnerability without structure is therapy, not positioning. A story post needs tension, but the tension has to resolve in a way that proves your authority, not undermines it.

The best example from client work is in [[Propelus_Transforming_the_Visa_Process_2024_03_10]]. Mehsum's LinkedIn post about his son's visa delay is the canonical story post. It opens with dialogue: "Dad, am I going to make it?" It names the stakes immediately: visa application under review, family trip three days away, first vacation since the divorce. Every paragraph moves the story forward. No filler. No "here's what I learned." The lesson emerges from the story itself.

The structure he used is the one I teach every client:

**Setup** — What was supposed to happen (family trip, mountains, photos, dinner as a family)
**Tension** — What went wrong (visa delayed, 48 hours left, 24 hours, 12 hours)
**Escalation** — What you tried (called everyone, delayed departure, rebooked flights, held onto hope)
**Turn** — The moment something changed (11 hours before rescheduled departure, visa arrived)
**Resolution** — What it meant (son's face at the airport, the hug, "We made it, Dad")
**Reflection** — Why it matters now (the question that haunted him, the business he built)

That post did more for Propelus positioning than a year of "here's why visa inequality matters" posts would have done. Because the reader didn't just learn about visa inequality. They felt what it costs.

In [[Eti_sadis_and_Daniel_Paul_2024_06_18]] I worked with Eti on her content workflow. She was overwhelmed trying to follow every WhatsApp thread, every Notion tutorial, every advanced technique other students were using. The breakthrough moment was when I said: "You don't need Notion. You don't need the advanced version. You need to post one thing this week about a client you helped." The pressure dropped immediately. She posted. It worked. That's a story I've used in three different contexts since then — workshop content, 1:1 calls, and a LinkedIn post about why complexity kills execution. Same story, different frame, always lands.

The pattern across all of these is the same: the story isn't about you. It's about the moment where something changed. The reader doesn't care about your morning routine unless your morning routine caused you to make a $50K decision. They don't care about your entrepreneurial journey unless there's a scene where you almost quit and something specific made you stay.

In [[Building_nasops_transcript-based_documentation_2026_03_11]] I'm working with Danny on the NasOps build. He opens by explaining what the app needs to do, but the real story is buried in the middle: "That one moment at the airport — a simple typo on a visa ruining my family's trip — wasn't just my story. It happens to millions of people every year." That's the hook. That's the moment the post became about something bigger than the product. The app is the resolution to the story. The story is why anyone cares about the app.

The most common mistake is opening with the lesson instead of the story. "Here's why stories matter on LinkedIn…" No. Open with the story. The lesson emerges at the end, or it doesn't need to be said at all.

The second most common mistake is making the story too long. A LinkedIn story post is 300-600 words, not 1,200. You're not writing a Medium essay. You're writing a scene. One moment. One turn. One resolution.

The third most common mistake is writing about something that didn't actually happen. Founders think they need a dramatic origin story or a major life event to justify a story post. The best stories are small. The call where a client said "I can't afford that" and you said "What would you charge if you were me?" and they named a number higher than your price. That's a story. It's also a closing technique. It's also a positioning claim. All in 200 words.

## Specific examples from the vault

The tension escalates in concrete time intervals. Not "we waited for days." 48 hours left. 24 hours. 12 hours. The reader feels the clock ticking.

The resolution lands with a specific image: "I still remember his face at the airport. The smile that made every struggle worth it. The hug that lasted longer than usual. The whispered 'We made it, Dad.'" That's not a summary. That's a scene.

The reflection shifts from personal to universal: "That night, watching my three children together, my wife squeezed my hand and said: 'How many families weren't this lucky today?'" The question reframes the entire story. It's no longer about one family's visa delay. It's about the system. That's the turn that makes the story a positioning asset.

## Scripts and exact phrases

"What's one thing that happened this week that surprised you, made you rethink something, or proved a belief you already had?" — The question I ask every client who's stuck on story posts.

"The story isn't about you. It's about the moment where something changed." — The reframe that shifts a personal brand post from therapy to positioning.

"Open with dialogue, not setup." — The single fastest way to make a story post feel like a story instead of a recap.

"You're not writing a Medium essay. You're writing a scene. One moment. One turn. One resolution." — The length rule for LinkedIn story posts.

"The reader doesn't care about your morning routine unless your morning routine caused you to make a $50K decision." — The test for whether a personal story is relevant.

"The lesson emerges from the story itself. If you have to explain it at the end, the story didn't work." — The rule for when to add a reflection paragraph and when to let the story stand alone.

"A story post isn't a list with a narrative frame." — The definition I use to push back when a client sends me a "story" that's actually a chronological bullet list.

"Vulnerability without structure is therapy, not positioning." — The 95% wins / 5% challenges rule explained in one line.

## Common mistakes

**Opening with the lesson instead of the story.** "Here's why stories matter on LinkedIn. Last week I had a client call that proved this…" No. Open with the call. The lesson comes last, or not at all.

**Making the story too long.** LinkedIn story posts are 300-600 words. If you're writing 1,200 words, you're writing a blog post, not a story post. Cut everything that doesn't move the story forward.

**Writing about something that didn't actually happen.** Founders think they need a dramatic origin story or a major life event. The best stories are small. The call where a client said "I can't afford that" and you said "What would you charge if you were me?" That's a story.

**Editing out all the texture.** The first draft has real dialogue, specific moments, observable details. The final draft replaces "He went quiet for six seconds" with "He considered the offer." All the life bleeds out. Keep the texture.

**Writing about yourself instead of about the moment.** The story isn't your entrepreneurial journey. The story is the moment where you almost quit and something specific made you stay. The story isn't your morning routine. The story is the decision you made before 9am that changed your business.

**Adding a moral at the end when the story already made the point.** If the story worked, the reader already knows what it means. The reflection paragraph should reframe the story in a bigger context, not explain what just happened.

**Using generic details instead of specific ones.** "I had a challenging client call" vs "The client said 'I need to think about it' and I said 'Walk me through what's still unclear — price, scope, fit, or timing?'" The second version is a story. The first is a summary.

## The contrarian read

Most advice on storytelling says "be vulnerable" and "share your struggles." I push back on that. Vulnerability without structure is therapy, not positioning. A story post on LinkedIn isn't a journal entry. It's a positioning asset disguised as a personal moment.

The 95% wins / 5% challenges rule exists for a reason. When you share a challenge, it has to be framed as a hero's journey: here's what I faced, here's what I learned, here's how I used it to get better. The challenge proves your authority. It doesn't undermine it.

The other contrarian take is that the best story posts are boring. Not boring in content. Boring in structure. They don't need twist endings or surprise reveals or narrative tricks. They need a person, a place, a problem, and a moment where something changed. That's it. The Luke Matthews story is "I hired someone with no experience and he was great." There's no twist. The power is in the specificity and the stakes.

Most storytelling advice also says "show, don't tell." I say show AND tell. The story is the showing. The reflection paragraph is the telling. You need both. The story makes it memorable. The reflection makes it usable. Without the reflection, the reader might miss the point. Without the story, the reflection is just advice.

## What it looks like when it's working

A story post that's working gets two types of engagement: emotional reactions and strategic saves. The emotional reactions are comments like "This hit hard" or "I felt this" or "Thank you for sharing." Those are vanity metrics. The strategic saves are DMs that say "I'm about to have that exact conversation with a client — can I steal your line?" or "This is exactly what I needed to hear today." Those are pipeline signals.

The other signal is when someone quotes your story back to you weeks later. Not paraphrased. Quoted. "You said [exact line from your post]. I used it on a sales call yesterday. It worked." That's when you know the story stuck.

The third signal is when the story becomes a referral asset. Someone sends your post to a colleague with "Read this. This is the kind of thinking we need." That doesn't happen with advice posts. It happens with story posts that make the reader see their own situation differently.

Quantitatively, a story post that's working does 2-5x the engagement of an advice post on the same topic. But the real measure isn't likes. It's replies. A story post that lands gets 10-20 replies minimum, and at least 3-5 of those replies are from people in your ICP saying some version of "I'm living this exact story right now."
