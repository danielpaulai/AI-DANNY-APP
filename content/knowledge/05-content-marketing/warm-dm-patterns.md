## The core principle

A warm DM is not a sales pitch. It's a continuation of a conversation that already started when they engaged with your content.

The pattern that closes: acknowledge the specific thing they liked, add one line of value they didn't see in the post, ask a question that costs them something to answer honestly.

## Why this matters

Most founders treat a like or comment like a lottery ticket. They send the same scripted pitch to everyone who engaged, hoping someone converts.

The result: 90% ghost rate. The 10% who reply say "not interested" or "too expensive" without context.

The cost of this approach is invisible until you run the math. If you post three times a week and average 20 engagements per post, that's 60 warm leads a week. At a 5% conversion rate to booked call (industry average for cold outreach), that's 3 calls a month. If your close rate is 30%, you're closing one client per month from 720 warm leads per year.

The same 720 leads, reached with pattern-aware DMs that reference the specific post they engaged with, convert at 15-25%. That's 9-15 calls a month. Four to five closed clients instead of one.

The mechanism: people buy from people who see them. A generic "hey I noticed you liked my post" sounds like a bot. "I saw you saved the post on the 7-day breakthrough pattern — what's the positioning block you're working through right now?" sounds like the start of a real conversation.

The founders who win on LinkedIn treat engagement like an opened door. They walk through it with something specific to say about what they saw on the other side.

## How it shows up in client work

The most common stuck point in client work is the DM script. Founders know they should follow up on engagement, but they freeze because they don't know what to say that doesn't sound like spam.

The pattern I see: they wait three days after someone likes a post, then send "Hey, thanks for engaging with my content. If you ever want to chat about [generic topic], let me know."

No response. They assume the lead was never real.

What actually happened: the lead forgot the post by the time the DM arrived. The message gave them no reason to respond. There was no question, no value add, no specific reference to what they engaged with.

In [[Personal_Branding_session_4_2024_03_12]], I walked Priyanka through setting up her outreach automation using LinkedIn Helper. The key move: export 2,000 accounts, filter by who engaged with the last five posts, and message them within 24 hours with a reference to the specific post they liked.

The script we built: "Saw you liked the post on [specific topic from the post]. The part I didn't have space to add: [one specific insight]. What's been your experience with [related question]?"

Three parts. Acknowledge, add value, ask. The question has to be something they can't answer with yes/no.

In [[Personal_Branding_Session_2_2023_11_15]], Malvika had a 2% reply rate on her DMs because she was sending the same message to everyone: "I help founders with [service]. Would you be open to a call?"

I rewrote her entire sequence. Instead of pitching the service, the first DM referenced the post they engaged with and asked about their current state: "You commented on the pricing post yesterday. Where are you stuck on pricing right now — too high and nobody buys, or too low and the wrong clients show up?"

Her reply rate jumped to 18%. The difference: the question forced them to name their real problem instead of just saying "not interested."

The biggest pattern across all client DM work: the reply rate is always highest when the DM feels like a continuation of the post, not a sales pitch tacked onto it.

In [[Seven_Figure_creators_2024_10_29]], Matthew and I discussed the content-to-DM flow for his creator clients. The insight: DMs convert when they reference a specific line from the post the person engaged with, then add one thing that line implies about their business.

Example: if the post was about "10K followers mean nothing if your bank account shows zero," and someone liked it, the DM might say: "Saw you liked the revenue-first post. Real question: are you currently optimizing for followers or for pipeline? Because I see a lot of people stuck in the middle and it kills both."

The reply comes back because the question is real. They either say "pipeline" and you have a conversation about what's broken, or they say "followers" and you challenge them on whether that's the actual goal.

The pattern that never works: "I saw you liked my post. Want to hop on a call?" No context, no value, no reason to say yes.

The pattern that always works: "I saw you [specific engagement action]. Here's the one thing I couldn't fit in the post: [specific insight]. What's your experience with [specific question related to their business]?"

In [[Nuri Personal branding]], during Nuri's ideation call, we discussed his outreach after speaking events. He was sending generic "great to connect" messages to people who attended his talks. I pushed him to reference a specific question they asked during Q&A, or a specific topic from his talk that resonated, and ask a follow-up question that moved the conversation forward.

The difference: instead of "let's stay in touch," it became "You asked about AGI definitions during Q&A — are you building toward that or just exploring? Because the two paths need different strategies."

That converts because it's not a pitch. It's a continuation of the conversation they already started by asking the question in public.

## Scripts and exact phrases

**The acknowledge-add-ask structure:**
"Saw you [specific action] on [specific post]. The one thing I didn't have space to add: [specific insight]. [Question that names their current state]?"

**After a like:**
"You liked yesterday's post on [topic]. Real question: [diagnostic question about their business]?"

**After a comment:**
"You commented on [post topic]. That tells me you're working through [specific problem]. Where are you stuck right now — [option A] or [option B]?"

**After a share:**
"You reshared the [topic] post. What's the part that resonated most? Because I see founders split on this and I'm curious where you land."

**After a save:**
"You saved the post on [topic]. What are you planning to implement first? Most people pick [common choice] but the higher leverage move is usually [contrarian choice]."

**The follow-up if they don't reply in 48 hours:**
"No reply needed if this isn't relevant right now. But if [problem from original post] is still on your radar, I'd be curious what you've tried so far."

**The transition to a call:**
"This feels like a longer conversation than DMs can handle. Want to jump on a 20-minute call and map this out properly?"

**Handling "not interested":**
"No worries. Out of curiosity, is it not relevant right now, or have you already solved [problem]? Always curious what's working for people."

## Common mistakes

**Mistake 1: Waiting too long to send the DM.** If you wait three days after someone likes your post, they've forgotten it. The window is 24 hours. After that, you're starting a cold conversation.

**Mistake 2: Opening with your service instead of a question.** "I help founders with X. Would you be open to a call?" That's a pitch, not a warm DM. The reply rate is under 5%. Start with a question about their current state.

**Mistake 3: Asking yes/no questions.** "Are you interested in improving your LinkedIn presence?" Of course they are — everyone is. But that question gives them an easy out. Ask a question that forces them to name their specific problem: "What's blocking you from posting consistently — lack of time, lack of ideas, or lack of confidence?"

**Mistake 4: Sending the same message to everyone who engaged.** A like, a comment, and a save are three different signals. The DM should acknowledge which one they did and why it matters. Someone who saved your post is closer to action than someone who just liked it.

**Mistake 5: Making the DM about you instead of about them.** "I noticed you work in [industry]. I specialize in helping [people like you] with [service]." That's a sales pitch. Flip it: "I noticed you work in [industry]. What's the positioning challenge you're hitting right now?"

**Mistake 6: Not adding value in the first message.** If your DM is just "thanks for engaging, let me know if you want to chat," you've given them no reason to reply. Add one line of insight they didn't see in the original post. That earns the reply.

**Mistake 7: Asking for a call too early.** Don't ask for a call in the first DM unless they explicitly asked a question in their comment that needs a longer answer. DM 1 is about starting a conversation. DM 2 or 3 is where you suggest a call, after you've established that there's a real problem to solve.

## The contrarian read

Most LinkedIn advice says to "add value" in DMs. I disagree with how that's interpreted.

Adding value doesn't mean sending a free resource or a link to a blog post. That's content theatre. It sounds helpful but it trains the person to expect free stuff without ever booking a call.

Real value in a DM is asking a question that helps them see their problem more clearly. The question itself is the value, because it reframes what they thought they were stuck on.

Example: someone likes your post about LinkedIn content strategy. The conventional "add value" move is to send them a free content calendar template. That might get a thank-you reply, but it doesn't lead to a call.

The contrarian move: "You liked the content strategy post. Real question: are you stuck on what to post, or are you stuck on what happens after you post? Most people fix the wrong one."

That question is more valuable than the template because it forces them to diagnose where they actually are. Once they name it, you have a real conversation.

Another contrarian take: I don't believe in multi-message nurture sequences in DMs. If someone doesn't reply to your first message within 48 hours, they're not going to reply to your second one unless you give them a reason.

The play isn't to send another "just following up" message. The play is to send one more message that names what you think their silence means: "No reply needed if this isn't relevant. But if [problem] is still live, I'd be curious what you've tried so far."

That converts at 8-12% because it gives them permission to ignore you while also making it easy to re-engage if the problem is still real.

## What it looks like when it's working

You know warm DM outreach is working when your reply rate is above 15%.

Below 10% means your DMs are indistinguishable from cold outreach. You're not referencing the specific post they engaged with, or you're opening with a pitch instead of a question.

Above 20% means you're in the top tier. Your DMs feel like a continuation of the post, not a sales pitch tacked on after.

The second signal: replies that name a specific problem. If people are replying with "yeah I'm interested, tell me more," that's lukewarm. You've gotten their attention but you haven't diagnosed anything yet.

If they're replying with "I'm stuck on [specific thing]," that's a qualified lead. The DM did its job: it moved them from passive engagement (like) to active diagnosis (naming their problem).

The third signal: booked calls within three DM exchanges. If it takes more than three messages to get someone on a call, the conversation is too vague. Tighten the question in DM 1, ask a follow-up in DM 2, suggest a call in DM 3.

Observable outcome: if you're sending 20 DMs a week to people who engaged with your posts, and booking 3-5 calls from those, your warm outreach system is working.

If you're booking fewer than 2 calls, the problem is almost always one of three things: you're waiting too long to send the DM (send within 24 hours), you're opening with a pitch instead of a question, or your question is too vague (make it binary or diagnostic, not open-ended).
