## The core principle

The "decision I made" post is the behind-the-scenes operator confession that builds trust faster than any credibility signal you can buy.

It works because it reveals the founder's internal logic at the exact moment they chose one path over another, including the cost of that choice and what they learned after making it.

## Why this matters

Most founders hide their decision-making process behind polished outcomes.

They post about what worked. They skip the part where they sat in a room staring at two terrible options and picked one anyway.

The result is content that sounds like a highlight reel, not a human being running a business.

The "decision I made" post fixes this. It names the fork in the road, explains why both options felt wrong, shows what the founder chose, and admits what happened next.

This structure matters because trust on LinkedIn is built through specificity, not credibility theatre. When a founder posts "We decided to fire our biggest client," the reader believes them because the decision has a real cost attached. When they post "5 tips for client management," the reader scrolls past because it could have been written by anyone.

The vulnerability is strategic, not performative. It positions the founder as someone who makes hard calls in real time, not someone who waits until the outcome is certain before talking about it.

The leverage comes from showing the mechanism behind the decision, not just the result. When a reader sees the founder's internal calculus laid out in plain language, they start borrowing that thinking for their own business. That's when content becomes useful, not just engaging.

## How it shows up in client work

I see three recurring patterns when founders try to write this structure without guidance.

**Pattern 1: They bury the decision under context.** The post opens with four paragraphs of setup before naming what they actually decided. By line five, the reader has already scrolled. The fix is brutal: open with "I decided to [specific thing]" in the first sentence. Everything else follows from that.

In [[PB_coaching_call_2024_09_12]], a founder wanted to write about switching from retainer to project work. The first draft opened with "Many agencies struggle with cash flow." I stopped them. The hook became "I fired three retainer clients in one week." The post booked two calls in 48 hours.

**Pattern 2: They explain the decision but skip the alternative.** The founder says "I chose X" but never says what Y was or why it felt like the wrong call at the time. This makes the post feel less like a confession and more like a humblebrag. The reader needs to see both options on the table, including the one that looked safer or smarter in the moment.

In [[Content_review_session_2024_11_03]], a client wrote about choosing to niche down. The first draft said "I decided to focus only on SaaS founders." I asked: what were you doing before, and why did you resist this change? The rewrite became "I decided to walk away from every non-SaaS lead, even though half my revenue came from them." Now it costs something.

**Pattern 3: They narrate the outcome too cleanly.** The decision works out, the founder celebrates, the post ends with "best choice I ever made." This kills the credibility because real decisions are messy. Even when they work, there's a cost. The reader needs to see both sides of the trade-off.

In [[Session_debrief_2025_01_08]], a founder posted about shutting down a product line that wasn't selling. The first draft ended with "Now we're fully focused and revenue is up." I pushed back: what did you lose? The final line became "Revenue is up 30%, but I still miss the creative freedom of building that product."

The strongest versions of this post structure name the decision in the hook, explain both options in 2-3 sentences each (including why the unchosen one felt safer), show what happened after the choice was made, and close with what the founder would tell someone facing the same fork.

I've seen this format book calls when nothing else was working. In [[Client_case_study_2024_10_22]], a founder had posted consistently for three months with zero inbound. We rebuilt their feed around five "decision I made" posts. Four of them hit over 20,000 impressions. Three booked discovery calls. The pattern held: specificity about internal logic converts better than generic advice.

## Specific examples from the vault

**Example 1: The pricing decision**

In [[Coaching_call_2024_08_15]], a consultant posted about doubling their rates overnight. The hook was "I told my next three prospects the new price before I believed it myself."

The setup explained the two options: keep charging $5K per engagement and stay fully booked, or charge <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->0K and risk going six months without a client. The post walked through the internal argument: "If I charge $5K, I'm pricing in my own fear. If I charge <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->0K, I might starve."

The decision was made in a single line: "I chose the higher price and sent the proposal before I could second-guess myself."

The outcome was messy: two prospects said no immediately. The third said yes. The post ended with "I made the same revenue in one month that used to take me three, but I also learned that most of my clients were never going to pay more. I was attracting the wrong room."

That post got 14 comments from founders asking how to have the same conversation with their own clients. Vulnerability about pricing fear converts because everyone has that exact fear.

**Example 2: The firing decision**

In [[Content_strategy_session_2024_09_28]], a founder posted about firing a co-founder six months into a startup. The hook was "I asked my co-founder to leave the company we built together, and I'd do it again."

The structure followed the same beat: two options (continue working with someone who wasn't pulling weight vs. go it alone and risk the whole thing falling apart), the internal conflict ("I felt like I was betraying him"), the decision ("I scheduled the call and wrote out exactly what I was going to say so I wouldn't chicken out"), and the messy result ("He hasn't spoken to me since. The company is healthier. Both of those things are true").

That post got saved 47 times. The comments were full of founders admitting they were in the same situation but didn't have the language to act on it.

**Example 3: The niche-down decision**

In [[PB_breakthrough_session_2025_02_04]], a founder posted about walking away from 60% of their leads to focus on one vertical. The hook was "I started saying no to everyone who wasn't a SaaS founder, even though I had no idea if SaaS founders would say yes."

The post named both options clearly: stay generalist and keep closing 40% of leads, or niche and risk going three months with zero revenue. The decision came down to one line of internal logic: "Generalist agencies die slowly. Specialist agencies die fast or grow fast. I'd rather know."

The outcome: "First month, zero revenue. Second month, one client. Third month, three clients, all referrals from that first one. The thing nobody tells you is that niching doesn't make it easier to close deals. It just makes it easier to know who to talk to."

That post booked four calls in a week. All four were SaaS founders who saw themselves in the story.

**Example 4: The content decision**

In [[Workshop_debrief_2024_11_19]], a founder posted about deleting 90% of their content calendar to focus on one message. The hook was "I threw out three months of planned posts and started writing only about the thing I was most afraid to say."

The two options: keep posting safe, engaging content that got likes but no clients, or post the one uncomfortable message that defined their positioning and risk crickets. The decision: "I wrote the post, deleted it twice, then published it at 11pm so I couldn't overthink it anymore."

The outcome: "The post got 40 likes. Average for me. But three people DM'd asking if I could help them with that exact problem. None of them had ever commented on my content before."

The takeaway the founder added: "Engagement is a vanity metric. The real test is whether someone messages you saying 'I have this exact problem.' If they do, the content worked."

## Scripts and exact phrases

**Opening hooks I've seen work:**

- "I decided to [specific action] even though [specific fear]."
- "Last [timeframe], I chose [option A] over [option B], and here's what I'd tell you if you're facing the same choice."
- "I made a decision that felt reckless at the time. Six months later, here's what I know."
- "I told [person] [decision] before I was ready. Here's why I'd do it again."

**The setup language:**

- "The choice came down to two options, both of them bad."
- "Option A: [describe it] — safe, but slow death. Option B: [describe it] — risky, but at least I'd know."
- "I sat in [place] staring at [thing] trying to decide if I had the guts to [action]."

**The decision reveal:**

- "I chose [option]. I sent the [email/message/proposal] before I could change my mind."
- "I made the call. I didn't feel ready, but I did it anyway."
- "I picked [option] knowing that if it went wrong, [specific consequence]."

**The messy outcome language:**

- "Here's what actually happened: [first result]. And also: [second result]. Both are true."
- "I expected [X]. I got [Y]. I learned [Z]."
- "Would I do it again? Yes. Would I do it the same way? No."

**The closing line:**

- "If you're facing this choice, here's what I'd ask yourself: [question]."
- "The thing I didn't expect: [insight]. That's the real cost of [decision]."
- "I don't regret it. But I also don't pretend it was easy."

## Common mistakes

**Mistake 1: Starting with the outcome instead of the decision.** The post opens with "Best decision I ever made" or "Here's what happened when I chose X." This removes all tension. The reader already knows it worked out. The post becomes a humblebrag instead of a confession. Start with the moment of choice, not the happy ending.

**Mistake 2: Making the alternative too obviously wrong.** If the founder presents two options but one is clearly stupid, it's not a real decision. The reader needs to see that both paths had merit. The tension comes from the fact that the wrong choice looked safer or smarter in the moment.

**Mistake 3: Skipping the internal conflict.** The post jumps from "I had two options" to "I chose this one." The reader needs the middle beat: the doubt, the fear, the specific moment the founder decided to act anyway. That's where the human connection happens.

**Mistake 4: Narrating the decision in past tense with no present-day reflection.** The post reads like a story that happened to someone else. It needs the founder's current voice saying "Here's what I know now that I didn't know then." Without that layer, it's just a case study, not a confession.

**Mistake 5: Ending with advice instead of admission.** The post closes with "So here's what you should do" or "3 lessons I learned." This flips the structure from vulnerable to prescriptive. The reader came for the confession, not the lesson plan. End with what the founder would tell themselves if they could go back, not what they'd tell the reader to do.

**Mistake 6: Writing it after the decision has paid off for months.** The best versions of this post are written close to the moment of decision, when the outcome is still uncertain or messy. Writing it a year later removes the rawness. The reader can tell when the founder is reconstructing a story versus living through one.

## The contrarian read

Most LinkedIn advice tells founders to post wins, not doubts.

The logic is that confidence sells and vulnerability repels. Show strength, hide weakness, project authority at all times.

I think that's backwards. Confidence without cost is noise. Every founder on LinkedIn is posting about their wins. The feed is saturated with "we hit X in revenue" and "just closed a huge client." None of it lands because none of it costs anything.

The "decision I made" post works because it shows the founder at the moment before they knew the outcome. That's the only time a reader actually believes the story.

The other contrarian piece: this format doesn't need engagement to work. A post with 30 likes and 2 DMs from the right people is more valuable than a post with 500 likes and zero leads. The "decision I made" structure self-selects. The people who resonate are the people facing that exact fork in the road. Everyone else scrolls past. That's not a bug, it's the filter.

I also push back on the idea that vulnerability needs to be paired with a lesson. The founder doesn't need to teach at the end of the post. Just showing the decision and what happened is enough. The reader will extract their own takeaway. Forcing a lesson at the end makes it feel like the founder is packaging the story for engagement, not living through it in real time.

## What it looks like when it's working

The post gets saved more than it gets liked. That's the signal.

Founders who are facing the same decision bookmark it because they need to come back to it when they're ready to act. Likes are social proof. Saves are utility.

The DMs are specific. People don't say "Great post." They say "I'm in this exact situation. Can I ask you how you knew it was time?" That's when you know the content hit the right person at the right moment.

The post gets referenced in discovery calls. A prospect says "I saw your post about [decision]. That's exactly where I am right now." That's the real conversion event. The post didn't just generate awareness, it qualified the lead before the call even started.

The engagement is uneven. Some sections get highlighted, some get skipped. That's fine. The post isn't written for everyone. It's written for the person who needs that exact decision logic right now. Everyone else is noise.

The founder gets quoted. Other people start using the same framing: "I read a post recently about [decision]. Here's my version of that." That's when the content becomes a template, not just a story. It gives other founders permission to admit the same doubts publicly.

The best versions of this post generate zero engagement but book one perfect client. That's the only metric that matters. Everything else is theatre.
