## The core principle

The three-line pitch is the most compressed format of your positioning that still does actual work in the world.

Not an elevator pitch. Not a tagline. Not a headline. A three-line structure that names the ICP, the transformation, and the mechanism in plain English a 14-year-old would understand. The first line sets up who and what problem. The second line states the outcome with a number and a timeframe. The third line names the how.

If you can't explain what you sell in three lines without jargon, you haven't built an offer yet. You've built a services menu.

## Why this matters

Most founders cannot say what they do in one breath without hedging, meandering, or losing the listener by line two.

They open with industry jargon ("we leverage AI to optimize content workflows"). They describe features instead of outcomes ("we build custom Claude Skills"). They list capabilities instead of solving a problem ("we do personal branding, LinkedIn strategy, content systems, and AI consulting").

The person listening has already checked out. Not because the offer is bad. Because the founder never said who it's for, what changes, or how.

The three-line pitch forces structural clarity. Who is this for. What do they get. How do you deliver it. If any of those three components are vague, the pitch fails. Which means the offer underneath is vague. Which means positioning work still needs to happen.

The leverage is this: once the three-line pitch is locked, every other marketing asset becomes easier to write. The homepage hero section. The first DM. The LinkedIn bio. The sales deck opener. They all flow from the same spine.

The cost of not having this is diffusion. Every conversation starts from scratch. Every piece of content sounds slightly different. Prospects can't repeat what you do to a colleague. Referrals dry up because nobody knows how to describe you.

## How it shows up in client work

The three-line pitch shows up in two contexts: when I'm building a founder's personal brand system, and when I'm reviewing why their content isn't booking calls.

In the first case, we start with the three-line pitch before we write a single post. If the founder can't land this in plain English, we don't move to content strategy. Because every post they write will be a different version of "here's what I think today." No throughline. No compounding. No trust.

Most founders resist the constraint at first. "But I do more than one thing." "But my clients hire me for different reasons." "But the market is more complex than three lines."

That resistance is the work. The three-line pitch is a forcing function. It makes you choose. One ICP. One transformation. One mechanism. Once that's locked, you can expand. But you can't start expanded.

I worked with a founder who ran a fractional CMO practice. He served SaaS companies, eCommerce brands, and B2B service businesses. Three different ICPs. Three different value propositions. Every sales call required him to re-explain himself depending on who was in the room.

We forced the three-line pitch. First line: "I work with B2B SaaS founders stuck at $500K–<!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->M ARR who've tried every growth tactic and nothing's working." Second line: "I take them from random marketing activity to a predictable pipeline system that books 10+ qualified demos a month." Third line: "I do it by building a 90-day content + outreach engine they own and run after I leave."

He hated it at first. "But I work with eCommerce too." I said: "You can. After you close ten B2B SaaS clients and prove the system works. Right now you're trying to be three businesses and you're zero businesses."

Three months later he had closed five clients. All B2B SaaS. All $500K–<!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->M. All from inbound LinkedIn + cold outreach. The pitch worked because it was specific enough to sound like it was written for one person. Cited from [[Client_fractional_CMO_positioning_2024_08_19]].

The second context is content audits. A founder will send me six months of LinkedIn posts and say "I'm posting consistently but nobody's booking calls." I read the posts and none of them connect to a clear offer. Every post is a different thesis. One week it's about AI tools. Next week it's about founder mindset. Next week it's a case study with no call to action.

I ask: "What's your three-line pitch?" They can't answer. Or they give me a paragraph. Or they say "it depends on the client."

That's the problem. If you don't know what you're selling in three lines, the reader doesn't either. Every post becomes content theatre. It gets likes. It doesn't book calls. Because there's no through-line from the post back to the offer.

I had a founder post about "building AI systems for founders" for four months straight. Good posts. Strong engagement. Zero booked calls. I asked for the three-line pitch. He said: "I help founders use AI to save time."

That's not a pitch. That's a category. I pushed: "Who specifically? What outcome specifically? How specifically?"

We rebuilt it: "I work with solo founders running <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->00K–$500K businesses who are doing five jobs manually and burning out." "I take them from 12 hours a week on content, admin, and follow-up to 2 hours by building them a team of 7 Claude Skills." "They own the system. I teach them to run it. Then I leave."

He rewrote his last six posts with that spine. The hooks changed. The CTAs changed. The case studies all pointed at the same transformation. He booked four calls in two weeks. Cited from [[Content_audit_solo_founder_AI_systems_2025_01_22]].

The pattern across every client engagement is the same. The three-line pitch is the diagnostic. If they can land it in one breath, the content strategy is straightforward. If they can't, we stop and fix positioning first.

One more example. A founder came to me with a workshop offer. "I teach founders how to use Claude to build content systems." Good. Who's it for? "Any founder who wants to save time." Not good enough.

We forced the three-line structure. Line one: "This workshop is for founders who post on LinkedIn but hate the grind of writing, editing, and repurposing every week." Line two: "By the end of 90 minutes, you'll have a working Claude Skill that writes posts in your voice and repurposes them into 5 formats." Line three: "You walk out with the Skill, the prompts, and a 7-day production workflow you run yourself."

That became the workshop landing page. 40 signups in 10 days. Every signup said the same thing on the post-event survey: "I knew exactly what I was getting before I joined." Cited from [[Workshop_offer_Claude_content_system_2024_11_03]].

## Specific examples from the vault

The best three-line pitches I've written all follow the same architecture. Setup. Transformation. Mechanism. Here are the specific moments where landing that structure changed the trajectory of a client engagement.

A founder running a LinkedIn ghostwriting agency was stuck at $8K MRR. He had five clients. He wanted to scale to 20. I asked him to write his three-line pitch. He said: "I write LinkedIn content for busy founders so they can build their personal brand and get more leads."

I said: "Who is a busy founder? What's 'more leads'? What's the mechanism?"

He rewrote it: "I work with B2B SaaS founders who need to post 3x a week but have zero time to write." "I take them from invisible on LinkedIn to 5+ inbound demos a month in 90 days." "I do it by writing 12 posts a month in their voice, running A/B tests on hooks, and coaching them on engagement strategy."

The difference is night and day. The first version could describe 10,000 people. The second version describes one person so specifically that every B2B SaaS founder at that stage reads it and thinks "that's me."

He rewrote his website homepage with the new pitch. Inbound inquiries doubled in 30 days. He closed four new clients at <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->K/month each. Cited from [[Ghostwriting_agency_repositioning_2024_09_12]].

Generic. Unmemorable. Sounds like every other AI trainer on LinkedIn.

That became the new workshop description. He posted it once. 50 signups in 48 hours. The comments were all the same: "This is exactly what I need."

The mechanism specificity did the work. "3 working Claude Skills" is tangible. "Content, sales follow-up, client research" names the exact pain points. "Walk out with the Skills" is a concrete deliverable. No fluff. No hype. Just a clear promise. Cited from [[Workshop_AI_Skills_training_2025_02_18]].

"I take them from posting randomly to a system that books 3–5 high-ticket calls a month." ## Scripts and exact phrases

Here are the actual lines I use when building three-line pitches with clients.

"Who is this for? Not 'founders' or 'business owners.' Describe one person with one specific problem."

"What's the outcome? Not 'save time' or 'grow their business.' A number and a timeframe."

"What's the mechanism? Not 'we help' or 'we support.' What do you actually build, deliver, or do?"

"If you were talking to one person at a coffee shop, how would you describe what you do in three sentences?"

"Read it out loud. If you wouldn't say it to a paying client on a real call, rewrite it."

"The first line is the filter. It should exclude 95% of people. That's the point."

"The second line is the promise. If it doesn't have a number or a timeframe, it's not a promise."

"The third line is the differentiator. If someone else in your industry could say the same thing, it's not specific enough."

## Common mistakes

**Mistake 1: Opening with a category instead of an ICP.** "I help founders use AI" is not a pitch. It's a LinkedIn bio. The first line must name a specific person with a specific problem. "I work with solo founders stuck at <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->00K–$300K who are burning out doing five jobs manually" is a pitch.

**Mistake 2: Promising a vague outcome.** "I help them save time and scale their business" could mean anything. "I take them from 12 hours a week on content to 2 hours by building a team of 7 Claude Skills" is a promise.

**Mistake 3: Describing what you do instead of what changes.** "We provide LinkedIn strategy and content coaching" is a service menu. "We take them from invisible on LinkedIn to 5+ booked calls a month in 90 days" is a transformation.

**Mistake 4: Using jargon or corporate language.** "We leverage AI to optimize workflows" sounds like a SaaS company. "We build you a team of 7 Claude Skills that replace a marketing department" is plain English.

**Mistake 5: Listing capabilities instead of solving a problem.** "We do personal branding, content strategy, AI consulting, and LinkedIn training" is four businesses. Pick one. Solve it completely. Then expand.

**Mistake 6: Trying to serve everyone.** "This works for any founder who wants to grow" is the opposite of positioning. The more specific the ICP, the more the pitch resonates.

## The contrarian read

Most positioning advice says "keep it simple." I say keep it specific.

Simple without specificity is vague. "I help founders grow their business with AI" is simple. It's also useless. It doesn't tell the reader who it's for, what changes, or how.

Specificity is what makes a pitch memorable. "I work with solo founders stuck at <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->00K–$300K who are burning out doing five jobs manually" is not simpler. It's longer. But it's infinitely more effective because it describes one person so precisely that they think "that's me."

The other contrarian take: most founders think the three-line pitch is marketing copy. It's not. It's a strategy document. It's the compressed version of your positioning. If you can't write it, you haven't done the positioning work yet.

## What it looks like when it's working

When the three-line pitch is locked, you see three signals.

First: strangers repeat it back to you. Someone hears your pitch on a call and says "so you help solo founders replace their marketing team with AI." That's the test. If they can repeat it, it's clear.

Second: referrals become easier. When someone asks "do you know anyone who can help with X," the person who knows you can describe what you do in one breath. "You should talk to Danny. He works with solo founders who are drowning in content work. He builds them a system that compresses 12 hours a week into 2. They own it after 90 days."

Third: your content starts booking calls. Every post connects back to the same offer. The reader sees the throughline. They book a call because they know exactly what you're selling.

Before the three-line pitch, you're explaining yourself every time someone asks what you do. After, the pitch does the work. That's when positioning compounds.
