## The core principle

The paragraph pitch is not a pitch. It's a filtering device disguised as a sales asset.

Most founders spend 40 hours building a landing page that tries to speak to everyone and converts nobody. The paragraph pitch forces them to write 200 words that name one problem, one person, one outcome, and one mechanism — then stop talking. If it doesn't repel the wrong people as violently as it attracts the right ones, it's not working yet.

## Why this matters

The paragraph pitch is the atomic unit of positioning. Every other marketing asset — the landing page, the LinkedIn bio, the DM opener, the call script — inherits its structure from this single block of text.

Without it, founders wing every conversation. They explain their business differently to every prospect. One day they're "an AI consultant." The next day they're "a LinkedIn agency." The week after that they're "helping founders scale with systems." None of it lands because there's no spine.

The cost shows up in three places. First, pipeline goes quiet because nobody knows what to refer. Second, sales calls take 40 minutes to get to the offer because the founder has to re-explain the context every time. Third, the founder burns out explaining themselves daily to tools, prospects, and platforms that should already know them.

The paragraph pitch fixes all three. Write it once, in their language, with their outcome. Then every asset becomes a remix of that core paragraph. The founder stops re-explaining. The tools inherit the structure. The referrals start naming the exact problem the founder solves.

Russell Brunson calls this "the one thing." Taki Moore calls it "the promise line." Donald Miller's StoryBrand calls it "the stakes statement." Every methodology agrees: if you can't say what you do in one tight paragraph, you don't have a business — you have a consulting menu.

## How it shows up in client work

The pattern repeats across every 1:1 engagement. Founder comes in with a vague offer like "I help businesses grow using AI." I ask: who's the business, what's the before state, what's the after state, how long does it take, and what's the one mechanism that makes it work? They freeze. They've never articulated it in those terms.

So we run the paragraph pitch drill. I pull up the StoryBrand SB7 framework and force them to fill in seven blanks: character (who), problem (external pain + internal frustration + philosophical villain), guide (you), plan (your process), call to action (the ask), failure (cost of inaction), success (life after). Then I collapse all seven into one 150–200 word block that sounds like spoken English.

The first draft is always too long. They list five problems instead of one. They name three ICPs instead of one. They try to say "entrepreneurs, consultants, coaches, and agencies" because they're afraid to cut anyone out. I make them pick one. The clarity comes from the constraint.

Every participant in that December workshop started with a vague statement like "I help people with marketing" or "I teach mindset coaching." By the end of 90 minutes, every single person read their refined one-liner on video using the "You know how most [ICP]..." format. The homework was to record it three times, pick the best take, and post it in the group. That's the forcing function. You don't leave until it's recorded.

The second pattern: founders resist naming one ICP because they're afraid of losing revenue. A client selling LinkedIn consulting to both coaches and SaaS founders kept hedging. "I work with service-based businesses and tech startups." I told him to pick one, run it for 30 days, and measure inbound quality. He picked coaches. Inbound doubled in two weeks because the referrals finally knew who to send. The SaaS founders didn't disappear — they just weren't his focus anymore. The third pattern: founders confuse features with outcomes. They write "We build AI content systems for founders" instead of "We compress 12 hours of content work into 2 hours a week." The second version lands because it names the before-state pain (12 hours), the after-state outcome (2 hours), and the timeframe (per week). The first version is a description. The second is a promise.

The fourth pattern shows up in workshops. I teach the "You know how most [ICP] [struggle with X]? We help them [achieve Y] by [mechanism Z], so they can [transformation]" structure. Half the room tries to start with "Imagine if..." or "What if you could..." I make them delete it and start with "You know how most..." because that phrase forces specificity. You can't say "You know how most people..." — it's too vague. You have to name the actual person.

The paragraph pitch structure became the spine of every output. Without that spine, the AI just generates average landing page copy.

The fifth pattern: founders bury the outcome six paragraphs down. They open with context, credentials, or philosophy. I make them flip it. Lead with the outcome. "We take founders from 12 hours of content work a week to 2 hours." Then explain the mechanism. Then handle objections. But the outcome comes first. The entire framework is built around "big promise upfront, mechanism in the middle, stack at the end." The paragraph pitch follows the same architecture.

Once a founder has their 200-word positioning paragraph, I load it into the Voice DNA doc, the ICP brief, and the system prompt for every content skill. Now the AI doesn't need to be re-explained. It already knows who the founder serves, what problem they solve, and what outcome they promise. ## Scripts and exact phrases

The opening frame for every paragraph pitch build: "If I stopped you in an elevator and asked 'What do you do?', and you had 30 seconds before I got off, what would you say?" This forces the founder to cut everything except the core.

The one-liner drill from [[Founder_OS_AI_Masterclass_4th_Session_2025_12_06]]: "You know how most [specific ICP] [struggle with specific pain]? We help them [achieve specific outcome] by [specific mechanism], so they can [transformation]."

The ICP specificity test: "Could I walk into a conference, spot your ideal client across the room, and know immediately that's the person you serve?" If the answer is no, the ICP is too broad.

The outcome specificity test: "Is there a number and a timeframe in the promise?" If not, it's not a promise — it's a feature list.

The repel test: "Would this paragraph make the wrong people immediately say 'That's not for me'?" If it doesn't repel anyone, it doesn't attract anyone either.

The StoryBrand SB7 compression: "Most [ICP] want [desire] but [obstacle]. That causes [external pain], makes them feel [internal frustration], and that's wrong because [philosophical stakes]. We guide them through [plan] so they can [success] and avoid [failure]." Then collapse all of that into 150 words.

The "so they can" phrase: Every paragraph pitch needs to end with this phrase. It forces the founder to name the life-after, not just the outcome. "We reduce content creation from 12 hours to 2 hours per week" is a feature. "We reduce content creation from 12 hours to 2 hours per week so founders can get back to the work only they can do" is the transformation.

## Common mistakes

**Too many ICPs.** Founders write "entrepreneurs, consultants, coaches, agencies, and small business owners" because they're afraid to cut anyone. The fix: pick one. Run it for 30 days. The others don't disappear — they just stop being the focus.

**No numbers.** Founders write "We help you grow your business" instead of "We help you go from 0 to 10 inbound calls in 90 days." The first is vague. The second is measurable. If there's no number, there's no promise.

**Leading with mechanism instead of outcome.** Founders write "We use AI to build content systems" instead of "We compress 12 hours of content work into 2 hours a week using AI." The second version leads with the pain and the outcome. The first version leads with the tool.

**Burying the outcome six lines down.** Founders open with credentials, backstory, or philosophy. The fix: lead with the transformation. "We take founders from 12 hours of content work to 2 hours a week." Say that first. Explain how later.

**Hedging the promise.** Founders write "We help you potentially improve your marketing" or "We aim to increase engagement." The fix: commit. "We double your inbound leads in 90 days or we keep working until we do." That's a promise. The hedged version is noise.

**Using generic terms.** "We help businesses scale" tells me nothing. "We help solo founders making <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->00K–$500K break through the <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-START::-->M ceiling without hiring a team" tells me everything. The second version names the revenue band, the ceiling, and the constraint.

**Listing features instead of naming one mechanism.** Founders write "We offer strategy, implementation, training, and support." The fix: name the one mechanism that makes it work. "We build you a five-person AI team inside Claude that runs your marketing so you don't need to hire." That's the mechanism.

## The contrarian read

Most positioning advice says "test five versions and see which converts best." I say write one version that scares you because it cuts too deep, then run it for 90 days without changing a word. Testing five versions is just hedging. Pick one ICP. Name one problem. Promise one outcome. Commit.

The second contrarian stance: the paragraph pitch should repel as many people as it attracts. Most founders want universal appeal. I want surgical precision. If a prospect reads your paragraph and says "That's not for me," you just saved yourself a bad sales call. The wrong leads cost more than no leads.

The third stance: don't ask prospects to validate your positioning. They'll always tell you to make it broader. Ask paying clients what problem you actually solved for them, then write the paragraph pitch around that one problem. Prospects want everything. Clients remember the one thing that changed.

## What it looks like when it's working

Referrals start naming the exact problem the founder solves. Instead of "You should talk to Danny, he does marketing," they say "You should talk to Danny — if you're spending 12 hours a week on LinkedIn and not booking calls, he compresses that to 2 hours using AI." That's a qualified referral. The first version is noise.

Sales calls shorten from 40 minutes to 15 minutes because the prospect already knows what they're buying. The founder doesn't spend 20 minutes explaining context. They spend 5 minutes confirming the problem, 5 minutes walking through the mechanism, and 5 minutes closing. The paragraph pitch did the pre-selling.

Every marketing asset inherits the same structure. The LinkedIn bio, the landing page, the DM opener, the email sequence — they all remix the same 200 words. The founder stops rewriting from scratch. They just adapt the core paragraph to the format.

The founder stops re-explaining themselves to AI tools. The paragraph pitch becomes the ICP brief, the system prompt, and the voice DNA doc for every Claude Skill. The tools already know who the founder serves, what problem they solve, and what outcome they promise. The founder types less and gets better output.
