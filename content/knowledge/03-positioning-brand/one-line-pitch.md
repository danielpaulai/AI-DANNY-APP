## The core principle

The one-line pitch is the single hardest sentence you will write in your business and the only one that actually matters.

Everything else negotiates itself once this line lands.

## Why this matters

Most founders spend months building a product and zero hours building the sentence that sells it.

They have a deck. They have a demo. They have case studies. They have testimonials. They have a pricing page with three tiers and a comparison chart.

But when someone asks "what do you do," they stumble through three paragraphs of setup before landing on something vague like "we help businesses leverage AI to optimize their workflows."

That sentence just cost them the sale.

The one-line pitch isn't marketing copy. It's not the tagline on your homepage. It's not the opening slide of your deck.

It's the sentence you say out loud when someone with buying power asks what you do and you have seven seconds before they decide whether to keep listening or politely nod and move on.

If you can't compress your entire value proposition into one sentence that a 14-year-old would understand and repeat correctly to someone else, you don't have an offer yet. You have a concept. Concepts don't close deals.

The cost of getting this wrong is silent. Nobody tells you your pitch was confusing. They just stop replying to your emails. They book the call and then ghost. They say "let me think about it" and you never hear from them again.

The leverage of getting this right is that every other conversation becomes easier. Your LinkedIn profile converts. Your cold outreach books calls. Your discovery calls close faster because the prospect already understands what they're buying before you even start explaining.

## How it shows up in client work

I have run this exercise with 47 founders in 1:1 coaching sessions since January 2024. Every single one came in thinking they had a clear pitch. Not one of them actually did.

The pattern is identical every time: I ask them to tell me what they do in one sentence. They give me three. I stop them and ask again. They give me two sentences and a qualifier. I stop them again. They get frustrated and say "it's complicated."

That's the tell. If your business is "complicated" to explain, your positioning is broken. [[PB_coaching_session_2024_08_15_positioning_breakthrough]]

One client ran a LinkedIn ghostwriting agency. When I asked what he did, he said: "We help B2B founders build their personal brand on LinkedIn through strategic content creation and engagement optimization so they can generate high-quality inbound leads without spending hours on content themselves."

I counted the words out loud. Thirty-two words. Three different value props. Zero specificity. No numbers. No outcome with a timeframe.

I asked him: "If I hired you today, what would I have in 90 days that I don't have now?"

He paused. Then he said: "A LinkedIn profile that books calls."

That's the pitch.

We rebuilt it: "I build LinkedIn profiles that book 5 to 10 inbound calls a month for B2B founders who don't have time to post."

Thirteen words. One outcome with a number. One ICP. One constraint that creates urgency.

He posted that line on LinkedIn three days later. Booked two discovery calls from the post alone. [[PB_coaching_client_win_2024_08_22]]

Another client sold AI implementation consulting to mid-market SaaS companies. His pitch was: "We help SaaS companies integrate AI into their product roadmap and go-to-market strategy to stay competitive in an AI-first market."

I asked: "What does 'integrate AI' actually mean to the buyer? What do they get?"

He said: "A working AI feature that their customers will pay for."

That's the pitch.

We rebuilt it: "I help SaaS companies ship their first revenue-generating AI feature in 90 days without hiring an ML team."

Sixteen words. One outcome. One constraint. One timeframe. One specific pain point removed.

He used that line on a sales call two days later. The prospect said: "That's exactly what we need. When can we start?" [[Sales_call_debrief_2024_09_10]]

The pattern across every breakthrough: the founder already knew what the outcome was. They just buried it under three layers of corporate jargon and feature-speak because they thought sounding professional meant sounding complicated.

It doesn't. Professional means the buyer understands what they're buying in one sentence and can repeat it correctly to their boss without you in the room.

## Specific examples from the vault

One founder came to me selling "AI-powered content repurposing systems for thought leaders." I asked what that meant. He said: "We take long-form content and turn it into social posts."

I said: "So you're a repurposing agency."

He said: "No, we build the system so they can do it themselves."

I said: "So you're a training company."

He said: "No, we give them Claude Skills they can run."

I said: "So what do they walk away with?"

He said: "A content system that turns one hour of recording into a week of posts."

That's the pitch. [[PB_coaching_session_2025_01_20_content_repurposing]]

We rebuilt it: "I build AI content systems that turn one hour of founder recording into seven days of posts."

Fifteen words. One input. One output. One timeframe. The buyer knows exactly what they're getting.

Another founder ran leadership coaching for tech executives. His pitch was: "I help senior leaders unlock their full potential and become more effective in their roles through evidence-based coaching methodologies."

I asked: "What changes in their life after they work with you?"

He said: "They stop working 70-hour weeks and their team performs better."

That's the pitch. [[PB_coaching_session_2024_11_03_executive_coaching]]

We rebuilt it: "I help tech executives cut their workweek from 70 hours to 50 without their team noticing."

Fifteen words. One starting state. One ending state. One specific outcome that sounds impossible but is exactly what the buyer wants.

He used that line in his LinkedIn headline. Booked four inbound calls in two weeks. Three of them closed. [[Client_win_report_2024_11_18]]

The breakthrough moment in every session is the same: I force them to name the outcome with a number and a timeframe. Most founders resist this because it feels too specific, too bold, too "salesy."

That resistance is the problem. Vagueness doesn't protect you from accountability. It just makes you invisible.

## Scripts and exact phrases

Here are the actual lines I use in coaching sessions when a founder's pitch isn't landing:

"If I hired you today, what would I have in 90 days that I don't have now?"

"Say that again in one sentence. Timer starts now."

"If your 12-year-old nephew asked what you do, what would you tell him?"

"What's the number? How many calls, how much revenue, how many hours saved, how many clients?"

"Cut everything before 'so that' and everything after 'without' and see what's left."

"If this sentence was on a billboard and you drove past it at 60mph, would you understand what you're buying?"

"Read that out loud. Would you buy that?"

"That's not a pitch. That's a mission statement. What does the buyer actually get?"

## Common mistakes

The biggest mistake is burying the outcome in the middle of a paragraph and opening with a setup about the market or the problem.

Bad: "In today's AI-driven landscape, businesses are struggling to keep up with the rapid pace of change. That's why we help companies implement AI solutions that drive real value."

Good: "I help mid-market SaaS companies ship their first AI feature in 90 days."

The second mistake is using corporate jargon as a substitute for specificity. "Leverage," "optimize," "strategic," "innovative," "cutting-edge," "transform." These words mean nothing. They're filler.

Bad: "We leverage innovative AI solutions to optimize business processes and drive strategic transformation."

Good: "I compress 12 hours of marketing work into 2 hours a week using AI."

The third mistake is naming the process instead of the outcome. Nobody buys a process. They buy the result.

Bad: "We run a 6-week sprint to audit your content, analyze your ICP, and build a 90-day content calendar."

Good: "I build content systems that book 10+ inbound calls a month for B2B founders."

The fourth mistake is hedging. "We help," "we support," "we enable," "we facilitate." These are weasel words. They signal you're not confident enough to claim the outcome.

Bad: "We help businesses improve their LinkedIn presence."

Good: "I build LinkedIn profiles that book calls."

The fifth mistake is trying to serve everyone. "We work with startups, SMBs, enterprises, solopreneurs, nonprofits, and agencies." If you serve everyone, you serve no one. Pick one ICP and own it.

Bad: "We help any business looking to grow their online presence."

Good: "I help B2B SaaS founders build LinkedIn audiences that convert to pipeline."

The sixth mistake is making it about you instead of the buyer. "We're passionate about," "we believe in," "our mission is." The buyer doesn't care about your mission. They care about their outcome.

Bad: "We're passionate about helping businesses unlock the power of AI."

Good: "I cut your marketing workload in half using AI."

## The contrarian read

Most pitch advice tells you to focus on the problem first. "Start with pain." "Make them feel the problem before you present the solution."

That's wrong for B2B.

Your buyer already knows the problem. They live it every day. Restating their pain doesn't build trust. It wastes time.

The one-line pitch should lead with the outcome, not the problem. The problem is implied.

"I help B2B SaaS founders book 10+ inbound calls a month from LinkedIn" implies the problem: they're not booking enough calls right now. You don't need to say it.

Another contrarian take: most founders think a good pitch needs to differentiate them from competitors. It doesn't.

Differentiation is a positioning choice, not a pitch requirement. Your pitch needs to be clear first, differentiated second.

"I build LinkedIn content systems for B2B founders" is a clear pitch even if 50 other people say the same thing. Clarity beats differentiation when the buyer doesn't understand what you do.

Once the pitch is clear, then you layer in differentiation through proof, specificity, or a contrarian mechanism. But clarity always comes first.

Third contrarian take: most people think the one-line pitch should be clever or memorable. It shouldn't.

Clever is for brand campaigns. The one-line pitch is for sales conversations. It needs to be understood, not remembered.

"I build personal brands that print money" is clever. "I build LinkedIn profiles that book 10 calls a month" is clear. Clear wins.

## What it looks like when it's working

You know your one-line pitch is working when:

The person you're talking to repeats it back to you correctly without you having to clarify.

Your LinkedIn profile converts cold traffic into booked calls without you having to explain what you do on the call.

Your cold outreach gets replies because the pitch lands in the first line of the message.

Your discovery calls close faster because the prospect already understands what they're buying before you even explain how it works.

You can say the pitch out loud at a networking event and the other person immediately knows whether they need what you're selling or they know someone who does.

When you post the pitch as a LinkedIn headline or a pinned post, you get DMs asking for the link to book a call, not asking for clarification about what you mean.

When a client refers you to someone else, they describe what you do in the exact same words you used because the pitch was clear enough to be repeated.

Before the pitch lands, you spend half of every sales call explaining what you do. After it lands, you spend half of every sales call answering "when can we start?"

The difference between a bad pitch and a good pitch is the number of clarifying questions you have to answer. A bad pitch generates three follow-up questions before the buyer even understands what you're selling. A good pitch generates zero.
