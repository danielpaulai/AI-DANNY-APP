## The core principle

A founder picks you because you solve their actual problem, not because you're impressive.

Consultants build credentials. Founders build outcomes. The gap between those two is where most positioning dies.

## Why this matters

Most service providers position themselves like consultants without realizing it. They lead with their methodology. They talk about their framework. They sell process over result. The founder hears "I'm smart and credible" when what they need to hear is "I will fix this specific thing in your business by this date."

The cost of consultant positioning is invisible pipeline. You get calls. People nod. They say "interesting." Then they ghost. Not because your offer is weak but because they left the call unclear on what changes in their world if they say yes.

The founder doesn't care about your LinkedIn growth framework. They care that their content takes 12 hours a week and books zero calls. They don't want to learn your system. They want the system running for them by Friday.

Consultants sell education. You sell implementation. Consultants get paid to advise. You get paid to deliver. The founder who needs advice already has ChatGPT. The founder who needs their problem gone picks you.

The leverage of getting this right is velocity. When positioning is outcome-first, sales cycles collapse. No "let me think about it." No multi-stakeholder approval. The founder sees the gap between where they are and where you'll take them, names a number, and says yes or no on the call.

## How it shows up in client work

The pattern repeats across every intake call. Founder opens with their resume. "I've been in tech 20 years. I was a director at Macy's. Now I'm a CTO at a Series A." They're positioning themselves like a consultant because that's what LinkedIn taught them.

The break happens when you ask: "What's the one thing in your business that if it were fixed next week, you'd call this the best money you spent this year?" They stop. They name the real pain. "My LinkedIn gets likes but nobody books calls." "I post three times a week and my pipeline is empty." "I spend 12 hours on content and get nothing back."

That's the gap. Your job is to name the after-state with a number. Not "I'll help you grow your LinkedIn." That's consultant talk. "In 90 days you'll have 10 inbound calls from ideal clients or we keep working until you do." That's founder talk.

The mistake most people make is thinking the founder wants options. They don't. They want one clear path. Consultants present three frameworks and let the client choose. You present one system and bet your guarantee on it. The founder picks clarity over flexibility every time.

Another pattern: founder positions around their bio instead of their buyer's pain. "I'm a cybersecurity expert with PE experience." Cool. What does the buyer get? The positioning shifted from "hire me" to "fix this."

The speed signal: when a founder sees outcome-first positioning, they don't ask "what's your process?" They ask "when do we start?" Consultant positioning triggers questions. Founder positioning triggers decisions.

The reframe: "What's the dumbest possible way to say what you do so a non-English speaker gets it in one line?" That's the test. If you can't pass it, you're still in consultant mode.

The highest-leverage move in these calls is forcing the constraint. Pick one ICP. Pick one outcome. Pick one timeframe. Consultants keep options open. Founders close options down. The clarity is the differentiation.

Clients who resist the constraint always say the same thing: "But I can help multiple types of people." Yes. And by trying to position for all of them you'll close none of them. ## Common mistakes

**Leading with methodology instead of outcome.** "I use the AIDA framework combined with audience segmentation." The founder hears process. They want result. Flip it: "Your content will book 5 calls a month by end of Q2."

**Positioning around your bio instead of their pain.** Twenty years of experience means nothing if you can't name what changes in their business when they hire you.

**Keeping options open to "serve more people."** You dilute the sale. Every extra ICP you name costs you a conversion. Pick one. Win it. Expand later.

**Using consultant language.** "Leverage," "synergy," "holistic," "empower," "robust." If it sounds like a McKinsey deck, rewrite it in eighth-grade English.

**Talking about frameworks before outcomes.** The founder doesn't care about your five-step system until they know what the system delivers. Lead with the after-state. Show the method second.

**Hedging the promise.** "I'll help you improve your LinkedIn presence." That's a consultant hedge. "Your LinkedIn will book 10 qualified calls in 90 days or we work until it does." That's a founder promise.

## The contrarian read

Most positioning advice tells you to niche down by industry or role. Tech founders. Healthcare CEOs. FinTech CMOs. That's consultant thinking. It assumes the buyer cares about industry fit.

They don't. They care about outcome fit.

A founder running a <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->M SaaS company and a founder running a <!--::DANNY-DISTILL-END::-->M consulting practice have the same problem: content takes 12 hours a week and generates zero pipeline. The solution is identical. The industry is irrelevant.

Positioning by outcome instead of industry lets you serve more people with the same system. You're not "the LinkedIn guy for SaaS founders." You're "the guy who compresses founder content workflows from 12 hours to 2." The SaaS founder and the consultant both buy that.

The second contrarian take: guarantees scare bad clients away. Good. If your positioning includes a risk-reversal strong enough to make you slightly uncomfortable, you've filtered for buyers who expect results, not advice. Consultants avoid guarantees because their value is subjective. Yours isn't. You promised a number. Either you hit it or you don't. The clarity repels tire-kickers and attracts closers.

Third: consultant positioning optimizes for looking smart. Founder positioning optimizes for closing fast. The trade-off is you sound less impressive and book more calls. Most people can't make that trade. They'd rather sound credible than be paid. Your job is the opposite.

## What it looks like when it's working

Sales cycles under two weeks. The call goes from "tell me about your services" to "when do we start" in under 20 minutes.

No "I need to think about it." If the founder says that, your positioning wasn't clear. The outcome-to-price ratio should make the decision obvious.

Inbound DMs that skip the qualification stage. "Saw your post. I have the exact problem you described. Do you have availability next week?" That's outcome-first positioning working.

Referrals that name your specific result, not your general expertise. "Talk to Danny. He took my content from 12 hours a week to 2." That's how you know the positioning landed.

Your LinkedIn content gets replies, not just likes. Likes mean "interesting." Replies mean "I have this problem." Founder positioning triggers replies. Consultant positioning gets saved.

Pipeline that doesn't dry up when you stop posting. If every quiet week kills your calendar, you're still in consultant mode. Founders build systems that feed pipeline whether they post or not.
