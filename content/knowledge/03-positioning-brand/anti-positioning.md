## The core principle

Anti-positioning is not what you refuse to be — it's what you refuse to pretend to be.

Most positioning work tells you what to claim. Anti-positioning tells you what to stop claiming. The work isn't addition. It's subtraction. The clarity comes from naming the businesses, the people, the offers, the labels that will forever confuse your ICP if you don't explicitly reject them.

## Why this matters

Every founder lives inside a category that already exists in the market's mind.

When someone lands on your profile, reads your headline, opens your site — they are pattern-matching you against every other thing they've seen that looks similar. If you look like a content agency, they assume you're a content agency. If you sound like an AI consultant, they treat you like every other AI consultant they've already ignored.

The cost of unclear anti-positioning is not "some confusion." The cost is invisible rejection. People scroll past you because they think they already know what you do — and they've already decided they don't need it.

The founders who never define their enemies end up looking like everyone. The founders who define their enemies look like no one. That's the entire game.

When [[Cain_Ransbottyn_One_on_One_Personal_Branding_(_advanced)_2025_04_22]] came to the call stuck, the problem wasn't that he didn't know what he wanted to be. The problem was he hadn't named what he refused to be. He'd rebranded everything to "Chaos Agency" without asking: does this make me sound like every other agency? The answer was yes. The work wasn't adding a new brand layer. The work was cutting off everything that made him generic.

Anti-positioning is the single highest-leverage clarity tool because it answers the question prospects actually ask: "Is this person another version of the thing I already said no to?"

## How it shows up in client work

The pattern appears in every single 1:1 personal branding session within the first 15 minutes.

Founder says: "I help founders scale with AI."

I ask: "So does every AI consultant on LinkedIn. Who are you NOT?"

Founder pauses. No answer.

That pause is the work. Until they can name the specific competitor, the specific category, the specific label they refuse — they cannot position themselves. They're floating. Invisible.

In [[Cain_Ransbottyn_One_on_One_Personal_Branding_(_advanced)_2025_04_22]], I asked Cain directly: "What is the outcome you expect out of changing this personality?" He wanted lazy consultant income, four hours a week, high-priced. But his brand looked like every chaotic agency doing everything for everyone. The breakthrough wasn't what to add. It was what to cut. He had to decide: are you the ninja, or are you chaos? Pick one. Kill the other. The clarity followed.

Say it explicitly or stay invisible.

## Scripts and exact phrases

"Who are you NOT?"

"Most people in this space do X. I don't. Here's why."

"I'm not an agency. I'm not a ghostwriter. I'm not an AI consultant. I'm a new category at the intersection."

"Unlike [competitor who does X], we do Y."

"This is not [generic category label]. This is [specific thing]."

"If you're looking for [thing I refuse to be], I'm not your person. But if you want [thing I actually am], let's talk."

"We reject [specific practice] because [specific reason]."

"I don't [verb that competitors do]. I [verb that I do instead]."

## Common mistakes

**Rejecting in private, embracing in public.** Founders tell themselves "I'm not an agency" but then write LinkedIn headlines that say "Helping businesses scale with content." That's agency language. The anti-positioning has to show up in every output.

**Rejecting categories without naming them.** "I'm different from other marketers" is not anti-positioning. "I'm not a brand strategist, not a growth hacker, not a content marketer — I'm a personal brand systems builder" is anti-positioning. Name the thing. Reject it explicitly.

**Rejecting competitors instead of categories.** "I'm not like CompanyX" is too specific. "I'm not a SaaS platform, I'm a done-with-you build" is category-level rejection. That's what matters. Prospects don't care about CompanyX. They care about the category.

**Anti-positioning without follow-through.** Saying "I'm not an agency" then offering agency services is worse than saying nothing. The anti-positioning has to match the offer, the process, the pricing, the delivery. All of it.

**Soft rejections.** "We take a different approach" is not anti-positioning. "We don't do retainers, we don't own your voice, we don't farm work to cheaper labor" is anti-positioning. Hard lines. Clear rejections.

**Rejecting things that don't confuse your ICP.** Anti-positioning is only valuable if it cuts off confusion. If your ICP never confuses you with X, rejecting X is noise. Focus on the categories that actually blur the lines.

## The contrarian read

Most positioning advice says: "Define what makes you unique."

Anti-positioning says: "Define what you refuse to be mistaken for."

The difference is everything.

Uniqueness is abstract. Rejection is concrete. Prospects don't evaluate you in a vacuum. They evaluate you against the last five things they saw that looked similar. If you don't explicitly reject those five things, you are those five things in their mind.

The other contrarian take: anti-positioning is more valuable than positioning.

Positioning tells me what you are. Anti-positioning tells me what you're not. In a noisy market, knowing what you're not is faster, clearer, more decisive. It cuts through the clutter immediately.

Most founders spend 90% of their time on positioning and 10% on anti-positioning. Flip it. Spend 90% of your time naming what you refuse to be. The positioning will clarify itself.

## What it looks like when it's working

Your LinkedIn headline explicitly rejects a category. Not "Different from X" but "Not X."

Prospects reply to your DMs with: "Oh, you're not like the other [category]? Tell me more."

When you introduce yourself at a networking event, people don't ask "So you're a [generic label]?" They ask "Wait, what's the difference between you and [competitor]?"

Your website has a section titled "We are not..." and it lists 3-5 specific rejections with reasons.

Your content includes at least one post per month that explicitly rejects a common practice in your industry. And it gets engagement because people feel seen.

Your sales calls start with prospects saying: "I know you're not [thing I've already rejected], so tell me what you actually are."

Your ICP can name what you're not before they can name what you are. That's the signal. If they know your anti-positioning, they'll figure out your positioning.
