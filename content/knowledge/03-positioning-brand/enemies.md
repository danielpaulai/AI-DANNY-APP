## The core principle

You cannot build a category without naming what you're against.

Every position requires an enemy. Not a competitor. An enemy. The thing you reject so completely that your entire offer is built in opposition to it.

## Why this matters

Most founders try to differentiate by listing features. Better this. Faster that. More personalized. More AI-powered. More scalable.

None of it lands. Because nobody buys features. They buy escape routes from the thing they hate.

If you can't name the enemy you're fighting, you don't have positioning. You have a list of capabilities that sound like every other founder on LinkedIn.

The cost of not naming enemies is invisibility. Your content gets scrolled past. Your offers get compared to cheaper alternatives. Your sales calls turn into "let me think about it" because the prospect doesn't feel the pain sharply enough to act.

The enemy clarifies everything. It tells prospects who you're NOT for. It tells you what content to write. It tells you which clients to turn away. It makes your offer the only logical choice for people who hate the thing you hate.

## How it shows up in client work

I see this pattern constantly in onboarding calls. Founder sits down. I ask what they do. They tell me they help companies with AI transformation. Or they build personal brands for executives. Or they provide fractional CMO services.

All true. All useless.

Then I ask: what are you sick of seeing in your industry? What makes you want to burn the whole thing down and rebuild it differently?

That's when the real positioning shows up. The ghostwriter model that owns the founder's voice. The AI consultant who sells tools instead of outcomes. The content agency that farms work to cheap labor in the Philippines and charges a premium for it.

One founder I worked with in [[Client_call_2024_09_12]] was selling LinkedIn content services. Completely invisible. Everyone sells LinkedIn content services. When I asked what he hated most about the space, he said: "Everyone treats it like a vanity game. Post count. Engagement rate. Likes. None of it connects to revenue."

That became his positioning. Not "I help you grow on LinkedIn." Instead: "I help founders turn LinkedIn into a client channel, not a vanity metric."

The enemy: content theater. Posting for likes instead of pipeline.

Suddenly every piece of content he wrote had a clear angle. Every sales call had a built-in disqualifier. If a prospect said "I want more engagement," he knew they weren't the client. If they said "I want more booked calls," that was the signal.

Another pattern from [[Personal_branding_coaching_2024_11_03]]. Client was a cybersecurity consultant trying to position against other consultants. I asked what pissed him off most about the industry.

He said: "Everyone sells fear. Compliance checklists. Scare tactics about breaches. Nobody talks about turning security into a competitive advantage or a revenue driver."

That flipped his entire offer. Not "I help you secure your systems." Instead: "I turn your security posture into a sales asset that closes enterprise deals faster."

The enemy: fear-based compliance selling.

Everything changed. His LinkedIn content became contrarian. His outreach scripts highlighted what he WASN'T doing. His discovery calls opened with: "I'm not here to scare you about breaches. I'm here to show you how security becomes a growth lever."

The pattern repeats. Founders try to position by addition. More features. More experience. More capabilities. It doesn't work because it's not memorable and it's not different.

Positioning by subtraction works. I am NOT that thing you hate. Here's what I do instead.

## Specific examples from the vault

The NAS Academy engagement in [[Danny_Nas_Academy_s_Zoom_Meeting_2023_12_15]] shows what happens when you don't have clear enemies. I was running social media for a curriculum company. Posted content that was working. Team jumped in with "this is too long to read" and "you need to add emojis."

None of them posted on social media. None of them understood the shift happening on LinkedIn where emoji-heavy posts now read as ChatGPT slop. They were applying 2019 rules to a 2023 game.

The mistake: I didn't have a documented enemy at that point. So every critique felt like it had equal weight. No clear line to say "this is the approach we reject and here's why."

If I'd had enemies defined, the response would have been simple: "We're not optimizing for LinkedIn's 2019 algorithm. We're positioning against AI-generated generic content. That means longer posts with real voice and zero emojis. That's the strategy."

The lack of enemies meant I was defending tactics instead of operating from a clear position.

Contrast that with [[Building_a_closing_playbook_381021dc]] where I built a sales skill based on Alex Hormozi's framework. Hormozi has extremely clear enemies. The "let me think about it" objection. The "I need to talk to my spouse" delay tactic. The "this is too expensive" non-buyer.

Every close script in that playbook is designed to kill a specific enemy. Not handle an objection politely. Kill it. Reframe it so completely that the prospect sees their own excuse as the problem, not your price.

Example from the playbook: when someone says "I need to think about it," the enemy isn't hesitation. It's the absence of a forcing function. The script doesn't accommodate the thinking. It forces a decision:

"Totally fair. Walk me through what's still unclear. Price, scope, fit, or timing?"

Then you isolate the real objection and work it in the room. The enemy is indecision. The weapon is immediate clarity.

In [[Building_nasops_transcript_based_documentation_78e52862]] I was building an operations app for NAS.IO. The team was drowning in manual work. Every session needed a summary doc. Every campaign needed custom emails. Every transcript needed to be formatted by hand.

The enemy wasn't "too much work." The enemy was agencies and freelancers who would own that work forever. If they hired someone to write summaries, that person became a bottleneck. If they outsourced email writing, they never learned the skill internally.

So the positioning became: "This app makes your team the system owner. Not dependent on an agency. Not waiting on a freelancer. You paste the transcript. The AI writes the doc. You own it."

Every feature decision after that was evaluated against the enemy. Does this keep the work internal or does it create external dependency? If it created dependency, we didn't build it.

## Scripts and exact phrases

When a founder tries to position without enemies, I use this script:

"Name three things you refuse to do that everyone else in your space is doing."

If they can't name three immediately, they don't have positioning yet.

When a client says "I don't want to be negative" or "I don't want to attack competitors," I use this reframe:

"You're not attacking people. You're rejecting approaches. There's a difference. Rejecting the ghostwriter model isn't attacking ghostwriters. It's defining what you won't do and why."

When someone names a weak enemy like "bad service" or "lack of expertise," I push harder:

"Everyone says that. What's the SPECIFIC thing you've seen that made you want to build this differently?"

That forces them past the generic complaint into the actual pattern they're fighting.

In sales calls, when someone asks why my approach is different, I don't list features. I name the enemy:

"Most agencies own your voice. I build the system with you so you own it. That's the entire difference."

When writing LinkedIn hooks, the enemy shows up in the first line:

"Most founders post content. Almost none of them ship a system."

"Your LinkedIn isn't a brand problem. It's a workflow problem."

"The AI hype is wrong about one thing: it doesn't replace marketers. It replaces marketing tools."

Each one rejects the obvious assumption in one sentence.

## Common mistakes

The biggest mistake is naming a competitor as the enemy instead of naming an approach.

"We're better than Jasper" is not an enemy. "We reject AI tools that don't learn your voice" is an enemy.

"We're not like other agencies" is not an enemy. "We reject the retainer model that traps founders in dependency" is an enemy.

Second mistake: naming an enemy that doesn't matter to the ICP. A founder told me his enemy was "corporate bureaucracy." His ICP was solo founders. They don't have corporate bureaucracy. The enemy was irrelevant.

The enemy has to be something your ICP actively experiences and hates. If they don't feel the pain daily, naming it as your enemy doesn't create positioning.

Third mistake: hedging the enemy with softening language. "We're not really fans of the ghostwriter approach" is weak. "Ghostwriters own your voice. We reject that model completely" is clear.

Fourth mistake: naming too many enemies. One client listed seven different things he was against. Content mills. AI slop. Vanity metrics. Engagement pods. Corporate jargon. Hashtag spam. Fake urgency.

All true. All diluted.

Pick one to three max. The sharpest positioning comes from the sharpest rejection.

Fifth mistake from [[Comprehensive_personality_analysis_05ec6ae2]]: thinking the enemy has to be external. Sometimes the enemy is an internal pattern. Perfectionism. Over-delivering. Saying yes to every request. Founders trying to do five jobs manually.

Those are valid enemies. Naming them helps prospects self-identify.

## The contrarian read

Conventional wisdom says you shouldn't be divisive. Don't alienate potential customers. Stay neutral. Appeal to everyone.

That's how you become invisible.

The entire personal branding industry teaches founders to be likeable. Relatable. Authentic. Share your story. Be vulnerable. Connect emotionally.

None of it matters if you don't have a clear position. And you can't have a position without rejecting something.

I'd rather have 100 people who strongly agree and 1000 who strongly disagree than 10,000 who mildly like the content but never buy.

Strong enemies create strong positioning. Weak enemies create weak positioning. No enemies means no positioning at all.

The other contrarian take: enemies aren't permanent. They evolve as the market evolves.

In 2019 the enemy was "doing everything manually." In 2023 the enemy became "using AI tools that don't know your voice." In 2025 the enemy is "content that sounds like everyone else's AI output."

The pattern you're fighting changes. The need for an enemy doesn't.

## What it looks like when it's working

You know your enemies are clear when prospects disqualify themselves in the first DM.

Example: I posted about rejecting the ghostwriter model. Someone messaged: "Do you do ghostwriting?" I said no. They said "Oh I'm looking for someone to just write for me." Conversation over in 30 seconds.

That's working. The enemy filtered the wrong fit before we wasted a call.

You know it's working when your content gets polarizing responses. Some people save it. Some people unfollow. If everyone just scrolls past, the enemy isn't sharp enough.

You know it's working when clients say your exact positioning back to you in discovery calls. "I saw you reject the agency model. That's exactly why I'm here."

You know it's working when pricing conversations get easier. If the prospect already knows what you're NOT doing, they're pre-sold on what you ARE doing. The close becomes: do you want this result or not?

Numbers: before naming enemies clearly, my sales calls converted at maybe 20%. After sharpening the enemy (agencies that own your voice, AI tools that don't learn you, content theater that doesn't drive pipeline), conversion went to 60%. Same offer. Same price. Sharper positioning.

The signal in content: posts with a clear enemy in the hook get 3x the engagement of posts that just state a position. "Most founders do X" performs. "Here's what I believe about X" doesn't.
